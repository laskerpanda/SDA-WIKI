    The Rights We Wrote Down — Studio Packet¶

    Studio Code: WH-02 Subject Area: World History — Era 3 (Age of Revolutions), with Era 1–4 connections Suggested Cycle: Year 1, Cycle 3 Duration: 6 weeks

    World History Era Timeline — highlighted segment(s) indicate this studio's historical period.

    The Essential Question¶

    When people write down their rights, what changes — and what doesn't?

    Why This Studio Matters¶

    From Hammurabi's Code (1750 BCE) to the UN Declaration of Human Rights (1948) to Malala's UN speech (2013), humans have been writing down their rights for 3,700 years. The list grows longer with each generation. And yet every document on this list was written in the shadow of specific injustices that were still happening — or that would happen again.

    This studio traces a single thread across all four WH eras: the recurring human project of codifying rights in writing. Students work with five to seven documents from the Rights Chain and investigate a deceptively simple question: Does writing rights down actually work?

    Active Standards¶

    Standard What You're Targeting
    WH.6_12.3 + Era 3 Revolutionary ideologies and the spread of rights-based political thought
    WH.6_12.5 + Era 3 Causes and effects of the Atlantic Revolutions
    WH.6_12.6 Historical continuity and change: how the rights tradition evolves
    9-10.R.9 Analyzing argument — how each document constructs its claims about rights
    9-10.W.4 Argument writing with multiple sources and a clearly defended claim

    ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk¶

    This studio is built for crosswalk — analyzing how rights arguments are constructed is both the ELA skill and the SS content at the same time.

    • Common Sense (Paine) — It's one of the highest-leverage dual-use texts in the library. Analyzing Paine's argument structure for R.9 and W.4 (it's a masterclass in persuasive writing) simultaneously satisfies WH.6_12.4-5 + Era 3. There's a dedicated crosswalk entry for it: Common Sense + W.4 Crosswalk.

    • Wollstonecraft, A Vindication of the Rights of Woman — R.9 analysis (how does Wollstonecraft construct her argument? who is her audience? what does she assume?) is identical to WH.6_12.4 + Era 3 analysis (how did Enlightenment ideas about rights extend — and fail to extend — to women?). It's in the ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk.

    • UN Declaration of Human Rights — Analyzing it for R.9 (what claims does it make? what evidence or reasoning does it provide? what does it assume?) also satisfies WH.6_12.6 + Era 4 (historical continuity: how does the rights tradition culminate here?). In the crosswalk table.

    • The rights chain as a whole — A Demo of Learning that traces how rights language evolves from Hammurabi to the UN Declaration is both a W.4 argument (ELA) and a WH.6_12.6 analysis of historical continuity (SS). One demo, two standards.

    A single Demo of Learning can satisfy both an ELA and a WH standard if your Studio Contract names both. See the ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk for the full map.

    The Rights Chain¶

    These documents trace a single lineage of rights-claiming across 3,700 years. You don't need to use all of them — choose at least three that span at least two eras.

    Document Date What Rights It Claims What It Leaves Out
    Hammurabi's Code c. 1750 BCE Protection under law; limits on arbitrary power Enslaved people; women; non-Babylonians
    Pericles' Funeral Oration 431 BCE Democratic participation; equal access to law Women; enslaved; metics (non-citizens)
    Magna Carta 1215 CE Limits on royal power; due process Commoners; anyone not a baron
    Common Sense — Paine 1776 Popular sovereignty; self-governance Enslaved people; women
    Declaration of the Rights of Man 1789 Liberty, equality, and the consent of the governed Women (see Wollstonecraft); enslaved in colonies
    Wollstonecraft — Vindication 1792 Women's full citizenship and education Class (she focuses on middle-class women)
    Haitian Declaration of Independence 1804 Racial equality; end to colonial slavery How the reparations demand complicates the legacy
    FDR — Four Freedoms 1941 Freedom of speech, religion, from want, from fear — globally Still "as a matter of principle" — no enforcement
    UN UDHR 1948 Universal rights for all humans everywhere No enforcement mechanism; 48–0 with 8 abstentions
    Malala — UN Speech 2013 Girls' right to education globally Still happening; UDHR Article 26 is 65 years old

    Inquiry Angle Menu¶

    Trailblazer angles: - Choose three documents from the Rights Chain and trace how the definition of "rights" changes across them. Who is included in each? Who is excluded? - Compare the Declaration of Rights of Man (1789) and the Haitian Declaration of Independence (1804). Why did Haiti have to write its own declaration? What did France's version leave out?

    Maverick angles: - The Rights Chain keeps getting longer — more documents, more rights, more people included. Does this mean progress is real? Or does the growth of the list reveal how incomplete each document was? - Wollstonecraft read the Declaration of Rights of Man and immediately wrote the Vindication of the Rights of Woman. What exactly did she see in the document that required her response? Analyze both. - Three documents on this list were written by people under direct threat: the Haitian Declaration (Dessalines was leading an armed revolution), Malala (nine months after being shot), Mandela (facing death at his sentencing). How does writing rights under threat change the document?

    Phoenix angles: - Design the argument for a rights document that would genuinely include everyone the previous ones left out. What would it say? What enforcement mechanism would it have? Use evidence from every document that preceded it. - The UN UDHR was adopted by 48-0 with 8 abstentions. Research: who abstained, and why? What does the abstention pattern reveal about the limits of "universal" rights?

    Six-Week Arc¶

    Week Phase What You're Doing
    1 Launch Read the Rights Chain overview table above. Choose three or more documents that interest you. Write a one-paragraph first reaction: Does this chain represent genuine progress, or the same exclusions repeated with different words? Submit your Studio Proposal.
    2 Dig Deep read your selected documents. For each: What rights does it claim? For whom? Who wrote it and why? What did they leave out — and was that omission deliberate? Build your source log with at least one secondary source (scholarly context on your chosen era).
    3 Build Draft your deliverable. Your draft should take a clear position on your inquiry angle — you're not just describing the documents, you're arguing something about them.
    4 Shape Peer review and advisor conference. Bring one passage from your texts that you think is the strongest evidence for your argument — and one that complicates your argument. Revise with those in mind.
    5 Finish Complete demonstration of learning. Polish deliverable. Prepare for exhibition.
    6 Publish Exhibition. Reflection. Archive.

    Demonstration of Learning Options¶

    Mode Prompt for This Studio
    1 — Written Assessment In 500–700 words: Select two documents from the Rights Chain at least 100 years apart. Analyze how the argument for rights changes between them — what's the same, what's new, and what each still leaves unresolved.
    2 — Extended Writing Argument essay (700–1,000 words): Does the Rights Chain represent genuine progress toward universal rights, or does it represent the same exclusions being redefined with each generation? Argue a clear position with evidence from at least three documents.
    3 — Verbal Conversation Advisor conference: Walk through your selected documents, explaining the central rights claim of each, what rhetorical strategy it uses, and what your analysis found about the continuity and change across the chain.
    4 — Visual/Creative Visual timeline or infographic: Map the Rights Chain with annotations showing what each document adds, what it leaves out, and how each responds to the documents before it. Must include analysis, not just dates and names.
    5 — Multimedia/Performance Documentary-style video or recorded presentation walking the audience through the Rights Chain — what the documents say, what the gaps reveal, and what your argument about the chain is. Minimum 8 minutes.
    6 — Portfolio Annotation Curate existing work that demonstrates WH.6_12.3+6 and R.9. Write metacognitive commentary connecting your work to specific passages from at least two Rights Chain documents.

    Deliverable Ideas¶

    1. "The Rights Gap Report" — A designed document that maps every Rights Chain document against a specific population (women, enslaved people, colonized peoples) and analyzes what each document promises them vs. what they actually received.

    2. "The Next Document" — A draft of a new rights document, written for 2025, that addresses what the UN UDHR still hasn't delivered. Include: the rights you're claiming, who is currently denied them, and an argument for why they must be recognized now. Accompanied by a research essay explaining your choices.

    3. "The Rights Chain Timeline" — A large-format visual exhibit tracing the 10 documents with annotations, quotes, and analysis. Exhibition format: gallery walk where you explain your analysis to visitors.

    4. "Two Declarations" — A comparative analysis essay or website examining the Declaration of Rights of Man (1789) and the Haitian Declaration of Independence (1804) — what Haiti's document reveals about France's, and what both reveal about the limits of "universal" in 1804.

    5. "Rights on Paper, Rights in Practice" — A research presentation examining one right from the UDHR (e.g., Article 26: right to education) and tracing the gap between what the document promises and what actually happened. Uses Malala's speech as a case study.

    Scale Tasks¶

    2.0 — Foundation¶

    • Correctly describe what each Rights Chain document claims and when it was written
    • Define: popular sovereignty, natural rights, declaration vs. treaty, civil rights, human rights
    • Identify at least one group excluded from each document

    3.0 — Target¶

    • Analyze the rhetorical strategy of at least two documents: How does each author argue for the rights they're claiming? What assumptions do they share? Where do they differ?
    • Trace the pattern across at least three documents: What grows in the Rights Chain? What never changes?
    • Identify the tension in the chain: every document responds to an injustice that was already happening. Does writing rights down create rights, or just name them?

    4.0 — Transfer¶

    • Argue a clear position on whether the Rights Chain represents real progress — using evidence that cuts against your position
    • OR: Identify the structural reason why every rights document leaves someone out — and argue whether this is a flaw in the specific documents or an unavoidable feature of how rights-claiming works
    • OR: Apply the Rights Chain framework to a contemporary rights claim that isn't on the list

    Why This Is Relevant Today?¶

    The rights chain this studio traces — from Hammurabi to Pericles to Magna Carta to the Enlightenment to the UDHR to Malala — is not finished. Every link in that chain was contested at the time, and every link is still contested now. The Magna Carta's argument that rulers must follow laws is the foundation of every constitutionalism debate happening in any country today. Wollstonecraft's argument that women have the same rational capacity as men — which seemed radical in 1792 — is still being contested in countries where women cannot vote, drive, or travel without permission. The UN Declaration of Human Rights was adopted in 1948 and has no enforcement mechanism; the gap between what it says and what governments do is a live political question every day. When students look at debates about refugee rights, indigenous rights, LGBTQ rights, or disability rights, they are looking at the current edge of this chain — arguments about who the rights humans wrote down actually apply to. Malala isn't historical — she's still alive, still working, and the question she's arguing (does every child have a right to education regardless of gender?) is still unsettled in dozens of countries.

    Exhibition Format¶

    Suggested format: Symposium with rotating presentations.

    Each student presents their deliverable for 5–7 minutes, then takes 3 minutes of questions. Audience members (classmates, invited guests) ask at least one question each.

    Alternatively: a gallery walk if deliverables are visual/designed artifacts.

    What gets scored at exhibition: - C.1 — formal presentation: register, organization, eye contact, handling of questions - C.6 — discussion engagement: how you respond to questions; how you engage with classmates' work

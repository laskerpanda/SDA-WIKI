## What This Episode Is About

Green uses the Thanksgiving myth as a lens for examining Puritan colonization — arguing that the story Americans tell about the Pilgrims is almost entirely constructed for political purposes (it became a national holiday during the Civil War). He covers who the Puritans actually were (English Calvinists trying to "purify" the Church of England), why they came (religious freedom for themselves, not tolerance for others), what their relationship with the Wampanoag actually looked like (complex political alliance, not a friendly feast), and how Puritan New England ended in King Philip's War — one of the most devastating wars per capita in American history.

---

## Key Concepts Covered

- Separatists vs. Puritans: the difference and why it matters
- The Mayflower Compact (1620): early self-governance document — often cited as a foundation of American democracy, but Green complicates this
- Squanto/Tisquantum: his actual story (previously kidnapped by English slave traders, spoke English, returned to find his entire village dead of disease) — not the friendly helper of the Thanksgiving myth
- Massasoit and the Wampanoag alliance with Plymouth: a calculated political decision, not goodwill
- The Massachusetts Bay Colony and John Winthrop's "City upon a Hill" speech (1630) — the origin of American exceptionalism rhetoric
- Puritan theology: covenant theology, predestination, the idea that New England was a sacred experiment
- Roger Williams and Anne Hutchinson: expelled from Massachusetts for disagreeing with Puritan leadership — the limits of Puritan "religious freedom"
- King Philip's War (1675–1678): Metacom (King Philip) leads a coalition against English expansion; ~600 English colonists and 3,000 Native Americans killed; proportionally one of the deadliest wars in US history
- When Thanksgiving was actually established: Lincoln's 1863 proclamation during the Civil War — what that context tells us about why the myth was needed

## Watch Prompt

Green is making an argument about why Americans believe a myth about the Pilgrims that isn't historically accurate.

**Track the argument:**
1. What is the Thanksgiving myth as most Americans know it?
2. What does Green say actually happened? (List 3 specific corrections)
3. Why does Green think the myth was created and when? (Be specific — he gives a date)
4. Whose interests does the myth serve?

After watching: Write one sentence — **Is it harmful to teach historical myths, or is there value in national stories even if they're not accurate?** You don't have to pick a side — but you have to back up what you say with evidence from the episode. This targets **9-10.R.9c** (evaluating how argumentative reasoning supports or undermines the author's purpose).

## Studio Ideas

1. **The Myth Audit:** Students list everything they "know" about Thanksgiving before watching. After watching, they mark each item: accurate / inaccurate / complicated. Then they write a paragraph: which inaccuracy surprised you most, and why does it matter? Builds 9-10.R.9 and forces honest engagement with prior knowledge.

2. **Winthrop's "City upon a Hill" Close Read:** Students read the 2-3 most famous lines of Winthrop's 1630 speech and analyze: What is he promising? Who is included in his vision? Who is excluded? Then they find one modern political speech that echoes this language and annotate the connection. Targets 9-10.R.7 (style, syntax, word choices) and WH.6_12.6 (past to present).

3. **King Philip's War — The Numbers:** Green mentions the casualty figures. Students contextualize them: if today's population (330 million) lost the same proportion as English colonists did in King Philip's War, how many deaths would that be? Why does scale matter for historical understanding? Then: why do most Americans know about the Pilgrims' "first Thanksgiving" but not King Philip's War? Targets historical thinking and OEC skill strand.

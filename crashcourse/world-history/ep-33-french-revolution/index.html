    CrashCourse World History: The French Revolution¶

    Episode: 33 · Playlist: World History · Runtime: ~13 min YouTube: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lTTvKwCylFY Era: Era 3 — Age of Revolutions WH Standard: WH.6_12.3 — Analyze the effects of different political systems on people's lives ELA Connection: R.9 — Analyze the development and interaction of informational and argumentative elements in nonfiction text; evaluate how argumentative reasoning supports or undermines the author's purpose

    What This Episode Is About¶

    Green covers the French Revolution from its origins in fiscal crisis and Enlightenment ideology through the Reign of Terror and Napoleon's rise — arguing that it represents the moment when political power became permanently detached from inherited birth and attached instead to "the people," however violently that idea was contested. He treats the Revolution not as a single event but as a decade-long process that kept eating its own leaders. The episode is particularly strong on why the Revolution turned violent: not because revolutionaries were inherently bloodthirsty, but because the logic of popular sovereignty creates a problem — who speaks for "the people" when the people disagree?

    Key Concepts Covered¶

    • Causes: fiscal crisis from American Revolution debts, grain shortage, Estates system (First/Second/Third Estate), Enlightenment ideas about natural rights
    • The Three Estates and the tax problem: clergy (1%) and nobility (2%) exempted from taxation while the Third Estate (everyone else) bore the burden
    • The Tennis Court Oath (1789): the National Assembly refusing to disband
    • The storming of the Bastille (July 14, 1789) as symbol vs. as event — only 7 prisoners freed
    • The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen (1789) — influenced by American Declaration, extended (in theory) to all men
    • Olympe de Gouges and the Declaration of the Rights of Woman (1791) — the revolution's limits
    • The execution of Louis XVI (1793) — regicide as political statement
    • The Reign of Terror under Robespierre: 17,000 official executions, 40,000 total deaths
    • Robespierre's logic: the Republic of Virtue required eliminating its enemies — the logic of revolutionary purity
    • Thermidorian Reaction: Robespierre's own execution; the Revolution devouring itself
    • Napoleon as heir and betrayer of revolutionary ideals
    • Green's argument: the French Revolution established the modern political vocabulary — left/right, citizen, nationalism — that still structures global politics

    Watch Prompt¶

    As you watch, track the central tension of this episode:

    The French Revolution was supposed to be about liberty, equality, and the rights of all people. But it produced the Reign of Terror — mass executions in the name of those same ideals.

    Annotation task — the paradox tracker: | Revolutionary ideal stated | How it was violated or distorted | Who paid the price | |---------------------------|----------------------------------|-------------------| | Liberty | | | | Equality | | | | Popular sovereignty | | |

    After watching: Write one sentence answering this question — Is it possible to create a free society by force? Then find one piece of evidence from the episode that either supports or complicates your answer.

    This connects to WH.6_12.3 (analyzing effects of political systems on people) and 9-10.R.9c (evaluating whether argumentative reasoning supports the author's purpose — Green argues a specific thesis about the Revolution's legacy).

    Key Lines (from memory / known content)¶

    "The French Revolution gave us the political vocabulary we still use today. Left and right, as political terms, come from where people sat in the National Assembly in 1789."

    "Robespierre believed that the Republic of Virtue was only possible if its enemies — internal and external — were eliminated. The problem is that list of enemies kept growing."

    "The guillotine was, in a perverse way, an egalitarian instrument. Before it, aristocrats were beheaded and commoners were hanged. The guillotine gave everyone the same death. Whether that's progress is another question."

    "The French Revolution didn't end with liberty, equality, and fraternity. It ended with Napoleon. But it changed what political power meant. After 1789, you had to at least claim to rule in the name of the people."

    Skinny Connection¶

    • WH.6_12.3 Political Systems — Era 3 — political power, revolution, and popular sovereignty
    • WH.6_12.2 Religion & Ideology — Era 3 — Enlightenment ideology driving the revolution
    • WH.6_12.5 Causes & Effects — Era 3 — fiscal crisis → revolution → Terror causal chain

    Studio Ideas¶

    1. The Purity Spiral: Students read a 1-paragraph secondary source description of Robespierre's logic (the Republic of Virtue required eliminating enemies) and connect it to Green's argument. Then they find one modern historical parallel — any case where a movement's internal purges destroyed the movement. This targets WH.6_12.6 (past to present) and 9-10.R.9 (evaluating argumentative reasoning).

    2. Olympe de Gouges Witness Account: Students write a first-person paragraph from de Gouges's perspective responding to the Declaration of the Rights of Man — using evidence from the episode about what the Revolution promised and who it excluded. Builds 9-10.R.9a (author's POV and purpose) while doing historical empathy work.

    3. Revolution Scorecard: Students create a simple rubric (Did this revolution achieve its stated goals? Partially? Not at all?) and score the French Revolution on liberty, equality, women's rights, and economic justice — using evidence from the episode. Then they compare it to the American Revolution (ep. 32) using the same rubric. Forces comparative historical analysis, WH.6_12.3.

    4. Left and Right Origins: The political vocabulary claim Green makes at the end (left/right seating in the National Assembly) is a strong "past to present" hook. Students research one current political debate framed as "left vs. right" and ask: does this framing come from 1789 thinking? What gets lost when we use this framework? Targets WH.6_12.6 and OEC skill strand.

    Why This Is Relevant Today?¶

    The French Revolution produced a template that has repeated itself across two centuries: popular uprising → declaration of universal rights → radicalization and factional infighting → state terror justified as necessary → conservative reaction → strongman who "restores order" while keeping the revolution's rhetoric. You can trace that arc through the Russian Revolution, the Iranian Revolution, and many others. Green's observation that the French Revolution invented our political vocabulary — "left" and "right" come from where delegates sat in the National Assembly — is a reminder that we're still arguing in the categories the Revolution created. Every time someone invokes "liberty, equality, fraternity" to justify a crackdown, or asks who the revolution is actually for when it arrives, they're repeating 1789's central argument. If you want to see how the revolution's promises played out elsewhere, compare this episode to ep-31: The Haitian Revolution — the most radical test of whether those promises were universal.

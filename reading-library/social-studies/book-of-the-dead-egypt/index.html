    The Egyptian Book of the Dead (c. 1550–50 BCE)¶

    Author: Ancient Egyptian scribes and priests Type: Primary Source · Religious/Funerary Text Era/Period: WH Era 1 — Ancient Civilizations Standards: WH.6_12.2 · WH.6_12.3 · WH.6_12.4 · R.9

    What This Text Is About¶

    The Weighing of the Heart from the Papyrus of Ani (c. 1275 BCE). British Museum. The deceased's heart is weighed against the feather of Ma'at.

    The Book of the Dead is a collection of spells, prayers, and instructions written on papyrus scrolls and placed in tombs to guide the dead through the Egyptian afterlife. The most important section — the Negative Confession and the Weighing of the Heart — reveals what ancient Egyptians believed about justice, morality, and what a good life looked like. It is one of the oldest moral codes in human history, predating the Ten Commandments by centuries.

    Read It¶

    Translation by E.A. Wallis Budge (public domain). Three key selections below.

    The Negative Confession (Spell 125) — Declarations Before the Gods¶

    When the dead person reaches the Hall of Two Truths, they must recite these declarations before 42 gods. Each declaration is a denial of a sin. This is the Egyptian version of a moral code — not "here is what you must do" but "here is what I have not done."

    1. Hail, Usekh-nemmt, who comest forth from Anu, I have not committed sin.

    2. Hail, Hept-khet, who comest forth from Kher-aha, I have not committed robbery with violence.

    3. Hail, Fenti, who comest forth from Khemenu, I have not stolen.

    4. Hail, Am-khaibit, who comest forth from Qernet, I have not slain men or women.

    5. Hail, Neha-her, who comest forth from Rasta, I have not stolen grain.

    6. Hail, Ruruti, who comest forth from Heaven, I have not purloined offerings.

    7. Hail, Arfi-em-khet, who comest forth from Suat, I have not stolen the property of the gods.

    8. Hail, Neba, who comest and goest, I have not uttered lies.

    9. Hail, Set-qesu, who comest forth from Hensu, I have not wasted food.

    10. Hail, Utu-nesert, who comest forth from Het-ka-Ptah, I have not uttered curses.

    11. Hail, Qerrti, who comest forth from Amentet, I have not committed adultery.

    12. Hail, Her-f-ha-f, who comest forth from thy cavern, I have made none to weep.

    13. Hail, Basti, who comest forth from Bast, I have not eaten the heart [i.e., I have not grieved uselessly or felt consuming envy].

    14. Hail, Ta-retiu, who comest forth from the night, I have not attacked any man.

    15. Hail, Unem-snef, who comest forth from the execution chamber, I am not a man of deceit.

    16. Hail, Unem-besek, who comest forth from Mabit, I have not stolen cultivated land.

    17. Hail, Neb-Maat, who comest forth from Maati, I have not been an eavesdropper.

    18. Hail, Tenemiu, who comest forth from Bast, I have not slandered a man.

    19. Hail, Sertiu, who comest forth from Anu, I have not been angry without just cause.

    20. Hail, Tutu, who comest forth from Ati, I have not debauched the wife of any man.

    21. Hail, Uamenti, who comest forth from the Khebt chamber, I have not debauched the wife of any man. [repeated for emphasis in the original]

    22. Hail, Maa-anuf, who comest forth from Per-Menu, I have not polluted myself.

    23. Hail, Her-uru, who comest forth from Nehatu, I have terrorized none.

    24. Hail, Khemiu, who comest forth from Kaui, I have not transgressed the law.

    25. Hail, Shet-kheru, who comest forth from Urit, I have not been wroth.

    26. Hail, Nekhenu, who comest forth from Heqat, I have not shut my ears to the words of truth.

    27. Hail, Kenemti, who comest forth from Kenmet, I have not blasphemed.

    28. Hail, An-astf, who comest forth from thy secret place, I am not a man of violence.

    29. Hail, Sera-kheru, who comest forth from Unaset, I have not been a stirrer up of strife.

    30. Hail, Neb-heru, who comest forth from Netchfet, I have acted with undue haste.

    31. Hail, Sekhiu, who comest forth from Uten, I have not judged hastily.

    32. Hail, Neb-abui, who comest forth from Sauti, I have not taken vengeance upon the god.

    33. Hail, Neferiu, who comest forth from Het-nefert, I have not multiplied my speech overmuch.

    34. Hail, Tem-Sepu, who comest forth from Tetu, I have never cursed the king.

    35. Hail, Ari-em-ab-f, who comest forth from Tebu, I have not fouled the water.

    36. Hail, Ihi, who comest forth from Nu, I have not spoken with a high voice.

    37. Hail, Uatch-rekhit, who comest forth from Sau, I have not cursed the god.

    38. Hail, Neheb-kau, who comest forth from thy cavern, I have not stolen from the god.

    39. Hail, Tcheser-tep, who comest forth from the shrine, I have not defrauded the offerings of the gods.

    40. Hail, An-a-f, who comest forth from Auker, I have not plundered the offerings to the blessed dead.

    41. Hail, Hetch-abhu, who comest forth from Ta-she, I have not filched the food of the infant, neither have I sinned against the god of my native town.

    42. Hail, Unem-hauau, who comest forth from Mant, I have not slaughtered with evil intent the cattle of the god.

    The Weighing of the Heart¶

    After the Negative Confession, the dead person's heart is weighed against the feather of Ma'at (truth/justice) on a scale. Anubis, the jackal-headed god, manages the scales. Thoth, the ibis-headed scribe god, records the result. If the heart is heavier than the feather — burdened by sin — the monster Ammit devours it, and the person ceases to exist. If it balances, the person enters eternal life.

    O my heart which I had from my mother! O my heart of my different ages! Do not stand up as a witness against me, do not be opposed to me in the tribunal, do not be hostile to me in the presence of the Keeper of the Scales! You are my ka which was in my body, the Khnum who made my limbs sound. Go to the happy place to which we hasten; do not make my name stink to the courtiers who make men. Do not tell lies about me in the presence of the god; it is indeed well that you should hear!

    Thus says Thoth, Judge of Truth of the Great Ennead which is in the presence of Osiris: Hear this word of very truth. I have judged the heart of the deceased, and his soul stands as a witness for him. His deeds are righteous in the great balance, and no sin has been found in him. He has not diminished the offerings in the temples, he has not destroyed what has been made, he has not gone about with deceitful speech while he was on earth.

    The Great Ennead then says to Thoth who dwells in Khemenu: This utterance of yours is true. The vindicated one, Osiris, is straightforward. He has no sin, there is no accusation against him before us. Ammit shall not be permitted to have power over him. Let there be given to him offerings which Osiris issued and a permanent grant of land in the Field of Offerings as for the Followers of Horus.

    Spell 17 (excerpt) — The Creation¶

    This short passage from Spell 17 describes the primordial moment of creation — before the world existed, there was only Nun, the watery chaos. The god Atum speaks himself into existence.

    I am Atum when I was alone in Nun. I am Ra in his first appearances when he began to rule that which he had made. Who is this? It is Ra, when he began to rule that which he had made, when he began to appear as a king, before the props of Shu had been created, when he was on the hill which is in Hermopolis.

    I am the Great God who came into being of himself. Who is this? The Great God who came into being of himself is Water, that is Nun, the father of the gods. Otherwise said: He is Ra. He created his names, the lord of the Ennead. Who is this? He is Ra, creator of the names of his limbs which came into being in the form of the gods in the retinue of Ra.

    ELA Standards This Connects To¶

    • R.9 — The Negative Confession is a structured argumentative form: 42 parallel claims of innocence before divine authority. Students analyze what each denial reveals about what Egyptian society considered a sin — this is evidence-based inference from a primary source.
    • R.7 — The repeated structure ("Hail, [god], who comest forth from [place], I have not...") is a deliberate rhetorical and ritual choice. Students analyze how the form of the Negative Confession creates its meaning.

    Social Studies Standards This Connects To¶

    • WH.6_12.2 — The Book of the Dead is direct primary source evidence of Egyptian religious belief: the afterlife, divine judgment, Ma'at (cosmic order/truth), and how religion shaped every aspect of Egyptian life including what counted as moral behavior.
    • WH.6_12.3 — The weighing of the heart by Osiris reveals a divine political system: the gods hold court, keep records (Thoth), enforce judgment (Anubis/Ammit), and dispense eternal justice. This is governance imagined as divine bureaucracy.
    • WH.6_12.4 — The 42 declarations reveal what Egyptian society valued economically and socially: property rights, honest weights and measures, care for the hungry, respect for the dead. Compare to Hammurabi's Code for another angle on ancient law.

    Key Vocabulary¶

    Term Definition
    Ma'at The Egyptian concept of truth, justice, balance, and cosmic order — also personified as a goddess whose feather is used to weigh the heart
    Negative Confession The list of 42 sins the dead person denies having committed; not a confession of wrongdoing but a declaration of innocence
    Ammit The "Devourer of the Dead" — a monster with the head of a crocodile, the body of a lion, and the hindquarters of a hippo. She eats hearts that fail the weighing, destroying the person completely
    Osiris The god of the dead and the afterlife; king of the Hall of Two Truths and judge of the dead
    Thoth The ibis-headed god of writing, wisdom, and record-keeping; he records the result of the weighing
    Papyrus The plant-based writing material used in ancient Egypt; the Book of the Dead was written on papyrus scrolls placed in tombs
    Hall of Two Truths The divine courtroom where the dead person's heart is weighed and their fate decided

    Key Passage¶

    O my heart which I had from my mother! O my heart of my different ages! Do not stand up as a witness against me, do not be opposed to me in the tribunal.

    This is the dead person speaking directly to their own heart before it is weighed — one of the oldest examples in human writing of a person confronting their own conscience and begging it not to betray them. It reveals that Egyptians believed the heart itself remembered every action.

    Studio Ideas¶

    1. "What Does Egypt Think a Good Person Does?" — EQ: What can a list of sins tell you about what a society values? · Standard: WH.6_12.2 + WH.6_12.4 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (analyze 8–10 of the 42 declarations and write: what does this reveal about what mattered in Egyptian society? Compare to a modern moral code.)

    2. "Divine Justice vs. Human Justice" — EQ: Who gets to decide what's right — gods or humans? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + WH.6_12.2 · Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation or W.4 argument (compare the Egyptian weighing of the heart to Hammurabi's Code: which system is more "just"? What does each assume about who has authority?)

    3. "The Weight of a Life" — EQ: What would your heart weigh? · Standard: R.9 · Demo Mode: Creative/Written (write your own Negative Confession in the Egyptian style — 20 declarations of what you have and have not done. Then reflect: what does your list reveal about what YOU think a good life looks like?)

    Why This Is Relevant Today?¶

    Every culture in history has had to answer the same questions the Egyptian Book of the Dead tries to answer: What happens when we die? Does how we live determine what comes after? What do we owe the dead? Ancient Egypt's answer — that the deceased's heart would be weighed against a feather representing truth and justice — is an early version of the idea that character matters more than social status. The Book of the Dead's emphasis on Ma'at (truth, justice, balance) as the measure of a life connects to every contemporary debate about fairness, accountability, and what it means to live well. It's also a reminder that questions modern people treat as belonging to religion or philosophy are ancient — humanity has been wrestling with mortality, meaning, and justice for as long as we have writing.

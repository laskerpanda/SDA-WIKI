    Julius Caesar (1599)¶

    Author: William Shakespeare Type: Literary Text · Play · Primary Source (Roman History and Elizabethan Political Thought) Source: Public domain — 1623 First Folio Full Text: Project Gutenberg — free full text · Folger Shakespeare Library — annotated Era/Period: Era 1 — Ancient Civilizations (Roman Republic) · Era 2 — Renaissance (Elizabethan England) WH Standards: WH.6_12.3 · WH.6_12.5 ELA Standards: 9-10.R.8 · 9-10.R.9 · 9-10.W.4

    What This Text Is About¶

    Julius Caesar is Shakespeare's 1599 dramatization of the assassination of Julius Caesar in 44 BCE and the civil war that followed. Based on Plutarch's Lives, it stages the central political question of the Roman Republic's collapse: when is it legitimate to kill a political leader to preserve democratic institutions? Brutus believes Caesar's ambition is a threat to the Republic and leads the assassination. Mark Antony uses Caesar's funeral to turn the Roman people against the conspirators and trigger a civil war. The Republic never recovers — Caesar's death leads directly to the Roman Empire under Augustus.

    This text is dual-use: It is an ELA 10 district text for studying rhetoric, dramatic structure, and character complexity. It is also a primary source for understanding how Shakespeare's Elizabethan audience thought about political legitimacy, tyrannicide, and the fragility of republics — concerns that were live political questions in 1599 England, where Elizabeth I had no heir and the question of succession was a constant source of anxiety.

    Read It¶

    Public domain. Full text at Project Gutenberg.

    Act I, Scene 2 — 'The Fault, Dear Brutus'¶

    Cassius persuading Brutus — 'The fault, dear Brutus, is not in our stars / But in ourselves that we are underlings'

    Cassius: The fault, dear Brutus, is not in our stars Hanging ourselves in their disdainful winks, But in ourselves that we are underlings. To whom do lions whine? Who'll go seek for leather When he can find no hide to make him a pair of pumps?

    The fault, dear Brutus, is not in our stars / But in ourselves that we are underlings

    Act II, Scene 1 — Brutus Justifies the Conspiracy¶

    Brutus' soliloquy reasoning that Caesar must die for Rome's good — not because he has acted badly but because he might

    Brutus, though thou hast no reason, To kill a man who is thy enemy, And yet by thy own act and valour proved A noble Roman.

    not because he has acted badly but because he might

    Act III, Scene 2 — Antony's Funeral Speech¶

    Antony's 'Friends, Romans, countrymen' speech — the key rhetorical turns

    Friends, Romans, countrymen, lend me your ears; I come to bury Caesar, not to praise him. The evil that men do lives after them, The good is oft interred with their bones; So let it be with Caesar. The noble Brutus Hath told you Caesar was an ambitious man; If it were so, it was a grievous fault, And grievously hath Caesar answered it.

    the key rhetorical turns

    Act IV, Scene 3 — 'There is a tide in the affairs of men'¶

    Brutus on timing, opportunity, and the need to act at Philippi

    There is a tide in the affairs of men, Which, taken at the flood, leads on to fortune; Omitted, all the voyage of their life Is bound in shallows and in miseries. On such a full sea are we now afloat, And we must take the current when it serves, Or lose our ventures.

    timing, opportunity, and the need to act at Philippi

    ELA Standards This Connects To¶

    • R.8 — Shakespeare constructs extraordinary character complexity through dramatic irony and self-deception. Brutus is honorable and wrong; Cassius is manipulative and not entirely wrong; Caesar is vain and genuinely dangerous; Antony is cynical and genuinely grieving. Students analyze how Shakespeare uses the same dramatic events to reveal different truths about each character's nature.
    • R.9 — The funeral speeches in Act III Scene 2 are the greatest single classroom text for rhetorical analysis in the English language. Brutus argues with logos and ethos; Antony systematically dismantles Brutus's argument with pathos, irony, and strategic repetition of "Brutus is an honorable man." Students analyze both speeches for argumentative structure, rhetorical technique, and audience manipulation.
    • W.4 — Antony's funeral speech is the best demonstration in any text of how to make an argument without appearing to make one. Use as a W.4 model for: How do rhetorical choices (irony, repetition, emotional escalation) do the argumentative work that explicit claims cannot?
    • R.7 — Shakespeare's language choices are precise and deliberate: the formal verse of powerful characters vs. the prose of crowds; the meter breaking down in Brutus's moment of doubt; Antony's speech escalating from calm to fury.

    Social Studies Standards This Connects To¶

    • WH.6_12.3 — The play dramatizes the actual collapse of the Roman Republic — the same historical event that WH Era 1 standards cover. Caesar's assassination and the civil war that followed ended the Republic and established the Empire. Students use the play to understand: What political conditions make democratic institutions vulnerable? Why did the conspirators fail to save the Republic by killing Caesar?
    • WH.6_12.5 — The play is an analysis of political cause and effect: What caused the Republic to collapse? Was it Caesar's ambition, or the conspirators' failure to plan beyond the assassination, or the Roman mob's susceptibility to Antony's manipulation, or structural weaknesses in Roman republicanism that made authoritarian takeover inevitable? Students construct a multi-cause argument.

    Key Vocabulary¶

    Term Definition
    Tyrannicide The killing of a tyrant — considered legitimate (even heroic) in classical thought under certain conditions; Brutus believes this is what he is doing
    Republic A system of government in which power is held by elected representatives — the Roman Republic was the model for the U.S. Senate and separation of powers
    Triumvirate A ruling group of three — after Caesar's assassination, Antony, Octavian, and Lepidus form the Second Triumvirate to govern Rome
    Plebeians Common Roman citizens — the "mob" whose allegiance Antony wins in Act III; their fickle loyalty is central to the play's political argument
    Elizabethan context Shakespeare wrote this in 1599 for an English audience deeply anxious about political succession — the play's questions about legitimate power were not abstract for them

    Key Passages¶

    Act III, Scene 2 — Brutus's funeral speech (logos):

    Romans, countrymen, and lovers! hear me for my cause, and be silent, that you may hear: believe me for mine honour, and have respect to mine honour, that you may believe: censure me in your wisdom, and awake your senses, that you may the better judge. If there be any in this assembly, any dear friend of Caesar's, to him I say, that Brutus' love to Caesar was no less than his. If then that friend demand why Brutus rose against Caesar, this is my answer: Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved Rome more.

    Act III, Scene 2 — Antony's funeral speech (pathos/irony):

    Friends, Romans, countrymen, lend me your ears; I come to bury Caesar, not to praise him. The evil that men do lives after them; The good is oft interred with their bones; So let it be with Caesar. The noble Brutus Hath told you Caesar was ambitious: If it were so, it was a grievous fault, And grievously hath Caesar answer'd it. Here, under leave of Brutus and the rest— For Brutus is an honourable man; So are they all, all honourable men...

    Why these passages matter: Brutus makes a clean, logical argument and loses the crowd. Antony makes no direct argument at all — he claims to be doing the opposite of what he's doing — and destroys Brutus. The crowd that cheered Brutus is calling for the conspirators' deaths within minutes. This is the most teachable demonstration of ethos/logos/pathos in any district text.

    Act and Scene Guide¶

    Act Historical Event ELA Focus
    I Conspiracy forms; Caesar warned Character motivation; dramatic irony; political argument
    II Brutus deliberates; Calpurnia's dream Soliloquy as inner argument; self-deception as character flaw
    III.1 Assassination Dramatic irony; staging violence; cause-effect
    III.2 Funeral speeches — the central scene R.9 rhetorical analysis; W.4 argument structure
    IV Civil war begins; Brutus and Cassius quarrel Complex character; tragedy of principled failure
    V Battle of Philippi; suicides Consequences; the Republic's failure

    Studio Ideas¶

    1. "Brutus Was Right (But Still Failed)" — EQ: Can a person be principled and wrong at the same time? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + R.8 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (analyze Brutus's reasoning for joining the conspiracy — use the text. Was his assessment of Caesar accurate? Was his plan rational? Was his failure inevitable? Argue a position with evidence.)

    2. "Antony's Speech: A Masterclass in Manipulation" — EQ: What is the difference between persuasion and manipulation? · Standard: R.9 + W.4 · Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation (analyze Antony's Act III speech line by line — where does he use irony? Where does he appeal to emotion? At what point does the crowd turn? Why does his method work when Brutus's doesn't?)

    3. "Republic to Empire" — EQ: How does a democracy become a dictatorship? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + WH.6_12.5 · Demo Mode: Visual/Creative (create a cause-and-effect map showing the collapse of the Roman Republic — from Caesar's rise through the assassination to the civil war to the Empire. Annotate: at what point could events have gone differently?)

    4. "This Is Still Happening" — EQ: Is the question of when it's legitimate to resist authority still unresolved? · Standard: WH.6_12.6 + W.4 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (find a 20th or 21st century case where people killed or attempted to kill a political leader to "save" a democracy. Was their reasoning similar to Brutus's? Did it work? Write a comparative argument with evidence from both cases.)

    Why This Is Relevant Today?¶

    Julius Caesar is about what happens after you remove a leader who is becoming a tyrant: someone else immediately fills the vacuum, using the same tools — rhetorical manipulation, popular grievance, military loyalty — in ways the conspirators didn't anticipate. The play is not pro-Caesar and not pro-Brutus; it's a study in how political violence plays out in practice rather than theory, and why the gap between political intention and political outcome is almost always larger than the actors expect. Antony's funeral speech — how he turns the crowd from mourning to rage in three minutes by pretending to do the opposite — is still the model for political demagoguery: repeat your opponent's virtues until the audience hears only your contempt, use the props of sincerity to convey resentment, give the crowd permission to feel what it already wants to feel. Every political speech class, every media literacy curriculum, and every analysis of how politicians move public opinion starts with this scene.

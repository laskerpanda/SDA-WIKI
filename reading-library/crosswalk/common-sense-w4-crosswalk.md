# Crosswalk: Common Sense as Argumentative Mentor Text

**Text:** *Common Sense* by Thomas Paine (1776)
**Primary Entry:** [[reading-library/social-studies/common-sense-paine]]
**Crosswalk Type:** ELA ↔ Social Studies
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.9 · 9-10.W.4
**SS Standards:** WH.6_12.4 + Era 3 · WH.6_12.5 + Era 3

---

## Why This Text Works for Both

*Common Sense* is the ideal crosswalk text because it was written *as* an argument. Its purpose is to persuade. This means it can be used simultaneously to:

1. **Hit W.4 (ELA)** — by studying how Paine constructs an argument
2. **Hit WH.6_12.4 (SS)** — by studying what the argument was about and how it caused the Revolution

Most crosswalk texts require a workaround — using a historical document for ELA or using a literary text for SS. *Common Sense* is genuinely both at once.

---

## ELA Use (W.4 Argument Writing — Reading as a Writer)

When using *Common Sense* for ELA, the question is: **How does Paine build his argument?**

### Structural Map

| Section | Move Paine Makes | What Students Learn |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Introduction | Distinguishes society (good) from government (necessary evil) | Setting up a framework before the claim |
| Part I | Uses the Bible against monarchy — a source his audience trusts | Using audience values as evidence |
| Part II | Traces the history of monarchy — all began in conquest | Historical evidence to undermine legitimacy |
| Part III | Argues American independence is both possible and necessary | Two-part argument (feasibility + morality) |
| Part IV | Addresses the logistics of a navy and government | Counterargument preemption |

### W.4 Mentor Text Questions
- What is Paine's main claim? Where does he state it explicitly?
- What types of evidence does he use? (Biblical, historical, economic, logical)
- How does he address counterarguments? Find a specific example.
- Why does he write the way he does — short punchy sentences mixed with long catalogs?
- What does he assume his reader already believes? How does he use those beliefs?

## Social Studies Use (WH.6_12.4 + Era 3)

When using *Common Sense* for SS, the question is: **How did this document contribute to the Revolution?**

### Historical Context Questions
- Why was anonymity important when Paine first published it?
- Who was the intended audience? Who was actually reading it?
- How did Enlightenment ideas (Locke, Rousseau) appear in Paine's argument?
- Why did the argument resonate with ordinary colonists more than elite political writing?

### Connections to Era 3 Standards
- **WH.6_12.4** — *Common Sense* is a direct cause document: it shifted public opinion in favor of independence. The pamphlet sold 500,000 copies in a population of 2.5 million — roughly equivalent to 60 million copies today.
- **WH.6_12.5** — Paine's economic argument (British trade restrictions, the cost of being part of the empire) is as central as his political argument. Revolution had economic as well as ideological causes.

## Combined Studio: "Paine's Argument Then and Now"

**Essential Question:** What makes an argument powerful enough to change history?

**Standards:** WH.6_12.4 + Era 3 · 9-10.W.4 (ELA)

**Six-Week Arc:**
- Weeks 1–2: Read and annotate *Common Sense* (R.9 analysis of argument structure)
- Week 3: Research the context (what was happening in 1776 that made this land?)
- Week 4: Draft an argumentative essay analyzing Paine's most effective move
- Week 5: Revision using peer feedback + Hermes feedback
- Week 6: Demo of Learning (Extended Writing) + Deliverable (exhibit-ready essay with historical context)

**Demo:** Extended Writing scored on W.4 rubric (WH.6_12.4 + Era 3 content embedded in the argument)
**Deliverable:** Published argumentative essay for the SDA Commons Reading Library

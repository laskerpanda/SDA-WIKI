    Era 2: Middle Ages and the Renaissance¶

    Course: English I + World History (Grade 9) Era span: Approximately 500–1600 CE Studios that use this era: Studio 3 — The Medieval Crisis · Studio 4 — Renaissance & Rebirth

    What This Era Is About¶

    When the Western Roman Empire collapsed in 476 CE, it left a power vacuum in Europe that would take centuries to fill. The Middle Ages (also called the Medieval period) is what happened during that filling-in: feudal hierarchies replaced Roman administration, the Catholic Church became the dominant organizing institution of Western Europe, and the remnants of Roman culture were preserved in monasteries and — crucially — in the Islamic world, which was in the middle of its own Golden Age.

    Era 2 covers: - Feudalism — the political-economic system of medieval Europe: kings, lords, knights, serfs, and the obligations that bound them together - The Catholic Church's political dominance — popes who could excommunicate kings, the Inquisition, the Crusades as religiously justified warfare - The Islamic Golden Age — while Europe was in its early medieval period, Islamic civilization preserved Greek knowledge and added to it: algebra, astronomy, medicine, philosophy - The Crusades — religiously motivated wars for control of Jerusalem; also a catalyst for European-Islamic cultural exchange - The Black Death — the bubonic plague that killed one-third of Europe's population in the 14th century and destabilized the entire social order - Medieval trade networks — Silk Road at its height, Saharan trade routes, Viking exploration and trade, Hanseatic League - The Renaissance — beginning in Italian city-states (~1300), spreading north: humanism, perspective in art, the printing press, a revival of classical learning, and the seeds of the Reformation

    The essential question running through this era: When does order protect people — and when does it control them?

    All Six Standards Applied to Era 2¶

    WH.6_12.1 + Era 2 — Science and Technology of the Middle Ages and Renaissance¶

    Full standard: Analyze historical achievements related to science and technology.

    In Era 2, this means: Analyze the scientific and technological achievements of both the Islamic Golden Age and the Renaissance, and explain how they preserved, advanced, and eventually transformed human knowledge.

    Key topics: - Islamic Golden Age contributions (~800–1200 CE): algebra (al-Khwarizmi), optics (Ibn al-Haytham), encyclopedic medicine (Ibn Sina/Avicenna), astronomical observation, preservation and translation of Greek texts, paper mills - Medieval European technology: heavy plow enabling northern European agriculture, water mills, windmills, Gothic cathedrals (an engineering achievement), advances in metallurgy and glassmaking - The printing press (Gutenberg, ~1440) — possibly the most significant technology of Era 2; mass production of text destroys the Church's information monopoly; Bibles, pamphlets, scientific texts all become accessible; the Protestant Reformation is unimaginable without it - Navigation technology: astrolabe, magnetic compass, improved ship design (lateen sail enabling sailing against the wind) — all enabling the Age of Exploration at the era's end - Renaissance scientific thought: Leonardo da Vinci's anatomical drawings and engineering sketches; Copernicus's heliocentric model (1543) challenging Church cosmology

    FPS Skill Strand: OEC Students read complex informational texts from Middle Ages and Renaissance and support inferences with textual evidence.

    ELA connection: 9-10.R.2 — Comprehend complex informational texts, develop inferences, and provide relevant textual evidence.

    Learning targets: - I can explain a specific scientific or technological achievement from the Islamic Golden Age and analyze its impact on later European thought. - I can explain how the printing press changed the flow of information and why that mattered. - I can analyze how Renaissance thinkers challenged received knowledge and what the consequences were.

    Example inquiry angles: - "How did Islamic scholars preserve and expand on Greek mathematics and science — and what happened to that knowledge when it reached Europe?" - "The printing press is often called the first 'disruptive technology.' What did it disrupt, who benefited, and who was threatened?" - "How did Copernicus's heliocentric model challenge not just astronomy but the entire worldview of the Catholic Church?"

    WH.6_12.2 + Era 2 — Religions and Ideologies of the Middle Ages and Renaissance¶

    Full standard: Explain historical changes related to religions and ideologies.

    In Era 2, this means: Explain how religious institutions changed — particularly how the Catholic Church rose to unprecedented power, then how that power began to fracture — and how new ideologies (humanism, early nationalism) began to challenge religious authority.

    Key topics: - The Catholic Church's political power: Pope Gregory VII and the Investiture Controversy (who controls who); Pope Innocent III at the height of papal authority; excommunication as a political weapon - The Crusades (~1095–1291): Pope Urban II's call; the People's Crusade; the capture and loss of Jerusalem; the Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople (1204); the long-term destruction of Christian-Muslim relations - The Great Schism (1054): formal split between Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox churches; a division that still shapes global Christianity - The Avignon Papacy and Western Schism — popes moving to France; multiple simultaneous popes; the Church's credibility in crisis before the Reformation - Humanism — the Renaissance philosophical movement placing human beings and their potential at the center (not just God and sin); Petrarch, Erasmus, Pico della Mirandola - Early challenges to Church authority: John Wycliffe (England), Jan Hus (Bohemia) — both burned or condemned for criticizing Church corruption - The end of this era's religious stability: Luther's 95 Theses (1517) is technically Era 3, but the ground for it is entirely prepared in Era 2

    FPS Skill Strand: Argumentation Students write explanatory texts describing shifts in religious power and ideology.

    ELA connection: 9-10.W.1 and W.2 — Write clearly and coherently with a logical organizational structure.

    Learning targets: - I can explain how the Catholic Church accumulated political power during the Middle Ages and analyze the methods it used. - I can identify at least two historical changes in religious thought or ideology during Era 2. - I can explain the emergence of cultural interaction between Europe and other civilizations through this era.

    Example inquiry angles: - "How did the Crusades change both European and Islamic society — and not in the ways either side intended?" - "How did Renaissance humanism represent a challenge to medieval Christianity without initially intending to be one?" - "The Church's political power was at its height in 1200 and in crisis by 1500. What changed?"

    WH.6_12.3 + Era 2 — Political Systems and Their Effects on People¶

    Full standard: Analyze the effects of different political systems on people.

    In Era 2, this means: Analyze how feudalism, the Church's political authority, and emerging nation-states affected the lives of ordinary people — what they owed, what they could do, and what they had no choice about.

    Key topics: - Feudalism's structure: king → lords → knights → serfs; the manorial system; what each level owed to those above and below; how feudal contracts worked - What feudalism meant for serfs: tied to the land; unable to leave without permission; owed labor and crops to the lord; few legal rights; but also: protection, land use, community structure - The Magna Carta (1215) — English barons forced King John to sign a document limiting royal power; for the first time in English history, the principle that even the king is subject to law; seeds of modern constitutionalism - Church courts and Church law — the Church had its own legal system (canon law); clergy could only be tried in Church courts (benefice of clergy); powerful people could appeal to Church courts instead of royal courts - The Holy Roman Empire — the complex, decentralized political structure of Central Europe; the conflict between popes and emperors over who had ultimate authority - Italian city-states — by 1300, northern Italian cities (Florence, Venice, Genoa, Milan) are economically powerful enough to operate as independent republics; merchant class rises; early capitalism; patronage of art

    FPS Skill Strand: Argumentation Students argue which political systems or institutions most affected individuals' lives.

    ELA connection: 9-10.W.4 — Argumentative writing; students argue whether feudal political systems improved or harmed ordinary people's lives.

    Learning targets: - I can explain what feudalism required of people at different levels of the hierarchy. - I can analyze how the Magna Carta represented a shift in the principle of political authority. - I can evaluate the growth of political institutions from the Middle Ages to the Renaissance.

    Example inquiry angles: - "Was feudalism a protection system or an exploitation system? (Argue using specific evidence about serfs' obligations AND protections.)" - "How did the Magna Carta matter for ordinary English people — or did it? (Who benefited from the 1215 document, and who didn't?)" - "How did Italian city-state republicanism preview later European political development?"

    WH.6_12.4 + Era 2 — Social, Cultural, and Economic Development¶

    Full standard: Analyze the influence of social, cultural, and economic development on individuals.

    In Era 2, this means: Analyze how feudalism, the medieval guild system, trade networks, the Black Death, and the cultural explosion of the Renaissance affected individual lives — what people could aspire to, what they produced, who they interacted with.

    Key topics: - The guild system — medieval trade associations controlling the production and sale of goods; apprentice → journeyman → master; economic protection AND economic control; guilds determined who could work in a trade - Silk Road at its height — 8th–13th centuries; not just silk and spices but glass, paper, mathematics, disease, and religion flowing in both directions; the Mongol Empire (early Era 2/Era 3 overlap) briefly made the entire route safe under "Pax Mongolica" - The Black Death (~1347–1351) — bubonic plague from Central Asia, carried by fleas on rats; killed 30–50% of Europe's population; shattered the feudal order (fewer peasants = peasants with more bargaining power); accelerated the end of serfdom in Western Europe; psychological devastation; anti-Jewish pogroms (Jews blamed for plague) - Renaissance art and culture — Florentine banking families (Medici) as patrons; perspective in painting (Brunelleschi, Masaccio); humanist literature (Petrarch, Dante, Boccaccio); the Decameron as a window into Black Death society; Michelangelo, Leonardo, Raphael - Women in medieval and Renaissance society — heavily circumscribed by Church doctrine; but noblewomen had some agency through arranged marriages; convents as the one pathway to literacy and administrative roles; Christine de Pizan as a rare female writer and intellectual - Trans-Saharan trade — gold and salt trade; rise of Mali Empire (Mansa Musa); Islamic scholarship in Timbuktu; how West African societies connected to the broader Islamic world

    FPS Skill Strand: Interpretation & Analysis Students explain how feudalism, trade, or cultural movements influenced people's lives.

    ELA connection: 9-10.W.3 — Explanatory writing about how feudalism, trade, or cultural movements influenced people's lives.

    Learning targets: - I can explain how the Black Death disrupted feudal social structures. - I can analyze how the guild system controlled economic life for medieval workers. - I can evaluate contributions of the Middle Ages and Renaissance to social, cultural, and economic development.

    Example inquiry angles: - "How did the Black Death change what peasants and workers could demand — and what does that tell us about how social power works?" - "How did the Medici banking family's wealth reshape what art looked like and what ideas were considered acceptable to express?" - "Why did the Renaissance start in Italian city-states specifically — and what economic conditions made that possible?"

    WH.6_12.5 + Era 2 — Causes and Effects Using Primary and Secondary Sources¶

    Full standard: Analyze causes and effects of global events using primary and/or secondary sources.

    In Era 2, this means: Use primary and secondary sources to analyze the causes and effects of major medieval and Renaissance events — especially the Crusades, the Black Death, and the fall of Constantinople.

    Key events to investigate: - The Crusades — causes: Pope Urban II's 1095 call, Muslim control of Jerusalem, European feudal surplus of fighting-age men; effects: cultural exchange, intensified anti-Semitism in Europe, long-term Muslim-Christian hostility, trade routes opened, military orders (Knights Templar) - The Black Death — causes: Central Asian origins, trade routes as vectors, poor sanitation in medieval cities, flea-rat-human transmission; effects: demographic collapse, labor market shift, psychological transformation, accelerated end of serfdom - Fall of Constantinople (1453) — causes: Ottoman military expansion, Eastern Roman Empire's long decline, failure of Western Crusader support; effects: end of Byzantine Empire, Ottoman control of eastern trade routes (motivating European interest in sea routes to Asia), Greek scholars flee to Italy carrying classical texts (fueling Renaissance) - The Hundred Years War (1337–1453) — English-French conflict over territory and succession; effects include rise of French national identity, Joan of Arc, decline of feudal cavalry, rise of professional armies

    FPS Skill Strand: Argumentation + OEC Students analyze differing accounts of events like the Crusades or Black Death to evaluate causes and consequences.

    ELA connection: 9-10.R.9a and R.9b — Analyze author's POV and bias; analyze various accounts of a subject told in different media.

    Learning targets: - I can use both a primary and secondary source to analyze the causes and effects of one major Era 2 event. - I can compare two accounts of the same event (e.g., a Crusader chronicle vs. an Islamic historian's account) and explain what each emphasizes or omits. - I can identify recurring historical themes across Era 1 and Era 2 (e.g., religion justifying violence; plague reshaping social structures).

    Example inquiry angles: - "The Crusaders and Islamic historians tell the story of the Crusades very differently. What does each version emphasize — and why?" - "Was the Black Death primarily a medical event or a social and political one? (Use evidence from multiple types of sources.)" - "How did the fall of Constantinople in 1453 accidentally accelerate the Renaissance and eventually the Age of Exploration?"

    WH.6_12.6 + Era 2 — Past to Present¶

    Full standard: Explain how past events connect to the present.

    In Era 2, this means: Identify the greatest social, cultural, or economic events of the Medieval and Renaissance era and analyze how those events influenced or informed the present day.

    Key connections: - Magna Carta → modern constitutionalism: the principle that authority is subject to law; foundational to English common law, U.S. Constitution, Universal Declaration of Human Rights - Feudal social structure → class systems in modern economies: the ways wealth is inherited and concentrated; land ownership patterns in Europe still partially trace to feudal grants - Printing press → information technology and democratic culture: the logic of the printing press anticipates the internet — both technologies democratize access to information and destabilize existing power structures - The Crusades → Muslim-Christian relations today: ongoing patterns of mistrust and conflict with roots in medieval history; the language of "crusade" still politically charged - Renaissance humanism → Enlightenment → modern liberal values: individual rights, secular governance, scientific rationalism all trace through Renaissance humanist thought - Islamic Golden Age → European Renaissance: the transmission of mathematics, science, and philosophy from the Islamic world to Europe is a direct ancestor of modern STEM fields

    FPS Skill Strand: Argumentation Students participate in discussions connecting medieval and Renaissance developments to modern institutions.

    ELA connection: 9-10.C.6 — Engage in respectful discussions or debates.

    Learning targets: - I can identify a specific medieval or Renaissance development and trace its direct influence on present-day institutions, ideas, or conflicts. - I can contribute to a discussion about past-to-present connections using evidence from my research. - I can respond to a peer's argument with a question or counterpoint that advances the discussion.

    Example inquiry angles: - "The Magna Carta is 800 years old. Which of its principles still function as law in the United States today?" - "How do the Crusades still shape the politics and culture of the Middle East and Europe?" - "Is the printing press or the internet the more significant information revolution? (What does the comparison reveal about how information technology changes power?)"

    Key Vocabulary for Era 2¶

    Term Definition
    feudalism The political-economic system of medieval Europe based on the exchange of land for military service and labor
    serf A feudal peasant bound to the land; not legally enslaved, but unable to leave without the lord's permission
    lord A feudal landowner who held land from a king and owed military service in return
    knight A mounted warrior who owed military service to a lord in exchange for land (a fief)
    fief Land granted by a lord to a vassal in exchange for military service and loyalty
    monastery A community of monks living under religious rules; preserved classical knowledge through the early medieval period
    Crusades A series of religiously sanctioned military campaigns (~1095–1291) by European Christians to recapture the Holy Land from Muslim control
    Magna Carta A 1215 charter signed by King John of England, limiting royal power and establishing the principle that the king is subject to the law
    guild A medieval association of craftspeople or merchants controlling production and trade in a specific craft or industry
    Black Death The bubonic plague that killed approximately 30–50% of Europe's population between 1347 and 1351
    Renaissance The "rebirth" of classical learning and arts in Europe, beginning in Italian city-states (~1300) and spreading north through 1600
    humanism The Renaissance intellectual movement emphasizing human potential, classical learning, and reason rather than religious dogma
    patron A wealthy person who financially supported artists, architects, and scholars (e.g., the Medici family in Florence)
    Ottoman Empire The Turkish empire that conquered Constantinople in 1453 and controlled much of the Middle East, North Africa, and southeastern Europe
    Silk Road The network of trade routes connecting China to the Mediterranean world; at its height during Era 2

    Why This Is Relevant Today?¶

    The Middle Ages invented institutions that still structure modern life: universities, hospitals, international banking, the concept of legal due process, the early arguments for limits on state power. The Magna Carta's argument that even kings must operate within law is the ancestor of every written constitution. The Reformation's argument that individuals can interpret sacred texts directly — without institutional intermediaries — is the ancestor of every argument about personal authority over institutional expertise, including contemporary debates about science, medicine, and media. The conflict between religious authority and political authority that dominated medieval Europe is still being fought in every debate about the role of religious institutions in public life. Ibn Battuta's travels remind us that the medieval world was globally connected long before European exploration — and that "globalization" is not a modern invention. See WH-03: Writing Against Empire and the Magna Carta in the Reading Library.

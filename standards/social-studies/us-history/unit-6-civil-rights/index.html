    Unit 6: Civil Rights and Social Movements¶

    Course: English II + US History (Grade 10) Approximate period: 1945–1970s FPS Learning Goals: Explain the economic and/or social issues of the Post-WWII era and their long-lasting effects. Compare and contrast how different groups competed for equality and the effectiveness of various movements.

    What This Unit Is About¶

    The civil rights movement is one of the most significant chapters in American history — and one of the most contested in how it is taught. In SDA, we treat it fully: the victories, the violence, the strategy debates, and the movement's connection to other equality struggles happening simultaneously around the world and in the U.S.

    This unit covers: - The legal and social architecture of Jim Crow - The Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–1956) - Little Rock Nine and "The Lost Year" (1957–1958) - Greensboro sit-ins (1960) and the student movement - Freedom Riders (1961) - Birmingham Children's March (1963) - MLK's "Letter from Birmingham Jail" as a primary source - March on Washington and "I Have a Dream" speech - Civil Rights Act (1964), Voting Rights Act (1965) - Malcolm X and Black Power - Women's Rights Movement - Native American Rights Movement - Latino Rights Movement - LGBTQ Rights Movement - Asian American Rights Movement

    The core argument of this unit: the civil rights movement was not a single, unified campaign that achieved everything it set out to do in 1965 and stopped. It was a complex of overlapping movements, strategic debates, and ongoing struggles — some still unresolved.

    All Six Standards Applied to Unit 6¶

    US.6_12.1 + Unit 6 — Primary and Secondary Sources¶

    Standard: Analyze primary and secondary sources with attention to reliability, impact, and purpose.

    In Unit 6: Analyze sources on the Post-WWII era and civil rights with attention to perspective, bias, and purpose.

    Key sources: - MLK's "Letter from Birmingham Jail" (1963) — one of the most important primary sources in American history; written in response to white moderate clergy who asked him to slow down - MLK's "I Have a Dream" speech (August 28, 1963) - FBI surveillance files on MLK — a primary source that reveals government hostility to the civil rights movement - Malcolm X speeches and interviews - Newspaper coverage of Birmingham vs. civil rights organizations' documentation of the same events - Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC) pamphlets and position statements

    FPS Skill Strand: OEC — Explain the economic and/or social issues of the Post-WWII era and their long-lasting effects.

    Learning targets: - I can analyze "Letter from Birmingham Jail" for its argument, its audience (white moderate clergy), and its rhetorical strategy. - I can compare FBI surveillance documents on MLK to civil rights organizations' own materials and explain what each source reveals. - I can evaluate different sources on the same civil rights event for what they emphasize and omit.

    US.6_12.2 + Unit 6 — Multiple Perspectives¶

    Standard: Examine the impact of multiple perspectives on social, political, and cultural development.

    In Unit 6 — key document: "Letter from Birmingham Jail."

    Key perspectives: - MLK and SCLC (Southern Christian Leadership Conference): nonviolent direct action; moral appeal; pressure on the conscience of white America; integration as the goal - SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee): more radical; grassroots organizing; questioning integration as sufficient; later led by Stokely Carmichael (Black Power) - Malcolm X and the Nation of Islam: Black nationalism; self-defense; integration as accommodation to a white supremacist system; "by any means necessary" - White moderate response (the audience of "Letter from Birmingham Jail"): support the goal but not the timing or the tactics; MLK's critique of the white moderate is one of the letter's most powerful sections - Southern white resistance: Citizens' Councils, the Ku Klux Klan, George Wallace ("segregation now, segregation tomorrow, segregation forever"), FBI under Hoover actively working against the movement - Women's perspective within the civil rights movement: women did the organizing work but were often excluded from leadership; Fannie Lou Hamer, Diane Nash, Ella Baker — leaders often written out of standard accounts

    Learning targets: - I can compare the strategic approaches of MLK, Malcolm X, and SNCC and analyze what each approach was responding to. - I can analyze how white moderate opposition (as described in "Letter from Birmingham Jail") differed from outright white supremacist opposition.

    US.6_12.3 + Unit 6 — Cause and Effect¶

    Standard: Explain the relationship of events focusing on the link(s) between cause and effect.

    In Unit 6 — FPS target: Argumentation: compare and contrast how different groups competed for equality and the effectiveness of various movements.

    Key cause-and-effect chains: - WWII (Double V campaign) + Truman desegregating the military (1948) → civil rights momentum: returning Black veterans unwilling to accept Jim Crow after fighting fascism abroad - Brown v. Board of Education (1954) → "massive resistance" in the South → Little Rock: school desegregation ruling triggers a wave of Southern resistance, including Governor Faubus's use of the National Guard to block Black students - Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56) → rise of MLK as a national figure: a 381-day boycott organized largely by women (including Rosa Parks, Jo Ann Robinson); successful; launches MLK's national profile - Birmingham Children's March (1963) + Bull Connor's fire hoses and police dogs → national outrage → Civil Rights Act (1964): the televised violence against children changed white Northern public opinion and provided political cover for LBJ to pass the Civil Rights Act - Voting Rights Act (1965) → Selma → Bloody Sunday → passage: the brutal attack on marchers on the Edmund Pettus Bridge, televised nationally, produced sufficient political pressure to pass the Voting Rights Act

    Learning targets: - I can trace the cause-and-effect chain from a specific civil rights event to a legal or political consequence. - I can compare the strategies of different equality movements and analyze which were more effective under what conditions. - I can explain why nonviolent direct action was strategic, not just moral.

    US.6_12.4 + Unit 6 — Change Over Time¶

    Standard: Compare how historical elements change over time.

    In Unit 6: Compare how different equality movements changed over time; evaluate the long-lasting effects of the civil rights era.

    Key comparisons: - Civil Rights Act (1964) → Voting Rights Act (1965) → Shelby County v. Holder (2013, gutted key VRA provisions) — rights can be won and then partially reversed - 1960s nonviolent direct action → 1992 LA Riots → 2020 George Floyd protests: different moments in the ongoing struggle; what changes and what stays the same? - Women's suffrage (1920) → women excluded from early civil rights leadership → feminist movement of 1960s–70s → #MeToo (2017) - American Indian Movement (AIM) and its connections to civil rights and the current indigenous rights movement

    US.6_12.5 + Unit 6 — Significant Contributions of People and Policy¶

    Standard: Analyze the significant contributions of people, policy, and the influence on an era.

    Key people and policies: | Person/Policy | Contribution | |--------------|-------------| | Martin Luther King Jr. | SCLC; "Letter from Birmingham Jail"; "I Have a Dream"; assassinated April 4, 1968 | | Rosa Parks | Montgomery Bus Boycott; her arrest was strategic, not accidental — she was a trained NAACP activist | | Fannie Lou Hamer | Mississippi voting rights organizer; beaten for trying to register to vote; powerful testimony before the 1964 Democratic Convention | | Malcolm X | Nation of Islam; Black nationalism; "by any means necessary"; assassinated February 21, 1965 | | Thurgood Marshall | NAACP legal director; argued Brown v. Board; first Black Supreme Court Justice | | Civil Rights Act (1964) | Prohibited discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin in employment and public accommodations | | Voting Rights Act (1965) | Prohibited discriminatory voting practices; authorized federal oversight of elections in states with history of discrimination |

    US.6_12.6 + Unit 6 — Past to Present¶

    Standard: Connect the past to the present using current events.

    Key connections: - Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act → ongoing litigation about voting rights, gerrymandering, voter ID laws - MLK's "Letter from Birmingham Jail" → still directly quoted in contemporary justice movements - Black Lives Matter (2013–present) → direct descendant of civil rights movement; same debates about tactics, goals, white moderate response - Women's Rights Movement → Title IX, Roe v. Wade (1973) and its 2022 overturn, pay equity debates - Native American rights → Standing Rock (2016), land and water rights, tribal sovereignty debates

    Key Vocabulary for Unit 6¶

    Term Definition
    Jim Crow The system of state and local laws enforcing racial segregation in the South from the 1870s through the 1960s
    nonviolent direct action The strategy of using peaceful protest, civil disobedience, and economic pressure to challenge unjust systems
    civil disobedience The deliberate, nonviolent refusal to obey unjust laws as a form of political protest
    desegregation The process of ending racial segregation in public institutions
    NAACP The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People; founded 1909; pursued legal challenges to segregation
    SNCC The Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee; student-led civil rights organization; Freedom Riders, sit-ins
    Black Power A political and cultural movement emphasizing Black self-determination, pride, and community organization
    Civil Rights Act (1964) Federal law prohibiting discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin
    Voting Rights Act (1965) Federal law prohibiting discriminatory voting practices; one of the most important pieces of civil rights legislation
    massive resistance The Southern strategy of organized opposition to racial desegregation after Brown v. Board

    Why This Is Relevant Today?¶

    The Civil Rights Movement was not a completed project — it was an ongoing negotiation between the promise of American democracy and the reality of American exclusion, and that negotiation is still happening. The gains of the 1950s and 60s — the Civil Rights Act, the Voting Rights Act, desegregation — were real, and they are still being contested. Voting rights protections are being rolled back in states. Fair housing enforcement gaps persist. The criminal justice system continues to show racial disparities in every stage from arrest through sentencing. The arguments activists made then — that formal legal equality is not enough if structural inequalities remain, that moral urgency requires direct action, that bystander silence is a form of complicity — are the arguments of current movements as well. This unit is not history; it's the foundation of current debates. See MLK Letter from Birmingham Jail and USH-01: When the System Fails.

    Why This Is Relevant Today?¶

    Every contemporary argument about voting rights, police accountability, affirmative action, and systemic racism is a direct continuation of debates that Unit 6 covers — many involving the same legal frameworks, the same tactics, and often the same geographic locations. The Civil Rights Movement's toolkit — nonviolent direct action, coalition building, litigation strategy, economic pressure — is still the most studied model for social movements globally; climate activists, disability rights organizers, and pro-democracy movements worldwide draw on it. MLK's Letter from Birmingham Jail argued that unjust laws have no moral authority — an argument that appears in every civil disobedience debate worldwide. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 has been systematically weakened by Supreme Court decisions in 2013 and 2021, and the debate about what "equal protection" actually requires is ongoing. See USH-01: When the System Fails and the Letter from Birmingham Jail in the Reading Library.

    Why This Is Relevant Today?¶

    The Civil Rights movement is living history — the people who marched are still alive, the laws they won are still being contested, and the conditions they fought are not fully resolved. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 was gutted by the Supreme Court in 2013 (Shelby County v. Holder); the subsequent proliferation of voter restriction laws is a direct consequence. The fair housing gains of the 1960s have partially reversed due to discriminatory lending, zoning, and disinvestment that created the residential segregation that still structures American cities and school districts. The standards that apply here (social movements, cause and consequence, civil disobedience as a political strategy) are the standards for evaluating every subsequent social movement — for understanding what strategies work, what conditions are necessary, and what opposition looks like when it uses legal mechanisms rather than fire hoses. See USH-01: When the System Fails for the studio that traces the reform thread from the Progressive Era through this movement.

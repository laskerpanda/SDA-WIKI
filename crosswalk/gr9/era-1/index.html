    Era 1 Crosswalk — Ancient Civilizations¶

    Grade 9 · World History Era 1 + ELA 9-10 Span: Prehistory through 600 CE · Earliest civilizations, first religions, ancient empires

    Start here — one Demo, two standards

    WH.6_12.3 + R.9 + W.4 — Read Hammurabi's Code. Analyze its claims about who the law protects (R.9 Skinny). Write your own argument about who should govern (W.4 Skinny). That's WH.6_12.3 content + two ELA standards in one Demo. Use the WH.6_12.3 Skinny to find your argument move.

    Project Ideas — Era 1¶

    These are proposed Studio inquiry angles. Any can be adapted for your own inquiry question.

    1. "Who gets to make the rules?"¶

    Essential question: When someone writes down a law, who benefits — and who is left out? WH Standard: WH.6_12.3 + Era 1 ELA Standard: R.9 + W.4 Source anchor: Hammurabi's Code + Pericles' Funeral Oration Suggested Demo Mode: Extended Writing (argument essay) or Written Assessment Possible deliverables: Comparative essay · Annotated primary source analysis · Podcast episode The argument: Students take a position on what Hammurabi's Code reveals about who the law was designed to protect — using R.9 to analyze the text's claims and W.4 to write their own argument.

    2. "Is the powerful person always right?"¶

    Essential question: When a stronger power tells a weaker one that might makes right, what can the weaker power do? WH Standard: WH.6_12.3 + Era 1 ELA Standard: R.9 + W.4 Source anchor: The Melian Dialogue Suggested Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation or Extended Writing Possible deliverables: Essay · Staged debate using Melian Dialogue format · Visual essay mapping the power argument The argument: Thucydides' Melian Dialogue is one of the oldest recorded arguments about power. Students analyze the Athenian argument structure (R.9) and write a response from the Melian position (W.4), connecting to WH.6_12.3.

    3. "How did belief become government?"¶

    Essential question: How did early societies use religion to justify political authority — and did it work? WH Standard: WH.6_12.2 + Era 1 ELA Standard: R.9 + W.3 Source anchors: Analects of Confucius + Pericles' Funeral Oration Suggested Demo Mode: Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation Possible deliverables: Explanatory essay · Documentary script · Annotated comparison chart The argument: Students compare how Confucius and Pericles each used their ideology to justify political organization — using W.3 to explain the mechanism, and WH.6_12.2 as content evidence.

    4. "What did ancient people actually know?"¶

    Essential question: How advanced were ancient civilizations — and what does the answer reveal about how we define 'advanced'? WH Standard: WH.6_12.1 + Era 1 ELA Standard: W.3 + R.4 Suggested Demo Mode: Extended Writing or Multimedia Possible deliverables: Documentary · Museum-style exhibit · Research presentation The argument: Students examine a specific ancient technology or scientific achievement (irrigation, medicine, the arch, astronomical calendars) and write an explanatory piece arguing for its significance. Challenges presentism.

    5. "What do laws reveal about values?"¶

    Essential question: Can you tell what a society cared about just by reading its laws? WH Standard: WH.6_12.3 + WH.6_12.6 + Era 1 ELA Standard: R.9 + W.4 Source anchor: Hammurabi's Code Suggested Demo Mode: Written Assessment or Portfolio Annotation Possible deliverables: Analysis essay · Comparative chart + written reflection · Podcast The argument: Students read selected laws from Hammurabi's Code and identify the values embedded in them (property, family, class), then connect to a modern law that reveals contemporary values. Direct WH.6_12.6 (past to present) entry point.

    Why This Is Relevant Today?¶

    The questions ancient civilizations asked — who should hold power, what makes a law just, whose life counts — are not settled. They're the same arguments happening in courtrooms, legislatures, and social media feeds right now. When the U.S. Supreme Court debates equal protection under the law, it's arguing with Hammurabi. When a democracy asks whether it has the right to intervene militarily in a weaker country, it's arguing with Thucydides. When a government claims that order requires inequality, it's making Hammurabi's argument. Every Era 1 text was written by someone trying to answer an urgent question about power and justice — and none of those questions have been fully answered yet. If you want to follow this thread into the present, start with WH-01: Who Should Govern? or the ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk.

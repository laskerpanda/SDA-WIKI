## What This Episode Is About

John Green unpacks Sophocles' *Oedipus Rex* as a play about the limits of human knowledge and the danger of pride — but also as a political text about what makes a good king and what happens when the ruler is corrupted by certainty. The episode walks through the play's use of dramatic irony (the audience knows what Oedipus doesn't) and connects that technique to the tragic structure Aristotle would later theorize. Green also addresses why this play still matters: it's about how power and self-deception interact.

---

## Key Concepts Covered

- Dramatic irony as the play's central literary engine — the audience's knowledge vs. Oedipus's blindness
- The tragic hero: hamartia (fatal flaw), hubris, and the arc of peripeteia (reversal) and anagnorisis (recognition)
- Fate vs. free will as the play's central philosophical tension
- The Chorus as a literary device — community voice, moral commentary, and dramatic pacing
- Oedipus as both victim and agent of his own downfall
- The political stakes: Thebes is sick because its king is corrupt (unknowingly), and leadership is connected to moral order
- Sophocles writing under Athenian democracy: the play's implicit questions about who deserves to rule
- Blindness and sight as the play's dominant metaphor — Tiresias sees without eyes; Oedipus has eyes but cannot see

## Studio Ideas

1. **Hamartia Analysis:** After watching the episode, students write a focused R.8 paragraph identifying Oedipus's hamartia and tracing how it drives the plot. Then they connect the specific flaw to WH.6_12.3: what does this say about what Athenian society believed made a ruler dangerous?

2. **Modern Tragic Hero:** Students use Green's framework for the tragic hero to analyze a figure from the current US History unit (a political leader whose certainty or pride led to disaster). They write a W.4 argument connecting the ancient dramatic formula to a historical pattern, linking R.8 and WH.6_12.3 or a USH standard.

3. **Chorus Rewrite:** Students write a short Chorus speech (8–12 lines) for a modern version of the play set in a contemporary political context. Then they annotate their own work: what literary device did you use, and what social/political commentary were you trying to make? This practices R.8 and W.3.

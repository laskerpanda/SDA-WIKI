## What This Episode Is About

John Green reads *The Great Gatsby* as a novel about the lie at the heart of the American Dream — specifically, the idea that wealth and reinvention are available to anyone willing to work hard enough. The episode focuses on Fitzgerald's use of symbols (the green light, the Valley of Ashes, the eyes of Doctor T.J. Eckleburg) to build a sustained critique of class and materialism in 1920s America. Green also examines Nick Carraway as an unreliable narrator whose complicity in the story is part of the point.

---

## Key Concepts Covered

- The green light as the novel's central symbol: desire, the American Dream, and the impossibility of recapturing the past
- The Valley of Ashes as Fitzgerald's indictment of what wealth costs — and who pays
- The eyes of Doctor T.J. Eckleburg: the faded God who no longer watches over a morally bankrupt world
- Nick as an unreliable narrator: his admiration for Gatsby filters the story in ways the reader must question
- Gatsby's fabricated identity as a critique of the American self-made man myth
- The distinction between "old money" (East Egg) and "new money" (West Egg) — class is not just about wealth but about belonging
- Daisy as a symbol rather than a fully realized person: what Fitzgerald is and isn't doing with his female characters
- The Jazz Age context: Prohibition, bootlegging, and the economy of spectacle in the 1920s

## Studio Ideas

1. **Symbol Analysis:** Students choose one of the novel's three major symbols (green light, Valley of Ashes, or the Eckleburg eyes) and write a focused R.8 analysis: what does the symbol mean at its first appearance, how does its meaning develop, and what theme does it ultimately support? They connect the symbol to one piece of historical evidence from US.6_12.4 about actual conditions in the 1920s.

2. **Is the American Dream Real?** Using Green's episode and the US History unit on the Roaring Twenties, students write a W.4 argument: is the American Dream a motivating ideal or a harmful myth? They must use evidence from both the novel (R.8) and from USH Unit 2 economics and social data (US.6_12.4).

3. **Nick on Trial:** Students take the position that Nick Carraway is morally responsible for the novel's tragedy because of his inaction and complicity. Or they defend him. Either way, they write a W.4 brief using textual evidence, then connect to US.6_12.2: what does it mean to be complicit in a system that harms people? This connects the literary question to the historical question about who benefited from and who was harmed by 1920s prosperity.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

*The Great Gatsby* is a novel about the gap between the American Dream's promise and the reality of class rigidity — and that gap has only widened since 1925. The "old money vs. new money" dynamic Fitzgerald uses is now "inherited wealth vs. earned wealth vs. no wealth," with mobility between categories harder than at any point in the postwar era. Gatsby's belief that he can reinvent himself, buy his way into acceptance, and recapture the past is both a critique and a eulogy for a particular American mythology about self-creation. Contemporary debates about student debt, the billionaire class, inherited advantage, and whether working hard actually leads to economic security are all debates about whether Gatsby was right or Fitzgerald was right. The green light Gatsby reaches for has not gotten any closer.

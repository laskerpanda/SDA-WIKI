## What This Episode Is About

John Green reads *Lord of the Flies* as a direct response to World War II — Golding wrote the novel immediately after serving in the Royal Navy, and Green argues the book is Golding's answer to the question: how did civilization produce the Holocaust and Hiroshima? The episode focuses on the novel's allegorical structure (the island as a miniature society, the characters as types rather than individuals) and Golding's deeply pessimistic argument that human evil is not a product of bad systems but of human nature itself. Green complicates this by asking whether Golding is right.

---

## Key Concepts Covered

- Allegory: each character as a symbol (Ralph = democratic order, Piggy = intellect and reason, Jack = authoritarian impulse, Simon = spiritual/moral clarity)
- The conch as a symbol of democratic process — and its destruction as the novel's political turning point
- The Beast: what it actually is, and why Simon is the only one who figures it out
- Golding's argument about human nature vs. Rousseau's counter-argument (the "noble savage" debate)
- The novel as post-WWII literature: Golding processing the reality that "civilized" Europeans committed genocide
- The naval officer's arrival at the end as a bitter irony — the "rescue" returns the boys to a world at war
- The fire: what it means to maintain it vs. abandon it — civilization requires effort; barbarism is the default
- Violence and group psychology: how the hunts escalate and what that says about mob behavior

## Studio Ideas

1. **The Conch Argument:** The conch represents democratic process in the novel. Students write a W.4 argument tracing the conch's symbolic life from its introduction to its destruction — what does Golding say happens to democratic process when fear takes over? They connect this to one historical example from WH.6_12.3 (e.g., the Weimar Republic, or another democratic collapse before or during WWII).

2. **Nature vs. Nurture Debate:** Golding says evil is built into human nature. Rousseau and others disagree. Students take a position in a structured W.4 argument, using evidence from both the novel (R.8) and from WH.6_12.5 historical examples of what actually causes atrocities. They must engage with at least one counter-argument.

3. **Allegory Map:** Students create an annotated character map identifying what each major character represents in Golding's allegory. Then they write a W.3 explanation of whether Golding's allegory is "fair" — does it oversimplify? What historical reality from WWII or its aftermath would challenge or support the allegory's logic?

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Golding wrote *Lord of the Flies* in 1954, shortly after WWII, and the novel is his argument against Enlightenment optimism: that civilization is a thin veneer over violence, that given the right conditions ordinary people become capable of systematic cruelty, and that democratic institutions do not survive contact with fear. The conditions under which the boys on the island abandon rescue in favor of the immediate rewards of belonging to a tribe, following a charismatic leader, and targeting a scapegoat are the conditions that political scientists call "democratic backsliding" — and they're currently visible in dozens of countries. The question of whether Jack or Ralph represents human nature more accurately is a question about whether democracy is sustainable, which is a live question. See [WH-01: Who Should Govern?](../../../units/studio-archive/wh-01-who-should-govern.md) for the governance frameworks these boys are failing to maintain.

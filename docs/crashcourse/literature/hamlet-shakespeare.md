## What This Episode Is About

John Green approaches *Hamlet* as a play about the failure of action — specifically, why a person who knows what they should do still cannot bring themselves to do it. The episode explores Hamlet's famous delay not as a character flaw but as Shakespeare's deliberate design to force the audience to sit inside radical uncertainty. Green traces the play's core themes of appearance vs. reality, corruption, and mortality (the "To be or not to be" soliloquy as a meditation on consciousness, not just suicide), and connects the political plot — a usurped throne, a poisoned king — to early modern anxieties about legitimate authority.

---

## Key Concepts Covered

- The soliloquy as the play's primary literary device: Hamlet's internal debates as the drama
- "To be or not to be" as a philosophical argument about consciousness, suffering, and inaction — not simply a suicide speech
- Appearance vs. reality as the play's structural theme: "Something is rotten in the state of Denmark"
- Hamlet's delay: multiple critical interpretations (Romantic, Freudian, existentialist) and Green's own take
- Ophelia as a character whose madness is structurally meaningful — not decorative
- The play-within-a-play as a device for testing reality and exposing Claudius
- Corrupt political succession: what happens to a state when its ruler is a murderer and usurper
- The revenge tragedy genre and how *Hamlet* subverts it by making the hero unable to be a conventional avenger

## Studio Ideas

1. **"To Be or Not to Be" Annotation:** Students close-read Hamlet's most famous soliloquy using Green's analysis as a starting framework. They annotate for literary devices (R.8) and then write a short W.3 paragraph: what philosophical question is Hamlet actually asking, and how does Shakespeare use language to make sure there's no easy answer? Students connect the political context (usurped throne, corrupt court) to WH.6_12.3.

2. **Delay and Decision:** Students identify a moment in history (from WH Era 2 or elsewhere in the curriculum) when a leader or group faced a "Hamlet problem" — knowing what was right but hesitating. They write a W.4 argument comparing that historical case to Hamlet's situation, using R.8 vocabulary for character and theme.

3. **Corruption and the State:** The play argues that a corrupted ruler poisons the entire kingdom. Students write a W.3 explanatory piece connecting this idea to WH.6_12.3's framework for political legitimacy: what does *Hamlet* suggest about the relationship between a leader's moral character and the health of the society they govern? They draw on both the play's text and their world history knowledge.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

*Hamlet* is 400 years old and the questions it raises about action, conscience, and moral certainty have not been answered. Every generation of students reading *Hamlet* is also a generation living through situations where the right course of action is genuinely uncertain — where institutions are corrupt, information is unreliable, and acting decisively might make things worse. The play is regularly read as a meditation on political paralysis, and that reading is currently very available: what does a person with values do when the system they're part of is corrupt but dismantling it might cause more harm? The question of political succession and legitimacy — the state of Denmark resting on who can legitimately hold power — is a question contemporary democracies are actively re-litigating. See [WH-03: Writing Against Empire](../../units/studio-archive/wh-03-writing-against-empire.md) for how people challenge powerful systems — the contrast with Hamlet's paralysis is instructive.

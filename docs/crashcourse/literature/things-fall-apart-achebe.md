## What This Episode Is About

John Green reads *Things Fall Apart* first and foremost as Achebe's deliberate response to the tradition of European fiction about Africa — particularly Conrad's *Heart of Darkness* — that depicted Africans as primitive, voiceless, and without complex inner lives. The episode traces how Achebe centers an African protagonist and an African community in all their internal complexity, contradiction, and richness before colonialism arrives to dismantle it. Green examines Okonkwo's tragedy as both personal (a man destroyed by his own rigidity) and historical (an entire society's destruction by an external force).

---

## Key Concepts Covered

- Achebe's counter-narrative project: writing *Things Fall Apart* as a correction to Eurocentric representations of Africa
- Okonkwo as a tragic hero: his fear of weakness (and its roots in his father's failure) as his hamartia
- Igbo culture depicted from the inside: proverbs, the Oracle, the Week of Peace, the kola nut — richness before the colonizers arrive
- The novel's three-part structure: Okonkwo's world, his exile, the colonial transformation
- The missionaries' arrival: how different characters respond, and why (Nwoye's conversion as a political and personal rejection)
- The District Commissioner and the final page: the shift to a European narrative voice and what Achebe does with it
- The Yeats epigraph "Things Fall Apart / the center cannot hold": what the poem adds to the novel's argument
- Colonialism's double destruction: not just political conquest but the dismantling of meaning systems, community bonds, and identity

## Studio Ideas

1. **Before and After the Missionaries:** Students trace the state of one specific aspect of Igbo society (justice system, gender roles, religious practice, or community decision-making) from Part 1 to Part 3 of the novel. They write a W.3 explanatory piece documenting the change and connecting it to WH.6_12.5 on colonialism's mechanisms of disruption. This grounds the historical standard in a specific, humanized example.

2. **Okonkwo or Colonialism: Whose Fault?** The novel gives Okonkwo a genuine tragic flaw, but it also makes clear that colonialism would have destroyed him regardless. Students write a W.4 argument: is Okonkwo's tragedy primarily personal or primarily historical? They must use evidence from the novel (R.8) and from WH.6_12.5 (what European colonialism actually did to African societies) to build their case.

3. **The Last Page:** Achebe's final page is narrated from the District Commissioner's perspective — a sudden, cold shift. Students write a close reading of that page (R.8), then explain in a W.3 paragraph what Achebe accomplishes by ending the novel this way. How does the Commissioner's tone and his plans for a book about Okonkwo connect to WH.6_12.5's analysis of how colonialism was justified and narrated by Europeans?

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Achebe wrote *Things Fall Apart* in 1958, explicitly as a response to Conrad's *Heart of Darkness* and to the entire tradition of African literature written by Europeans who never asked Africans about their own experience. The novel is therefore both a work of literature and a political act: it insists on the humanity, complexity, and interiority of a pre-colonial Igbo community and refuses to let colonialism be the main character of the story. That project — of restoring narrative agency to people whose stories were told by those who conquered them — is ongoing in contemporary debates about decolonizing curricula, who gets to write whose history, and whose perspective counts as "objective." Achebe also demonstrates what he wanted to show: that colonialism didn't encounter backward societies in need of civilization — it destroyed functioning ones. See [WH-03: Writing Against Empire](../../units/studio-archive/wh-03-writing-against-empire.md) for the full context of colonial power and resistance.

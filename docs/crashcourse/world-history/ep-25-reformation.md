## What This Episode Is About

Green argues that the Protestant Reformation was not primarily a theological dispute but a political and economic revolution wearing religious clothing. Luther's 95 Theses created a crisis for the Catholic Church not because the arguments were new — critics of Church corruption had been making them for centuries — but because of a new technology: the printing press. The episode traces how Luther's ideas went viral, how the German princes used religion as cover for a power grab against Rome, and how the Reformation shattered European Christendom into competing factions that would fight each other for the next century and a half. Green's central provocation: the Reformation produced religious freedom almost entirely by accident, as an unintended consequence of political fragmentation — nobody planned for pluralism.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Pre-Reformation Church: indulgences, simony, clerical corruption; Johann Tetzel and the indulgence trade
- Martin Luther's 95 Theses (1517): the specific complaints, why they resonated, and how printing spread them across Germany in weeks
- Luther's core theological breaks: faith alone (sola fide), scripture alone (sola scriptura), the priesthood of all believers
- The Diet of Worms (1521): Luther refusing to recant; "Here I stand"
- German princes and why they backed Luther: seizure of Church lands, independence from Rome, political advantage
- Zwingli and Calvin: Reformed Protestantism; predestination and the elect
- The English Reformation: Henry VIII's divorce, the break with Rome, and how personal desire created national religion
- The Wars of Religion (1562–1648): 80+ years of conflict between Protestant and Catholic powers
- The Peace of Augsburg (1555) and Westphalia (1648): cuius regio, eius religio — whose region, his religion
- Green's argument: religious freedom emerged from exhaustion, not from anyone believing in it

## Studio Ideas

1. **The Printing Press as Social Media:** Students draw an analogy between the printing press's role in spreading Luther's ideas and a modern platform of their choice. They must identify: what made both capable of spreading ideas quickly? What made both dangerous? What couldn't be controlled? Targets WH.6_12.2 (ideologies) and WH.6_12.6 (past to present).

2. **Luther's 95 Theses — Close Read:** Students read 5–6 of the actual theses (selected from the reading library entry) and annotate: What specific complaint is this? Who benefits if this practice stops? Is this primarily theological, economic, or political? Builds R.9a (author's point of view) and WH.6_12.5 (primary source analysis).

3. **The Accidental Reformer:** Green argues no one planned for religious pluralism — it emerged from exhaustion. Students write a short argumentative paragraph: Was the Reformation's most important legacy intentional or accidental? They must use two pieces of evidence from the episode. Builds W.4 (argument) and analytical thinking about cause/effect.

4. **Reformation Scorecard:** Students assess: did the Reformation achieve Luther's stated goals? They use evidence from the episode to score: (a) end to Church corruption, (b) return to scripture-only authority, (c) freedom of conscience. Then they answer: Who actually won the Reformation? Targets WH.6_12.5 and forces students to distinguish between intentions and outcomes.

## What This Episode Is About

Green opens by asking whether Alexander deserves the title "the Great" — a provocation he never fully settles. By age 32, Alexander had conquered an empire stretching from Greece to modern Pakistan, defeating the Persian Empire, Egypt, and much of Central Asia. Green examines the military genius (the oblique charge, the Companion cavalry, the use of the phalanx) and then asks the harder question: what did Alexander actually leave behind? His answer is Hellenization — the spread of Greek language, architecture, philosophy, and culture across three continents. The episode traces how this cultural diffusion shaped the conditions for early Christianity (the New Testament was written in Greek), Islamic philosophy, and the concept of a "universal" civilization. Green’s central tension: is Alexander great because of what he built, or because of what he destroyed?

## Key Concepts Covered

- Alexander’s military innovations: the Macedonian phalanx, Companion cavalry, siege engineering
- Conquest timeline (334–323 BCE): Persia, Egypt, Bactria, northwestern India
- The founding of Alexandria (Egypt) and 70+ other "Alexandria" cities as nodes of Greek culture
- Hellenization: the deliberate spread of Greek language, architecture, and intellectual tradition
- The problem of succession: Alexander died without a named heir, fragmenting the empire into the Diadochi kingdoms
- Cultural diffusion: how Greek philosophical and artistic traditions merged with Persian, Egyptian, and Indian traditions
- The Ptolemaic Kingdom (Egypt) as a model of multicultural empire that preserved and transmitted knowledge
- Whether "greatness" is defined by conquest, cultural legacy, or something else entirely

## Studio Ideas

1. **Defining Greatness:** Students write a structured argument: by what criteria should we judge a historical figure “great”? Then they apply those criteria to Alexander. Targets W.4 and WH.6_12.4 + WH.6_12.3.

2. **Hellenization Map Analysis:** Using a map of Alexander’s empire and a list of Alexandrias, students trace where Greek cultural influence landed. They annotate: what later civilizations benefited from this diffusion? What was lost? Targets WH.6_12.4 and R.7.

3. **Conquest vs. Legacy Debate:** Students debate: should a leader be judged by the method of their rise or by the long-term consequences of their rule? Uses Alexander as the case study. Targets C.6 and WH.6_12.4.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The "great man" question Alexander raises — can one person’s decisions change the trajectory of civilization? — is still how most people understand political leadership. History is full of arguments about whether systems or individuals drive change. Hellenization also raises a question that’s live today: when a powerful culture spreads — through conquest, through trade, through media — what do local cultures preserve and what do they lose? The American cultural export (Hollywood, Silicon Valley platforms, English as global business language) is the 21st-century version of the question Alexander’s conquests first posed.

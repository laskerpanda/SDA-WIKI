## What This Episode Is About

Green focuses this episode on the European theater: Operation Barbarossa (Germany's invasion of the Soviet Union), the Holocaust, D-Day, the collapse of Nazi Germany, and the moral questions that the war's conduct forces. He argues that understanding WWII means taking the Holocaust seriously as the ideological endpoint of Nazi ideology — not a side effect of war, but its deliberate program. Green is honest about the difficulty: Allied powers knew about the Holocaust earlier than they acted; the Soviet Union was a partner in defeating Nazism while simultaneously running its own terror state. The war raises the question of whether there is a version of this where the "good guys" are uncomplicated — and Green's answer is no. He ends with the war's immediate consequences: 70–85 million dead, the United Nations, the Cold War beginning, and the decolonization movements the war accelerated.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Operation Barbarossa (June 1941): the largest military invasion in history; Germany attacking the Soviet Union; why Hitler violated the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact
- The Eastern Front: casualty scale — an estimated 30 million Soviet dead; the Battle of Stalingrad as turning point
- The Holocaust: the ideological origins (Nazi racial ideology, Social Darwinism, antisemitism); the progression from discrimination to genocide
  - Nuremberg Laws (1935) → Kristallnacht (1938) → ghettos → Einsatzgruppen mobile killing units → death camps (Auschwitz, Treblinka, Sobibor)
  - Six million Jewish dead; Romani, disabled, LGBTQ+ communities, political prisoners also targeted
- Allied response: when did they know? What did they do? The bombing of railways debate
- D-Day (June 6, 1944): Operation Overlord; the scale; why it succeeded
- The fall of Berlin (May 1945): Hitler's death; German surrender
- The atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki (August 1945): the debate over justification
- Immediate aftermath: 70–85 million dead; the Nuremberg Trials; the United Nations; the Universal Declaration of Human Rights
- Decolonization accelerated: colonial subjects who fought for empires now demanding independence
- The Cold War beginning: Soviet and American spheres emerging from the Allied victory

## Studio Ideas

1. **Night + Episode 41:** Students read a passage from Night (reading library) and then write a paragraph that uses both sources: What does the episode explain (institutional, political, ideological)? What does Wiesel convey (experiential, emotional, moral)? What can you only understand by reading both? Builds R.9 (comparing argumentative and narrative texts) and WH.6_12.5.

2. **The Hiroshima Debate:** Green presents the atomic bomb as morally contested. Students take a position (was the bombing of Hiroshima justified?), use two pieces of evidence from the episode to support it, and identify the strongest counterargument. The goal is not to "win" but to hold two true things in tension. Builds W.4 and WH.6_12.5.

3. **FDR's Four Freedoms — Did the War Deliver?** Students read FDR's speech (reading library) and use evidence from this episode to assess: at war's end, were the Four Freedoms more or less achieved? For whom? They must consider colonial subjects, Holocaust survivors, Soviet citizens, and American veterans separately. Builds R.9 and WH.6_12.6.

4. **The "Good War" Test:** Green resists calling WWII uncomplicated. Students create a rubric: what would make a war "good"? (Just cause, proportional means, non-combatant protection, achieves stated goals?) Then they score WWII on each dimension — using evidence from both WWII episodes. Builds analytical thinking and WH.6_12.5.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The postwar international order Green describes — the United Nations, NATO, the Bretton Woods institutions, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, nuclear deterrence — is the order we still live in, and it is under more stress now than at any point since its creation. The UN was designed to prevent great-power war by giving great powers a veto; that same veto now prevents the UN from responding to great-power aggression. NATO was designed around the assumption that the U.S. would lead it; that assumption is now contested. The nuclear deterrence logic — Mutually Assured Destruction as stability — requires both sides to believe the other will actually use the weapons, which means the stability depends on maintaining credible threats of civilizational destruction. The [Universal Declaration of Human Rights](../../reading-library/social-studies/un-declaration-human-rights.md) was adopted in 1948 as the world's answer to what had just happened — and every subsequent debate about genocide, refugee rights, and international law tests whether that answer was sufficient. The postwar order isn't background history; it's the architecture of the world students are inheriting.

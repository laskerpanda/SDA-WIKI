## What This Episode Is About

Green opens by confronting the scale of the Atlantic slave trade directly — 12.5 million Africans transported across the Middle Passage over roughly 400 years — and spends the episode explaining how a system of this magnitude could exist and persist. His argument is not that the slave trade happened because of individual evil, but because of an economic system: sugar. The sugar economy of the Caribbean required intensive labor in conditions where workers died at catastrophic rates, which required a continuous replacement supply, which made the trade self-perpetuating and enormously profitable. Green is careful to discuss African agency in the trade — the role of African states and merchants who sold captives — while being clear that this complexity does not distribute blame equally or excuse the system. He ends by tracing the slave trade's demographic, economic, and political legacies in ways that reach directly to the present.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Scale: 12.5 million Africans transported across the Atlantic; estimated 1.8 million who died during the Middle Passage
- Why sugar? The demand logic: European consumption of sugar exploded; sugar cultivation required intensive labor in brutal conditions; labor died faster than it could be trained
- The Triangle Trade: manufactured goods from Europe → enslaved people from Africa → sugar, cotton, tobacco from Americas → back to Europe
- The Middle Passage: conditions, mortality rates, resistance
- African states' role: some African kingdoms sold captives; the economic incentives; how this complicates a simple narrative
- Slave resistance: shipboard rebellions; maroon communities; slow resistance (work slowdowns, sabotage)
- Where enslaved people went: the Caribbean and South America received far more enslaved people than North America (about 5% of the trade went to British North America)
- Economic scale: the slave trade funded the Industrial Revolution; profits financed banks, insurers, and manufacturers in Britain
- Abolition: the Haitian Revolution as the most decisive blow; British abolition in 1807 and 1833; American Civil War
- Green's argument: the slave trade's legacy is not past — it structured the modern world economy

## Studio Ideas

1. **Douglass + Green:** Students read a passage from the Douglass narrative (reading library), then write a paragraph that uses both the episode and the text: what does Green explain that Douglass can't? What does Douglass convey that Green can't? Builds R.9 (comparing argumentative and narrative texts) and WH.6_12.5.

2. **The Economic Logic:** Green argues that the slave trade persisted because it was profitable. Students work through the economic chain: sugar demand → plantation labor → slave trade → Triangle Trade profits → European industrialization. They must answer: if the economic logic drove the system, what kind of force could actually stop it? Forces W.4 (argument writing) and systems thinking.

3. **Scale Exercise:** 12.5 million people. Students put this in context: How many people live in your city? In your state? How long would it take to count to 12.5 million? This is a numeracy + empathy exercise that precedes deeper analysis — students can't engage with the history if the numbers remain abstract.

4. **Who Ended It?** Green says the Haitian Revolution was the most decisive blow against Atlantic slavery. Students assess this claim using evidence from the episode and the Haitian Revolution episode (ep-31). Was abolition achieved primarily by moral argument, by political pressure, or by the enslaved themselves? Builds W.4 and WH.6_12.6 (past to present).

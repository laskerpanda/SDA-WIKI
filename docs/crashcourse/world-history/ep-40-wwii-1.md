## What This Episode Is About

Green opens by rejecting the "Hitler was a madman" explanation for WWII, arguing that this framing lets everyone else off the hook — the German voters who elected him, the Western powers who appeased him, the economic system whose collapse created his opportunity. This episode focuses on origins and the Pacific theater: the rise of fascism in Germany, Italy, and Japan; the Great Depression's political consequences; the failure of the League of Nations; Japan's expansionism in Asia; and the attack on Pearl Harbor. Green's central argument: WWII was not a surprise. It was the predictable result of WWI's unresolved grievances, the Depression's political desperation, and democratic nations' unwillingness to stop what they could see coming.

## Key Concepts Covered

- WWI's unresolved aftermath: the Treaty of Versailles's war guilt clause, reparations, territorial losses; German resentment as political fuel
- The Great Depression (1929): how economic collapse destroyed democratic legitimacy and created space for authoritarian alternatives
- Fascism: what it is (nationalism + authoritarianism + anti-communism + charismatic leader), why it appealed to people experiencing chaos
- Hitler's rise: 1923 Beer Hall Putsch failure; 1933 election; enabling act; legal seizure of power
- Mussolini's Italy: fascism's original model
- Imperial Japan: expansion into Manchuria (1931) and China; the logic of resource scarcity driving militarism
- The League of Nations' failure: why it couldn't stop Japan or Germany; U.S. never joining; lack of enforcement mechanism
- Appeasement: Munich Agreement (1938); Neville Chamberlain; the logic and the failure
- The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact (1939): Nazi-Soviet non-aggression; why both sides signed it
- Germany's blitzkrieg: fall of France in six weeks; what shocked everyone
- Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941) and U.S. entry

## Studio Ideas

1. **The Conditions Argument:** Green says Hitler's rise required specific conditions (depression, Versailles, democratic failure). Students write a paragraph arguing: are these conditions unique to Germany in 1933, or could they occur elsewhere? They must use at least two pieces of evidence from the episode. Builds W.4 and WH.6_12.6 (past to present).

2. **Appeasement Simulation:** Students receive a simplified briefing on Munich 1938 (Britain's options: fight now, appease, sanction). They make a decision as Chamberlain, then assess: given what you knew in 1938 (not what we know now), was appeasement rational? After the debrief, they discuss why knowing the outcome changes our judgment of decisions. Targets WH.6_12.5.

3. **Fascism's Appeal:** Green argues fascism appealed to people experiencing chaos. Students identify three specific things fascism offered (security, national pride, a scapegoat) using evidence from the episode. Then: are any of these appeals still visible in contemporary politics? Builds WH.6_12.2 and WH.6_12.6 — and requires the kind of careful, evidence-based claim-making that guards against both under-claiming and over-claiming.

4. **Night + Episode 40:** Students read the opening chapter of Night (reading library) after watching this episode. Assignment: Green describes the political conditions that made the Holocaust possible. Wiesel describes a family that didn't believe it could happen to them. Write a paragraph explaining: what does Wiesel's experience reveal about how ordinary people experience political extremism from the inside? Builds R.9 and WH.6_12.5.

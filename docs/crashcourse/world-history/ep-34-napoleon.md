## What This Episode Is About

Green examines Napoleon as both the product and the destroyer of the French Revolution. Born in Corsica, Napoleon rose through the Revolutionary army to become First Consul and then Emperor — technically restoring the monarchical form he had risen to power fighting against. Green covers the military campaigns (from Egypt to Moscow), the Napoleonic Code that spread legal equality across Europe, and the contradiction at the center of Napoleon’s legacy: he carried Enlightenment ideals about law and citizenship into every country he conquered, while simultaneously making himself emperor and reinstating slavery in Haiti. Green’s argument: Napoleon is the first modern strongman — a leader who uses the language of popular sovereignty and national liberation while consolidating personal power. His defeat at Waterloo produced a century of relative European peace (the Concert of Europe) and simultaneously planted the seeds of nationalism that would explode in 1914.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Napoleon’s rise: Corsican origins, Revolutionary army, coup of 18 Brumaire (1799)
- The Napoleonic Code (1804): legal equality, property rights, religious toleration across Europe
- Military campaigns: Egyptian expedition, Italian campaigns, the Moscow disaster (1812)
- The Continental System: economic war against Britain, its failure and consequences
- The contradiction: Emperor who spread revolutionary ideals; restored slavery in Haiti (1802)
- Nationalism as unintended consequence: occupied peoples developed national identity against Napoleon
- The Hundred Days and Waterloo (1815)
- The Concert of Europe: Vienna settlement attempting to restore pre-revolutionary order

3. **Haiti and the Revolution:** Students examine Napoleon’s decision to restore slavery in Haiti and trace its consequences: the Haitian Revolution, the Louisiana Purchase, Napoleon’s retreat from the Americas. How did his Haiti decision change the history of the Western Hemisphere? Targets WH.6_12.3 and R.9.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Napoleon’s legacy is directly visible in contemporary politics. The Napoleonic Code is the foundation of civil law in approximately 75 countries. The pattern he established — a charismatic leader claiming to represent the popular will while concentrating power in himself — is the template for 20th and 21st-century authoritarianism. Contemporary debates about democratic backsliding, executive overreach, and the fragility of republican institutions are essentially debates about Napoleonic political innovation. When political scientists warn that democracies die from within, Napoleon is the case study they are pointing at.

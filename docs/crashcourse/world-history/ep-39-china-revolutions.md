## What This Episode Is About

Green covers China’s turbulent transformation from imperial dynasty to communist state in one of the most consequential political stories of the 20th century. He begins with the fall of the Qing Dynasty (1912) — the last imperial dynasty, weakened by Western imperialism and internal rebellion — and the founding of the Republic of China under Sun Yat-sen. He traces the split between the Nationalist Party (Kuomintang, led by Chiang Kai-shek) and the Communist Party (led by Mao Zedong), their brief alliance against Japan during World War II, and the eventual Communist victory in 1949. Key moments: the Shanghai Massacre (1927), the Long March (1934-35), the Japanese invasion and the Nanjing Massacre (1937), and the People’s Republic founded on October 1, 1949. Green’s argument: China’s revolutions were about more than ideology — they were about who would modernize China, on whose terms, and at what cost to the Chinese people.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Fall of the Qing Dynasty (1912): Western pressure, Opium Wars legacy, internal rebellion (Taiping Rebellion)
- Republic of China: Sun Yat-sen, Three Principles (nationalism, democracy, people’s livelihood)
- The Communist Party of China: founded 1921, influenced by Russian Revolution
- Nationalist-Communist split: Shanghai Massacre (1927), Chiang Kai-shek’s purge
- The Long March (1934-35): 6,000-mile Communist retreat, Mao consolidating leadership
- Japanese invasion (1937): Second Sino-Japanese War, Nanjing Massacre
- Nationalist-Communist United Front against Japan (1937-1945)
- Chinese Civil War outcome: Mao’s victory, People’s Republic of China (October 1, 1949)

## Studio Ideas

1. **Why Did Mao Win?:** Students write a structured causal argument: identify the three factors most responsible for the Communist Party’s victory in 1949 and rank them by importance. They must engage with both internal Chinese dynamics and external factors (Japan, Soviet support). Targets WH.6_12.3 and W.4.

2. **Competing Visions of Modernization:** Students compare Sun Yat-sen’s Three Principles and Mao’s communist program: what did each promise Chinese peasants? What did each deliver? Targets WH.6_12.3 and R.9.

3. **The Long March as Mythology:** Students examine how the Long March is depicted in Chinese Communist Party history and ask: what is the difference between a historical event and a founding myth? How do states use mythology? Targets WH.6_12.3 + IR.1-5.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

China is the world’s second-largest economy, the United States’ primary strategic competitor, and a country that most American students know almost nothing about in historical depth. The People’s Republic of China’s self-understanding — the Communist Party’s legitimacy, the "century of humiliation" framework, the meaning of Taiwan — all flow directly from the events of 1912-1949. Contemporary American foreign policy debates about China, trade, Taiwan, and the South China Sea cannot be understood without understanding why and how the Communist Party came to power. This episode is basic preparation for being an informed citizen of a country in strategic competition with China.

## What This Episode Is About

Green argues that the Cold War is misunderstood as a military confrontation when it was primarily an ideological one — a competition between two competing claims about how human society should be organized, with proxy wars, propaganda, and nuclear brinkmanship replacing direct combat. He traces the Cold War from its origins in the WWII alliance's mutual suspicion through to the late 1980s, covering the major flashpoints (Berlin blockade, Korean War, Cuban Missile Crisis, Vietnam) while keeping his focus on the central question: what was each side actually fighting for? Green is particularly good on the Cold War's costs outside the U.S. and USSR — the proxy wars fought in Korea, Vietnam, Angola, and Latin America, where millions died while the superpowers avoided direct confrontation. He ends by asking what the Cold War's outcome tells us about the 21st century.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Origins: WWII alliance's mutual suspicion; the Yalta and Potsdam conferences; Soviet expansion in Eastern Europe; Churchill's "iron curtain" speech
- Ideological clash: American liberal democracy + capitalism vs. Soviet communism; why both sides saw this as total and global
- The Truman Doctrine (1947) and Marshall Plan: containment strategy; economic reconstruction as anti-communist strategy
- NATO vs. Warsaw Pact: the alliance systems
- The Berlin Crisis and Blockade (1948–49): Berlin as Cold War symbol
- Nuclear arms race: why MAD (Mutually Assured Destruction) paradoxically prevented direct conflict
- Korean War (1950–53): hot war within cold war; the forgotten war
- McCarthyism: the Cold War at home; the domestic cost of the ideological struggle
- Cuban Missile Crisis (1962): the closest moment; what actually happened
- Vietnam: Cold War logic applied to nationalist revolution
- Non-Aligned Movement: nations refusing to choose sides
- Détente and the end: Reagan, Gorbachev, the fall of the Berlin Wall (1989), USSR dissolution (1991)
- Green's argument: the Cold War was won not by military force but by the Soviet economic system's internal collapse — and its legacy shapes every major 21st-century conflict

## Studio Ideas

1. **Orwell + the Cold War:** Students read a passage from 1984 (reading library) after watching this episode. Assignment: What specific Cold War conditions is Orwell responding to? Identify two claims Orwell makes about totalitarianism and connect each to historical evidence from the episode. Builds R.9 (text in historical context) and WH.6_12.2.

2. **Fahrenheit 451 + McCarthyism:** Students read the McCarthyism section recap from this episode and a passage from Fahrenheit 451 (reading library). Assignment: What is Bradbury afraid of? How does the historical context of McCarthyism change your reading of the novel? Builds R.9 and WH.6_12.2.

3. **MAD Logic:** Green explains Mutually Assured Destruction as paradoxically stabilizing. Students evaluate: was MAD a rational strategy? They must define "rational," use two pieces of evidence from the episode, and identify the strongest argument against their position. Builds W.4 and systems-level thinking.

4. **The Proxy War Map:** Green says millions died in proxy wars while the superpowers stayed cold. Students map the proxy conflicts he mentions (Korea, Vietnam, Angola, Afghanistan) and for each write: who was actually fighting, what were they fighting about, and what did the U.S./USSR have at stake? Then they argue: were proxy wars "cold"? Targets WH.6_12.5 and WH.6_12.6 — and directly addresses whose lives the Cold War cost most.

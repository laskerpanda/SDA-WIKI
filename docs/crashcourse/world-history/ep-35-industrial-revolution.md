## What This Episode Is About

Green asks why the Industrial Revolution started in Britain, in the late 18th century, and not somewhere else, earlier. His answer involves multiple converging factors: coal deposits close to the surface and navigable rivers to transport it; the Enclosure Acts that drove peasants off common land and into cities (creating a labor supply); colonial capital that funded early factories; patent law protecting inventors’ profits; and a culture that, unusually for the time, celebrated commercial wealth. He then echoes his Agricultural Revolution argument: the Industrial Revolution made Britain vastly wealthier in aggregate while making many individual workers’ lives worse. Early factory workers faced 14-hour days, child labor, and conditions that shortened lives. Surplus was extracted upward. This is the contradiction that Karl Marx was watching when he wrote the Communist Manifesto — a text that will not make sense without this episode.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Why Britain? Coal, geography, Enclosure Acts, colonial capital, patent law
- The Enclosure Acts: removing peasants from common land → forced urbanization → factory labor supply
- Textile industry first: the spinning jenny, the water frame, the power loom
- James Watt’s steam engine (1769): converting coal energy to mechanical work at scale
- Factory system replacing cottage industry: discipline, schedules, child labor
- The aggregate/individual gap: national wealth increased while workers’ conditions worsened
- Urbanization: Manchester, Birmingham, Leeds — cities growing faster than infrastructure
- Setting up for Marx: the class structure industrialization creates

## Studio Ideas

1. **The Aggregate/Individual Gap:** Students argue: when a technology increases national wealth while worsening conditions for many individuals, is it progress? They use the Industrial Revolution as evidence but must also identify a contemporary parallel. Targets WH.6_12.1 and W.4.

2. **Why Britain? Causal Analysis:** Students rank Green’s five factors explaining why industrialization started in Britain (coal, Enclosure Acts, colonial capital, patent law, culture) and argue: which is most important? Which could have been replicated elsewhere? Targets WH.6_12.1 and IR.1-5.

3. **Child Labor Primary Sources:** Students examine 2-3 primary sources from child factory workers in 19th-century Britain and write a structured response: what argument does the evidence make about the costs of industrialization? Who was bearing those costs? Targets WH.6_12.1 + R.9.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The factory system the Industrial Revolution created is still the operating model for most manufacturing. The questions it raised — who owns the surplus produced by workers, what conditions are acceptable, what obligations does an employer have to employees — are the questions behind every contemporary debate about minimum wage, labor unions, gig economy classification, and Amazon warehouse conditions. The specific argument that Green makes — that aggregate GDP growth can mask widespread individual decline — is the argument economists make about contemporary inequality. The Industrial Revolution didn’t end. It moved.

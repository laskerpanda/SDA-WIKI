## What This Episode Is About

Green argues that the Haitian Revolution was the most radical of all the Atlantic revolutions — more radical than the American or French Revolutions — and that it has been systematically underweighted in world history. Where the American Revolution preserved slavery and the French Revolution produced a decade of elite-dominated chaos, the Haitian Revolution was carried out by enslaved people against their enslavers, produced the first Black republic in the world, and sent shock waves through every slave society in the Atlantic world. The episode traces the complex layering of Haitian society (white colonists, free people of color, and enslaved Africans all in conflict) and shows how Toussaint L'Ouverture navigated those divisions. Green's central argument: Haiti's revolution was treated as a threat by every surrounding slaveholding power precisely because it succeeded, and that deliberate marginalization explains why most students have never heard of it.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Saint-Domingue (colonial Haiti) as the most profitable colony in the world by the 1780s: sugar, coffee, the brutal plantation system
- Three-tier social structure: grand blancs (white planters), affranchis (free people of color, some slaveholders themselves), enslaved Africans — each group with different grievances
- The French Revolution's impact: liberty and equality claims resonating in the colony; Vincent Ogé leading the free people of color uprising (1790); its failure
- The slave uprising of 1791: Bois Caïman ceremony; Boukman Dutty; the scale of the revolt
- Toussaint L'Ouverture: formerly enslaved, military genius, strategic diplomat; navigating Spanish, British, and French pressure
- The ideological contradiction: France abolished slavery in 1794 (partly to gain Saint-Domingue's allegiance) but Napoleon reinstated it
- Napoleon's expedition (1802): capturing Toussaint by deception; yellow fever defeating the French army
- Jean-Jacques Dessalines declaring Haitian independence: January 1, 1804
- Haiti's isolation: France demanding reparations (the "independence debt"); U.S. refusing recognition until 1862
- Green's argument: Haiti's marginalization in history is itself evidence of the revolution's threat to the Atlantic order

## Studio Ideas

1. **The Comparison Essay:** After watching this and the French Revolution episode (ep-33), students write a paragraph arguing: which revolution was more radical? They must define "radical" first (a vocabulary exercise), then use evidence from at least one episode. Builds W.4 (argument) and WH.6_12.3 (political systems comparison).

2. **The Reparations Problem:** Haiti paid France for 122 years. Students research: what was the total amount? In today's dollars, what would that be? Then they write a short response: was this just? They must make a claim and support it with evidence from the episode. Directly targets WH.6_12.6 (past to present) — Haiti's 20th century poverty is directly connected to this.

Students select one Enlightenment idea (natural rights, popular sovereignty, etc.) and write a paragraph showing how Toussaint applied it — using evidence from the episode. Forces close reading and ideological analysis (WH.6_12.2 + WH.6_12.3).

Connects to WH.6_12.6 and the historian's craft.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The Haitian Revolution is the most radical revolution in modern history — the only successful revolution by enslaved people, who took the Enlightenment's claims about universal liberty seriously enough to apply them without exception. The world's response proved what the revolutionary powers actually meant: France demanded reparations for the "lost property" (enslaved people who had freed themselves), and Haiti paid that debt until 1947. The United States didn't recognize Haitian independence until 1862. Haiti's ongoing economic and political challenges cannot be understood without the two-century context of isolation, debt, and foreign intervention that followed its independence. The Haitian Revolution is also the most complete test of whether the ideals of the Enlightenment were ever meant to be universal — and the answer it produced is still relevant to every debate about who universal rights actually apply to.

## What This Episode Is About

Green examines European imperialism in the 19th century by asking: how did a handful of European nations come to control most of the world's surface by 1900, and what were the actual arguments made to justify it? He presents three types of imperialism apologists — the economic exploiters (colonies as markets and resource extraction), the "civilizing mission" advocates (Europeans genuinely believed they were doing good), and the plain Social Darwinists (conquest as proof of superior fitness). Then he presents the counterargument: what the colonized actually experienced. Green's central argument is that imperialism's most enduring damage was not economic extraction but cultural destruction — the dismantling of existing political structures, languages, and self-concepts in colonized populations, effects that outlasted formal colonialism by generations.

## Key Concepts Covered

- The scale of European imperialism: by 1900, European powers controlled roughly 85% of the world's land surface
- Industrial Revolution enabling imperialism: steam ships, repeating rifles, telegraphs, quinine
- Economic arguments: markets for manufactured goods, raw materials (rubber, cotton, diamonds, oil), investment opportunities
- The "civilizing mission" (mission civilisatrice): genuine belief among some Europeans that colonialism was a humanitarian project
- Social Darwinism applied to nations: Herbert Spencer's "survival of the fittest" borrowed by empire advocates
- Kipling's "White Man's Burden": the poem as primary source for colonial ideology
- Specific case studies: the Berlin Conference (1884-85), the Scramble for Africa, the Congo under Leopold II
- Indian resistance to British rule: the 1857 rebellion ("Sepoy Mutiny" vs. "Indian Rebellion" — naming as argument)
- The economics of colonialism: who actually benefited; the drain theory (Indian economists arguing colonialism impoverished India)
- Green's argument: the cultural and political damage outlasted economic extraction; colonialism's deepest effects were psychological and structural

## Studio Ideas

For each, write a sentence showing what evidence from the episode supports or refutes it. Builds R.9 (evaluating argumentative reasoning in primary sources) and WH.6_12.5.

2. **The Genuine Believer Problem:** Green says some imperialists genuinely believed they were helping. Students discuss: does genuine belief in your own good intentions matter morally? They must use evidence from the episode (Leopold vs. "civilizing mission" advocates) to support their position. Forces engagement with the ethics of historical judgment.

3. **Drain Theory Economics:** Green mentions the drain theory — Indian economists arguing colonialism impoverished India. Students work through a simple version: if Britain extracted resources and taxes from India for 200 years, what would the economic math look like? Then they argue: was colonialism economically beneficial to colonized people or only to colonizers? Builds WH.6_12.4 and WH.6_12.6.

4. **Naming as Argument:** Green notes that what the British called the "Sepoy Mutiny" the Indians called the "First War of Independence." Students collect three other examples from the episode where naming reveals perspective (Berlin Conference, "civilizing mission," "White Man's Burden"). Then they write: what names in *current* events reveal whose perspective the name-giver holds? Targets WH.6_12.6 and critical vocabulary analysis.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Almost every major conflict in the Middle East, Africa, and South Asia unfolds inside borders drawn by European colonial powers at conferences where the people living there were not invited. The Berlin Conference of 1884–85 divided Africa into 50+ countries by drawing straight lines through ethnic, linguistic, and political communities — and those lines became the borders of independent states after decolonization. The wars those borders produce aren't "ancient tribal hatreds"; they're the predictable result of 19th-century administrative decisions made for European convenience. The "drain theory" — the argument that colonialism systematically transferred wealth from colonized to colonizing nations — is still being debated in economics and international development, with direct implications for arguments about debt relief, reparations, and foreign aid. And neo-imperialism arguments — that wealthy nations exert colonial-style control over poorer nations through debt, trade terms, and military presence rather than formal occupation — are central to contemporary international relations debates. For more on how imperialism's legacy shapes the texts in this course, see [WH-03: Writing Against Empire](../../units/studio-archive/wh-03-writing-against-empire.md).

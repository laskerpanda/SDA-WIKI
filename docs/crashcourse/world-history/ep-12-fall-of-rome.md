## What This Episode Is About

Green takes on one of history’s most famous "collapses" and complicates it. He walks through the standard causes of Rome’s fall: the Antonine Plague (165-180 CE) killed 5-10 million people, the "Crisis of the Third Century" produced 26 emperors in 50 years, military overextension required crushing taxation, and Germanic groups steadily encroached on frontier territory. But then Green makes his central argument: Rome didn’t "fall" in 476 CE. The Eastern Roman Empire — what we call the Byzantine Empire — kept going for another 1,000 years, until 1453. What actually fell in 476 was the Western Roman Empire, and even then it transformed rather than collapsed. The episode asks what it means to say a civilization "falls" when its institutions, laws, language, and religion keep shaping the world.

## Key Concepts Covered

- The Antonine Plague (165-180 CE): estimated 10% population loss, military and agricultural collapse
- The Crisis of the Third Century (235-284 CE): 26 emperors in 50 years, economic instability
- Military overextension: the Rhine-Danube frontier required a standing army Rome struggled to fund
- The split between Western and Eastern Roman Empires (286 CE under Diocletian)
- Germanic incursions: Visigoths, Vandals, Ostrogoths — not simple "barbarian invasions"
- 476 CE: Romulus Augustulus deposed — the conventional "end date" of Rome
- The Byzantine Empire: Eastern Rome survived 1,000 more years, until Constantinople fell in 1453
- What "collapse" actually means: transformation vs. disappearance

3. **Plague & Power:** Students examine the Antonine Plague’s effects as a case study in how public health crises destabilize political systems. They connect to a modern pandemic (their choice) and ask: what does this comparison reveal? Targets WH.6_12.5 and IR.1-5.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The Roman Empire collapsed slowly, over centuries, with warning signs that were visible to contemporaries who largely couldn’t do anything about them. Its fall is the reference point every subsequent civilization uses when it feels unstable — "this is how Rome fell" is a rhetorical move in political arguments from the 5th century to today. The question Green raises — what does it mean for a civilization to "fall" when its legal codes, architectural forms, language (Latin became French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian), and religion survive it? — is actually a question about what civilizations are made of. The institutions outlast the empire. Which raises an uncomfortable question about which of our institutions outlast us.

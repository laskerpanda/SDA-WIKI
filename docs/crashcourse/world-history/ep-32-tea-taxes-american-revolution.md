## What This Episode Is About

Green examines the American Revolution not as a heroic founding myth but as a specific argument about taxation, representation, and imperial authority. He walks through the sequence of British taxation acts following the Seven Years’ War (Stamp Act, Townshend Acts, Tea Act), the colonial response (Sons of Liberty, the Boston Massacre, the Tea Party), and the philosophical framework the colonists used: the Lockean argument that government derives authority from the consent of the governed and that taxation without representation is illegitimate. Green’s central challenge to the founding myth: the American Revolution was also a civil war. An estimated one-third of colonists were Loyalists. The "Founding Fathers" were mostly wealthy white landowners protecting their economic interests. And the revolution’s most radical claim — that all men are created equal — was written by a slaveholder who knew the contradiction and left it for future generations to resolve.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Seven Years’ War (1756-1763): Britain in debt, colonies expected to help pay
- Taxation without representation: the colonial argument grounded in English legal tradition
- Key legislation: Stamp Act (1765), Townshend Acts (1767), Intolerable Acts (1774)
- Colonial response: boycotts, Sons of Liberty, Boston Massacre (1770), Boston Tea Party (1773)
- Common Sense (Paine, 1776): making the case for independence to ordinary colonists
- Declaration of Independence (1776): Lockean natural rights in political document form
- The revolution as civil war: Loyalists (estimated one-third of colonists)
- The contradiction: "all men are created equal" — who counts?

## Studio Ideas

1. **Was it Revolutionary?:** Students apply a definition of "revolution" to the American Revolution and argue: does it qualify? They must engage with both what changed (British rule ended, new constitutional system) and what didn’t change (slavery, property restrictions on voting, Indigenous land). Targets WH.6_12.3 and W.4.

2. **The Loyalist Perspective:** Students read a primary source from a Loyalist and write a 1-page analysis: what was their argument? Is it coherent? Why did history mostly forget them? Targets WH.6_12.3 and R.9.

3. **"All Men Are Created Equal":** Students trace the phrase through history: what did Jefferson mean? What did abolitionists say it meant? What did suffragettes say? What do contemporary activists say? How does meaning change when different groups claim the same language? Targets WH.6_12.3 + WH.6_12.6 and IR.1-5.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The American founding documents are the most cited and most contested texts in American political life. Every argument about rights — gun rights, abortion, free speech, voting rights, immigration — eventually reaches back to the Declaration or the Constitution. Understanding those documents as products of a specific historical moment, written by specific people with specific interests and specific contradictions, is the difference between civic literacy and civic mythology. Green’s "conservative revolution" argument is not cynicism about the founding — it is realism about what revolutions actually produce and what work remains to be done.

## What This Episode Is About

Green covers the 18th-century Enlightenment as a philosophical revolution that asked whether human reason — rather than divine authority or tradition — could ground political and social institutions. He traces the major thinkers: Locke (natural rights, government by consent), Voltaire (religious toleration, anticlerical satire), Rousseau (the social contract, the "noble savage"), and Montesquieu (separation of powers). Green’s argument is that Enlightenment ideas didn’t just circulate as abstract philosophy — they provided the vocabulary for three revolutions: American, French, and Haitian. But he also asks the uncomfortable question: if Enlightenment thinkers argued for human equality grounded in reason, why did they simultaneously defend or ignore slavery? The answer reveals that "reason" in the Enlightenment was often understood as a characteristic of propertied, educated, white, European men — and its limits matter as much as its achievements.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Enlightenment premises: reason, empiricism, human progress, individual rights
- John Locke: natural rights (life, liberty, property), government by consent, right of revolution
- Voltaire: religious toleration, criticism of Church and monarchy, "Crush the Infamous Thing"
- Rousseau: the social contract, general will, the "noble savage" and corruption of civilization
- Montesquieu: separation of powers, checks and balances, The Spirit of the Laws
- The Encyclopedia (Diderot & D’Alembert): systematizing Enlightenment knowledge
- Enlightenment ideas fueling the American Revolution (1776), French Revolution (1789), Haitian Revolution (1804)
- The Enlightenment’s limits: who counts as "rational"? The exclusion of women, enslaved people, colonized peoples

## Studio Ideas

1. **Who Counts as Rational?:** Students examine one Enlightenment thinker’s specific exclusion of a group (women, enslaved people, colonized peoples) and analyze: what argument did the thinker use to justify this? Is the argument internally consistent with Enlightenment premises? Targets WH.6_12.2 and R.9.

2. **Enlightenment to Revolution:** Students trace one specific Enlightenment idea from philosopher to revolution (e.g., Locke’s consent → Declaration of Independence, or Rousseau’s general will → French Revolutionary rhetoric). How was the idea transformed in application? Targets WH.6_12.2 + WH.6_12.3 and IR.1-5.

3. **Is the Enlightenment Over?:** Students research one contemporary political debate that uses Enlightenment language (natural rights, consent of the governed, free speech) and argue: is Enlightenment thinking still our operating framework, or have we moved past it? Targets WH.6_12.2 and W.4.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Every major political argument in the contemporary American landscape draws on Enlightenment vocabulary: natural rights, consent of the governed, separation of powers, freedom of speech and religion. The Constitution is an Enlightenment document. So is the Declaration of Independence. So is the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. When politicians argue about what the Founders intended, they are arguing about which parts of Enlightenment philosophy to apply and to whom. Green’s question — who counted as "rational" enough to have rights? — is the question underneath every debate about voting rights, immigration, criminal justice reform, and what equal citizenship actually means.

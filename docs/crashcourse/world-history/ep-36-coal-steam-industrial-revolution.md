## What This Episode Is About

The second Industrial Revolution episode focuses on energy and infrastructure. Green examines coal extraction and the steam engine as the twin engines of industrialization, then traces how steam power reorganized not just production but time, space, and social life. Railroads compressed distance: a journey that took weeks took hours. Telegraphs compressed time: information moved at the speed of electrical signals rather than horse and rider. Green argues that these technologies didn’t just make existing things faster — they changed what was possible to think about organizing. They enabled the mass coordination of capital, labor, and resources that made the modern corporation possible. He also traces the dark side: coal mining mortality, the emerging labor movement, and the way industrial capitalism created the class structure that Marx named. This episode is the bridge from the Industrial Revolution to imperialism — industrial powers needed raw materials their home territories couldn’t supply.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Coal as the energy source of industrialization: extraction, transportation, conversion
- Watt’s steam engine improvements: from pumping mines to powering factories
- Railroads: compressing distance, enabling mass movement of goods and people
- The telegraph: information moving faster than physical transport for the first time
- The modern corporation: enabled by mass coordination across distance
- Labor movement emergence: Luddites (1811), Chartists, early trade unions
- Time discipline: factory schedules imposing clock-time on agricultural populations
- Bridge to imperialism: industrial economies needing raw materials from colonies

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The infrastructure questions the Industrial Revolution raised are the questions of the contemporary technology economy: who owns the rails (or the fiber optic cables, or the satellite constellations), who gets access, and on what terms? Every debate about net neutrality, platform monopoly, and who controls digital infrastructure is downstream of the 19th-century debate about who owned the railroads. The Luddite question — when automation eliminates jobs, whose responsibility is the displaced worker? — is the question being asked about AI right now. Coal’s legacy is the most literal: the carbon dioxide from 200 years of burning ancient sunlight is still accumulating in the atmosphere.

## What This Episode Is About

Green dismantles the concept of the "Dark Ages" by pointing out that it is a story Western European historians told about themselves. The term was coined by Petrarch in the 14th century to make the Renaissance look impressive by contrast — a rhetorical move, not a historical assessment. Green’s argument: if you lived in the Byzantine Empire, or in the Islamic world during the Golden Age, or in Tang Dynasty China (618-907 CE), or in the Mali Empire, the years 500-1000 CE were not dark at all. They were periods of remarkable intellectual, economic, and artistic flourishing. What the "Dark Ages" actually describes is a narrow regional perspective — Western Europe after Rome. And even there, Green argues, monasteries preserved literacy, Charlemagne attempted to revive learning, and the period laid foundations for the later medieval flowering. The episode is ultimately about who gets to define what counts as "progress."

## Key Concepts Covered

- The term "Dark Ages" coined by Petrarch (14th century) as a rhetorical contrast — not a historical category
- What was actually happening 500-1000 CE: Byzantine Empire, Islamic Golden Age, Tang Dynasty China
- The Mali Empire and Sub-Saharan African kingdoms — thriving outside the European frame
- Western European monasticism: preservation of Latin texts, agricultural innovation, literacy centers
- Charlemagne (768-814 CE): Carolingian Renaissance, standardization of script (Carolingian minuscule)
- The problem of Eurocentric periodization: calling an era "dark" because one region declined
- Technology in the supposed Dark Ages: heavy plow, horse collar, water mills, crop rotation
- What "progress" means — and who defines it

## Studio Ideas

1. **Who Gets to Define Progress?:** Students write a 1-page argument: is the label "Dark Ages" defensible or misleading? They must use evidence from at least two different regions (Europe AND somewhere else). Targets WH.6_12.1 and W.4.

2. **Non-European 500-1000 CE:** Students choose one non-European civilization from this period (Islamic Golden Age, Tang China, Byzantine Empire, Mali Empire) and research one specific intellectual or technological contribution. They present: why should this be in the story we tell about this era? Targets WH.6_12.1 and IR.1-5.

3. **Historiography Audit:** Students find a textbook description of the "Dark Ages" and annotate it: what perspective does it center? What does it leave out? What would change if you rewrote it from a non-European vantage point? Targets R.7 and WH.6_12.1.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The "Dark Ages" concept is a lesson in how historical narratives are constructed — who tells the story, from whose vantage point, and what gets called "progress" or "decline." The same move (calling an era dark because your preferred civilization wasn’t dominant) gets made constantly in contemporary political arguments: the framing of immigration, the description of non-Western countries as "developing" or "underdeveloped," the assumption that Western liberal democracy is the endpoint of history. Green’s episode is training for a skill students will use for the rest of their lives: asking whose perspective a historical claim is told from before accepting it.

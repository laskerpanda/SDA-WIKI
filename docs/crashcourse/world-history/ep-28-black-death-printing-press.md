## What This Episode Is About

Green covers two transformative forces that remade European society within a century of each other. The Black Death arrived in Europe via Mongol trade routes in 1347 and killed an estimated 30-60% of the European population within five years — the most catastrophic mortality event in recorded history. Green examines not just the death toll but the social consequences: labor scarcity gave surviving peasants bargaining power that hadn’t existed under feudalism; the plague's apparent randomness — striking the pious alongside the sinful — undermined the Church’s authority as an explanatory system; and the "dance of death" tradition in art signals a cultural obsession with mortality that reshaped how Europeans thought about individual life. The second transformation: Gutenberg’s printing press (c. 1450) didn’t just make books cheaper — it shifted who controlled information. Green connects both transformations to the conditions that made the Reformation possible.

## Key Concepts Covered

- Bubonic Plague origins: Yersinia pestis bacterium, rat-flea transmission, origins in Central Asia
- Mongol trade route transmission: the Silk Road carrying plague from Central Asia to Crimea to Europe
- Death toll: estimated 75-200 million globally, 30-60% of European population 1347-1353
- Social consequences: labor scarcity, peasant bargaining power, feudal system strain
- Cultural consequences: memento mori tradition, danse macabre, obsession with individual mortality
- Church authority undermined: if prayer didn’t stop the plague, what was prayer for?
- Gutenberg’s printing press (c. 1450): movable type enabling mass production of texts
- Information control shift: from Church scriptoria to commercial printers serving a literate public

## Studio Ideas

1. **Catastrophe as Catalyst:** Students argue: did the Black Death ultimately make European society better or worse? They must engage with both the death toll and the social changes (labor power, Church authority) it triggered. Targets WH.6_12.4 and W.4.

2. **Information Control Then and Now:** Students compare the printing press to one modern information technology (the internet, social media) using the same criteria: who controlled information before, who controls it after, what changed about power? Targets WH.6_12.4 and R.9.

3. **The Plague and the Church:** Students examine primary sources from survivors of the Black Death and analyze: how did the plague change how ordinary people related to the Church? What does this reveal about the relationship between catastrophe and belief? Targets WH.6_12.4 + WH.6_12.2 and R.9.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The printing press argument Green makes — that a technology shifting who controls information reshapes political power — is the argument being made about the internet right now. The same disruption: institutions that controlled information (the Church, then newspapers, then broadcast television) lost the monopoly on narrative that made their authority possible. What fills the vacuum is unpredictable. In the 16th century, it was the Protestant Reformation — which produced both religious liberty and a century of religious war. The contemporary version of this disruption is still in progress.

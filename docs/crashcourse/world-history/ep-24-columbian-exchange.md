## What This Episode Is About

Green argues that the Columbian Exchange — the biological transfer of plants, animals, and diseases between the Old World and New World after 1492 — was the single most transformative event in world history, and that most people massively underestimate it. The episode leads with disease: 90% of the indigenous population of the Americas may have died within a century of contact, not from conquest but from epidemic illness against which they had no immunity. Then Green pivots to food: the potato, corn, tomatoes, and cacao transformed European diets and economies; in return, horses, pigs, and wheat transformed the Americas. His argument is that the Columbian Exchange matters not because of what Columbus did, but because of what microscopic organisms and plants did when they crossed an ocean — often faster and with more lasting consequence than any army.

## Key Concepts Covered

- The biological context of 1492: Old World and New World had been isolated for 10,000+ years, developing separate disease environments
- Disease as the primary agent of depopulation: smallpox, measles, typhus arriving ahead of conquistadors; the 90% mortality estimate
- Why indigenous peoples had no immunity and Europeans did: domesticated animals as disease reservoirs; zoonotic illness
- Plants going east: potato (feeding Europe's poor, enabling population growth), corn, tomatoes, chocolate, tobacco, rubber
- Animals going west: horses (transformed Plains cultures), pigs, cattle, sheep; ecological transformation
- Plants going west: wheat, rice, sugar (with its plantation system and slave labor implications)
- Alfred Crosby's term "Columbian Exchange" and why naming matters
- The demographic collapse and its political consequence: depopulation made European colonization easier than conquest could have
- Green's argument: the most important agents of the Columbian Exchange were not people but diseases and plants

## Studio Ideas

1. **The Unintended Consequences Argument:** Green claims no one "planned" the Columbian Exchange — it happened because of biological forces larger than any human decision. Students write a paragraph arguing for or against this claim: Was the suffering caused by the Columbian Exchange anyone's "fault"? They must use at least two pieces of evidence from the episode. Builds W.4 and forces engagement with WH.6_12.5.

2. **What's on Your Plate?** Students inventory a meal they eat regularly and trace the origins of its ingredients — which came from the Old World? Which from the New? Then they write a short reflection: what does this meal reveal about the Columbian Exchange? This is a personal entry point into WH.6_12.4 (economic and cultural development affecting individuals).

3. **The 90% Figure:** Green cites the 90% mortality estimate. Students examine: what kind of evidence would support this number? What kind of source could tell us this? They practice the WH.6_12.5 skill of evaluating primary and secondary sources by asking: how do historians know what they know about populations that left few written records?

4. **Las Casas + Green:** Students read the Las Casas excerpt (reading library) alongside a recap of this episode. Assignment: Green explains what happened structurally (disease, biology). Las Casas tells you what it looked like on the ground. Write a paragraph that uses both sources to answer: What was lost in the Columbian Exchange that Green's framework doesn't fully capture? Builds R.9 (argumentative reasoning across texts).

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The Columbian Exchange is the most consequential ecological event since the ice age, and it is still happening. The potato that came from the Americas to Europe fed the Industrial Revolution's workforce. The corn that came from the Americas now feeds more humans than any other crop on earth. The diseases that went the other way killed an estimated 90% of Indigenous populations in the Americas — the largest demographic collapse in human history. The contemporary version of this exchange is ongoing: invasive species spread along global trade routes, COVID-19 traveled the modern Columbian Exchange routes, and debates about GMOs and food security are debates about who controls the crops that came from this exchange. 1492 did not end — it is the beginning of the world most people reading this were born into.

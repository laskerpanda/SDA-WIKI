## What This Episode Is About

Green uses WWI to make an argument about causation: how do historians decide what "caused" an event? He walks through the standard MAIN causes (Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism) while pushing back on the "one thing caused it" narrative. His central argument: WWI is the case study in unintended consequences — every major power entered the war expecting it to be short, manageable, and politically useful. None of them anticipated four years of industrial-scale slaughter. Green is particularly good on the human cost: trench warfare, chemical weapons, the futility of sending men across no man's land. He ends with the question the war forces: when political leaders make decisions that lead to millions of deaths, and no one wanted that outcome, who is responsible?

## Key Concepts Covered

- The assassination of Franz Ferdinand (June 28, 1914): the spark and why it was not sufficient cause on its own
- MAIN causes: Militarism (arms race, Germany's military build-up), Alliances (Triple Alliance vs. Triple Entente), Imperialism (European competition for colonies), Nationalism (Serbian nationalism, Austro-Hungarian anxiety)
- The mobilization logic: why each country's calculation made war feel safe — and why all of them were wrong
- Schlieffen Plan: Germany's two-front war plan; why it required invading Belgium; how Belgium's neutrality brought Britain in
- The Western Front: trench warfare; the stalemate; the logic of attrition
- Technology of mass killing: machine guns, barbed wire, artillery, chemical weapons (chlorine, mustard gas)
- The Battle of the Somme: 60,000 British casualties on the first day; 1 million total casualties
- The home front: women in manufacturing, propaganda, civilian hardship; the war changing who does what work
- Colonial troops: Africans, South Asians, and Australians fighting in a war over European ambitions
- Green's argument: WWI destroyed the Enlightenment faith that reason and progress would prevent catastrophe

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The alliance system that turned a regional assassination into a world war is a structural problem that hasn't been solved — it's been rebuilt. NATO's Article 5 (an attack on one is an attack on all) is a direct descendant of the 1914 alliance logic: the commitment that makes the alliance credible is also the commitment that makes a small conflict capable of pulling everyone in. Nuclear deterrence adds another layer: the weapons are designed to make war too costly to start, but the same design makes any conflict that starts enormously dangerous to stop. Green's episode also raises the question of decision-making speed: in 1914, mobilization orders moved faster than diplomatic communication, and leaders found themselves locked into sequences they couldn't reverse. That gap between military momentum and political decision-making is, if anything, larger today — modern weapons can escalate a conflict in hours. And the question of who fights empires' wars — colonial troops, working-class conscripts, populations with no say in the decision — is still the question being asked about every war. See also [WH-05: The Cost of War](../../units/studio-archive/wh-05-the-cost-of-war.md) for how the consequences of WWI played out in literature and politics.

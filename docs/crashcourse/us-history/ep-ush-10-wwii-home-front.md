## Episode Summary

While CrashCourse World History covers WWII's global scope (see ep-40 and ep-41), this episode focuses on what the war meant for Americans at home. Green covers the economic transformation — the Depression finally ends as war production employs everyone; women enter the industrial workforce in massive numbers ("Rosie the Riveter"); Black Americans migrate north and west in the second wave of the Great Migration. He also covers what the war demanded: rationing, propaganda, war bonds, and the enormous human cost (400,000 American dead). The darkest moment: Japanese American internment — 120,000 US citizens imprisoned by Executive Order 9066 without charges or trial. Green's argument: WWII is remembered as "the good war," but its moral complexity — fighting fascism abroad while practicing racism at home — is essential to understanding what it actually was.

## Key Concepts

- **Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941)** — Japanese attack on US naval base; US enters WWII the next day
- **War production mobilization** — US factories retool; GDP doubles; unemployment drops to 1%
- **Women in the workforce** — 6 million women take industrial jobs; "Rosie the Riveter" as cultural symbol
- **Second Great Migration** — 1.5 million Black Americans move north/west 1940–1970 for war jobs
- **Japanese American internment** — Executive Order 9066; *Korematsu v. United States* upholds it (1944)
- **The Holocaust and American response** — US knew about death camps by 1942; chose not to bomb rail lines
- **Atomic bombs** — Hiroshima and Nagasaki (August 1945); Japan surrenders; raises moral questions that persist

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The WWII home front established what industrial democracy looks like under total war mobilization — and also what civil liberties look like when national security is used to justify suspending them. Japanese American internment was ruled constitutional by the Supreme Court in 1944 (Korematsu v. United States) — a decision not formally overturned until 2018. The military remained racially segregated through a war fought against racial ideology. These contradictions between stated values and actual wartime practice are not historical accidents; they're what happens when security fears override constitutional protections. Every subsequent debate about civil liberties in wartime — detention without trial, surveillance programs, travel bans — engages with the same tension the home front experience revealed.

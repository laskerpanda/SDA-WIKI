## What This Episode Is About

Green examines English colonization through the lens of Jamestown and the Powhatan Confederacy, arguing that the standard story — brave English settlers overcoming hardship to build a new world — erases the political sophistication of the indigenous peoples they encountered. The Powhatan Confederacy was a complex political alliance of 30+ tribes; their initial accommodation of English settlers was a calculated strategy, not weakness. Green traces how that relationship soured: English settlers demanded food during famines, introduced tobacco monoculture, and expanded relentlessly onto Native land. The episode asks why we remember John Smith but not Opechancanough.

---

## Key Concepts Covered

- Roanoke Colony (1585–1590): the "Lost Colony" and what its failure reveals about early English colonization strategies
- Jamestown (1607): why it was founded (profit, not religious freedom), who settled it (mostly "gentlemen" unaccustomed to labor), and why it nearly failed
- The Starving Time (1609–1610): 80% of Jamestown's population died
- Powhatan Confederacy: 30+ tribes, sophisticated political structure, Wahunsenacah (Chief Powhatan) as a political strategist
- Pocahontas: separating myth from history — she was approximately 10–12 when she "saved" John Smith (if she did); her actual story involves capture, conversion, and use as a political hostage
- Tobacco as the colony's salvation and catastrophe: economic success required land, which required displacing Native peoples
- The Anglo-Powhatan Wars (1610–1646): three wars ending in Native defeat and removal
- Opechancanough: the Powhatan war leader who nearly destroyed Jamestown in 1622 and again in 1644
- Indentured servitude as the first labor system before slavery

## Studio Ideas

1. **Pocahontas: The Three Versions:** Students compare (1) the Disney version, (2) Green's version, and (3) a brief primary-source-adjacent account of her capture and death in England. They identify: what does each version emphasize? What does each leave out? Who benefits from each version? Targets 9-10.R.9b (multiple accounts in different media).

2. **The Starving Time Math:** Jamestown had ~500 settlers in fall 1609; 60–90 survived to spring 1610. Students calculate the mortality rate and then research one explanation for why it happened — was it drought? Conflict? Poor planning? They write a 1-paragraph causal argument using evidence. Builds WH.6_12.5 (causes and effects using sources) + argument writing.

3. **Opechancanough's Strategy:** Students read a brief secondary-source description of the 1622 attack (Opechancanough's coordinated strike on 31 English settlements simultaneously). Then they write a paragraph from his strategic perspective: What was the goal? Why did it fail long-term? Forces empathy and analytical thinking about power, WH.6_12.3.

## Episode Summary

This episode covers WWI from the American angle — why the US stayed neutral until 1917, what changed (the Zimmermann Telegram, unrestricted submarine warfare, economic ties to Britain), and what the war did to American society once the country entered. Green examines the propaganda machine that turned public opinion, the suppression of dissent through the Espionage and Sedition Acts, and the experience of soldiers who arrived on the Western Front to find a war nothing like what they'd been told. He also covers what the war did at home: the Great Migration begins as Black Americans move north for war industry jobs; women enter the workforce in large numbers. Green's argument: America's entry into WWI reshaped both the war and American society in ways that outlasted the armistice.

## Key Concepts

- **American neutrality 1914–1917** — economic ties to Britain, Wilson's "too proud to fight"
- **Zimmermann Telegram** — Germany proposes Mexico attack the US; intercepted by Britain
- **Unrestricted submarine warfare** — Germany sinks Lusitania (1915) and resumes sinking US ships (1917)
- **War propaganda** — Committee on Public Information; "Four Minute Men"; Liberty Bond posters
- **Espionage Act (1917) / Sedition Act (1918)** — criminalizes anti-war speech
- **The Great Migration** — 500,000 Black Americans move north for war industry jobs 1916–1920
- **Wilson's Fourteen Points** — America's vision for post-war world; Senate rejects League of Nations

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

American entry into WWI established patterns that have repeated in every subsequent war: the transformation of public opinion through propaganda campaigns, the suppression of dissent through wartime legislation (the Espionage Act and Sedition Act — both still on the books and used in prosecutions today), and the gap between stated war aims and actual geopolitical interests. The Espionage Act used to prosecute Daniel Ellsberg for leaking the Pentagon Papers was passed in 1917. The same Act was used in the prosecution of Edward Snowden and Chelsea Manning. Understanding the WWI-era origins of these laws matters for evaluating how they're being applied today and whether the suppression of dissent they enabled then is what their contemporary application is producing now.

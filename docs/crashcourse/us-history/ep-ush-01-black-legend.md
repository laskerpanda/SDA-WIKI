## What This Episode Is About

Green opens US History by challenging the default Eurocentric framing of American history — the idea that US history starts with the Pilgrims, or even Columbus. Instead he begins with the complexity of Spanish colonization and the "Black Legend": the historical narrative (largely promoted by England) that the Spanish were uniquely brutal colonizers. Green complicates this, arguing that while Spanish colonization was brutal, the Black Legend was partly propaganda — English colonizers were not gentler, just better at winning the narrative war. The episode establishes a core habit for the course: question whose story is being told and why.

---

## Key Concepts Covered

- The "Black Legend" — the 16th–18th century English and Dutch narrative portraying Spanish colonizers as exceptionally cruel
- Origins: Bartolomé de las Casas's *A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies* — a Spanish priest's own condemnation of Spanish atrocities
- Why England amplified the Black Legend: commercial and military rivalry with Spain
- The Encomienda system: forced labor of indigenous peoples in exchange for "Christianization"
- The actual scale of indigenous population collapse: 50–90% in a century — disease as the primary cause
- Hernán Cortés and the conquest of the Aztec (Mexica) Empire
- The complexity of indigenous alliances: not all Native peoples opposed the Spanish — many allied with them against common enemies
- The Pueblo Revolt (1680): successful indigenous uprising against Spanish rule in New Mexico — 400 Spanish killed, 21 priests killed, Spanish expelled for 12 years
- Green's argument: US history should start with its true starting point — the full hemisphere, not just the thirteen colonies

## Studio Ideas

1. **The Propaganda Audit:** Students read a 1-paragraph excerpt from the Black Legend (English propaganda) alongside a paragraph from de las Casas. They identify: What claim is each making? What evidence do they use? What might each writer be leaving out? Targets 9-10.R.9a and OEC skill strand.

2. **Who Gets to Tell the Story?** Students write two 3-sentence summaries of the Spanish conquest: one from the perspective of a Spanish colonizer, one from a Tlaxcalan ally of Cortés. Then they compare: what changes? What stays the same? Forces perspective-taking and builds toward WH.6_12.5 work with primary/secondary sources.

3. **The Pueblo Revolt:** Green gives this event about 90 seconds. Students research it for 15 minutes and add three facts Green left out — then discuss: Why do you think he left those out? What does that tell us about how CrashCourse makes choices? Builds critical media literacy alongside historical content.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The Black Legend is not just a 16th-century story — it's the blueprint for how nations still frame their rivals today. When one country calls another "barbaric" to justify military or economic pressure, it is using the same rhetorical move English colonizers used to discredit Spain. Modern foreign policy debates are full of this: the language of human rights used selectively, accusations of atrocities deployed against rivals while the accuser's own record goes unexamined. Las Casas — who documented Spanish violence to demand the Church intervene — also shows the other move: using the powerful institution's own standards against it. If you want to go deeper on Las Casas and the question of who gets to tell the story, see the [Las Casas reading library entry](../../reading-library/social-studies/las-casas-short-account.md) and [WH-03: Writing Against Empire](../../units/studio-archive/wh-03-writing-against-empire.md).

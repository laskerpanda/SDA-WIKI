## Episode Summary

John Green covers the Civil Rights Movement from the legal strategy of the NAACP through the sit-ins, Freedom Rides, March on Washington, and legislative victories of the mid-1960s. He traces how the movement built its strategy — Thurgood Marshall's courtroom approach (*Brown v. Board*, 1954), MLK's philosophy of nonviolent direct action, SNCC's grassroots organizing, and the role of televised violence (Bull Connor's fire hoses in Birmingham) in shifting public opinion. He covers the major legislation: Civil Rights Act (1964), Voting Rights Act (1965). Green also raises the tensions: Malcolm X's challenge to nonviolence; the limits of legal victory without economic equality; the backlash that followed. His argument: the Civil Rights Movement succeeded at dismantling legal segregation — but the question of whether legal equality produces actual equality remains open.

## Key Concepts

- **NAACP legal strategy** — incremental courtroom wins leading to *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954)
- **Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955)** — 381 days; Rosa Parks; MLK emerges as leader
- **Sit-ins and Freedom Rides** — nonviolent direct action to expose and shame segregationists
- **Birmingham Campaign (1963)** — Bull Connor's violence on national television; turns public opinion
- **March on Washington (August 28, 1963)** — "I Have a Dream" speech; 250,000 people
- **Civil Rights Act (1964)** — prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin
- **Voting Rights Act (1965)** — eliminates literacy tests and other barriers to Black voting
- **Malcolm X and Black Power** — challenge to nonviolence and integration as the only path

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The Civil Rights movement is living history: the people who were there are still alive, the legal gains are still being contested, and the underlying conditions the movement addressed have not been fully resolved. Voting rights, affirmative action, school integration, police reform, and the racial wealth gap are not historical questions — they are policy debates happening in legislatures and courtrooms right now. The movement is also the most thoroughly studied case in American history of how social change actually happens: what combination of legal strategy, direct action, political pressure, media coverage, and coalition-building is required to move institutions that have strong incentives not to move. That model is studied and applied by every subsequent social movement.

## Episode Summary

Green covers America's longest war and the political scandal that destroyed a presidency. On Vietnam: why the US got in (containment doctrine, domino theory), why it couldn't get out (political cost of losing), and what the war actually looked like on the ground (guerrilla warfare, civilian casualties, the draft, the credibility gap as the government's public statements diverged from reality). On the home front: massive anti-war protest, Kent State (1970), the Pentagon Papers revealing systematic government deception. Then Watergate: Nixon's 1972 re-election campaign authorizes a break-in at Democratic headquarters; the cover-up unravels over two years; Nixon resigns August 9, 1974. Green's argument: Vietnam and Watergate together produced a collapse in American trust in government that reshaped politics for decades — and that hasn't fully recovered.

## Key Concepts

- **Containment doctrine** — US foreign policy since Truman: stop Soviet/communist expansion everywhere
- **Domino theory** — if Vietnam falls to communism, all of Southeast Asia follows; used to justify intervention
- **Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964)** — gives LBJ authority to escalate; later revealed as based on exaggerated/false reports
- **Tet Offensive (1968)** — North Vietnamese attack across South Vietnam; US claims it's winning; public realizes it isn't
- **The credibility gap** — divergence between government statements and reality; fundamental to understanding the era
- **Kent State (May 4, 1970)** — National Guard kills 4 student protesters at Ohio university
- **Pentagon Papers (1971)** — classified study showing systematic government deception on Vietnam; *New York Times* publishes; Supreme Court allows it
- **Watergate (1972–1974)** — break-in → cover-up → Saturday Night Massacre → tapes → resignation
- **War Powers Act (1973)** — Congress tries to limit presidential war-making authority

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Vietnam and Watergate together produced the collapse of institutional trust that defines American political culture today. The public's default assumption that government officials are not telling the truth, that official statements should be verified rather than believed, and that powerful people routinely abuse their positions — all of that dates to the 1970s and has never fully recovered. The question of whether the institutions that exposed Nixon (the press, the Senate, the special prosecutor, the Supreme Court) would hold in a future crisis has been tested repeatedly since Watergate. Understanding what those institutions actually did in 1972–74, and what conditions made them work, is necessary for evaluating whether they're adequate now and what it would take to strengthen them.

## Episode Summary

John Green traces the transformation of the American economy between the Civil War and 1900 — from a nation of farms and small workshops to the world's largest industrial power. The episode covers the railroad boom (which stitched the continent together and created a national market), the rise of corporations and trusts, the brutal conditions in mines and factories, and the emergence of organized labor as workers pushed back. Key figures: Andrew Carnegie (steel), John D. Rockefeller (Standard Oil), and the workers — immigrant and native-born — who ran the machines. Green's argument: industrialization created enormous wealth AND enormous inequality simultaneously, and the question of who should benefit from economic growth became the defining political question of the era.

## Key Concepts

- **Vertical and horizontal integration** — Carnegie buys everything (ore to rail to steel); Rockefeller buys all competitors
- **Trusts and monopolies** — Standard Oil controls 90% of US oil refining by 1880
- **Railroad as infrastructure** — creates national market, enables mass production
- **Labor conditions** — 12-hour days, no safety regulations, child labor, no job security
- **Immigration and the workforce** — waves of Southern/Eastern European immigrants fuel factory growth
- **Knights of Labor → AFL** — two different strategies for labor organizing

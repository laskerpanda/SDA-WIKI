## What This Episode Is About

Green covers two developments that seem unrelated but both, he argues, contributed to the conditions for the American Revolution: the Great Awakening (a religious revival that challenged established authority and created a more egalitarian Protestant culture) and the Seven Years War (the first truly global war, which left Britain broke and convinced Parliament that colonists should help pay for their own defense). The episode's main argument: colonial American identity was forged in the mid-18th century by these two forces — one spiritual, one military — and both made the colonists more willing to resist British authority.

---

## Key Concepts Covered

- The Great Awakening (1730s–1740s): mass religious revival led by preachers like George Whitefield and Jonathan Edwards
- "Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God" (Edwards, 1741): the most famous sermon in American history — fire and brimstone, but also deeply democratic (God's wrath applies to everyone equally, regardless of social rank)
- New Light vs. Old Light: the split between revival-oriented and traditional Calvinist churches
- Why the Great Awakening mattered politically: it democratized religious authority — if you could have a direct relationship with God, you didn't need a minister, and by extension, maybe you didn't need a king
- The Seven Years War (1756–1763) — called the French and Indian War in American history, but actually fought on four continents
- Origins: conflict over the Ohio River Valley, where Virginia land speculators (including George Washington's family) competed with France and its Native allies
- George Washington's early military career — and early failure (Fort Necessity, 1754)
- Britain wins the war but goes broke: £122 million in debt
- The Proclamation of 1763: Britain prohibits colonists from settling west of the Appalachians — meant to prevent more expensive Native wars, experienced by colonists as tyranny
- Salutary neglect: Britain had largely left the colonies alone for decades; the Seven Years War ended that
- Green's argument: the Revolution didn't come from nowhere — it came from colonists who had developed their own identity (Great Awakening) and then felt betrayed by a government that suddenly started treating them like subjects instead of partners (Seven Years War aftermath)

## Studio Ideas

1. **The Awakening's Political Logic:** Students read the key claim: "If you can have a direct relationship with God without a minister, why do you need a king?" They write a paragraph either agreeing or disagreeing with this logical chain — using evidence from the episode and one example from elsewhere. Forces logical analysis (9-10.R.9c) and historical thinking.

2. **The Proclamation of 1763 — Two Perspectives:** Students write two 2-sentence reactions to the Proclamation: one from a British Parliament member (we just spent a fortune keeping colonists safe — they owe us) and one from a Virginia land speculator (we fought this war for the right to expand). Then they discuss: Was the British position unreasonable? This builds WH.6_12.5 causal analysis and perspective-taking.

3. **Young George Washington:** Green mentions that Washington's early military career included notable failures. Students research Fort Necessity (1754) — Washington's surrender to French forces — and write a paragraph: what does this failure reveal about Washington that the standard "founding father" story doesn't include? Then they connect: how might failure have shaped his later leadership? Targets 9-10.R.8b (how a character's background shapes their actions) applied to historical figure.

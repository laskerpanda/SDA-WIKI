# Era 1: Emergence of Civilizations and Religions Around the Globe

**Course:** English I + World History (Grade 9)
**Era span:** Prehistory through approximately 600 CE
**Studios that use this era:** Studio 1 — Power & Belief · Studio 2 — Builders & Breakers

---

## What This Era Is About

Before nations, before borders, before the internet, before books — human beings figured out how to organize large societies. That took about ten thousand years and happened simultaneously in multiple places across the globe.

Era 1 covers the emergence of:
- **Agriculture** — when humans stopped following food and started growing it (Fertile Crescent, Yellow River Valley, Indus Valley, Mesoamerica)
- **Cities and civilizations** — Mesopotamia (Sumer, Babylon, Assyria), Egypt, the Indus Valley, China's first dynasties, Greece, Rome
- **Writing systems** — cuneiform, hieroglyphics, early Chinese characters — because complex societies need records
- **Political systems** — from tribal leadership to city-states to empires; from Hammurabi's Code to Roman law to Greek democracy
- **World religions** — the foundational belief systems that still shape billions of lives: Hinduism (earliest organized), Judaism, Buddhism, Christianity, Islam (emerging at the end of this era)
- **Science and technology** — irrigation systems, astronomical observation, mathematics, medical practices, engineering (the Colosseum, the Pyramids, the Great Wall's early forms)
- **Trade networks** — the beginning of global economic connection: early Silk Road routes, Mediterranean sea trade, trans-Saharan connections

The essential question running through this era: **What does it take to build a society — and what does it cost?**

## All Six Standards Applied to Era 1

### WH.6_12.1 + Era 1 — Science and Technology of Early Civilizations { #wh6-121 }

**Full standard:** Analyze historical achievements related to science and technology.

**In Era 1, this means:** Analyze the scientific and technological achievements of early civilizations and explain how they shaped society and human development.

**Key topics:**
- Mesopotamian contributions: writing (cuneiform), the wheel, the plow, irrigation systems, the 60-minute hour
- Egyptian contributions: pyramids (engineering + mathematics), papyrus, embalming/medicine, astronomy for calendar
- Greek contributions: geometry (Euclid), logic (Aristotle), the foundations of Western medicine (Hippocrates), the atomic theory (Democritus)
- Roman contributions: concrete, aqueducts, roads, the Julian calendar
- Chinese contributions: papermaking, bronze casting, silk production, early gunpowder precursors
- Indus Valley: advanced urban planning, sewage systems, standardized weights and measures

**FPS Skill Strand:** OEC (Obtain, Evaluate, Communicate)
*Students read complex informational texts about early scientific and technological achievements and support inferences with textual evidence.*

**ELA connection:** 9-10.R.2 — Reading complex informational texts, developing inferences with textual evidence.

**Learning targets:**
- I can identify a specific technological achievement from an early civilization and explain how it worked.
- I can analyze how that achievement changed how people lived (not just what it was, but what it did to society).
- I can connect a specific early technology to a present-day equivalent or descendant.

**Example inquiry angles:**
- "How did Roman engineering (aqueducts, concrete, roads) make empire expansion possible — and what happened to infrastructure when the empire collapsed?"
- "How did the development of writing systems change the nature of political power? (You can't enforce a law you can't write down.)"
- "How did Islamic Golden Age mathematical and astronomical discoveries preserve and advance Greek knowledge during Europe's early medieval period?"

### WH.6_12.2 + Era 1 — Religions and Ideologies of Early Civilizations { #wh6-122 }

**Full standard:** Explain historical changes related to religions and ideologies.

**In Era 1, this means:** Explain how early belief systems developed and shaped civilizations — how religion and politics, religion and law, religion and daily life were often inseparable.

**Key topics:**
- **Hinduism** — oldest continuous religious tradition; caste system, dharma, karma, multiple deities as aspects of one divine reality; emerged in Indus Valley, developed in Vedic period
- **Judaism** — monotheism, covenant, law (Torah), the concept of a chosen people; Babylonian exile; diaspora; foundational to Christianity and Islam
- **Buddhism** — founded by Siddhartha Gautama (~500 BCE); Four Noble Truths, Noble Eightfold Path, concept of impermanence; spread along Silk Road trade routes into China, Southeast Asia
- **Christianity** — rooted in Judaism; Jesus of Nazareth; Pauline missions spreading throughout Roman Empire; Constantine's Edict of Milan (313 CE) and Christianity as Roman state religion
- **Early Islam** — Muhammad's revelations beginning 610 CE; the Quran; five pillars; immediate political unification of Arabian Peninsula; rapid spread after Muhammad's death (632 CE)
- **Greek and Roman polytheism** — gods as personifications of natural forces and human qualities; religious festivals as political events; the relationship between civic religion and political authority (the emperor as divine or semi-divine)
- **Confucianism** — not a religion in the traditional sense, but a framework for social and political order (filial piety, hierarchy, virtue in governance); shaped Chinese bureaucracy for millennia

**FPS Skill Strand:** Argumentation
*Students write explanatory pieces describing how early belief systems developed and shaped civilizations.*

**ELA connection:** 9-10.W.3 — Explanatory writing with a clear thesis and evidence from multiple reliable sources.

**Learning targets:**
- I can explain the origins and core beliefs of at least two early world religions.
- I can explain how a specific religion shaped the political structure or social organization of a civilization.
- I can identify a place where religious authority and political authority were fused — and analyze what that fusion meant for ordinary people.

**Example inquiry angles:**
- "How did the concept of divine kingship (pharaoh in Egypt, emperor in China, Roman emperor-gods) function as a political tool?"
- "How did early Islam create a political system at the same time it created a religious one — and why did that dual nature make it spread so fast?"
- "How did Confucian values shape the Chinese civil service examination system, and how did that change who could hold power?"

### WH.6_12.3 + Era 1 — Political Systems and Their Effects on People { #wh6-123 }

**Full standard:** Analyze the effects of different political systems on people.

**In Era 1, this means:** Analyze how early political systems — from city-states to empires, from law codes to democratic assemblies — affected the daily lives, rights, and freedoms of the people living under them.

**Key topics:**
- **Hammurabi's Code** (~1750 BCE) — one of the earliest written legal codes; establishes the principle that law should be public and knowable; also reveals stark class and gender inequalities ("an eye for an eye" meant different things for slaves vs. free citizens)
- **Athenian democracy** — limited to adult male citizens (excludes women, slaves, foreigners); but establishes the radical idea that ordinary citizens should have a voice in government; the Assembly, the Agora, ostracism
- **The Roman Republic and Empire** — Senate and popular assemblies in the Republic; gradual slide to one-man rule; how Roman citizenship was used as a tool of empire (extend citizenship to control conquered peoples)
- **Chinese imperial system** — Mandate of Heaven as justification for rule; Han Dynasty centralization; the bureaucracy; peasant obligations and rebellions
- **Mesopotamian city-states** — early experiments in governance; different systems in different cities; the ziggurat as political and religious center
- **Caste system in early India** — a political-religious system that determined occupation, marriage, and social status by birth; Brahmin (priests), Kshatriya (warriors), Vaishya (merchants), Shudra (laborers), Dalits (untouchables)

**FPS Skill Strand:** Interpretation & Analysis
*Students analyze political texts describing early governments and evaluate perspective and bias.*

**ELA connection:** 9-10.R.9a — Analyze the author's point of view or purpose and possible biases; compare texts from differing perspectives.

**Learning targets:**
- I can explain how a specific early political system determined what rights people had.
- I can analyze Hammurabi's Code (or another primary source) for what it reveals about who had power and who didn't.
- I can explain how political systems in Greece and Rome connect to present-day democratic systems.

**Example inquiry angles:**
- "Is Hammurabi's Code evidence of justice or evidence of oppression? (Argue using the text itself.)"
- "How did Roman citizenship work as a political tool — and what does that tell us about empire-building?"
- "Greek democracy excluded women and enslaved people. Does that make it not actually democratic, or is it still meaningful as a political innovation?"

### WH.6_12.4 + Era 1 — Social, Cultural, and Economic Development { #wh6-124 }

**Full standard:** Analyze the influence of social, cultural, and economic development on individuals.

**In Era 1, this means:** Analyze how the shift from hunter-gatherer to agricultural to urban society changed what individual human lives looked like — and how social stratification, trade, and cultural exchange affected ordinary people.

**Key topics:**
- **The agricultural revolution** — settled farming changes everything: food surplus → population growth → specialization → social hierarchy → writing (to track the surplus) → civilization
- **Social stratification** — every early civilization develops class systems; priests, warriors, merchants, farmers, artisans, slaves — how did your birth position determine your life?
- **Trade networks** — Silk Road (China to Rome), trans-Saharan routes, Mediterranean sea trade; what moved along with goods: ideas, diseases, religions, technologies
- **Cultural diffusion** — how civilizations influenced each other through trade, conquest, and migration; the spread of alphabets, crops, religious ideas, technologies
- **Gender and social roles** — women in early civilizations had varying levels of rights depending on the society; Egyptian women had more legal rights than Greek women; Mesopotamian women could own property; Roman women's status changed across centuries
- **Slavery** — present in virtually every early civilization; different forms (chattel, debt, war captive); how slavery shaped the economy and social structure

**FPS Skill Strand:** Interpretation & Analysis
*Students identify and support central ideas about how social and economic structures shaped daily life.*

**ELA connection:** 9-10.R.4 — Determine main ideas, claims, or themes as they develop over the course of a text and support with textual evidence.

**Learning targets:**
- I can explain how the development of agriculture changed social structures in early civilizations.
- I can analyze how one social, cultural, or economic influence from an early civilization affected individual lives.
- I can connect a social structure from Era 1 (trade network, caste system, gender roles) to its influence on the present.

**Example inquiry angles:**
- "How did the development of agricultural surplus create social inequality? (Use at least two civilizations.)"
- "How did the Silk Road change what people believed — not just what they owned?"
- "What did it mean to be a woman in Athens vs. Egypt vs. Rome? (What does the variation tell us about the relationship between economic systems and gender rights?)"

### WH.6_12.5 + Era 1 — Causes and Effects Using Primary and Secondary Sources { #wh6-125 }

**Full standard:** Analyze causes and effects of global events using primary and/or secondary sources.

**In Era 1, this means:** Use primary and secondary sources to investigate major global events of the ancient world — the rise and fall of empires, the spread of religions, the development of trade — and analyze what caused them and what they caused.

**Key topics + events to investigate:**
- The fall of the Western Roman Empire (~476 CE) — multiple causes: military overextension, economic decline, internal political instability, external pressure from Germanic tribes; the effects: feudalism in Europe, preservation of Roman legacy in Eastern Empire (Byzantine)
- The rise of Islamic civilization — causes: Muhammad's life and teachings, tribal unity, Byzantine-Persian exhaustion after prolonged war; effects: rapid spread of a new religion and political system across North Africa, Middle East, Spain within 100 years of Muhammad's death
- The Mongol conquests — in Era 1/Era 2 overlap: causes in Mongolian steppe conditions and Genghis Khan's unification; effects on trade routes, plague spread, cultural destruction and preservation
- The Punic Wars — Rome vs. Carthage; causes in Mediterranean trade competition; effects in Roman domination of the Mediterranean and the destruction of Carthaginian civilization
- The Peloponnesian War — Athens vs. Sparta; causes in Athenian empire-building and Spartan fear; effects in Greek political fragmentation and the eventual vulnerability to Macedonian conquest

**FPS Skill Strand:** OEC
*Students compare multiple historical accounts of early global developments to analyze cause-and-effect relationships.*

**ELA connection:** 9-10.R.9b — Analyze various accounts of a subject told in different media, including determining which details are emphasized or omitted in each account.

**Learning targets:**
- I can identify the causes and effects of a significant global event in Era 1 using both a primary and secondary source.
- I can compare two accounts of the same event and explain what each one emphasizes or omits.
- I can explain why different sources might present the same event differently.

**Example inquiry angles:**
- "The fall of Rome: was it caused by internal rot or external invasion? (Compare Roman sources vs. modern historians.)"
- "How do Islamic accounts of Muhammad's early community differ from Western European accounts of early Islamic expansion?"
- "What do the sources that survive from ancient civilizations tell us about who had the power to create records?"

### WH.6_12.6 + Era 1 — Past to Present { #wh6-126 }

**Full standard:** Explain how past events connect to the present.

**In Era 1, this means:** Identify the greatest social, cultural, or economic events of early civilizations and analyze how those events still influence or inform the present day.

**Key connections:**
- Roman law and governance → U.S. Constitutional structure (Senate, checks and balances, rule of law)
- Greek democracy → modern democratic theory and practice
- World religions originating in Era 1 (Christianity, Islam, Judaism, Hinduism, Buddhism) → still the dominant belief systems of the 21st century
- The agricultural revolution → the food system, land ownership, and rural-urban divides that still shape economics
- Early trade networks → the logic of globalization; Silk Road trade anticipates container shipping
- Hammurabi's Code → the principle that law should be written, public, and knowable — foundational to modern legal systems

**FPS Skill Strand:** Argumentation
*Students present connections between early civilizations and modern systems.*

**ELA connection:** 9-10.C.1 — Construct and deliver formal and informal presentations incorporating multimedia when appropriate.

**Learning targets:**
- I can identify a specific development from Era 1 and trace a direct connection to a present-day institution, belief, or practice.
- I can present that connection to an audience using a format appropriate to the claim.
- I can answer questions about the connection, including challenges to it.

**Example inquiry angles:**
- "How does the Roman concept of citizenship still shape who gets rights in the United States?"
- "How do the foundational texts of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam still influence political decisions in 2026?"
- "The Silk Road is over — or is it? How does China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) reproduce the logic of ancient trade networks?"

## Key Vocabulary for Era 1

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| civilization | A complex society with cities, a government, social stratification, writing, and specialized labor |
| city-state | An independent city that governs itself and surrounding territory (Athens, Sparta, Uruk) |
| polytheism | Belief in multiple gods |
| monotheism | Belief in one god |
| theocracy | A government ruled by religious authority or in the name of a deity |
| democracy | Government by the people (in Athens: by some adult male citizens) |
| republic | Government where power is held by elected representatives, not a monarch |
| empire | A large territory controlled by one central power, often through conquest |
| code of law | A written, publicly available legal system (Hammurabi's Code is the earliest surviving example) |
| caste system | A hereditary social hierarchy where birth determines your occupation and social status |
| trade network | Routes along which goods, people, and ideas move between civilizations |
| cultural diffusion | The spread of ideas, practices, and technologies from one culture to another |
| social stratification | The division of society into layers (classes) with different levels of power and access |
| agricultural revolution | The transition from nomadic hunter-gatherer life to settled farming (began ~10,000 BCE) |

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The questions Era 1 raises — who should govern, what authority is legitimate, what a civilization owes its people — are being argued in every democracy today. Hammurabi's Code asked who the law was actually for; that question is at the center of criminal justice reform debates right now. Confucian meritocracy is the basis of civil service systems across East Asia and has influenced debates about college admissions and credentialism in the U.S. Greek democracy and the Athenian empire exist side by side in the same texts — the same tension between self-governance at home and coercion abroad that characterizes every modern great power. The world religions that emerged in Era 1 — Hinduism, Buddhism, Judaism, Christianity, Islam — are not historical artifacts; they shape the political worldviews of billions of people and drive conflicts from the Middle East to South Asia to American school board meetings. Nothing in Era 1 is settled. See [WH-01: Who Should Govern?](../../../units/studio-archive/wh-01-who-should-govern.md) for the studio that works most directly with these questions.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The questions Era 1 asks — who should govern? what gives a law its authority? how do societies organize themselves across wide distances? — are not ancient history problems. Every contemporary debate about democracy, judicial authority, constitutional legitimacy, and the relationship between religion and political power traces directly to the frameworks this era produced. Hammurabi's claim that law comes from the gods, Confucius's claim that good governance requires good character, Pericles' claim that those who are governed should have a voice in their governance: these are not three answers from 2,500 years ago — they are three answers that are still competing today. When you study Era 1, you're learning the vocabulary for debates that are happening right now. See [WH-01: Who Should Govern?](../../../units/studio-archive/wh-01-who-should-govern.md) and the [Era 1 Crosswalk](../../../crosswalk/gr9/era-1.md).

# World History Standards Overview

**Course:** English I + World History (Grade 9)
**Standards:** WH.6_12.1 through WH.6_12.6

---

## The Key Fact: Six Standards, Four Eras

World History in SDA uses **six standards that repeat across all four eras**. This is not a mistake — it is the design.

The same standard code (WH.6_12.1) means something different depending on which era you're studying. When you see a studio standard listed, you will always see both the code and the era:

> **WH.6_12.3 + Era 2** = Analyze the effects of medieval political systems (feudalism, church power) on people's lives.
> **WH.6_12.3 + Era 4** = Analyze the effects of 20th-century political systems (fascism, communism, democracy) on people's lives.

Same skill. Different content. That's how standards work in SDA.

**How to read a standard + era tag:** `[Standard Code] + [Era Number]`
Example: `WH.6_12.5 + Era 3` = Analyze causes and effects of global events during the Age of Revolutions using primary and secondary sources.

## The Six Standards

### WH.6_12.1 — Science & Technology { #wh6-121 }
Analyze historical achievements related to science and technology.

### WH.6_12.2 — Religions & Ideologies { #wh6-122 }
Explain historical changes related to religions and ideologies.

### WH.6_12.3 — Political Systems { #wh6-123 }
Analyze the effects of different political systems on people.

### WH.6_12.4 — Social & Economic Development { #wh6-124 }
Analyze the influence of social, cultural, and economic development on individuals.

### WH.6_12.5 — Causes & Effects { #wh6-125 }
Analyze causes and effects of global events using primary and/or secondary sources.

### WH.6_12.6 — Past to Present { #wh6-126 }
Explain how past events connect to the present.

### FPS Social Studies Skill Strands (Cross-Cutting)

These three skill strands cut across all six standards in all four eras. Every assessment in Social Studies touches at least one of these:

| Skill Strand | What It Means |
|-------------|--------------|
| **Argumentation** | Produce well-reasoned arguments supported with valid, relevant, and sufficient evidence |
| **Interpretation & Analysis** | Draw accurate, relevant, and sufficient conclusions from historical sources and information |
| **Obtain, Evaluate & Communicate (OEC)** | Research, evaluate source credibility, and communicate valid/reliable findings |

## The Four Eras at a Glance

| Era | Approximate Dates | Core Focus |
|-----|------------------|-----------|
| **Era 1** — Emergence of Civilizations and Religions Around the Globe | Prehistory through ~600 CE | Ancient empires, early religions, Greek and Roman political systems, development of agriculture, first trade networks |
| **Era 2** — Middle Ages and the Renaissance | ~500–1600 CE | Feudalism, Catholic Church dominance, Crusades, Black Death, Islamic Golden Age, Renaissance art and thought, early global trade |
| **Era 3** — Age of Revolutions | ~1400–1900 | Protestant Reformation, Age of Exploration, Scientific Revolution, Enlightenment, French/Haitian/American Revolutions, Imperialism, Industrial Revolution |
| **Era 4** — Age of Global War and Globalization | ~1900–present | WWI, Russian Revolution, WWII, Cold War, decolonization, civil rights movements, globalization, contemporary geopolitics |

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Students sometimes ask why they study history when "it's in the past." The answer is that the past is not finished: it lives in the laws we follow, the borders we live inside, the institutions we rely on, and the arguments we repeat without knowing where they came from. The WH standards aren't a list of facts to memorize — they're a toolkit for understanding how the world got to be the way it is. Every standard in this framework was designed to help students see patterns: how power gets organized, how ideas spread, how societies respond to crisis, how the language people use to justify power changes while the underlying dynamics stay the same. That kind of pattern recognition is the most useful skill there is when the world is changing fast and the information environment is designed to confuse. For the texts that make these patterns visible, see the [Reading Library](../../../reading-library/index.md) and the [Studio Archive](../../../units/studio-archive/index.md).

# Unit 4: World War II

**Course:** English II + US History (Grade 10)
**Approximate period:** 1939–1945 (U.S. involvement: 1941–1945)
**FPS Learning Goal:** Explain the causes and consequences of U.S. involvement in World War II.

---

## What This Unit Is About

WWII is the defining event of the 20th century. It produced the deadliest conflict in human history (70–85 million deaths), the Holocaust, two atomic bombings, and the international order that still governs the world today.

The United States spent the first two years of the war officially neutral while supplying Britain through Lend-Lease. Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941) ended neutrality. The U.S. fought simultaneously in the Pacific (against Japan) and in Europe and North Africa (against Nazi Germany and Italy). The war reshaped American society: it ended the Great Depression, brought women into the industrial workforce in unprecedented numbers, exposed the contradiction between fighting fascism abroad and practicing racism at home, and ended with the U.S. as the world's dominant power.

This unit covers:
- Causes of WWII: Treaty of Versailles, rise of fascism, Great Depression, appeasement
- The Holocaust: causes, implementation, Allied knowledge, Nuremberg Trials
- Pacific theater: Pearl Harbor, island-hopping, Midway, the decision to use atomic bombs
- Atlantic/European theater: D-Day (June 6, 1944), liberation of France, fall of Berlin
- Homefront: Rosie the Riveter, war bonds, rationing, Japanese American internment
- FDR's Four Freedoms speech as a primary source
- The decision to drop the atomic bomb — the most contested moral question of Unit 4

### US.6_12.2 + Unit 4 — Multiple Perspectives

**Standard:** Examine the impact of multiple perspectives on social, political, and cultural development.

**In Unit 4 — primary focus: the Atomic Bomb debate.**

**Key perspectives:**
- **Pro-bomb (U.S. government)**: the bomb ended the war faster, saving the lives of hundreds of thousands of American soldiers who would have died in a land invasion of Japan
- **Anti-bomb (critics then and now)**: Japan was already defeated; the bomb was primarily a message to the Soviet Union; dropping on civilians was a war crime
- **Japanese perspective**: Hiroshima (August 6) and Nagasaki (August 9) killed 130,000–226,000 people, most civilians; survivors (hibakusha) faced radiation sickness and social stigma
- **Japanese American internment**: 120,000 Japanese Americans, two-thirds of them U.S. citizens, incarcerated without charge or trial; Supreme Court upheld it (Korematsu v. United States, 1944); formally apologized in 1988
- **African American soldiers**: WWII accelerated the civil rights movement — the Double V campaign; Tuskegee Airmen; the irony of fighting Nazi racial ideology for a segregated country

**Learning targets:**
- I can examine multiple perspectives on the atomic bomb decision, using primary sources from multiple sides.
- I can analyze Japanese American internment from the perspective of both the government and the incarcerated families.

### US.6_12.3 + Unit 4 — Cause and Effect

**Standard:** Explain the relationship of events focusing on the link(s) between cause and effect.

**In Unit 4:** Explain the causes and consequences of U.S. involvement in WWII.

**Key cause-and-effect chains:**
- **Tripartite Pact (1940) → Axis alliance**: Germany, Italy, Japan commit to mutual defense; the alliance that draws the U.S. into a two-front war
- **Pearl Harbor attack → U.S. declaration of war**: December 7, 1941; Germany and Italy declare war on the U.S. four days later (per Tripartite Pact)
- **Allied Forces coordination → D-Day success**: the logistics of coordinating 150,000 troops from multiple nations in the largest seaborne invasion in history
- **Holocaust implementation → Nuremberg Trials → international law**: the systematic murder of Jews, Roma, disabled people, and others → war crimes tribunals → Geneva Conventions, Genocide Convention, concept of crimes against humanity
- **Atomic bomb → Japanese surrender → Cold War nuclear arms race**: the bomb ends the Pacific war but begins a new kind of global threat

**Learning targets:**
- I can trace the cause-and-effect chain from Pearl Harbor to U.S. entry into the Pacific and European theaters.
- I can explain how the Holocaust produced changes in international law.
- I can explain the cause-and-effect relationship between the atomic bomb's use and the Cold War arms race.

### US.6_12.4 + Unit 4 — Change Over Time

**Standard:** Compare how historical elements change over time.

**In Unit 4:** Compare WWII-era changes over time; analyze the significant changes produced by the war.

**Key comparisons:**
- Women in the workforce during WWII → "pushed back" after the war → feminist movement of the 1960s–70s responding to that reversal
- The Holocaust → "Never Again" promise → genocides in Rwanda, Bosnia, Darfur; how well the world kept that promise
- Atomic bomb → nuclear proliferation; the number of nuclear-armed states has grown since 1945
- WWII military-industrial complex → still the dominant structure of U.S. defense spending

### US.6_12.5 + Unit 4 — Significant Contributions of People and Policy

**Standard:** Analyze the significant contributions of people, policy, and the influence on an era.

**Key people and policies:**
| Person/Policy | Contribution |
|--------------|-------------|
| **FDR** | Led the U.S. through WWII; Four Freedoms speech; death in April 1945, just before Allied victory |
| **Dwight D. Eisenhower** | Supreme Allied Commander in Europe; D-Day planning; later president who warned of military-industrial complex |
| **Harry Truman** | Authorized atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki; Marshall Plan; Truman Doctrine |
| **Manhattan Project** | The U.S. scientific program that developed nuclear weapons; included over 100,000 people |
| **Tuskegee Airmen** | African American military aviators who flew over 1,500 missions; a direct challenge to segregation's logic |
| **Executive Order 9066** | Authorized Japanese American incarceration; one of the most significant civil liberties violations in U.S. history |
| **Lend-Lease Act (1941)** | Allowed the U.S. to supply Britain and later the Soviet Union before entering the war |

### US.6_12.6 + Unit 4 — Past to Present

**Standard:** Connect the past to the present using current events.

**Key connections:**
- United Nations (1945) → still the primary international organization; WWII-era architecture still in place
- Nuclear weapons → 13,000 still exist globally; non-proliferation challenges; Iran, North Korea
- NATO → created 1949 post-WWII; still central to European security (Russia-Ukraine war)
- German reparations to Holocaust survivors → still paid; contrast to U.S. never paying reparations for slavery
- Japanese American internment → formal apology and reparations (1988); lessons for post-9/11 civil liberties debates

## Key Vocabulary for Unit 4

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Holocaust | The systematic, state-sponsored murder of six million Jews and millions of others by the Nazi regime |
| appeasement | The policy of making concessions to aggressive powers to avoid war; associated with Chamberlain's agreement at Munich (1938) |
| D-Day | June 6, 1944: the Allied amphibious invasion of Normandy, France; the turning point of the European theater |
| Tripartite Pact | The 1940 mutual defense agreement between Germany, Italy, and Japan |
| Manhattan Project | The secret U.S. program that developed the first nuclear weapons |
| internment | Confinement of a group of people, especially in wartime; Japanese American internment was the incarceration of 120,000 Japanese Americans under Executive Order 9066 |
| hibakusha | Japanese term for atomic bomb survivors; people who experienced Hiroshima or Nagasaki |
| Nuremberg Trials | Post-WWII trials of Nazi war criminals; established the precedent for international criminal accountability |
| Double V | The African American WWII-era slogan calling for victory against fascism abroad and racism at home |
| Lend-Lease | The U.S. policy of supplying Allied nations with war materials before entering WWII |

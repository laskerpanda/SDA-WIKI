# Unit 5: Cold War

**Course:** English II + US History (Grade 10)
**Approximate period:** 1945–1991
**FPS Learning Goals:** Explain the United States' reactions toward the growth of communism. Explain the impact of rapid technological change on society. Explain the economic and/or social issues of the Post-WWII era (1945–1962) and their long-lasting effects.

---

## What This Unit Is About

The Cold War was a 45-year confrontation between the United States and the Soviet Union — the world's two nuclear-armed superpowers. It was "cold" because they never directly fought each other, but it shaped every hot war, every political crisis, and most domestic policy in both countries for half a century.

The Cold War was simultaneously:
- An ideological conflict (capitalism vs. communism; liberal democracy vs. one-party state)
- A military competition (nuclear arms race, NATO vs. Warsaw Pact, conventional forces in Europe)
- A technological race (space race, nuclear weapons delivery systems, spy satellites)
- A series of proxy conflicts (Korea, Vietnam, Angola, Nicaragua — all fought with U.S. or Soviet support)
- A domestic political phenomenon (McCarthyism, the Red Scare, civil liberties crisis)

This unit covers:
- Origins: the division of Germany, Iron Curtain, containment policy, Truman Doctrine, Marshall Plan
- NATO, Warsaw Pact, Berlin Airlift
- Korean War (1950–1953): the first Cold War "hot" proxy conflict
- McCarthyism and the domestic Red Scare (1950–1954)
- Sputnik (1957) and the space race
- Eisenhower era: massive retaliation, brinkmanship, U-2 incident
- Cuban Missile Crisis (1962) — thirteen days that almost ended the world
- Cold War culture: fallout shelters, CONELRAD, duck-and-cover drills

## All Six Standards Applied to Unit 5

### US.6_12.1 + Unit 5 — Primary and Secondary Sources

**Standard:** Analyze primary and secondary sources with attention to reliability, impact, and purpose.

**In Unit 5:** Analyze sources on U.S. reactions to communism and on the post-WWII era.

**Key sources:**
- McCarthy's 1950 speech claiming 205 Communists in the State Department
- NSC-68 (the 1950 policy document defining U.S. Cold War strategy)
- Kennan's "Long Telegram" and "X Article" on containment
- Eisenhower's Farewell Address ("beware the military-industrial complex")
- Khrushchev's "Secret Speech" (1956) denouncing Stalin
- Documents from the Cuban Missile Crisis (JFK's ExComm meetings)

**FPS Skill Strand:** OEC — Explain the United States' reactions toward the growth of communism and the impact of rapid technological change.

**Learning targets:**
- I can analyze McCarthy's speech for its rhetorical techniques and evaluate it as a historical source.
- I can explain why Eisenhower's Farewell Address warning about the military-industrial complex is still cited today.
- I can analyze the Cuban Missile Crisis through primary source documents from both the American and Soviet sides.

### US.6_12.2 + Unit 5 — Multiple Perspectives

**Standard:** Examine the impact of multiple perspectives on social, political, and cultural development.

**In Unit 5 — key document: McCarthy's speech.**

**Key perspectives:**
- **American Cold Warriors**: containment is necessary; communism must be stopped everywhere or it will spread (domino theory); nuclear deterrence is preferable to disarmament
- **Soviet perspective**: the U.S. is encircling the Soviet Union with military alliances; the Marshall Plan is economic imperialism; the West refused to open a second front in WWII, costing millions of Soviet lives
- **Post-WWII Americans**: GI Bill prosperity vs. the fear of nuclear annihilation; the contradiction of building suburbs while building bomb shelters
- **McCarthy's targets**: scientists, artists, union organizers, civil rights activists — anyone deemed "un-American"; 10,000+ careers destroyed
- **Developing world perspective**: Cold War proxy conflicts (Korea, Vietnam, Angola) fought on their soil, with their populations; superpower competition as a form of ongoing imperialism

**Learning targets:**
- I can examine multiple perspectives on U.S. Cold War policy, including from the Soviet side.
- I can analyze McCarthyism from the perspective of both its supporters and its targets.

### US.6_12.3 + Unit 5 — Cause and Effect

**Standard:** Explain the relationship of events focusing on the link(s) between cause and effect.

**In Unit 5 — FPS target:** Post-WWII U.S. foreign policy: argue about a specific Cold War decision.

**Key cause-and-effect chains:**
- **Soviet atomic bomb (1949) → NSC-68 → arms race**: the U.S. loses its nuclear monopoly; NSC-68 recommends massively increasing military spending; the arms race escalates
- **Communist victory in China (1949) + Korean War (1950) → McCarthyism intensifies**: "Who lost China?" becomes a political attack; fear of communist infiltration at home grows
- **Sputnik (1957) → NASA, NDEA → space race and science education reform**: Soviet satellite shocks American confidence; Congress creates NASA and funds science education
- **Bay of Pigs failure (1961) → Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)**: U.S.-backed invasion of Cuba fails; Khrushchev sees weakness; installs missiles in Cuba; thirteen days of nuclear standoff
- **Containment doctrine → Vietnam commitment**: the same logic that defended Korea commits the U.S. to defending South Vietnam

**Learning targets:**
- I can trace the cause-and-effect chain from Soviet atomic bomb to the arms race.
- I can explain how Sputnik changed American education and science policy.
- I can explain how the Cuban Missile Crisis was resolved and what it revealed about Cold War decision-making.

### US.6_12.4 + Unit 5 — Change Over Time

**Standard:** Compare how historical elements change over time.

**In Unit 5 — FPS targets:** Analyze significant contributions of presidents on domestic/foreign policy. Create a logical prediction on the absence of an existing historical event (counterfactual).

**Key comparisons:**
- Truman Doctrine (1947) → Reagan Doctrine (1980s) → Bush Doctrine (2002): how U.S. interventionism has changed across Cold War and post-Cold War eras
- Cold War civil defense (fallout shelters, duck-and-cover) → contemporary emergency preparedness (how fear shapes public behavior)
- McCarthy's communist witch hunt → post-9/11 surveillance and civil liberties debates

**Counterfactual question:** "If JFK had not been assassinated, would the U.S. have escalated in Vietnam? Create a logical prediction based on existing historical evidence."

### US.6_12.5 + Unit 5 — Significant Contributions of People and Policy

**Standard:** Analyze the significant contributions of people, policy, and the influence on an era.

**Key people and policies:**
| Person/Policy | Contribution |
|--------------|-------------|
| **Harry Truman** | Truman Doctrine ("support free peoples who are resisting attempted subjugation"); Marshall Plan (economic rebuilding of Western Europe) |
| **George Kennan** | Author of containment doctrine; argued the Soviet Union would eventually collapse if Western democracies held firm |
| **Dwight Eisenhower** | Massive retaliation and brinkmanship; ended Korean War; warned of military-industrial complex |
| **Joseph McCarthy** | Gave his name to McCarthyism; destroyed careers through accusation without evidence; "Have you no decency?" (Welch to McCarthy) |
| **John F. Kennedy** | Bay of Pigs; Cuban Missile Crisis management; New Frontier; assassination (1963) |
| **Containment doctrine** | The foundational U.S. strategy for the Cold War: prevent Soviet expansion without direct military conflict |
| **Marshall Plan (1948)** | $13 billion in economic aid to rebuild Western Europe; simultaneously humanitarian and strategic (rebuild allies, prevent communist political gains) |
| **Warren Court decisions** | Additional: Brown v. Board (1954); Gideon v. Wainwright; Miranda v. Arizona — Cold War era domestic constitutional developments |

### US.6_12.6 + Unit 5 — Past to Present

**Standard:** Connect the past to the present using current events.

**Key connections:**
- NATO → still the central Western military alliance; Russia-Ukraine war reactivated NATO expansion debates
- U.S.-Russia relationship → Cold War patterns still visible; nuclear arsenals still deployed
- Space race legacy → GPS, weather satellites, telecommunications satellites all trace to Cold War space investment
- Marshall Plan model → still cited in debates about foreign aid as a tool of stability
- McCarthyism → civil liberties debates about surveillance, loyalty oaths, political persecution

## Key Vocabulary for Unit 5

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| containment | The U.S. Cold War strategy of preventing Soviet expansion without seeking to destroy the Soviet Union |
| Iron Curtain | Churchill's term for the political and ideological divide between Soviet-controlled Eastern Europe and the West |
| NATO | The North Atlantic Treaty Organization (1949); collective defense agreement among Western democracies |
| Warsaw Pact | The Soviet-led military alliance of Eastern European communist states |
| domino theory | The belief that if one country fell to communism, neighboring countries would fall too |
| McCarthyism | The practice of making unfounded accusations of communist sympathy or subversion; associated with Senator Joseph McCarthy |
| Truman Doctrine | The 1947 U.S. policy committing to support free peoples resisting communism, used to justify aid to Greece and Turkey |
| Marshall Plan | The 1948 U.S. program providing $13 billion to rebuild Western European economies after WWII |
| brinkmanship | The Cold War strategy of pushing dangerous situations to the brink of war to force the opponent to back down |
| Cuban Missile Crisis | The 13-day confrontation (October 1962) between the U.S. and Soviet Union over Soviet missiles in Cuba; the closest the world came to nuclear war |
| proxy war | A conflict in which two powers support opposing sides without fighting each other directly |
| military-industrial complex | Eisenhower's term for the relationship between the military and the defense industry; his warning that it could distort government priorities |

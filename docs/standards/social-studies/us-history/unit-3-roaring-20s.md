# Unit 3: Roaring 20s and the Great Depression

**Course:** English II + US History (Grade 10)
**Approximate period:** 1920–1941
**FPS Learning Goals:** Explain the political, social, and economic changes of the 1920s and 1930s.

---

## What This Unit Is About

The 1920s felt, to many Americans, like the country had finally arrived. The war was over, the economy was booming, consumer culture exploded, and new forms of music, fashion, and social freedom challenged Victorian norms. It was also a decade of profound reaction: the Red Scare, the revival of the KKK, Prohibition, anti-immigrant legislation, and racial violence made the decade far more complicated than "roaring."

Then the crash came. The Great Depression (1929–1941) was the worst economic catastrophe in American history. One in four workers was unemployed at its peak. Farmers watched their land blow away in the Dust Bowl. President Hoover believed government should not intervene in the economy — so FDR was elected on a platform promising that it would.

This unit covers:
- The Red Scare (1919–1920): fear of communism and anarchism
- Consumer culture of the 1920s: cars, radios, advertising, credit
- Cultural shifts: Flappers, Jazz Age, Harlem Renaissance, Prohibition
- The KKK revival and racist violence
- Stock market crash (1929) and its causes
- The Great Depression: unemployment, Dust Bowl, Hoovervilles
- FDR's New Deal: relief, recovery, reform
- Political cartoons as a primary source type in this era

### US.6_12.2 + Unit 3 — Multiple Perspectives

**Standard:** Examine the impact of multiple perspectives on social, political, and cultural development.

**In Unit 3:** Examine how 1920s cultural changes (jazz, flappers, consumer culture) and the Depression looked from different perspectives.

**Key perspectives:**
- **The Roaring 20s' winners**: middle-class consumers with access to credit, cars, radios; the cultural elite of the Harlem Renaissance
- **The 1920s' losers**: Black Americans facing continued racial violence; farmers already in depression before 1929; immigrants facing restriction (Immigration Act of 1924 dramatically reduced immigration)
- **Harlem Renaissance perspective**: Black intellectual and artistic flourishing in the 1920s — Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Duke Ellington; a counter-narrative to the dominant white cultural story
- **Prohibition's contested reception**: celebrated by temperance advocates and religious conservatives; resisted by immigrant communities, workers, and anyone who liked alcohol; created organized crime
- **Depression-era perspectives**: unemployed workers vs. Hoover's "pull yourself up by your bootstraps"; FDR's coalition including Southern Democrats (who supported the New Deal but maintained Jim Crow)

**Learning targets:**
- I can explain how the 1920s looked different for white middle-class Americans vs. Black Americans vs. recent immigrants.
- I can analyze the Harlem Renaissance as a specific cultural and intellectual response to racial oppression.

### US.6_12.3 + Unit 3 — Cause and Effect

**Standard:** Explain the relationship of events focusing on the link(s) between cause and effect.

**In Unit 3 — FPS target:** Explain the political, social, and economic events of the 1930s. Produce a 1930s argumentation summative.

**Key cause-and-effect chains:**
- **Overproduction + speculation + easy credit → stock market crash (1929)**: the economy of the 1920s was built on credit and speculation; the crash revealed how fragile the foundation was
- **Stock market crash → bank failures → unemployment → Great Depression**: the financial panic triggered a cascade of bank runs, business closures, and mass unemployment
- **Drought + agricultural overproduction + Great Plains soil depletion → Dust Bowl**: the "3 B's" (Black Sunday, Black Blizzard, Black Roller) driving 3.5 million people off the land
- **Great Depression + Hoover's inaction → FDR's New Deal election**: the political consequence of economic disaster
- **New Deal → modern American welfare state**: Social Security (1935), FDIC, SEC, minimum wage — institutions still governing American economic life

**Learning targets:**
- I can explain the causes of the Great Depression using multiple connected cause-and-effect chains.
- I can analyze how the New Deal changed the relationship between the federal government and the economy.
- I can produce a written argument about a specific 1930s event or policy.

### US.6_12.4 + Unit 3 — Change Over Time

**Standard:** Compare how historical elements change over time.

**In Unit 3 — FPS target:** Apply historical knowledge as a tool for examining current events: Great Depression, current economic policy, voting rights, government aid programs (stimulus), unionism, imperialism, U.S. involvement in Americas.

**Key comparisons:**
- 1929 crash → 2008 financial crisis: the same patterns (speculation, deregulation, cascading financial failure); same debates (should government bail out banks and businesses?)
- Dust Bowl → climate change migration: environmental crisis driving population movement
- New Deal government programs → current debates about the size of government (stimulus checks, unemployment benefits)
- 1930s labor movement → current labor organizing (Amazon, Starbucks)
- Prohibition → current drug policy debates

### US.6_12.5 + Unit 3 — Significant Contributions of People and Policy

**Standard:** Analyze the significant contributions of people, policy, and the influence on an era.

**In Unit 3 — FPS target:** Design, develop, and create a primary or secondary source rooted in Unit 3; evaluate its reliability, impact, and purpose.

**Key people and policies:**
| Person/Policy | Contribution |
|--------------|-------------|
| **FDR (Franklin Delano Roosevelt)** | New Deal architect; 3 R's: Relief, Recovery, Reform; transformed the presidency and federal government's role |
| **Herbert Hoover** | Believed in "rugged individualism"; opposed direct federal relief; blamed for the Depression deepening; Hoovervilles named after him |
| **Eleanor Roosevelt** | Expanded the role of First Lady; advocate for New Deal; civil rights ally; her own column and political network |
| **Dorothea Lange** | FSA photographer; "Migrant Mother" became the iconic image of the Depression |
| **Social Security Act (1935)** | Retirement insurance for workers; still the foundation of American retirement security |
| **New Deal (1933–1939)** | Complex of relief, job creation, financial regulation, and agricultural programs; restructured the relationship between government and economy |

### US.6_12.6 + Unit 3 — Past to Present

**Standard:** Connect the past to the present using current events.

**Key connections:**
- Social Security and FDIC → still functioning institutions
- New Deal coalition → Democratic Party politics for 40 years
- Labor movement gains → minimum wage, 40-hour week, workplace safety

## Key Vocabulary for Unit 3

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Roaring 20s | The 1920s decade of economic prosperity, consumer culture, and cultural change |
| Red Scare | The 1919–1920 fear of communist revolution in the U.S., following the Russian Revolution |
| Prohibition | The constitutional ban on alcohol production and sale (18th Amendment, 1920–1933) |
| Harlem Renaissance | The African American cultural, artistic, and intellectual movement centered in Harlem, NYC, in the 1920s |
| stock market crash | The collapse of stock prices in October 1929, triggering the Great Depression |
| Great Depression | The severe economic crisis of the 1930s; 25% unemployment at its peak |
| Dust Bowl | The ecological disaster in the Great Plains (~1930–1936) caused by drought and unsustainable farming |
| New Deal | FDR's program of relief, recovery, and reform programs designed to respond to the Great Depression |
| rugged individualism | Hoover's belief that self-reliance and private charity, not government, should address economic hardship |
| Social Security | The 1935 federal program providing retirement income for American workers |

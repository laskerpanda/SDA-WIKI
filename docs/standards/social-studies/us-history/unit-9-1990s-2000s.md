# Unit 9: 1990s and Early 2000s

**Course:** English II + US History (Grade 10)
**Approximate period:** 1991–2010
**FPS Learning Goal:** Connect the changing political and social climate to the United States' involvement as a global superpower.

---

## What This Unit Is About

The Cold War ended in 1991. For a moment, it seemed like the world would become simpler. It didn't.

The 1990s brought the internet, the longest economic expansion in U.S. history, and the sense that America had "won" the 20th century. But it also brought: the OJ Simpson trial as a national racial Rorschach test, the LA Riots, the Oklahoma City bombing, ethnic cleansing in Bosnia and Rwanda that the U.S. watched without intervening, and Bill Clinton's impeachment over a personal scandal.

Then September 11, 2001, changed everything. Two wars (Afghanistan and Iraq) that the U.S. is still processing. The PATRIOT Act and civil liberties debates that echo McCarthy-era fears. And the 2008 financial crisis, which revealed how fragile the global economic system built on deregulation actually was.

This unit covers:
- Cold War end and its aftermath: Desert Storm (1990–91), the "peace dividend"
- 1990s economy: technology boom, NAFTA, rising inequality despite prosperity
- Social and political events: OJ Simpson trial, LA Riots (1992), Clinton impeachment
- Rise of the internet and social media
- 2000 election and its contested resolution (Bush v. Gore)
- September 11, 2001: the attacks, immediate response, context
- War on Terror: Afghanistan and Iraq invasions, WMDs that weren't there
- PATRIOT Act and civil liberties
- Great Recession (2008): causes, consequences, bailouts

## All Six Standards Applied to Unit 9

### US.6_12.1 + Unit 9 — Primary and Secondary Sources

**Standard:** Analyze primary and secondary sources with attention to reliability, impact, and purpose.

**In Unit 9:** Analyze sources on the U.S. as global superpower and changing political/social climate.

**Key sources:**
- NAFTA speeches: Clinton (pro) vs. Pat Buchanan (anti) — two different visions of globalization
- George W. Bush's "Mission Accomplished" speech (May 2003) — a primary source for analyzing political messaging and accountability
- Osama bin Laden's post-9/11 communications — understanding the other side's perspective
- Congressional Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF, 2001) — the legal basis for two decades of war
- Intelligence community assessment of WMDs in Iraq (2002) — what did the government know, what did it claim to know?
- 2008 financial crisis: congressional testimony from bank executives; TARP documents

**FPS Skill Strand:** OEC — Connect the changing political and social climate to the United States' involvement as a global superpower.

**Learning targets:**
- I can compare Clinton's and Buchanan's arguments about NAFTA and identify the evidence and reasoning each uses.
- I can analyze the Bush administration's WMD claims using the intelligence documents that have since been released.
- I can evaluate Bush's "Mission Accomplished" speech for what it claimed and what actually happened.

### US.6_12.2 + Unit 9 — Multiple Perspectives

**Standard:** Examine the impact of multiple perspectives on social, political, and cultural development.

**In Unit 9:** Examine multiple perspectives on U.S. global superpower status and the events of the 1990s–2000s.

**Key perspectives:**
- **The "unipolar moment" (American triumphalism)**: the U.S. won the Cold War; democracy and capitalism should spread globally; American military power should enforce this
- **International perspectives on U.S. power**: many nations saw U.S. power as threatening rather than liberating; the Iraq invasion without UN Security Council approval alienated allies
- **Muslim world perspectives on 9/11 context**: U.S. foreign policy (support for authoritarian governments, troops in Saudi Arabia, Israeli-Palestinian conflict) as the context for anti-American radicalization — distinct from endorsing terrorism
- **OJ Simpson trial and race**: Black Americans disproportionately believed Simpson was innocent (given history of police evidence planting); white Americans disproportionately believed he was guilty; the same evidence, two different readings based on lived experience
- **The 2000 election**: 537 votes in Florida; Supreme Court decision (Bush v. Gore); questions about democratic legitimacy that predate contemporary debates

**Learning targets:**
- I can examine the 9/11 attacks from multiple perspectives, including context that explains (not justifies) the attacks.
- I can analyze the OJ Simpson trial verdict through the lens of different racial communities' relationships with the justice system.

### US.6_12.3 + Unit 9 — Cause and Effect

**Standard:** Explain the relationship of events focusing on the link(s) between cause and effect.

**In Unit 9 — FPS target:** Connect the changing political and social climate to the United States' involvement as a global superpower.

**Key cause-and-effect chains:**
- **Cold War end → "peace dividend" debate → continuation of military spending**: after the Soviet Union collapsed, the question was whether the U.S. would reduce military spending; instead, new threats were found
- **Desert Storm (1990–91) → U.S. troops in Saudi Arabia → al-Qaeda's stated grievances → 9/11**: this is not a justification of the attacks; it is the causal chain that explains how they occurred
- **9/11 attacks → PATRIOT Act → NSA surveillance → Snowden revelations (2013)**: the civil liberties trade-offs made in the name of security
- **2001 AUMF → Afghanistan and Iraq wars → ongoing U.S. military involvement**: the authorization that has never been repealed still legally justifies military action in 2026
- **Deregulation of financial industry (1990s–2000s) → housing bubble → 2008 crash → Great Recession**: the same logic that produced the 1929 crash produced the 2008 crash; what's different and what's the same?

**Learning targets:**
- I can trace the causal chain from the Cold War's end to September 11.
- I can explain how the decision to invade Iraq without WMDs being found changed the credibility of U.S. intelligence claims.
- I can trace the cause-and-effect chain from financial deregulation to the 2008 crash.

### US.6_12.4 + Unit 9 — Change Over Time

**Standard:** Compare how historical elements change over time.

**In Unit 9:** Compare historical changes over time in the modern era.

**Key comparisons:**
- The internet in 1995 → the internet in 2026: the same technology with completely different political and social implications
- 1990s globalization enthusiasm (NAFTA) → 2016 anti-globalization politics (Trump, Brexit): what changed?
- 9/11 civil liberties erosion → ongoing debates about surveillance, due process, habeas corpus
- 2008 financial crisis → COVID-19 economic crisis → response comparison
- Clinton impeachment (1998, perjury about an affair) → Trump impeachments (2019, 2021, different charges): how does the threshold for impeachment function?

### US.6_12.5 + Unit 9 — Significant Contributions of People and Policy

**Standard:** Analyze the significant contributions of people, policy, and the influence on an era.

**In Unit 9 — FPS target:** Project identifying a person who changed history during this era and a turning point in U.S. history during this era.

**Key people and policies:**
| Person/Policy | Contribution |
|--------------|-------------|
| **George H.W. Bush** | Desert Storm coalition; NAFTA negotiation; handling of Cold War end; lost 1992 election to Clinton partly due to recession |
| **Bill Clinton** | Longest peacetime economic expansion; NAFTA; welfare reform; "Don't Ask Don't Tell"; impeachment over Lewinsky affair |
| **George W. Bush** | 9/11 response; Afghanistan invasion; Iraq invasion based on false WMD intelligence; PATRIOT Act; tax cuts; 2008 financial crisis under his watch |
| **NAFTA (1994)** | North American Free Trade Agreement; eliminated tariffs between U.S., Canada, Mexico; boosted trade, eliminated manufacturing jobs in the U.S. |
| **PATRIOT Act (2001)** | Expanded government surveillance powers; weakened Fourth Amendment protections; still largely in force |
| **2008 TARP bailout** | Troubled Asset Relief Program: $700 billion to rescue financial institutions; prevented collapse but created political backlash about "too big to fail" |

### US.6_12.6 + Unit 9 — Past to Present

**Standard:** Connect the past to the present using current events.

**Key connections:**
- 9/11 → still shaping U.S. foreign policy, civil liberties debates, and Middle East policy in 2026
- 2008 financial crisis → rising populism (Occupy Wall Street, Tea Party), anti-establishment politics, Bernie Sanders, Trump
- NAFTA → USMCA (2020 revision); ongoing globalization and manufacturing debates
- Rise of social media → misinformation, election interference, polarization in 2024 and beyond
- War on Terror → end of Afghanistan war (2021); ongoing questions about lessons learned
- Obama presidency (2009–) as the beginning of this era's continuation → racial politics, healthcare (ACA)

## Key Vocabulary for Unit 9

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Desert Storm | The 1990–91 U.S.-led coalition war to expel Iraq from Kuwait; the first major post-Cold War military operation |
| NAFTA | North American Free Trade Agreement (1994); eliminated tariffs among the U.S., Canada, and Mexico; controversial for its effects on American manufacturing workers |
| unipolar moment | The brief post-Cold War period when the United States was the world's sole superpower with no rival |
| September 11 | The September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks by al-Qaeda on the World Trade Center and Pentagon; 2,977 killed; transformed U.S. foreign and domestic policy |
| War on Terror | The U.S.-led military and political campaign in response to 9/11; included invasions of Afghanistan (2001) and Iraq (2003) |
| PATRIOT Act | The 2001 law expanding government surveillance powers; weakened Fourth Amendment protections in the name of national security |
| WMD | Weapons of Mass Destruction; the stated primary justification for the 2003 Iraq invasion; no such weapons were found |
| Great Recession | The severe economic downturn (2007–2009) triggered by the housing market collapse and financial system crisis |
| TARP | Troubled Asset Relief Program; $700 billion government program that purchased toxic assets from banks to prevent financial system collapse |
| subprime mortgage | A mortgage loan issued to borrowers with poor credit; the proliferation of subprime mortgages and their bundling into financial products was a primary cause of the 2008 crash |

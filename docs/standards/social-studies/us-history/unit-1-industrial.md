# Unit 1: Industrial Revolution and the Progressive Era

**Course:** English II + US History (Grade 10)
**Approximate period:** 1870s–1920
**FPS Learning Goal:** Explain the causes and consequences of the Industrial Revolution on American society.

---

## What This Unit Is About

In the decades after the Civil War, the United States transformed from a mostly agricultural nation into the world's leading industrial economy. That transformation created enormous wealth — and enormous inequality. The progressive movement was the political response to that inequality: the first major organized effort in American history to use government power to regulate capitalism.

This unit covers:
- The rise of industrial capitalism: railroads, steel, oil, and the men who controlled them
- Immigration and the ethnic transformation of American cities (Ellis Island, Angel Island, nativism)
- The Homestead Act and its effects on Native American displacement
- Labor conditions: the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire, child labor, the 14-hour workday
- The Progressive movement: muckrakers, settlement houses, political reform
- Key figures: Carnegie, Rockefeller, J.P. Morgan, Teddy Roosevelt, Jane Addams, Upton Sinclair
- Gilded Age political cartoons as a primary source form

## All Six Standards Applied to Unit 1

### US.6_12.1 + Unit 1 — Primary and Secondary Sources { #us6-121 }

**Standard:** Analyze primary and secondary sources with attention to reliability, impact, and purpose.

**In Unit 1:** Analyze primary sources about the Industrial Revolution — factory testimony, political cartoons, newspaper reporting, government documents — with attention to who created them and why.

**Key sources:**
- Gilded Age political cartoons (Thomas Nast on robber barons, Tammany Hall corruption)
- Congressional testimony from factory workers and child laborers
- Upton Sinclair's *The Jungle* (1906) — muckraker journalism as a primary source type
- Teddy Roosevelt's speeches on trust-busting
- Company reports vs. worker testimony on the same factory

**FPS Skill Strand:** OEC — Explain the causes and consequences of the Industrial Revolution on American society.

**Learning targets:**
- I can analyze a Gilded Age political cartoon for its purpose, bias, and argument.
- I can compare a factory owner's account of working conditions to a worker's account and explain what each source emphasizes or omits.
- I can evaluate the reliability of a muckraker article as a primary source.

**Inquiry angles:**
- "How do political cartoons make an argument — and are they reliable historical sources?"
- "How did Upton Sinclair's *The Jungle* change U.S. food safety law? What does that tell us about the relationship between journalism and policy?"

### US.6_12.2 + Unit 1 — Multiple Perspectives { #us6-122 }

**Standard:** Examine the impact of multiple perspectives on social, political, and cultural development.

**In Unit 1:** Examine how different groups experienced the Industrial Revolution — factory owners vs. workers, immigrants vs. native-born Americans, progressives vs. laissez-faire conservatives.

**Key perspectives:**
- **Industrialists' view**: free enterprise, innovation, job creation; Carnegie's "Gospel of Wealth" — the wealthy have a duty to give back
- **Workers' view**: exploitation, dangerous conditions, no safety net; labor organizing as self-defense
- **Immigrant experience**: the promise of America vs. the reality of tenement housing and industrial labor; nativism and anti-immigrant violence
- **Native American perspective**: the Homestead Act opened land taken through displacement; the Dawes Act (1887) attacking tribal identity and land ownership
- **Progressive reformers**: government has a responsibility to regulate capitalism for public good; Jane Addams, Ida Tarbell, TR

**Learning targets:**
- I can explain how the Industrial Revolution looked different depending on a person's class, ethnicity, and gender.
- I can analyze how nativism shaped immigration policy and immigrant experience.

### US.6_12.3 + Unit 1 — Cause and Effect { #us6-123 }

**Standard:** Explain the relationship of events focusing on the link(s) between cause and effect.

**In Unit 1 — the core FPS learning target:** Explain the causes and consequences of the Industrial Revolution on American society.

**Key cause-and-effect chains:**
- **Transcontinental Railroad (1869) → national market economy**: the railroad connected previously isolated regional economies; created demand for steel, coal, and lumber; enabled mass distribution of manufactured goods
- **Industrialization → immigration**: labor demand attracted 20+ million immigrants between 1880–1920; most settled in industrial cities
- **Industrial labor conditions → Progressive movement**: the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire (1911, 146 dead, factory doors locked) → labor safety laws; Sinclair's *The Jungle* → FDA (1906); child labor photos → child labor laws
- **Monopolies and trusts → antitrust law**: Standard Oil, Carnegie Steel, railroad trusts → Sherman Antitrust Act (1890); TR's trust-busting
- **Homestead Act (1862) → Native displacement**: 160-acre land grants encouraged westward settlement on land taken from Native nations; Wounded Knee (1890) as the end of armed Native resistance

**Learning targets:**
- I can trace a cause-and-effect chain from an Industrial Revolution development to a political or social consequence.
- I can explain how the Triangle Factory Fire changed labor law.
- I can explain how the Homestead Act's "opportunity" was built on Native American dispossession.

### US.6_12.4 + Unit 1 — Change Over Time { #us6-124 }

**Standard:** Compare how historical elements change over time.

**In Unit 1:** Apply historical knowledge as a tool for examining current events — how do patterns from the Industrial Era (economic inequality, immigration debates, labor conditions) compare to the present?

**Key comparisons:**
- Gilded Age wealth concentration → 21st-century wealth inequality (top 1% in the 1880s vs. today)
- Anti-immigrant nativism of the 1890s → current immigration debates
- Child labor in 1900 American factories → child labor in supply chains of companies selling to American consumers today
- Early labor organizing (AFL, Knights of Labor) → current labor movements (Amazon unionization)
- 1890s Populist political movement → contemporary populism (left and right)

**Learning targets:**
- I can identify a specific pattern from Unit 1 and explain whether and how it is visible in the present.
- I can compare the Progressive Era's response to inequality to contemporary responses.

### US.6_12.5 + Unit 1 — Significant Contributions of People and Policy { #us6-125 }

**Standard:** Analyze the significant contributions of people, policy, and the influence on an era.

**Key people and policies:**
| Person/Policy | Contribution |
|--------------|-------------|
| **Andrew Carnegie** | Dominated steel production; "Gospel of Wealth" — philanthropy as justification for monopoly |
| **John D. Rockefeller** | Standard Oil trust — controlled 90% of U.S. oil refining; model for monopoly capitalism |
| **Teddy Roosevelt** | Trust-busting; Pure Food and Drug Act; conservation; presidency as bully pulpit for reform |
| **Jane Addams** | Hull House settlement; social work profession; women's suffrage; Nobel Peace Prize |
| **Ida Tarbell** | Investigative journalism exposing Standard Oil; model for muckraker journalism |
| **Upton Sinclair** | *The Jungle* — exposing meatpacking industry; directly caused the FDA |
| **Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)** | First federal attempt to break up monopolies |
| **James J. Hill** | Great Northern Railroad — railroad baron; complicated figure in Upper Midwest history (Fargo-connected) |

**FPS Skill Strand:** Argumentation — Formulate an argument identifying a key figure or policy that had the most significant impact.

**Learning target:** I can argue which person or policy in the Industrial/Progressive era had the greatest impact — positive or negative — using specific evidence.

## Key Vocabulary for Unit 1

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Industrial Revolution | The shift from agricultural/artisan production to factory manufacturing, beginning in Britain and accelerating in the U.S. after the Civil War |
| monopoly | Control of a market or industry by a single company or group |
| trust | A combination of businesses that reduces competition (Standard Oil Trust) |
| robber baron | A pejorative term for 19th-century industrial tycoons who used aggressive and sometimes illegal tactics to build their fortunes |
| muckraker | An investigative journalist who exposed corruption, dangerous conditions, or corporate abuses during the Progressive Era |
| Progressive Era | The period of American reform (~1890–1920) characterized by efforts to regulate corporate power, improve labor conditions, and expand democracy |
| nativism | A preference for native-born citizens over immigrants, often expressed as political opposition to immigration |
| Homestead Act | The 1862 law that gave 160-acre land grants to settlers — distributed land taken from Native nations |
| settlement house | A community center in poor urban neighborhoods (Jane Addams's Hull House) offering services to immigrants and workers |
| antitrust | Laws or policies intended to prevent monopolies and promote market competition |

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The Gilded Age described in Unit 1 — massive wealth concentration, industrial monopolies, dangerous working conditions, child labor — is not ancient history. The debates about antitrust law, labor organizing rights, and the regulatory state that emerged from the Progressive Era are being argued again today: tech monopolies, gig economy workers without benefits, minimum wage fights, and union organizing at Amazon and Starbucks. *The Jungle*'s exposé of meatpacking conditions directly produced the FDA — and debates about food safety regulation, OSHA enforcement, and corporate liability continue. The income inequality ratio between the wealthiest and everyone else in the 2020s is comparable to Gilded Age levels, which is why the history of how the Progressive Era responded — and what it didn't fix — matters right now.

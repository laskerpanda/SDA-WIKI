# Unit 7: 1960s, Vietnam War, and Counterculture

**Course:** English II + US History (Grade 10)
**Approximate period:** 1960–1975
**FPS Learning Goal:** Identify and explain how domestic issues changed the United States' perceptions of government.

---

## What This Unit Is About

The 1960s began with idealism — the New Frontier, the space race, the civil rights movement gaining momentum — and ended with assassinations, riots, and a profound crisis of confidence in American institutions.

Vietnam was the event that crystallized that crisis. The United States spent 10 years fighting a war in Southeast Asia that it could not win, that it misled the American public about, and that killed 58,000 Americans and an estimated 3.5 million Vietnamese. The anti-war movement, the counterculture, and the revelations of the Pentagon Papers shattered the assumption that the government told the truth.

This unit covers:
- JFK's presidency: New Frontier, Bay of Pigs, Cuban Missile Crisis, civil rights, assassination
- The Great Society: LBJ's domestic programs (Medicare, Medicaid, Voting Rights Act)
- Vietnam: origins, escalation, Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964), anti-war movement
- The military draft and its inequities
- Kent State massacre (1970): National Guard kills four student protesters
- Pentagon Papers (1971): Daniel Ellsberg leaks evidence that the government systematically lied about Vietnam
- The assassinations: JFK (1963), MLK (April 1968), RFK (June 1968)
- Counterculture: Woodstock, hippies, protest music, second-wave feminism
- Anti-war songs as primary sources: "Eve of Destruction" (Barry McGuire), "Okie from Muskogee" (Merle Haggard) — the debate AS the cultural artifact

## All Six Standards Applied to Unit 7

### US.6_12.1 + Unit 7 — Primary and Secondary Sources

**Standard:** Analyze primary and secondary sources with attention to reliability, impact, and purpose.

**In Unit 7:** Analyze sources on how domestic issues changed perceptions of government.

**Key sources:**
- Pentagon Papers (declassified 1971) — government documents proving systematic deception about Vietnam
- 1960s political assassinations: Oswald-Ruby-Conspiracy debates as a source analysis problem
- Anti-war music: "Eve of Destruction" (1965) vs. "Okie from Muskogee" (1969) — competing visions of America in song
- Kent State photographs and news coverage
- Walter Cronkite's 1968 Vietnam editorial ("We are mired in stalemate") — when a trusted TV anchor said this, LBJ reportedly said "If I've lost Cronkite, I've lost Middle America"
- Gulf of Tonkin Resolution documents vs. later revelations about what actually happened

**FPS Skill Strand:** OEC — Identify and explain how domestic issues changed the United States' perceptions of government.

**Learning targets:**
- I can analyze an anti-war song as a primary source for its argument, audience, and historical context.
- I can explain what the Pentagon Papers revealed and why their publication was significant.
- I can compare Gulf of Tonkin resolution documents to later declassified materials and explain what was concealed.

### US.6_12.2 + Unit 7 — Multiple Perspectives

**Standard:** Examine the impact of multiple perspectives on social, political, and cultural development.

**In Unit 7:** Examine multiple perspectives on the Vietnam War, the anti-war movement, and the counterculture.

**Key perspectives:**
- **Pro-war perspective**: domino theory; containment; Cold War commitment; the military's belief that more resources and less political interference would have produced victory; veterans who felt betrayed by anti-war protesters
- **Anti-war perspective**: the war was unjust, unwinnable, and based on lies; the draft was disproportionately falling on poor and Black men; the Vietnamese had a right to self-determination
- **Vietnamese perspective**: for the North Vietnamese and Viet Cong, this was a war of independence against foreign intervention (first French, then American); the "communist invasion" narrative doesn't account for how many South Vietnamese supported reunification
- **Military families**: people with loved ones deployed in Vietnam; "support the troops" as distinct from support for the war
- **Merle Haggard's "Okie from Muskogee"**: written from the perspective of rural, conservative America that viewed the counterculture with contempt; a genuine counter-perspective, not a parody

**Learning targets:**
- I can analyze anti-war and pro-war perspectives on Vietnam using primary sources from both sides.
- I can explain the counterculture's values and the backlash against them.
- I can analyze "Eve of Destruction" and "Okie from Muskogee" as competing primary sources representing different American perspectives in the late 1960s.

### US.6_12.3 + Unit 7 — Cause and Effect

**Standard:** Explain the relationship of events focusing on the link(s) between cause and effect.

**In Unit 7 — FPS target:** Domestic response to Vietnam argumentation.

**Key cause-and-effect chains:**
- **Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964) → massive escalation**: Congress authorizes the president to use force without a formal declaration of war; U.S. troop levels go from ~16,000 advisors (1963) to 543,000 combat troops (1969)
- **Tet Offensive (January 1968) → shift in American public opinion**: massive coordinated Viet Cong attack on 100+ South Vietnamese cities; a military failure for North Vietnam but a psychological victory; showed the American public that the government's optimistic briefings were lies
- **MLK assassination (April 4, 1968) → riots in 100+ American cities**: the death of the advocate for nonviolent change triggered the violence he had argued against
- **Kent State (May 4, 1970) → massive student strikes**: National Guard kills four students at Kent State University, Ohio; 4 million students go on strike; 900 colleges close
- **Pentagon Papers (1971) → Nixon's "Plumbers" → Watergate**: Ellsberg's leak leads Nixon to create an illegal covert operations unit that eventually breaks into the Democratic National Committee

**Learning targets:**
- I can trace the cause-and-effect chain from the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution to the domestic anti-war movement.
- I can explain how specific events (Tet Offensive, Kent State) changed American public opinion about Vietnam.
- I can produce a written argument about how domestic issues changed Americans' perceptions of government.

### US.6_12.4 + Unit 7 — Change Over Time

**Standard:** Compare how historical elements change over time.

**In Unit 7 — FPS target:** Judge the multiple perspectives that emerged regarding social, political, and cultural developments in the era 1941–2001.

**Key comparisons:**
- Vietnam War support and opposition → Iraq War support and opposition (2003); "weapons of mass destruction" as the 21st-century Gulf of Tonkin
- Draft protest → All-volunteer military (post-Vietnam); who serves and who doesn't
- Pentagon Papers → subsequent government deception exposed (Iran-Contra, WMD intelligence)
- Counterculture's influence: civil rights, feminism, environmentalism, LGBTQ rights — all connected to 1960s social movements
- Public trust in government: pre-Vietnam high trust → post-Vietnam/Watergate collapse → still not recovered

### US.6_12.5 + Unit 7 — Significant Contributions of People and Policy

**Standard:** Analyze the significant contributions of people, policy, and the influence on an era.

**Key people and policies:**
| Person/Policy | Contribution |
|--------------|-------------|
| **LBJ (Lyndon B. Johnson)** | Great Society (Medicare, Medicaid, Voting Rights Act); escalated Vietnam; decided not to run in 1968 because of Vietnam |
| **JFK (John F. Kennedy)** | New Frontier; Cuban Missile Crisis; civil rights; assassination created the myth of "Camelot" that shaped his legacy |
| **Daniel Ellsberg** | Pentagon Papers leak; proved the government had systematically deceived the public about Vietnam |
| **Ho Chi Minh** | North Vietnamese leader; framed the war as anti-colonial independence struggle; fought the French before the Americans |
| **Robert McNamara** | Secretary of Defense who prosecuted the Vietnam War and later admitted it was a mistake |
| **Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964)** | The congressional authorization that enabled massive Vietnam escalation; based on an exaggerated or fabricated incident |

### US.6_12.6 + Unit 7 — Past to Present

**Standard:** Connect the past to the present using current events.

**Key connections:**
- Government credibility crisis from Vietnam/Watergate → still not recovered; baseline public distrust of institutions
- Anti-war music as protest art → hip-hop, punk, contemporary political music
- Vietnam veterans' treatment → PTSD recognition, veterans' services, the politics of "supporting the troops"
- All-volunteer military → who serves, economic draft, military-civilian gap
- "Never another Vietnam": the lesson politicians claimed to draw → used to justify both military restraint AND military action in subsequent conflicts

## Key Vocabulary for Unit 7

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| domino theory | The Cold War belief that if one country fell to communism, neighboring countries would follow |
| Gulf of Tonkin Resolution | The 1964 congressional authorization allowing the president to use military force in Southeast Asia; the legal basis for massive Vietnam escalation |
| draft | Compulsory military service; the Vietnam-era draft fell disproportionately on working-class and Black men |
| Viet Cong | The South Vietnamese communist guerrilla force allied with North Vietnam |
| Tet Offensive | The January 1968 coordinated North Vietnamese/Viet Cong attack on 100+ South Vietnamese targets; psychologically decisive in turning American public opinion |
| counterculture | The 1960s youth culture that rejected mainstream American values; associated with anti-war activism, folk and rock music, civil rights, and social experimentation |
| Pentagon Papers | The classified Defense Department study of U.S. involvement in Vietnam, leaked by Daniel Ellsberg in 1971; revealed systematic government deception |
| Kent State | May 4, 1970: Ohio National Guard killed four student anti-war protesters at Kent State University; turned millions more Americans against the war |
| New Frontier | JFK's domestic program; framed as a challenge to Americans to achieve greatness through public service and innovation |
| Great Society | LBJ's ambitious domestic reform program: Medicare, Medicaid, Voting Rights Act, Elementary and Secondary Education Act, immigration reform |

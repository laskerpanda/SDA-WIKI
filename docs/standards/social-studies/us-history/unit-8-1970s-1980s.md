# Unit 8: 1970s and 1980s

**Course:** English II + US History (Grade 10)
**Approximate period:** 1970–1991
**FPS Learning Goal:** Explain the economic and/or social issues of the post-Cold War era and their long-lasting effects.

---

## What This Unit Is About

The 1970s began with Watergate and ended with an energy crisis, stagflation, and a hostage crisis in Iran. It was a decade of national humiliation and declining confidence. The 1980s were, for many Americans, a decade of recovery and renewal — but the Reagan Revolution also produced growing inequality, the AIDS crisis, the Iran-Contra scandal, and the beginning of the end of the Cold War.

This unit covers:
- Watergate scandal (1972–1974): the break-in, the cover-up, Nixon's resignation
- Nixon's other legacy: EPA, détente, opening China
- Environmental movement: first Earth Day (1970), Clean Air Act, MADD (Mothers Against Drunk Driving)
- Gerald Ford and Jimmy Carter: economic malaise, energy crisis, Iran hostage crisis
- Reagan and Reaganomics: supply-side economics, deregulation, union-busting (PATCO)
- Nicaragua and the Iran-Contra affair: another government lie, another scandal
- AIDS crisis and the government's slow response
- Challenger disaster (1986)
- Cold War endgame: Reagan's military buildup, Gorbachev, fall of the Berlin Wall (1989)
- Pop culture: music, video games, MTV — culture as a primary source

## All Six Standards Applied to Unit 8

### US.6_12.1 + Unit 8 — Primary and Secondary Sources

**Standard:** Analyze primary and secondary sources with attention to reliability, impact, and purpose.

**In Unit 8:** Analyze sources on post-Cold War era social and economic issues.

**Key sources:**
- Nixon's resignation speech (August 8, 1974) — a primary source for rhetorical analysis; does he accept responsibility?
- Republican Party National Platform Plank on the Equal Rights Amendment — the party's shift on women's rights as a primary source
- Oliver North Email to National Security Advisor John Poindexter (August 23, 1986) — Iran-Contra documents revealing covert operations
- Nixon's 1972 Environmental Program message to Congress — Nixon as environmental president
- Jimmy Carter's "Crisis of Confidence" speech (July 15, 1979) — Carter tells America it has a malaise; the political consequences
- Reagan's First Inaugural Address

**FPS Skill Strand:** OEC + Argumentation — Explain the economic and/or social issues of the post-Cold War era. Develop an argument about a 1941–2001 topic and how historical interpretation has changed over time.

**Learning targets:**
- I can analyze Nixon's resignation speech for what he admits and what he denies — and evaluate it as a primary source.
- I can analyze Oliver North's emails for what they reveal about how covert government operations work around democratic oversight.
- I can compare Carter's "Crisis of Confidence" to Reagan's rhetoric about American renewal and analyze the different political approaches.

### US.6_12.2 + Unit 8 — Multiple Perspectives

**Standard:** Examine the impact of multiple perspectives on social, political, and cultural development.

**In Unit 8:** Examine multiple perspectives on Watergate, Reagan, and 1970s–1980s social and political changes.

**Key perspectives:**
- **Nixon's defenders**: Watergate was no worse than what other presidents did; Nixon accomplished real things (EPA, China, détente); the press destroyed him
- **Nixon's critics**: the rule of law required accountability; a president who believed he could authorize crimes destroyed the foundations of constitutional government
- **Reagan supporters**: morning in America; economic growth; military strength ending the Cold War; optimism
- **Reagan critics**: growing inequality; union destruction; AIDS response failure (Reagan didn't say the word "AIDS" publicly until 1987, when 20,000 Americans had already died); Iran-Contra criminality; homelessness increasing during economic boom
- **AIDS activism perspective**: Act Up, Larry Kramer, the quilt project; the government's failure to respond to a public health crisis because it primarily affected gay men
- **Working-class perspective on Reaganomics**: tax cuts for the wealthy; trickle-down economics that didn't trickle; PATCO strike-breaking as a message to all unions

**Learning targets:**
- I can examine multiple perspectives on Watergate's significance and on Reagan's legacy.
- I can analyze how different political perspectives led to different interpretations of the same historical events.

### US.6_12.3 + Unit 8 — Cause and Effect

**Standard:** Explain the relationship of events focusing on the link(s) between cause and effect.

**In Unit 8 — FPS target:** To what degree were the 1970s and 1980s a period of change in United States history?

**Key cause-and-effect chains:**
- **Watergate → Nixon resignation → erosion of trust in government**: the cover-up worse than the crime; Congress passes War Powers Act (1973) and campaign finance reform; public cynicism about government deepens
- **OPEC oil embargo (1973) → energy crisis → Carter's solar panels on the White House → Reagan's removal of solar panels**: a story about U.S. energy policy and political signaling
- **AIDS crisis + government inaction → LGBTQ political mobilization**: the decimation of the gay community by AIDS, combined with Reagan's silence, accelerated the formation of a politically organized LGBTQ rights movement
- **Reagan's PATCO strike-breaking (1981) → weakening of American labor movement**: when Reagan fired 11,000 striking air traffic controllers, it signaled that union-busting was acceptable; private sector unions decline sharply through the 1980s
- **Reagan military buildup + Gorbachev's reforms → Cold War end (1989)**: the mechanisms of the Soviet collapse — Reagan's challenge + internal Soviet dysfunction + Gorbachev's glasnost and perestroika

**Learning targets:**
- I can trace the cause-and-effect chain from Watergate to the post-Watergate reforms and changes in government trust.
- I can explain how Reagan's labor policies changed the balance of power between workers and employers.
- I can analyze the factors that led to the Cold War's end.

### US.6_12.4 + Unit 8 — Change Over Time

**Standard:** Compare how historical elements change over time.

**In Unit 8 — FPS target:** Develop an argument about a 1941–2001 topic (civil rights, nuclear power, technology/privacy, perceptions of political leaders, U.S. foreign policy) and how historical interpretation has changed over time.

**Key comparisons:**
- Nixon's resignation → Bill Clinton's impeachment → Trump's two impeachments: how presidential accountability has evolved
- 1973 oil crisis → 2020s energy debates: the politics of fossil fuel dependence
- Reagan's trickle-down economics → current debates about taxation and wealth inequality
- AIDS crisis government response → COVID-19 pandemic response: how does government respond when a disease initially affects a stigmatized group?
- Environmental regulation (1970s EPA) → contemporary climate debates: what changed?

### US.6_12.5 + Unit 8 — Significant Contributions of People and Policy

**Standard:** Analyze the significant contributions of people, policy, and the influence on an era.

**Key people and policies:**
| Person/Policy | Contribution |
|--------------|-------------|
| **Richard Nixon** | Watergate and resignation; also EPA, OSHA, Title IX, opening China, détente with Soviet Union — one of the most complicated presidential legacies |
| **Gerald Ford** | Pardoned Nixon ("Our long national nightmare is over"); most unelected president in history; the pardon may have cost him the 1976 election |
| **Jimmy Carter** | Energy policy, Camp David Accords, Iran hostage crisis; after presidency, Habitat for Humanity, election monitoring — arguably a better ex-president than president |
| **Ronald Reagan** | Supply-side economics, PATCO, AIDS response failure, Iran-Contra, Cold War buildup, Berlin Wall speech |
| **Reaganomics** | Supply-side/trickle-down economics: cut top marginal tax rates, deregulate industries, reduce government spending (except military) |
| **Iran-Contra** | Illegal sale of weapons to Iran; profits funneled to Nicaraguan rebels (Contras) in violation of congressional ban; Oliver North took the fall |

**Standard:** Connect the past to the present using current events.

## Key Vocabulary for Unit 8

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Watergate | The 1972 break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters, the subsequent cover-up, and Nixon's resignation when facing impeachment |
| stagflation | The combination of high inflation and high unemployment; seemed impossible under standard economic theory; characterized the 1970s |
| détente | The relaxation of Cold War tensions between the U.S. and Soviet Union under Nixon; included arms control agreements and opening of China relations |
| Reaganomics | Reagan's economic policy: cut top tax rates, deregulate industries, reduce social spending, build up military; also called "supply-side economics" or "trickle-down economics" |
| Iran-Contra | The illegal scheme in which Reagan administration officials sold weapons to Iran and used the profits to fund Nicaraguan Contras in violation of Congressional law |
| PATCO | Professional Air Traffic Controllers Organization; Reagan fired striking PATCO workers in 1981, signaling that union-busting was permissible |
| glasnost | Gorbachev's policy of political openness in the Soviet Union |
| perestroika | Gorbachev's policy of economic restructuring in the Soviet Union |
| AIDS crisis | The epidemic of acquired immunodeficiency syndrome; first identified in 1981 primarily in gay men; Reagan's government was slow to respond |
| malaise | Carter's term for America's "crisis of confidence" in 1979; his speech about it contributed to his electoral defeat |

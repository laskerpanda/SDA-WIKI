# Writing Standards Deep Dive

**Standards covered:** W.1 · W.2 · W.3 · W.4 · W.6 · L.1

Writing is the spine of SDA. Every studio requires a writing standard. This page explains how the six writing-related standards work together, why W.3 and W.4 are different, and what the "writing chain" means.

For scale breakdowns (1.0–4.0 scores) of each standard, see [ELA 9-10 Scales](gr9-10-scales.md).
For the per-studio rotation, see [Priority by Studio](gr9-10-priority.md).

---

## The Writing Chain

> Writing Standards → Written Demonstration → Showcase

Every studio produces a written product of some kind. That product serves two purposes at once:
1. It is your **demonstration of learning** — evidence that you can do the writing standard
2. It is the substance of your **deliverable** — the thing that gets exhibited

The chain means these aren't separate. You don't "do the writing standard" in one document and then "do the deliverable" in another. They are the same act. Writing *is* how you demonstrate learning, and it *is* what gets put into the world.

## The Six Writing Standards and How They Fit Together

Think of these six standards as a system, not a checklist:

| Standard | What It Controls | Analogy |
|----------|-----------------|---------|
| **W.1** | Clarity, format, and style for purpose + audience | The *what* of your writing: is it the right kind of document for this situation? |
| **W.2** | Organizational structure | The *shape* of your writing: does it have a beginning, middle, and end that make sense? |
| **W.3** | Explanatory/informational writing | The *informational mode*: you teach the reader something with a clear thesis + evidence |
| **W.4** | Argumentative/persuasive writing | The *argumentative mode*: you convince the reader of a position + refute counterarguments |
| **W.6** | The writing process | The *how you got there*: drafting, feedback, revision, refinement |
| **L.1** | Language and usage | The *mechanical layer*: syntax, punctuation, parallel structure, pronoun reference |

W.1 and W.2 are always operating beneath the surface. Even in a studio where W.4 is the active standard, your writing still needs to be clear (W.1) and organized (W.2). You're just not being scored on W.1 and W.2 separately — they're expected as baseline competencies.

W.6 and L.1 are also always present. The writing process happens every cycle. Language correctness is always a factor. But the primary scored writing standard per studio is W.3 or W.4.

## W.3 vs W.4: The Critical Distinction

This is the most important distinction in SDA writing. Students who mix these up will struggle.

### W.3 — Explanatory Writing

**What it is:** Informational writing. You take something complex and explain it clearly. Your job is to help the reader understand — not to persuade them of anything.

**Key moves in W.3:**
- A clear thesis that makes a specific claim about your topic (not just a topic announcement)
- Supporting claims that each address one aspect of the thesis
- Evidence from multiple relevant and reliable sources (not just one)
- The evidence is explained — not just dropped into the paragraph
- Sources are cited

**What W.3 is NOT:** W.3 does not have a counterclaim section. You're not arguing against anything. You're explaining.

**A W.3 thesis sounds like:** "The development of Islam in the 7th century CE fundamentally altered the political structure of the Arabian Peninsula by creating a legal and spiritual authority that overrode existing tribal systems."

That's a claim that can be explained and supported with evidence. It doesn't argue "and therefore Islam was good/bad." It explains *how* and *what*.

**Example studio context:** Studio 1 — Power & Belief uses W.3. Students explain *how* early religions justified political authority. The thesis makes a specific explanatory claim. The essay teaches the reader how that justification worked.

---

### W.4 — Argumentative Writing

**What it is:** Persuasive writing. You take a position and defend it. Your job is to convince the reader your argument is correct — which means you have to acknowledge the opposing argument and beat it.

**Key moves in W.4 that W.3 does NOT have:**
- Establishing **context** — the situation, stakes, and background the reader needs before you argue
- A clear **position/thesis** — not just a topic, but a defensible stance
- Evidence to support your claims (same as W.3 — this part is similar)
- **Counterclaim** — acknowledge what the other side would say, then refute it with evidence and logic
- **Logical reasoning** — your argument must hold together without fallacies

**What W.4 is NOT:** W.4 is not "writing that sounds aggressive." Many students write emotional arguments with no evidence, or they state their position repeatedly without engaging the counterargument. That is not W.4 at 3.0.

**A W.4 thesis sounds like:** "The French Revolution, while ideologically rooted in Enlightenment principles of liberty, ultimately produced a political structure more repressive than the monarchy it replaced — a consequence of its internal contradictions, not external threats."

That's a *position*. Someone could disagree with it. You have to defend it against people who disagree. That defense, done with evidence and logic, is what W.4 is.

**Example studio context:** Studio 3 — The Medieval Crisis asks "Is order worth obedience?" That's a W.4 studio. Students take a position on whether feudal order justified the constraints it placed on people. They argue a position, acknowledge the counterargument (yes, order provided stability and protection), and refute it or qualify it with specific historical evidence.

## Why They Alternate

The alternation is intentional pedagogy. Over a full year in SDA, you'll write both types in equal measure. That matters because:

1. **Explanatory writing builds different muscles.** You have to understand something deeply enough to explain it clearly. You can't hide behind rhetorical moves — the explanation either makes sense or it doesn't.

2. **Argumentative writing builds different muscles.** You have to be willing to take a position, know what the opposition says, and defeat it with evidence. Students who only write explanatory pieces tend to write arguments that never take a real stance.

3. **Real writing in the world is both.** A historian writes explanatory prose. An op-ed writer argues. A policy brief does both. You need both modes.

## W.6 — The Writing Process

W.6 is the metacognitive standard. It's not about what you write — it's about *how* you got there.

**The writing process in SDA:**

| Stage | When | What Happens |
|-------|------|--------------|
| Planning | Week 1–2 | Outline, thesis development, source identification |
| Drafting | Week 3 | First draft — get it down, don't over-edit |
| Feedback | Week 4 | Peer review + advisor conference. Document specific feedback received. |
| Revision | Week 4–5 | Substantive changes based on feedback — not just grammar fixes |
| Editing | Week 5 | Grammar, citation, formatting pass |
| Final | Week 5–6 | Exhibition-ready product |

**The difference between revision and editing:** Revision is big-picture — restructuring a paragraph, replacing a piece of evidence, rewriting a thesis. Editing is small-picture — fixing commas, correcting citations, cleaning prose. Most students skip revision and only edit. That's why their writing doesn't improve.

**W.6 at 3.0** means you can show your process — a draft with changes, feedback documented, a revision note explaining what you changed and why. It's not just "I made it better." It's "I changed the second paragraph because the evidence I had (X) didn't actually support my claim (Y). I replaced it with (Z) because..."

## L.1 — Language Knowledge in Context

L.1 is the standard that handles grammar and usage — but not as a list of rules to memorize. L.1 is about applying language knowledge for your specific task, purpose, and audience.

**The L.1 sub-skills that show up most in SDA writing:**

| Sub-skill | What It Means in Practice |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Syntax and phrases** | Sentence variety — not all simple, not all compound. Using subordinate clauses to show relationships between ideas. |
| **Colon and semicolon** | Colons introduce what follows; semicolons connect two complete related thoughts. Both show sentence-level control. |
| **Misplaced modifiers** | "Walking down the hall, the fire alarm surprised me." (The alarm wasn't walking.) These matter — they create unintended meanings. |
| **Pronoun reference** | Every pronoun needs a clear antecedent. "They did it because of this" — who is "they"? What is "this"? |
| **Parallel structure** | "She studied, worked, and slept" — not "She studied, went to work, and sleeping." Parallel structure in thesis statements and topic sentences signals control. |

**L.1 at 3.0** means you apply these skills for your specific task. A formal argument essay should read differently than a podcast script. The vocabulary, sentence structure, and punctuation choices should reflect knowledge of audience and purpose.

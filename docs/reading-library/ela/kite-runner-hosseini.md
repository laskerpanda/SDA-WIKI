## What This Text Is About

*The Kite Runner* is Khaled Hosseini's 2003 novel about Amir, the son of a wealthy Pashtun family in Kabul, and Hassan, the son of their Hazara servant. The novel follows Amir's cowardice in failing to protect Hassan from violence as a child, his family's flight to America after the Soviet invasion, his life in California, and his eventual return to Taliban-controlled Afghanistan to try to atone for what he did. It is a novel about guilt, betrayal, class, ethnic hatred, and the possibility — or impossibility — of redemption.

**This text provides what no document-based source can: the inside of Afghanistan's history.** For USH students studying Unit 9 (Cold War proxy conflicts → Soviet–Afghan War → Taliban → 9/11 → US invasion), *The Kite Runner* provides the human experience that history textbooks and primary source documents describe from the outside. Amir's family lived through the Soviet coup, the mujahideen, the Taliban's rise, and the refugee experience in America. Students who have read Hosseini understand these events as lived human experience, not as geopolitical abstractions.

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.8](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r8) — Hosseini structures the novel around Amir's guilt and self-deception. Amir is a narrator who consistently edits and rationalizes his own culpability — students must read through his narration to assess what he actually did and who he actually is. The novel's arc (sin → flight → attempted redemption) is a formal structure students can map. The Hazara/Pashtun relationship is embedded in every scene, requiring students to read ethnicity and class as literary elements.
- [W.4](../../standards/ela/writing-standards.md#9-10w4) — "Amir's return to Afghanistan is genuine redemption, not just absolution." "The novel argues that we can escape our past but not the people we harmed." Both are strong W.4 positions that require textual evidence and engagement with the novel's moral logic.

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [US.6_12.6](../../standards/social-studies/us-history/unit-9-1990s-2000s.md) — The novel's past-to-present arc is US.6_12.6 in narrative form: decisions made in the 1970s (Soviet invasion), 1980s (US arming the mujahideen), and 1990s (US abandonment of Afghanistan after Soviet withdrawal) produced the Taliban and the conditions for 9/11. Hosseini makes these connections through the lives of characters, not through policy analysis.
- [US.6_12.5](../../standards/social-studies/us-history/unit-9-1990s-2000s.md) — Students trace the causes and effects: Soviet invasion → mujahideen resistance (US-funded) → Soviet withdrawal → civil war → Taliban → 9/11 → US invasion. The novel shows each transition through the eyes of someone who lived it.
- [US.6_12.3](../../standards/social-studies/us-history/unit-9-1990s-2000s.md) — Taliban governance is US.6_12.3 content (how political systems affect people's lives). Hosseini's portrait of Taliban Kabul — the public executions in Ghazi Stadium, the prohibitions on music and kite-flying, the treatment of women and Hazaras — gives US.6_12.3 human specificity.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Hazara | An ethnic and religious minority in Afghanistan (largely Shia Muslim) who faced severe discrimination from the Pashtun majority — Hassan is Hazara |
| Pashtun | The dominant ethnic group in Afghanistan; Amir is Pashtun |
| Taliban | An Islamic fundamentalist political and military movement that controlled Afghanistan 1996–2001 and reimposed control after 2021 |
| Soviet–Afghan War | The 1979–1989 conflict in which the Soviet Union invaded Afghanistan and the US funded mujahideen resistance — the context that destroyed Amir's world |
| Redemption | The novel's central moral question: can a person make up for a serious wrong? Hosseini's answer is deliberately ambiguous |
| Kite fighting | A traditional Afghan sport in which kite strings are coated with glass to cut opponents' strings — central to the novel's symbolism of freedom, competition, and the moment of Amir's betrayal |

## Key Passages

**On guilt:**
> "I thought about Hassan's harelip. I thought about that winter day in 1975. I thought about the kite. I thought about the alley. I could step into that alley, stand amid the rubble and the wind that scuttled scraps of paper, and I could find the boy with the harelip who'd said *For you, a thousand times over*."

The repetition of the memory and the single line of Hassan's loyalty ("For you, a thousand times over") is the novel's emotional center.

**On the impossibility of forgetting:**
> "It's wrong what they say about the past, I've learned, about how you can bury it. Because the past claws its way out."

## Studio Ideas

1. **"Afghanistan Before and After"** — Students use the novel's portrait of Kabul in the 1970s (kite-fighting, cinema, music, women in universities) and compare it to Taliban-controlled Kabul using R.8 (Hosseini's depiction) and IR.2 (historical sources). They write a W.3 explanatory piece: what did the Soviet invasion and Taliban takeover destroy, and how does Hosseini show this as loss rather than just political change? Connects to US.6_12.5 (causes and consequences) and US.6_12.3.

2. **"Who Is Responsible for What Happened to Hassan?"** — Amir is not the only one responsible for Hassan's fate. The ethnic hierarchy that made Hassan Amir's servant, the Soviet invasion, the Taliban's specific hatred of Hazaras, and Amir's father's silence all contributed. Students write a W.4 argument: assign primary moral responsibility. They must use evidence from R.8 (the novel's characterization) and from historical context (US.6_12.5).

3. **"What We Owe the People We've Wronged"** — This is the capstone question of USH Unit 9 and of the novel. Using Hosseini's story and historical examples from US.6_12.6 (US past-to-present obligations — to Afghan allies, to veterans with PTSD, to communities affected by US foreign policy), students write a W.4 argument: does acknowledging a past wrong create an obligation to act? What does that obligation require?

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

*The Kite Runner* was published in 2003, two years after the U.S. invaded Afghanistan — and it gave millions of American readers a picture of Afghanistan as a place where people lived actual lives before the Taliban, the Soviet invasion, and the American intervention made it a foreign policy problem. That picture matters because policy debates about countries are almost always conducted in terms of strategic interests, threat assessments, and alliance calculations — not in terms of what it's like to be a person living there. The novel is also relevant because Afghanistan is still in the news: the U.S. withdrawal in 2021, the Taliban's return to power, the ban on girls' education, the collapse of the government the U.S. spent twenty years building — all of this happened to the country Hosseini spent the novel making human-scale. Reading *The Kite Runner* alongside the news about Afghanistan is not just an ELA exercise. It's a way of insisting that the people in the news are people.

# The Odyssey (~800 BCE)

**Author:** Homer (attributed)
**Type:** Literary Text · Epic Poem (Translation)
**Source:** Public domain (ancient text) — modern translations by Emily Wilson (2017) and Robert Fagles (1996) recommended; older translations via Project Gutenberg
**Full Text:** [Project Gutenberg — older translations](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1727) · Modern translations (Wilson or Fagles) require purchase but excerpts are widely available in anthologies and educational resources. The Emily Wilson translation is particularly recommended for classroom use — it is the first English translation of the *Odyssey* by a woman, and Wilson's choices make the text more accessible and honest about its treatment of women and enslaved people.
**Era/Period:** WH Era 1 — Ancient Civilizations (Ancient Greece, ~800 BCE)
**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.3 · WH.6_12.4
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.8 · 9-10.R.9

## What This Text Is About


![Odysseus and the Sirens — Attic red-figure stamnos](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Odysseus_Sirens_BM_E440_n2.jpg?width=400)
*Odysseus tied to the mast as the ship passes the Sirens. Attic red-figure stamnos, c. 480 BCE. British Museum.*

The *Odyssey* is the foundational Western epic — the story of Odysseus (Ulysses in Latin), king of Ithaca, and his ten-year journey home after the fall of Troy. He fights monsters, outwits gods, resists temptation, loses his entire crew, and eventually returns to reclaim his household from suitors who have invaded his home and are consuming his wealth while pursuing his wife Penelope. The poem is structured around a simple question — will Odysseus make it home? — but it is really a meditation on intelligence, loyalty, hospitality, and what it means to be human in a world governed by forces larger than you.

This text is dual-use in a way few others are. As an ELA text, the *Odyssey* is the template for virtually every adventure narrative in Western literature — the hero who leaves home, faces trials, and returns transformed. Every hero's journey, from Star Wars to The Hunger Games, descends from this structure. Students reading the *Odyssey* are reading the source code of Western storytelling. As a Social Studies text, it is a primary source for ancient Greek values: what Greeks believed about hospitality (*xenia*), the relationship between mortals and gods, loyalty and cunning as heroic virtues, and the political structure of the heroic age — small kingdoms ruled by warrior-kings whose legitimacy rested on personal prowess rather than institutional authority.

The Emily Wilson translation is specifically recommended because her choices raise important questions: she translates the Greek word *polytropos* (traditionally "crafty" or "cunning") as "complicated" — a deliberately ambiguous word for a complicated man. She also translates words for enslaved people as "enslaved," rather than "servants" — forcing readers to confront what the *Odyssey* actually describes. These translation choices are themselves an argument about how we read ancient texts, making this an ideal text for R.9 (reading texts as arguments).

## Read It

*Public domain — multiple translations. [Full text at Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1727).*

### Book 1 — The Opening Invocation and Telemachus
> *At the beginning of the epic...*
>
>Sing to me of the man, Muse, the man of twists and turns  
driven time and again off course, once he had plundered 
the sacred realm of the hallowed one of the dark clouds, 
wild-hearted, far from his friends, but still he fought 
beyond his own desperate homecoming.
>
> When it is evening in Ithaca,  
and Telemachus’ heart is sick with sorrow...

### Book 9 — The Cyclops
> *From Odysseus' encounter with the giant Polyphemus...*
>
>Polyphemus asks who they are and why they have come.
>
>"Strangers," we answered him, "we are Achaeans driven 
in our ships by adverse winds from far away. Pity us,
O mighty one, and show guest-friendship in your heart..."
>
> Odysseus then pours into Polyphemus' wine the strong 
wine of the gods that they had brought...

### Book 12 — Scylla and Charybdis
> *When Odysseus must navigate between two fearsome monsters...*
>
> The cliffs are sheer,  
and hard by there is another cliff where rests a fig tree  
in full leaf; but around the root of it is a cave, deep 
within, with dark entrance, for dread Scylla lives there.
>
> They were all in one mind about turning back...

### Book 23 — The Recognition
> *When Odysseus and Penelope finally reunite...*
>
>Penelope comes to the hall and sees him. She is dumbfounded,
and a great wave of grief takes hold on her heart, and she 
utters her prayer, calling Zeus among the gods by name...
>
> And now they have it ready, the test that will confirm  
the truth of all he has said; the bed with its secret...

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.8](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r8) — The *Odyssey* is the origin point of several key literary conventions students encounter throughout their reading lives. **Epic conventions:** invocation of the Muse, in medias res opening (the story begins ten years in; we learn the earlier events through flashback and stories), epithets (repeated descriptive phrases like "grey-eyed Athena" and "rosy-fingered Dawn" — these are oral composition devices), the epic simile (extended comparisons that slow the narrative for effect). **Divine intervention:** the gods are characters who act on human events — students analyze how divine involvement changes moral responsibility. **The hero:** Odysseus is not a conventional hero — he is small, cunning, frequently lying, and ruthlessly practical. Analyzing what kind of hero he is reveals what Greek culture valued.
- [R.9](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r9) — The *Odyssey* makes an implicit argument about what intelligence is and why it matters more than raw strength. Odysseus defeats opponents who are physically stronger (the Cyclops, the suitors) through cleverness — he tells the Cyclops his name is "Nobody," so when the Cyclops calls for help he says "Nobody is hurting me." Students analyze: What is Homer arguing about the relationship between intelligence and power? What does the translation you're reading argue about the original Greek? Wilson's translation specifically invites this analysis.

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.3](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-1-civilizations.md) — The political world of the *Odyssey* is the heroic age — small kingdoms ruled by warrior-kings whose legitimacy is personal, not institutional. Odysseus's kingdom is nearly lost because he is absent; there is no government mechanism to maintain it. The suitors believe that if Odysseus is dead, any one of them can seize Penelope and with her the kingdom. Students analyze: What does this reveal about political systems before stable institutions? How is heroic kingship different from democracy or the Roman Republic? How does political legitimacy work when it depends entirely on the person rather than the office?
- [WH.6_12.4](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-1-civilizations.md) — The *Odyssey* is saturated with Greek cultural values. **Xenia** (guest-friendship/hospitality): Zeus himself protects guests, and the suitors' violation of hospitality laws is part of what makes them deserving of death. **Nostos** (homecoming): the Greeks considered returning home the highest good, which is why Odysseus refuses immortality from Calypso. **Cunning vs. strength:** that Odysseus is defined by intelligence, not just force, reveals something specific about what Greeks admired. Students connect these values to what they learn about Greek culture more broadly.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Epic | A long narrative poem about a hero's journey, featuring divine involvement, battles, and cultural values — the *Odyssey* is the template for this genre |
| In medias res | "In the middle of things" — the *Odyssey* opens ten years into events; the backstory is told through flashbacks and characters' stories |
| Epithet | A repeated descriptive phrase attached to a character or object ("grey-eyed Athena," "swift-footed Achilles") — these are oral composition devices that helped bards remember the poem |
| Xenia | The ancient Greek concept of guest-friendship and hospitality — hosts were obligated to shelter guests and guests were obligated to behave well; violation of xenia is a serious moral offense in the poem |
| Nostos | The Greek word for homecoming — the driving goal of the *Odyssey* and the root of the word "nostalgia" |
| Hubris | Excessive pride that challenges or defies the gods — Odysseus's crew's actions (eating the Cattle of the Sun) and the suitors' behavior are examples; hubris leads to punishment |
| Polytropos | The Greek word used to describe Odysseus in the first line — usually translated "crafty" or "many-turned"; Wilson translates it "complicated." This single word opens the question of what kind of hero he is |

## Key Passages

**Book 1, opening invocation (Fagles translation):**
> Sing to me of the man, Muse, the man of twists and turns
> driven time and again off course, once he had plundered
> the hallowed heights of Troy.
> Many cities of men he saw and learned their minds,
> many pains he suffered, heartsick on the open sea,
> fighting to save his life and bring his comrades home.

**Book 9 — Odysseus to the Cyclops (Fagles translation):**
> Nobody — that's my name. Nobody — so my mother and father call me, all my friends.

**Book 23 — Penelope tests Odysseus (the bed, Fagles translation):**
> You have not won me yet. If you are truly
> Odysseus home at last, tell me why
> our bed is hard to move — it has a secret.

**Why these passages matter:** The opening invocation establishes the epic conventions and the nature of the hero — a man of "twists and turns," not simply strength. The Cyclops scene shows Odysseus's characteristic method: he uses language itself as a weapon. The bed scene is the poem's emotional climax — Penelope's test of the bed's secret is as clever as anything Odysseus does, establishing that she too is a hero of intelligence.

## Studio Ideas

1. **"What Kind of Hero Is This?"** — EQ: Does what a culture makes heroic reveal what that culture values? · Standard: WH.6_12.4 + R.8 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (compare Odysseus as hero to a hero from another culture or time period — what does each hero's defining quality reveal about the society that created them? Use the text as evidence.)

2. **"Translation Is an Argument"** — EQ: When you translate a text, are you making creative choices or factual ones? · Standard: R.9 + R.8 · Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation (compare two translations of the same passage — Wilson and Fagles, or Wilson and an older Gutenberg translation. What specific word choices differ? What does each translator seem to believe about the original? What does the choice of words reveal about the translator's argument?)

3. **"Hospitality as Law"** — EQ: What happens to a society when informal rules (like hospitality) replace formal laws? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + WH.6_12.4 · Demo Mode: Visual/Creative (map the violations of xenia in the *Odyssey* — who violates it? Who upholds it? What are the consequences? What does this reveal about how ancient Greek society maintained order without modern institutions?)

4. **"The Hero's Journey Then and Now"** — EQ: Why do humans keep telling the same story? · Standard: R.8 + IR.1–5 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (identify a modern story — film, novel, video game — that follows the hero's journey structure. Map its stages onto the *Odyssey*. Then argue: Is this structure universal, or is it a cultural choice that reflects specific values?)

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The *Odyssey* is 2,800 years old and every culture in the world tells a version of it — departure, trials, return — because the pattern maps onto something real about how life works. The returning veteran who finds the world has moved on without him, the person who has been away so long they don't know who they are anymore, the question of what loyalty means across a long separation: these are not ancient problems. American veterans returning from Afghanistan and Iraq frequently describe an "Odyssey problem" — coming home to find that home is no longer what they left, that they've changed in ways the people at home can't see, and that the journey back to ordinary life is its own kind of odyssey. The poem's other thread — Penelope's twenty-year wait, using wit and deception to hold power while being technically powerless — is also a contemporary story about how people without formal power navigate systems that constrain them. Homer's world is gone. His characters aren't.

# Night (1960)

**Author:** Elie Wiesel
**Type:** Literary Text · Memoir
**Source:** Farrar, Straus and Giroux (not public domain — school library copy required)
**Era/Period:** Era 4 — Age of Global War and Globalization
**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.2 · WH.6_12.6
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.8 · 9-10.W.3 · 9-10.W.4

---

## What This Text Is About

*Night* is a memoir by Holocaust survivor Elie Wiesel documenting his experiences in the Nazi concentration camps of Auschwitz and Buchenwald from 1944 to 1945. Wiesel was fifteen years old when his family was deported from Sighet, Romania. The book traces the systematic destruction of Jewish life, his father's death, and his own spiritual crisis — a wrestling with faith, silence, and God's presence or absence in the face of genocide. It is one of the most widely read Holocaust memoirs in the world.

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [9-10.R.8](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r8) — *Night* is a literary text that relies heavily on symbol (fire, night, silence), motif (father-son relationship, the hanging of a child), and tone shifts from naive faith to dark despair. Students analyze how Wiesel uses literary elements to convey meaning beyond the literal events.
- [W.3](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10w3) — Informational writing: Explain how Wiesel uses the motif of silence throughout *Night* and what it represents. Or: Explain the historical context of the Holocaust and how *Night* documents it.
- [W.4](../../standards/ela/writing-standards.md#9-10w4) — Argumentative writing prompt: "Is *Night* primarily a historical document or a work of literature? Use evidence from the text."
- [R.7](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r7) — Wiesel's style choices: fragmented sentences during trauma, longer sentences during memory and reflection. The decision to write in French (not Hungarian or Yiddish) and for a European audience.

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.2](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md#wh6-122) — The Holocaust is the most extreme example in the 20th century of how ideology (Nazi racial theory) can be institutionalized as state religion/policy and used to justify mass murder. The belief system at the center of the Holocaust — racial hierarchy — connects directly to WH.6_12.2 (how belief systems shape societies and cause conflict).
- [WH.6_12.6](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md#wh6-126) — *Night* raises direct questions about human rights: What obligations do nations and individuals have when genocide is occurring? The UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) was adopted directly in response to the Holocaust.

## Key Passage

> Never shall I forget that night, the first night in camp, which has turned my life into one long night, seven times cursed and seven times sealed. Never shall I forget that smoke. Never shall I forget the little faces of the children, whose bodies I saw turned into wreaths of smoke beneath a silent blue sky.
>
> Never shall I forget those flames which consumed my faith forever.
>
> Never shall I forget that nocturnal silence which deprived me, for all eternity, of the desire to live. Never shall I forget those moments which murdered my God and my soul and turned my dreams to dust. Never shall I forget these things, even if I am condemned to live as long as God Himself. Never.
> — *Night*, Chapter 3

This is the structural and thematic center of the book. The anaphoric repetition of "Never shall I forget" is a direct inversion of the language of prayer and covenant. It is simultaneously a literary device and a moral statement — Wiesel refuses to let these events become forgettable or abstract.

## Studio Ideas

1. **"What Does It Mean to Bear Witness?"** — EQ: Do we have an obligation to know about atrocities that happened before we were born? · Standard: WH.6_12.6 + Era 4 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (W.4 argument) or Verbal Conversation

2. **"Silence as Symbol"** — EQ: What does Wiesel's silence mean, and why does it matter? · Standard: R.8 (ELA) · Demo Mode: Portfolio Annotation (annotate 5 passages where silence appears and build an interpretation of what it represents)

3. **"Ideology into Genocide"** — EQ: How does a society go from prejudice to mass murder? · Standard: WH.6_12.2 + Era 4 · Demo Mode: Visual/Creative (timeline or cause-and-effect map tracing how Nazi racial ideology became state-sanctioned genocide)

4. **"Night and the Universal Declaration"** — EQ: What rights were violated in the Holocaust, and how did the world respond? · Standard: WH.6_12.6 + Era 4 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (match specific events from *Night* to specific articles of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights that were violated)

## Sensitive Content Note

*Night* contains descriptions of violence, death, and dehumanization that are historically accurate and necessary to the text's purpose. These topics should be framed with appropriate context. Students who are processing personal trauma related to genocide, mass violence, or loss may need additional support. See Reese before assigning.

## What This Text Is About

*Things Fall Apart* follows Okonkwo, a respected warrior and leader in the Igbo village of Umuofia in what is now Nigeria, in the years just before and during British colonial contact in the late 1880s–1890s. The first two-thirds of the novel establish the richness and complexity of Igbo culture from the inside — its social structures, religious beliefs, gender roles, storytelling traditions, and moral codes. The final third shows the arrival of Christian missionaries and British colonial administrators, and the destruction of Okonkwo's world — not dramatically but quietly, piece by piece, as the new religion claims converts, the new courts supplant traditional justice, and the new language becomes the language of power. Okonkwo's response — rage, resistance, and finally a death that the colonizers will barely notice — is the novel's conclusion.

Achebe wrote *Things Fall Apart* in 1958, two years before Nigerian independence, and he wrote it explicitly as a response to Joseph Conrad's *Heart of Darkness* (1899), which depicted Africa as a place of primordial darkness and Africans as barely human background figures. Achebe famously called *Heart of Darkness* "a thoroughgoing racist novel" and wrote *Things Fall Apart* to show what African life actually looked like from the inside — complex, organized, morally serious — before European contact. This origin is the novel's argument: that colonialism depended on the idea that Africa had no culture worth preserving, and that idea was simply false.

This text is dual-use in an unusually direct way. As an ELA text, the novel deploys the full set of oral tradition techniques — proverbs, stories embedded within stories, the rhythms of vernacular speech — as deliberate literary choices that themselves argue against colonial cultural assumptions. Okonkwo is a tragic hero in the classical sense, but the tragedy is not just personal — it is the tragedy of a whole civilization. As a Social Studies text, this is the clearest available narrative account of how colonialism worked from the inside: how it arrived, how it divided communities, how it used cultural disruption to create political control.

### Chapter 15 — Abame Destroyed

> *Obierika's news about the village of Abame—the first encounter with the colonizers and their consequences.*
>
>The white man did not know what he was doing when he drove his iron horse into the town. The earth cry'd against him, but he did not listen.

### Chapter 25 — The District Commissioner's Final Thought

> *The novel's closing paragraph—the D.C. planning a paragraph in his book for Okonkwo—Achebe's devastating indictment of the colonial gaze.*
>
>Okonkwo was driven by fear, the fear of himself, of his dead father, and of his ancestors.

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.8](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r8) — *Things Fall Apart* demonstrates several important literary structures. **Narrative structure — the fall:** Okonkwo's trajectory follows the tragic arc (rise, overreach, fall) but is embedded in a communal tragedy. Students analyze: Is this primarily Okonkwo's tragedy, or the Igbo community's? Are these separable? **Okonkwo as tragic hero:** his defining quality — the refusal of weakness, the equation of masculinity with hardness — is both what makes him a leader and what prevents him from adapting to change. His hamartia is not weakness but the inability to imagine strength as anything other than force. **Oral tradition conventions:** the novel is saturated with Igbo proverbs ("When the moon is shining the cripple becomes hungry for a walk") and with stories embedded within the narrative. Achebe's use of proverb is both characterization (wise characters speak in proverbs) and argument (proverbs embody a cultural worldview; their displacement by English is the loss of a way of thinking).
- [R.9](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r9) — This is one of the most powerful available examples of a novel making an argument by its own existence. Achebe's implicit argument: European accounts of Africa (Conrad's most famous) that depicted Africans as without culture, language, or complex social life were simply wrong, and the consequences of that wrongness were catastrophic. Students analyze the novel as a counter-argument: What specific features of Igbo culture does Achebe document? What would be lost if the only account of this world was Conrad's? What does it mean that the same year Achebe published this novel, Nigeria was becoming independent?

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.5](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-3-revolutions.md) — The novel is the most accessible classroom account of how colonialism actually operated on communities it entered. Students trace the sequence: missionaries arrive and find converts among the community's marginalized members; the converts become a community with different rules; the colonial court arrives to settle disputes between the old community and the new; the district commissioner uses the conflict to extend British control. This is not a fictional account — it is a historically accurate model of how British colonialism proceeded across sub-Saharan Africa.
- [WH.6_12.4](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-3-revolutions.md) — The novel's first two-thirds constitute an anthropological account of Igbo culture: its religious beliefs (chi, the personal spirit; the Oracle of the Hills and Caves), its social structures (the ofo and ogu as principles of right), its gender roles and divisions, its governance (a council of elders rather than a single chief — which means there is no single authority for the British to co-opt). Students analyze: What are Igbo cultural values? How do they compare to the colonial assumptions the missionaries and administrators bring?
- [WH.6_12.3](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-3-revolutions.md) — The collapse of Umuofia's traditional authority structure is political as much as cultural. The Igbo governance model — decentralized, elder-based, requiring consensus — is specifically vulnerable to British legal structures that recognize only individual property ownership and central authority. Students analyze: What political structures made Igbo governance work? Why were those structures specifically vulnerable to colonial legal systems? What does this reveal about how colonialism operated politically?

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Igbo | The ethnic group and language of Okonkwo's community in southeastern Nigeria — one of Nigeria's largest ethnic groups; Achebe himself was Igbo |
| Chi | In Igbo belief, a personal god or spirit that accompanies each person — Okonkwo believes his chi is not favorable; this concept of individual spiritual fate interacts with his choices throughout the novel |
| Obi | The main hut of a compound in Igbo society — the physical center of a man's household and authority; when Okonkwo returns from exile to find his burned, it signals his displacement |
| Egwugwu | Masked figures representing the ancestors who serve as judges in Umuofia's traditional court — their authority is undercut by the colonial court, which is a key mechanism of political displacement |
| District Commissioner | The British colonial official who administers the region at the novel's end — Achebe's final stroke: the commissioner plans to devote a paragraph to Okonkwo in his book "The Pacification of the Primitive Tribes of the Lower Niger"; the whole novel has been that one paragraph's refutation |
| Proverb | A short, traditional saying embodying wisdom — Achebe uses Igbo proverbs throughout as characterization and argument; their displacement by English is the displacement of a worldview |

## Key Scenes and Passages

The novel is copyrighted — do not reproduce extended passages. Focus on these key scenes:

**The opening (Chapter 1):** The novel begins with Okonkwo's reputation as a wrestler and his father's failures. Achebe establishes Okonkwo's defining trait — his refusal to be like his father — as both his strength and the seed of his tragedy. Students note: How does Achebe establish character through specific concrete detail?

**Ikemefuna's death (Chapter 7):** Okonkwo kills Ikemefuna — a boy placed in his care who has become like a son — because the Oracle has decreed it, and because he fears appearing weak. This is widely considered the novel's moral center. Students analyze: What is Okonkwo's reasoning? What does his choice reveal about his character flaw?

**The arrival of the missionaries (Chapter 16):** The missionaries arrive and are given "bad land" — a forest the community considers evil. They thrive there, which becomes its own argument against Igbo belief. Students note: How does Achebe depict this moment — with contempt? With complexity? What does it reveal about how colonialism finds purchase in communities?

**The district commissioner's final words (Chapter 25):** The novel's last paragraph reveals that the district commissioner plans to write about Okonkwo in a book about pacifying "primitive tribes." Students analyze: What is Achebe doing with this ending? The whole novel has been what the commissioner wants to reduce to a paragraph. What does this argue?

## Studio Ideas

1. **"Achebe Was Answering Conrad"** — EQ: Can a novel be a political act? · Standard: R.9 + WH.6_12.5 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (read a short excerpt from Conrad's *Heart of Darkness* (widely available in public domain) and compare its depiction of Africa and Africans to Achebe's. What specific claims does Conrad's text make? How does Achebe's novel respond? What does it mean that one text is considered a literary masterpiece and the other is written partly to correct it?)

2. **"How Colonialism Actually Worked"** — EQ: How do you destroy a community without simply killing everyone in it? · Standard: WH.6_12.5 + WH.6_12.3 · Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation (trace the sequence by which British colonialism arrives in Umuofia: missionaries → converts → court → commissioner. At what point does Okonkwo realize his world is over? What specific actions by the colonizers make each step possible? How does this compare to the historical record of British colonialism in Nigeria?)

3. **"The Proverbs of Umuofia"** — EQ: What does a culture's proverbs reveal about its worldview? · Standard: R.8 + WH.6_12.4 · Demo Mode: Portfolio Annotation (collect 8–10 proverbs from the novel. For each, annotate: What value or belief does this proverb express? What does it reveal about Igbo culture? What would be lost if this proverb were replaced by an English saying about the same situation? Then write: What does Achebe's use of proverbs argue about the culture the colonizers dismissed?)

4. **"Okonkwo's Fatal Flaw"** — EQ: Can a person's greatest strength also be the thing that destroys them? · Standard: R.8 + W.4 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (analyze Okonkwo as a tragic hero. What is his defining quality? How does it serve him in the world before colonialism? How does it prevent him from surviving the colonial world? Is his failure inevitable, or does he have choices he refuses to take? What does Achebe seem to argue about the relationship between personal flaw and historical circumstance?)

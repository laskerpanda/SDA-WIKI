# Romeo and Juliet (~1597)

**Author:** William Shakespeare
**Type:** Literary Text · Play · Tragedy
**Source:** Public domain — written ~1594–1596, published 1597
**Full Text:** [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1513) · [Folger Shakespeare Library — annotated](https://shakespeare.folger.edu/shakespeares-works/romeo-and-juliet/)
**Era/Period:** WH Era 2 — Renaissance (set in Renaissance Verona; written in Elizabethan England)
**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.3 · WH.6_12.4
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.8 · 9-10.W.4

## What This Text Is About

*Romeo and Juliet* is Shakespeare's tragedy of two young people from feuding noble families in Verona — the Montagues and the Capulets — who fall in love, secretly marry, and die within the span of four days because private family war and public civic authority cannot solve a conflict they have made everyone else's problem. Romeo kills Juliet's cousin Tybalt in a street fight; Juliet's parents arrange her marriage to someone else; a message fails to arrive on time. The tragedy is not just about two unlucky individuals — it is about what happens when private violence (the family feud) operates in the middle of a public city and the public authority (Prince Escalus) lacks the power to stop it.

This text is dual-use in ways that are easy to overlook. As an ELA text, *Romeo and Juliet* is one of the most formally sophisticated plays in the Shakespeare canon — the prologue is a perfect sonnet; the play contains another sonnet hidden in Romeo and Juliet's first meeting; the verse structure tracks social status; the foil characters (Romeo vs. Mercutio, Juliet vs. the Nurse) are precisely constructed. As a Social Studies text, the play dramatizes the political reality of Renaissance Italy: the survival of private family power (the feudal clan structure) within a city trying to establish civic authority. Prince Escalus is repeatedly unable to enforce the peace because the families are more powerful than the state. This is a documented feature of Renaissance Italian political life — the struggle between emerging civic governments and entrenched noble families.

Shakespeare's audience in 1597 would have read the feud as the problem — not the love story. The modern tendency to focus entirely on the romance inverts what the play actually argues.

## Read It

*Public domain. [Full text at Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1513).*

### Act I, Scene 5 — 'Did my heart love till now?'

> Did my heart love till now? Forswear it, sight!  
For I ne'er saw true beauty till this night.

> — *Romeo sees Juliet for the first time at the Capulet ball, and Tybalt recognizes Romeo, feeling a deep hatred towards him.*

>

### Act II, Scene 2 — The Balcony Scene

> But soft, what light through yonder window breaks?  
It is the east, and Juliet is the sun.  
Arise, fair sun, and kill the envious moon,  
Who is already sick and pale with grief...

> > Two hours of love's precious dawnings will shine  
> > Away eight long nights, having little strength  
> > With which to keep on our perpetual night.

> — *Romeo stands below Juliet's balcony in a famous declaration of his love for her.*

>

### Act III, Scene 1 — The Turning Point

> This day’s black fate on more days doth depend;  
This but begins the woe other days predict.  
The worm i’ th’ bud! O, woeful day!

> > Romeo kills Tybalt in a duel and is banished by Prince Escalus.

— *Mercutio's death at the hands of Tybalt and subsequent revenge killing of Tybalt by Romeo mark the pivotal moment where the play shifts from romance to tragedy.*

>

### Act V, Scene 3 — The Final Scene

> Here’s to my love!  
O true apothecary, thy bare neelds  
Are sharp enough to sheathe another man.
— *Romeo in the tomb, having taken poison to be with Juliet and about to die.*

>

> For never was a story of more woe  
Than this of Juliet and her Romeo.

— *The Prince's final speech upon discovering the tragic deaths of both young lovers, highlighting the devastating cost of the long-standing feud between their families.*

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.8](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r8) — *Romeo and Juliet* offers one of the richest sets of literary structures available in a single text. **Sonnet form:** the Prologue is a perfect Shakespearean sonnet (14 lines, ABAB CDCD EFEF GG); Romeo and Juliet's first conversation (Act I, Scene 5) is a hidden sonnet split between their dialogue — Elizabethan audiences would have recognized this as a signal of romantic destiny. **Foil characters:** Mercutio is everything Romeo is not — cynical, realistic, funny, unwilling to be absorbed into romantic fantasy; Juliet is more rational than Romeo almost from the start (the balcony scene, which is actually Juliet's scene, not Romeo's). **Dramatic structure:** students map the classic five-act tragic structure. **Dramatic irony:** the audience is told in the Prologue that both die — every hopeful moment is shadowed by this knowledge.
- [W.4](../../standards/ela/writing-standards.md#9-10w4) — The central argument the play invites is: **Who is responsible for Romeo and Juliet's deaths?** Shakespeare provides multiple candidates — the feuding families, the Friar's plan, the failed message, fate, the lovers' own haste, Prince Escalus's inability to enforce the peace. Students construct a position using evidence from the text, which requires weighing competing causal claims — exactly what W.4 argument writing demands.

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.3](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-2-middle-ages.md) — The feud between the Montagues and Capulets is not just personal grudge — it represents the political structure of Renaissance Italian city-states, where noble families wielded private military and judicial power within cities theoretically governed by civic authorities. Prince Escalus's repeated failures to end the feud document this tension. Students analyze: What does the play reveal about the relationship between private power (noble families) and public authority (the Prince) in Renaissance Italy? How is this different from modern government? How does this connect to the broader Renaissance political context (Machiavelli's *The Prince* was written in 1513 for exactly this political world)?
- [WH.6_12.4](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-2-middle-ages.md) — The play dramatizes Renaissance social norms around gender, honor, and marriage that are specific to this era and culture. Juliet is 13; her parents arrange a marriage without asking her. Honor violence (Tybalt's challenge, Romeo's revenge killing) is presented as a social norm that men are obligated to participate in, even when they don't want to. Students examine: What does the play reveal about gender roles and family structure in Renaissance society? What does the honor culture require of its participants?

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Sonnet | A 14-line poem with a specific rhyme scheme — Shakespeare used the form ABAB CDCD EFEF GG; the Prologue is a sonnet, and Romeo and Juliet's first meeting contains a hidden sonnet split between their dialogue |
| Foil | A character whose qualities deliberately contrast with another character's to highlight both — Mercutio is Romeo's foil (cynical vs. romantic); the Nurse is Juliet's foil (practical vs. idealistic) |
| Dramatic irony | The audience knows the outcome (announced in the Prologue) before the characters do — every hopeful scene is shadowed by knowledge of the ending |
| Honor culture | A social system in which men are required to defend their personal and family reputation through violence — central to the play's conflict and to Renaissance Italian and Elizabethan social reality |
| Prologue | The opening speech that announces the play's story — in *Romeo and Juliet*, it is a sonnet that tells the audience both characters die, creating dramatic irony from the first scene |
| Feudal clan | A noble family that maintains private military power and private courts of justice — the Montagues and Capulets are Renaissance versions of this medieval institution, operating inside a city trying to establish civic government |

## Key Passages

**Prologue — the sonnet (Shakespeare's framing of the whole play):**
> Two households, both alike in dignity,
> In fair Verona, where we lay our scene,
> From ancient grudge break to new mutiny,
> Where civil blood makes civil hands unclean.
> From forth the fatal loins of these two foes
> A pair of star-cross'd lovers take their life;
> Whose misadventured piteous overthrows
> Do with their death bury their parents' strife.

**Act II, Scene 2 — The balcony scene (Juliet, not Romeo, is the rational one):**
> What's in a name? that which we call a rose
> By any other name would smell as sweet;
> So Romeo would, were he not Romeo call'd,
> Retain that dear perfection which he owes
> Without that title.

**Act V, Scene 3 — Prince Escalus's final verdict:**
> See, what a scourge is laid upon your hate,
> That heaven finds means to kill your joys with love.
> And I for winking at your discords too
> Have lost a brace of kinsmen: all are punish'd.

**Why these passages matter:** The Prologue announces that the lovers are "star-cross'd" — but it also frames their deaths as the result of the "ancient grudge." Shakespeare is telling us from the start what this play is actually about. Juliet's balcony speech is the play's most philosophical moment — she is arguing that identity is not fixed by name or family, which is the argument the feud refuses to accept. Escalus's final lines acknowledge his own complicity — he "winked at" the violence and paid for it.

## Studio Ideas

1. **"Who Killed Romeo and Juliet?"** — EQ: Can a tragedy have more than one cause? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + W.4 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (identify at least four contributing causes to the lovers' deaths — structural, personal, political, and chance. Construct a ranked argument: which cause bears the most responsibility? Use specific evidence from the play. Consider: Is the play's answer the same as yours?)

2. **"Honor Culture Then and Now"** — EQ: What does a culture's concept of honor require its members to do? · Standard: WH.6_12.4 + R.8 · Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation (identify every moment in the play where a character acts because honor requires it. What would have happened if Tybalt had refused to fight? If Romeo had walked away? What does this reveal about how honor cultures operate? Are there modern equivalents?)

3. **"The Hidden Sonnet"** — EQ: What does it mean that their first conversation is a sonnet? · Standard: R.8 · Demo Mode: Portfolio Annotation (find Romeo and Juliet's first conversation in Act I, Scene 5. Identify the 14 lines that form a sonnet between them. Annotate: Why would Shakespeare structure their first meeting as a sonnet? What does the form signal? What happens to the sonnet when Juliet's mother interrupts them at line 14?)

4. **"Private War in a Public City"** — EQ: What happens when powerful private groups don't follow public law? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + WH.6_12.4 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (research one real Renaissance Italian city — Florence, Venice, or Verona itself — and find evidence of noble family violence within the city. How did civic authorities respond? Write a comparison: How does the real history compare to what Shakespeare depicts?)

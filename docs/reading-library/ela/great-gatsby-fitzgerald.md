# The Great Gatsby (1925)

**Author:** F. Scott Fitzgerald
**Type:** Literary Text · Novel
**Source:** Public domain — entered US public domain January 1, 2021
**Full Text:** [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/64317)
**Era/Period:** USH Unit 2 — The Roaring Twenties / Jazz Age (1920s)
**USH Standards:** US.6_12.4 · US.6_12.1
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.8 · 9-10.R.9 · 9-10.W.4

## What This Text Is About

*The Great Gatsby* is set in the summer of 1922 on Long Island, New York. Jay Gatsby is a fabulously wealthy man who throws enormous parties every weekend but attends none of them — he is waiting for one specific guest who never comes: Daisy Buchanan, the woman he loved five years ago, who is now married to the careless and brutal Tom Buchanan. The story is narrated by Daisy's cousin Nick Carraway, who moves into the house next to Gatsby and slowly becomes his only genuine friend. Fitzgerald tracks the intersecting lives of the very rich, the newly rich, and the working poor across a single summer that ends in violence and moral catastrophe.

This text is dual-use in a way that makes it one of the most teachable novels in the American tradition. As an ELA text, it is extraordinarily dense with symbol and narrative craft — the green light, the Valley of Ashes, the eyes of Doctor T.J. Eckleburg, Gatsby's parties, his pink suit, Nick's narration. Almost every detail means something. As a Social Studies text, it is an American primary source document: Fitzgerald was writing about the 1920s as they happened, and the novel captures the specific social reality of the decade — the explosion of new wealth from Prohibition-era bootlegging, the class anxiety between old money (the Buchanans in East Egg) and new money (Gatsby in West Egg), the consumer culture explosion, and the racial anxieties articulated explicitly by Tom Buchanan. The 1920s ended three years after Gatsby was published; Fitzgerald seemed to know they would.

The novel's deepest argument is about the American Dream itself — whether the promise of self-invention and upward mobility is genuine or is a story the wealthy tell to maintain their own position while keeping newcomers striving just out of reach.

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.8](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r8) — *The Great Gatsby* is one of the richest texts for symbolic analysis in American literature. **The green light:** Gatsby reaches toward the green light at the end of Daisy's dock throughout the novel; it transforms from a personal longing into a symbol of the American Dream itself in the final pages. **The Valley of Ashes:** the industrial wasteland between West Egg and New York where working people live in the shadow of the wealthy — it is both a real place and a symbol of what the Roaring Twenties cost those who didn't benefit from them. **The eyes of Doctor T.J. Eckleburg:** an enormous faded billboard advertising an optometrist, overlooking the Valley of Ashes — widely interpreted as the faded eyes of God watching over a society that has abandoned moral values. **Nick as unreliable narrator:** Nick claims to be "one of the few honest people" he has ever known while regularly withholding information from the reader and rationalizing the behavior of people he admires.
- [R.9](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r9) — Fitzgerald's implicit argument runs through every page: that the American Dream is fraudulent — not because self-invention is impossible, but because the society it operates in does not actually respect self-made people. Gatsby achieves everything the Dream promises, and the old-money establishment destroys him without a second thought. Students analyze: What specific details make this argument? What does Nick's final narration argue explicitly? Is the novel's argument that the Dream is false, or that the society that hosts it is?
- [W.4](../../standards/ela/writing-standards.md#9-10w4) — The novel invites a W.4 argument at multiple levels: Is the American Dream a lie? Is Gatsby sympathetic or pathetic? Is Tom the villain or is he exposing a truth? Students construct a position on any of these questions using textual evidence — the novel is dense enough that opposing positions can both find real support.

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [US.6_12.4](../../standards/social-studies/us-history/unit-3-roaring-20s.md) — *The Great Gatsby* is a primary source for 1920s class and economic reality. The novel documents the specific social geography of new wealth vs. old wealth, the explosion of consumer spending, the glittering surface of prosperity, and the invisible underside (the Valley of Ashes, Myrtle Wilson's death). Tom Buchanan's casual racism — he cites a eugenics book and lectures Nick about "the rise of the colored empires" — is a documented feature of upper-class 1920s white culture. Students use the novel as a primary source: What did 1920s America look like from inside, for people of different classes?
- [US.6_12.1](../../standards/social-studies/us-history/unit-3-roaring-20s.md) — The novel captures the 1920s technological and consumer revolution. Cars are everywhere — and kill people. Gatsby's parties feature technologies that didn't exist a generation earlier (electric light, phonographs, bootleg liquor on an industrial scale). The consumer culture that Fitzgerald depicts — where people buy things to signal status — is a product of 1920s mass production and advertising. Students analyze: What specific technologies and economic developments made this world possible?

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| The American Dream | The belief that through hard work and ambition, any person can succeed in America regardless of their origin — the novel interrogates whether this is genuinely available or a myth maintained to serve those who already have power |
| Old money vs. new money | A social distinction in the 1920s (and beyond) between inherited wealth (East Egg — the Buchanans) and recently acquired wealth (West Egg — Gatsby) — old money considered new money vulgar regardless of its amount |
| Prohibition | The constitutional ban on alcohol from 1920–1933 — Gatsby made his fortune bootlegging illegal alcohol; this is what enabled the explosion of "new money" wealth the novel depicts |
| Unreliable narrator | A narrator whose account cannot be fully trusted — Nick claims to be honest but consistently withholds, distorts, and rationalizes; readers must read around his stated perspective |
| The Valley of Ashes | The industrial wasteland between Long Island and New York in the novel — where working people live in literal ash and shadow; a symbol of what the boom economy costs those who don't benefit from it |
| The green light | The light at the end of Daisy's dock that Gatsby reaches toward — it begins as a personal symbol of longing and expands in the final pages to represent the American Dream itself |

## Key Passages

**Chapter 1 — Nick's opening credo (establishing his "reliability"):**
> In my younger and more vulnerable years my father gave me some advice that I've been turning over in my mind ever since. "Whenever you feel like criticizing anyone," he told me, "just remember that all the people in this world haven't had the advantages that you've had."

**Chapter 6 — Gatsby's real past (the moment the illusion cracks):**
> The truth was that Jay Gatsby of West Egg, Long Island, sprang from his Platonic conception of himself. He was a son of God—a phrase which, if it means anything, means just that—and he must be about His Father's business, the service of a vast, vulgar, and meretricious beauty.

**Final page — The novel's argument:**
> So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past.

**Why these passages matter:** Nick's opening lines establish him as apparently generous and fair — which makes his subsequent behavior and judgments more troubling to track. The Chapter 6 passage reveals what Gatsby actually is: someone who invented himself from scratch to serve his own ideal image — which Fitzgerald calls both magnificent and "meretricious" (showy, false). The final line is the novel's thesis: the American Dream is about forward motion, but the current pulls everyone backward into the past they came from. Gatsby cannot escape being James Gatz from North Dakota.

## Studio Ideas

1. **"Is the American Dream a Lie?"** — EQ: Does the American Dream apply equally to everyone, or is it a story that benefits some people more than others? · Standard: US.6_12.4 + W.4 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (use the novel and at least one historical source from the 1920s to construct an argument about the American Dream. What does Fitzgerald seem to argue? Do you agree? What evidence from the 1920s economic data supports or complicates the novel's argument?)

2. **"Nick as Unreliable Narrator"** — EQ: What does it mean to tell a story in a way that hides as much as it reveals? · Standard: R.8 + R.9 · Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation (identify three moments where Nick's narration seems to be withholding, distorting, or rationalizing. What does he leave out? What would the story look like told from Daisy's perspective? From Tom's? From Jordan's? What does Fitzgerald gain by choosing Nick?)

3. **"Mapping the 1920s"** — EQ: What does a novel tell you about its time that a history textbook doesn't? · Standard: US.6_12.1 + US.6_12.4 · Demo Mode: Visual/Creative (create a social map of the novel's world: East Egg, West Egg, the Valley of Ashes, New York. For each location, identify who lives there, what economic class they represent, and what historical reality they document. Annotate with evidence from both the novel and 1920s history.)

4. **"Tom Buchanan's Bookshelf"** — EQ: What does a character's stated beliefs reveal about the culture they come from? · Standard: US.6_12.4 + IR.1–5 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (Tom Buchanan cites a fictional eugenics book and talks about "the rise of the colored empires." Research real eugenics books that circulated in 1920s upper-class America. What beliefs did they contain? Who read them? Write an analysis: What does this reveal about the specific racial ideology of the 1920s elite class?)

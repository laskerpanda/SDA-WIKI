# Lord of the Flies (1954)

**Author:** William Golding
**Type:** Literary Text · Novel · Allegory

**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.3 · WH.6_12.5
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.8 · 9-10.W.4

## What This Text Is About

*Lord of the Flies* begins with a plane carrying British schoolboys being shot down during a nuclear war. The boys — all male, all British, trained in the values of civilized society — land on an uninhabited island with no adults. At first they organize themselves democratically: Ralph is elected leader, Piggy provides the intellectual framework, the conch shell is the symbol of legitimate speech and order. Within weeks, the democratic structure has collapsed. Jack's tribe of hunters, operating on fear and ritual, has overthrown Ralph's order. By the end, they are hunting Ralph to kill him, and two boys are dead — one murdered, one sacrificed. A naval officer arrives to rescue them and is disturbed by what he finds.

Golding wrote this novel in 1954, nine years after the end of World War II, in which he served. His direct experience of what human beings do to each other — the Holocaust, the firebombing of Dresden, the atomic bombs — is the raw material of this book's argument. He wrote it as a direct response to *The Coral Island* (1857), a Victorian adventure novel in which British boys on a desert island behave splendidly and prove the superiority of British civilization. Golding's boys prove the opposite.

This text is dual-use in a way that is unusually direct. As an ELA text, the allegory is dense and carefully constructed: the conch as democratic legitimacy, Piggy's glasses as science and reason, the beast as the externalization of the boys' own violence, the pig's head (the Lord of the Flies) as the embodiment of evil that exists within. As a Social Studies text, the novel is a post-WWII argument about what the war revealed: that civilization is not a human default but a fragile construction, and that the conditions for fascism and mob violence are not unique to Germany or Japan but exist in every human community.

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.8](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r8) — *Lord of the Flies* is one of the most extensively allegorical novels taught at the secondary level. **Allegory:** nearly every significant object and character represents an abstract idea. **Symbolism:** the conch (democratic authority and legitimate speech — it loses its power as the boys lose their civilization); Piggy's glasses (rational thought and science — stolen by Jack, who cannot produce fire through reason, only through force); the beast (the boys' own capacity for violence, projected outward onto the island so they don't have to acknowledge it is inside them); the pig's head on a stick, the Lord of the Flies (the title comes from a Hebrew word for "Beelzebub" — a name for the devil; Golding is arguing that evil is not external but internal). **Democratic vs. authoritarian leadership:** Ralph and Jack represent two models of leadership — one that requires consent and participation, one that operates through fear, ritual, and force. Students analyze how Golding constructs each model and what the novel argues about why the authoritarian one wins.
- [W.4](../../standards/ela/writing-standards.md#9-10w4) — The central argument the novel forces: **Are humans naturally violent, or does civilization make us peaceful — and what happens when civilization is removed?** Golding has a clear answer; students are not required to agree with it. Students construct their own position — using evidence from the novel, from psychology (Golding was influenced by early 20th-century ideas about the unconscious), and from historical examples — then engage with Golding's counterargument.

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.3](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md) — The collapse of the boys' democratic structure and its replacement by Jack's authoritarian tribe is a small-scale model of a process that happened at national scale in the 20th century. Students analyze: What political conditions led the boys to abandon Ralph's democracy? How does Golding's model compare to historical accounts of how democratic systems fail? What does the novel suggest about what democracy requires of its participants to survive?
- [WH.6_12.5](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md) — Golding wrote this novel in direct response to WWII — his argument is that the war did not reveal something unique about German or Japanese culture but something universal about human nature under certain conditions. Students examine: What does the novel argue WWII revealed about human beings? Is this argument historically defensible? What evidence would support or contradict it? How did intellectuals in the 1940s and 1950s respond to the atrocities of WWII?

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Allegory | A narrative in which characters, events, and objects systematically represent abstract ideas — in *Lord of the Flies*, almost every significant element has an allegorical meaning |
| The conch | The shell Ralph uses to call meetings and establish the right to speak — it represents democratic legitimacy; as it loses power, so does the boys' civilization |
| The beast | The boys' name for a creature they believe lives on the island — Simon is the first to understand it is not external; it is their own capacity for violence |
| Lord of the Flies | The severed pig's head on a stick that Jack's tribe leaves as an offering to the beast — Beelzebub in Hebrew; Golding names his novel after it to argue that evil is internal |
| Simon | The novel's mystic/prophet figure — the boy who understands intuitively what the beast is; his death at the hands of the group in a ritual frenzy is the novel's moral center |
| Civilization | The novel treats civilization as a fragile construction maintained by specific institutional and cultural practices — not a natural human condition but an achievement that must be actively sustained |

## Key Scenes and Passages

The novel is copyrighted — do not reproduce extended passages. Focus on these key scenes:

**The election (Chapter 1):** Ralph is elected leader rather than Jack — and Jack immediately starts undermining his authority. Students note: what gives Ralph legitimacy? What makes Jack dangerous even when he loses?

**Simon's conversation with the Lord of the Flies (Chapter 8):** The pig's head "speaks" to Simon and tells him the beast is inside the boys. Simon is the only character who grasps the novel's central argument. Students analyze: What is Golding doing by making the novel's moral insight come from its most fragile character?

**Simon's death (Chapter 9):** The boys, caught up in ritual, kill Simon — who is trying to bring them truth — while mistaking him for the beast. Students note: this is the novel's argument enacted rather than stated.

**Piggy's death (Chapter 11):** Roger deliberately levers a boulder to crush Piggy and smash the conch simultaneously — science and democratic legitimacy destroyed in the same moment by a character who has become purely id. The conch shatters.

## Studio Ideas

1. **"Are Humans Naturally Violent?"** — EQ: Does civilization create good people, or do good people create civilization? · Standard: WH.6_12.5 + W.4 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (construct a position on Golding's central argument. Use evidence from the novel, from WWII history, and from at least one piece of contemporary research on human behavior under stress. Does the novel's argument hold? What would disprove it?)

2. **"Why Did Ralph Lose?"** — EQ: What does democracy require to survive? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + R.8 · Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation (identify the specific moments when Ralph's democratic authority breaks down. What did Jack do to undermine it? What did the other boys do? What does this reveal about the conditions democracy requires? Find a historical parallel — a democracy that collapsed — and analyze whether the same factors were at work.)

3. **"Golding Was Writing About WWII"** — EQ: What did WWII reveal about human nature that previous generations didn't believe? · Standard: WH.6_12.5 + IR.1–5 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (research the specific atrocities of WWII — the Holocaust, the Rwandan genocide, the experiments of Milgram and Zimbardo in the 1960s and 70s — that led social scientists and artists to argue that ordinary people are capable of extraordinary evil under the right conditions. Write an argument: Is Golding's claim about human nature accurate? What's the strongest counter-evidence?)

4. **"The Leadership Map"** — EQ: What model of leadership does this novel recommend? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + R.8 · Demo Mode: Visual/Creative (create a side-by-side comparison of Ralph's and Jack's leadership styles — what each one offers, what each requires of followers, what each one's weaknesses are. Then apply to a real historical example: find a moment when a democratic leader and an authoritarian leader competed for the same population. Who won, and why?)

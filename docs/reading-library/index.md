# Reading Library

**SDA Commons Wiki · South High School**

This is where you find texts for your studio. Every entry has the full passage, plain-language context, vocabulary, and connections to your standards — everything you need to start reading and building an argument.

---

## Start Here

**What week are you in?**

- **Week 1 — Launch:** Browse the table below by era or text type. Pick one passage from each of your studio's four texts. You don't need to read everything — just enough to write a first reaction.
- **Week 2 — Dig:** Come back to the 1–2 texts that grabbed you. Use the full entry — read the key passage, check the vocabulary, note the standard connections.
- **Week 3–5 — Build, Shape, Finish:** Use the "Studio Ideas" section of each entry to sharpen your argument. Check the crosswalk entries if your studio spans both ELA and SS.
- **Stuck?** If you don't know which texts fit your studio, go to your **Studio Packet page** first — it lists the four recommended texts and why they matter.

## How to Read an Entry

Every text in this library has the same structure — once you know it, you can navigate any entry in about 30 seconds:

| Section | What it tells you |
|---------|------------------|
| **What This Text Is About** | Two sentences. Plain language. Read this first. |
| **Read It** | The actual passage — a key excerpt embedded directly on the page |
| **ELA Standards This Connects To** | Which reading/writing standards this text helps you demonstrate |
| **Social Studies Standards** | Which SS standard + era this connects to |
| **Key Vocabulary** | Words you need to know before you read |
| **Studio Ideas** | Ready-made Essential Questions built around this text |
| **Why This Is Relevant Today?** | How this old text connects to something happening right now |

### Social Studies Primary Sources

#### Era 1 — Ancient Civilizations (c. 3000 BCE – 500 CE)

These texts ask: *Who gets power, and why? How do societies decide what is just?*

| Text | Author | What it's about |
|------|--------|----------------|
| [Egyptian Book of the Dead](social-studies/book-of-the-dead-egypt.md) | Ancient Egyptian scribes | What ancient Egyptians believed about death, judgment, and the soul |
| [Hammurabi's Code](social-studies/hammurabi-code.md) | Hammurabi | One of the world's first written legal codes — 282 laws carved in stone |
| [The Dhammapada](social-studies/dhammapada-buddhism.md) | Attributed to the Buddha | Core Buddhist teachings on the mind, suffering, and how to live |
| [The Twelve Tables of Rome](social-studies/twelve-tables-rome.md) | Roman Decemviri | Rome's first public law code — written so citizens could know their rights |
| [Polybius — On the Roman Republic](social-studies/roman-republic-polybius.md) | Polybius | A Greek historian explains why Rome's mixed government made it so powerful |
| [The Analects](social-studies/analects-confucius.md) | Confucius | Short teachings on leadership, virtue, and how to run a just society |
| [Pericles' Funeral Oration](social-studies/pericles-funeral-oration.md) | Thucydides | Athens' most famous speech defending democracy — and what it cost |
| [The Melian Dialogue](social-studies/melian-dialogue.md) | Thucydides | Athens tells a small island: surrender or be destroyed. The island refuses. |

> **Read these two together:** Pericles' Funeral Oration and the Melian Dialogue are by the same author, set 15 years apart. One celebrates Athenian democracy. The other shows what that democracy did when nobody was watching.

#### Era 3 — Age of Revolutions and Industry (1750–1914)

These texts ask: *What makes a revolution legitimate? Who gets left out?*

| Text | Author | What it's about |
|------|--------|----------------|
| [Common Sense](social-studies/common-sense-paine.md) | Thomas Paine | The pamphlet that convinced a generation of colonists that independence was obvious |
| [Declaration of the Rights of Man](social-studies/declaration-rights-man.md) | National Assembly of France | France's version of universal rights — written during one of history's bloodiest revolutions |
| [Declaration of the Rights of Woman](olympe-de-gouges-declaration-woman.md) | Olympe de Gouges | Written in response to the Declaration above — because it didn't include women |
| [A Vindication of the Rights of Woman](social-studies/wollstonecraft-vindication.md) | Mary Wollstonecraft | The argument that women's subjugation is taught, not natural — 1792 |
| [Haitian Declaration of Independence](social-studies/haitian-declaration-independence.md) | Dessalines / Boisrond-Tonnerre | The only successful slave revolt in history announces itself to the world |
| [The Communist Manifesto](social-studies/communist-manifesto.md) | Marx & Engels | The argument that history is a story of class conflict — and that workers will win |
| [Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass](social-studies/narrative-frederick-douglass.md) | Frederick Douglass | A formerly enslaved man describes what slavery actually was — and dismantles every argument for it |
| ["The White Man's Burden" + H.T. Johnson response](social-studies/kipling-white-mans-burden.md) | Kipling / Johnson | A pro-empire poem and a direct rebuttal — read them together |
| [The Jungle](social-studies/jungle-sinclair.md) | Upton Sinclair | A novel about immigrant meatpacking workers that accidentally exposed the entire food industry |
| ["Dulce et Decorum Est"](social-studies/dulce-et-decorum-est.md) | Wilfred Owen | A WWI soldier's poem about what it actually looks like when someone dies from a gas attack |

#### Era 4 — Global War and Globalization (1914–Present)

These texts ask: *What do we owe each other? Who decides what freedom means?*

| Text | Author | What it's about |
|------|--------|----------------|
| [FDR — Four Freedoms Speech](social-studies/fdr-four-freedoms.md) | Franklin D. Roosevelt | FDR defines what America is fighting for in WWII — including freedom from fear and want |
| [UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights](social-studies/un-declaration-human-rights.md) | United Nations | The world's attempt, after WWII, to say: these rights belong to every human |
| [Letter from Birmingham Jail](social-studies/mlk-letter-birmingham.md) | Martin Luther King Jr. | MLK responds to white clergymen who told him to slow down — one of the great arguments in American history |
| [Rivonia Trial Speech](social-studies/mandela-rivonia-speech.md) | Nelson Mandela | Mandela speaks at his own trial for fighting apartheid — knowing he might be sentenced to death |
| [UN Speech — "One Child, One Teacher"](social-studies/malala-un-speech.md) | Malala Yousafzai | A teenager shot for going to school addresses the United Nations |

> **Rights chain:** If you're tracing the argument for universal human rights across history, read these in order: Hammurabi's Code → Magna Carta → Common Sense → Declaration of Rights of Man → Haitian Declaration → FDR Four Freedoms → UN UDHR → Malala. Each one builds on — or challenges — the one before it.

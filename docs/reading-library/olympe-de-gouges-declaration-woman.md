## What This Text Is About
The "Declaration of the Rights of Woman and of the Female Citizen" is a political manifesto published in 1791 by Olympe de Gouges, a French playwright and political activist who sought to extend the revolutionary principles of liberty, equality, and fraternity to women. De Gouges' declaration mirrors the famous "Declaration of the Rights of Man," but it specifically addresses the rights of women, challenging the gender inequalities that persisted in post-Revolutionary France. This document is a cornerstone in the history of feminism and human rights advocacy, advocating for women's political, social, and economic equality.

This text is crucial for high school students to understand because it introduces them to the foundational concepts of civil rights and feminist philosophy during the Age of Revolutions. It also encourages critical thinking about the role of gender in society and its impact on individual freedoms and collective progress.

## Read It
*Source: Declaration of the Rights of Woman and of the Female Citizen by Olympe de Gouges, 1791*

### Preamble
> Women, wake up! A revolution is being prepared in France but are we to be excluded from its benefits? No. Take courage then, a day is coming when it will be announced: "Woman, you are no longer enslaved; woman, learn to know yourself and to arm yourself with the knowledge of your rights."
>
> The natural rights of women and of men are liberty, equality, property, and resistance to oppression.

### Articles 1–6 — Core Rights
> Article I. Women are born free and remain equal to men in rights.
>
> Article II. Distinctions based on sex are contrary to nature; they should be abolished by law.
>
> Article III. The right to participate in the making of laws is a fundamental guarantee of women's freedom.
>
> Article IV. Liberty consists in being able to do anything which does not harm others; thus, the exercise of the natural rights of every woman has no bounds other than those that ensure to the community at large the enjoyment of its own liberty.
>
> Article V. The law has only the right to prohibit such actions as are hurtful to society. Everything else is beyond the pale of its authority because it would be prejudicial to personal freedom.

### Articles 10–11 — Freedom of Speech
> Article X: "Woman has the right to mount the scaffold; she must equally have the right to mount the rostrum."
>
> [This article emphasizes that if women are subject to punishment under law, they should also be afforded the same opportunities as men to express their opinions and engage in public debate.]

### The Social Contract — Postscript
> A society where half of its members suffer from a lack of freedom cannot be considered free. Women must have an equal voice in shaping laws and participating in governance because this is essential for the realization of true liberty and equality.

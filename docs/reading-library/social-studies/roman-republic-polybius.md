**Author:** Polybius

## What This Text Is About

Polybius was a Greek historian taken to Rome as a hostage in 168 BCE, who ended up spending decades among the Roman ruling class and became fascinated by Rome's rise to power. In Book VI of his *Histories*, he explains Rome's success by analyzing the structure of its government — and argues that Rome works because it mixes three forms of rule into one system: monarchy (the consuls), aristocracy (the Senate), and democracy (the popular assemblies). This is one of the oldest systematic analyses of how a government works and why — and it directly influenced the writers of the U.S. Constitution nearly 2,000 years later.

### The Three Forms of Government and Rome's Mixed Constitution (Book VI, Chapters 11–14)

> The three kinds of government — monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy — were all found at Rome, and so equally and fairly blended that no one, even of the Romans themselves, could say with certainty whether the whole system was aristocratic, democratic, or monarchical. And this was natural. For if we fixed our eyes on the power of the consuls, the constitution seemed purely monarchical; if on the senate, it seemed aristocratic; and if on the power of the people, it seemed democratic.
>
> The consuls, before leading out their legions, exercise authority in Rome itself over all public affairs. Other magistrates are subject to them and must obey. They introduce foreign ambassadors to the Senate. They consult the Senate on urgent matters and carry out the decrees. They have absolute power over everything connected with preparation for war and the general conduct of campaigns in the field. They may spend public money as they think fit and have a quaestor who is completely at their orders.
>
> The Senate controls the treasury and regulates all revenue and expenditure. It controls all crimes committed in Italy requiring a public investigation — such as treason, conspiracy, poisoning, and assassination. The Senate also settles disputes among Italian cities, and if any city needs a reprimand or requires the help of Roman troops, the Senate is responsible. When embassies arrive from outside Italy, the Senate decides how to receive them and how to answer them.
>
> The people ratify or reject laws. They deliberate on the question of war and peace. They sanction or reject the agreements and treaties made with foreign states. When the Senate brings a proposal before them, the people vote upon it.
>
> Since all three branches have the ability to help or hinder each other, the union is permanent. For whenever any danger from without compels them to unite and work together, the strength which is developed by the state is so extraordinary that every essential matter is attended to eagerly. And this is the reason that the Romans are invincible.

### On Anacyclosis — The Cycle of Governments (Book VI, Chapters 4–9)

*Before analyzing Rome, Polybius explains his theory of anacyclosis — the idea that governments naturally degrade over time if they are not checked.*

> Kingship is the original form of government — it arises naturally when the strongest and most intelligent man leads a group and others follow him because he serves the common good. But kingship degrades into tyranny, when the king begins to serve his own interests and not the public good.
>
> Aristocracy arises when the best men, angered by tyranny, overthrow the tyrant and govern in the interest of the people. But aristocracy degrades into oligarchy, when those who inherit power begin to use it for their own enrichment rather than the common welfare.
>
> Democracy arises when the people, angered by oligarchy, overthrow the powerful few and take control themselves. And democracy too degrades — into mob rule — when the people forget the laws and each man begins to pursue his own interest, thinking only of himself.
>
> This cycle — kingship, tyranny, aristocracy, oligarchy, democracy, mob rule, and back to kingship — has recurred throughout history. The Romans saw this cycle and asked: can a government be designed to stop it?

---

### Rome's Answer: Balance (Book VI, Chapter 18)

> Such, then, is the constitution of Rome, as I see it. From what I have said it will be clear that each individual element can, if it chooses, obstruct or co-operate with the others.
>
> When one part swells with pride and tends to become over-ambitious, as may happen with any of the three elements, it is evident that none of them is self-sufficient. Each is dependent on the others. Any one element can be checked and impeded by the others, so that none of them can for long be dominant or unduly powerful.
>
> Thus the constitution remains in equilibrium for many generations, because each part is afraid to be seen trying to dominate, remembering the resistance it will face from the others.

---

### Why Carthage Failed and Rome Succeeded (Book VI, Chapter 51)

> The Carthaginian constitution had been well designed in its earlier period. They had kings, a council of elders with aristocratic power, and a people with its own rights. On the whole the balance was good.
>
> But when Hannibal and the Carthaginians were at the height of their power, the Carthaginian constitution had already begun to degenerate. The people had already gained more power than they should have.
>
> At Rome, on the other hand, the Senate had the chief authority, and the people were still content to take a secondary role. As a result, at Rome the wisest men were making the decisions; at Carthage, the masses were deciding by vote — and the masses make the worst decisions.
>
> This is why Rome overcame Carthage.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Anacyclosis | Polybius's theory that governments cycle through six forms — monarchy → tyranny → aristocracy → oligarchy → democracy → mob rule — and then back again |
| Mixed constitution | A government that combines elements of monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy so that each checks the others; Polybius's term for the Roman Republic |
| Consul | One of two chief executives of the Roman Republic, elected annually; the monarchical element in Polybius's analysis |
| Senate | The council of aristocratic elders that controlled Roman finances and foreign policy; the aristocratic element |
| Assemblies | The popular bodies through which Roman citizens voted on laws and elected magistrates; the democratic element |
| Oligarchy | Rule by a small group of wealthy elites who use power for their own benefit rather than the common good |
| Carthage | Rome's great rival city-state in North Africa; Polybius uses Rome's victory over Carthage to argue that Rome's constitution was superior |

## Key Passage

> Since all three branches have the ability to help or hinder each other, the union is permanent. For whenever any danger from without compels them to unite and work together, the strength which is developed by the state is so extraordinary that every essential matter is attended to eagerly. And this is the reason that the Romans are invincible.

This is Polybius's core argument: mutual dependence and mutual checking creates stability and strength. James Madison read Polybius closely before drafting the U.S. Constitution. The checks and balances system in American government is directly descended from this analysis.

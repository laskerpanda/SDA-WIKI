# Haitian Declaration of Independence (1804)

**Author:** Jean-Jacques Dessalines (proclaimed); Louis Boisrond-Tonnerre (drafted)
**Type:** Primary Source · Founding Document
**Source:** Public domain — translated text available via [Yale Gilder Lehrman Center](https://glc.yale.edu/haitian-declaration-independence-1804)
**Era/Period:** Era 3 — Revolution and Industrialization
**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.3 · WH.6_12.5 · WH.6_12.6
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.9 · 9-10.W.4

---

## What This Text Is About

On January 1, 1804, the former French colony of Saint-Domingue declared itself the independent Republic of Haiti — the first and only nation in history founded by formerly enslaved people through successful revolution. The declaration is strikingly different from the American Declaration of Independence (1776) and the French Declaration of the Rights of Man (1789): where those documents appealed to universal reason and natural rights, the Haitian declaration is openly furious. It names France directly as a perpetrator of atrocities, declares that Haitians will never again live under European colonial rule, and — critically — extends citizenship to all people regardless of race, years before any other nation did so. The declaration was so threatening to slaveholding nations that the original French-language version was lost for nearly 200 years; the full text was only rediscovered in a British archive in 2010.

---

## Read It
*Public domain. The 1804 declaration was long thought lost; a copy was discovered in 2010 at the British National Archives. Translation from the French original.*

### The Proclamation

> In the name of the Supreme Being, and by order of General-in-Chief Jean-Jacques Dessalines, Governor-General of Hayti:  
>
> Whereas the tyranny of France has oppressed our country for more than two hundred years; whereas it was necessary to fight against this tyranny in order to secure freedom for ourselves; whereas we have triumphed over the enemy and achieved independence:
>

### The Oath

> We swear to renounce France forever, and never to submit to any foreign power.  
>
> In testimony whereof, we have caused these presents to be signed with our hand at the City of Gonaïves, this first day of January one thousand eight hundred and four.
>

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.9](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r9) — The Haitian Declaration is an argumentative founding document, but its argument is structured very differently from the U.S. Declaration or the French Declaration of the Rights of Man. Where Jefferson uses natural law philosophy, Dessalines uses historical grievance and direct accusation. Students analyze how the same type of document (founding declaration) can make fundamentally different arguments to fundamentally different audiences.
- [W.4](../../standards/ela/writing-standards.md#9-10w4) — The declaration models argument-by-historical-record: rather than abstract principles, it lists specific acts and their consequences. Use as a W.4 model for evidence-based argument that depends on documented wrongs rather than philosophical premises.
- [R.2](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r2) — The tone of this document requires careful reading — it is angrier than students expect a founding document to be. Inference work: Why is the tone this way? What does it tell you about the audience, the purpose, and the historical context?

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.3](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-3-revolutions.md) — The Haitian Declaration represents a distinct model of revolutionary political thought — one that extends Enlightenment ideals of freedom and rights to enslaved people in a way that neither the American nor French revolutions were willing to do. It forces students to examine whose revolution the Enlightenment actually was.
- [WH.6_12.5](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-3-revolutions.md) — The Haitian Revolution's causes and effects are among the most complex in the era: a slave rebellion → ideological crisis for slaveholding nations → French bankruptcy (Haiti was forced to pay France 150 million francs in "reparations" for lost slave property) → Haitian economic isolation that persists to the present. The declaration is the beginning of that chain.
- [WH.6_12.6](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md) — Haiti was economically isolated and undermined by France and the United States for over a century following independence. The legacy of those reparations (not paid off until 1947) directly shaped Haiti's present-day poverty — one of the clearest historical causation chains from 1804 to 2026.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Saint-Domingue | The French colonial name for what became Haiti — the most profitable colony in the world at the time of the revolution |
| Dessalines | Jean-Jacques Dessalines, formerly enslaved, general of the revolutionary forces, first ruler of independent Haiti |
| Reparations (reversed) | France demanded 150 million francs from Haiti in 1825 as "compensation" for lost slave property — Haiti paid this until 1947 |
| Universal citizenship | The Haitian constitution declared all citizens "black" regardless of actual skin color — a deliberate inversion of racial hierarchy |
| Boisrond-Tonnerre | The drafter of the declaration, who reportedly said it should be written "with the skin of a white man for parchment, his skull for an inkwell, his blood for ink, and a bayonet for a pen" |

## Key Passages

> It is not enough to have expelled the barbarians who have bloodied our land for two centuries; it is not enough to have restrained those ever-evolving factions that one after another mocked the specter of liberty that France dangled before you... We must, with one last act of national authority, forever assure the empire of liberty in the country that gave us birth...
> — Haitian Declaration of Independence, January 1, 1804

> Let us ensure, however, that a missionary spirit does not destroy our work; let us allow our neighbors to breathe in peace; may they live quietly under the laws that they have made for themselves, and let us not, as revolutionary firebrands, declare ourselves the legislators of the Caribbean, or make glory out of disturbing the peace of the neighboring islands...
> — Haitian Declaration of Independence, January 1, 1804

**Why these passages matter:** The first passage establishes that the declaration is not just political — it is a reckoning with history. The second is surprising: the new nation explicitly commits to NOT spreading its revolution to other islands. This is a strategic choice; they understood they were surrounded by hostile slaveholding nations. Students often expect the opposite — this requires real historical inference.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Haiti's Declaration of Independence in 1804 was the first successful revolution by enslaved people in history, and the world's response was to isolate and punish the new nation for having the audacity to win. France demanded reparations — payment for the "value" of the enslaved people who had freed themselves and the colony they had built — and Haiti was still paying that debt until 1947. The U.S. didn't recognize Haitian independence until 1862, when the Confederacy had left Congress and the slaveholders' objection to a free Black republic was temporarily removed. Haiti's subsequent history — poverty, political instability, foreign intervention — cannot be understood without understanding what was done to it in the decades after independence. The question of reparations for slavery is partly a question about Haiti: a country that has been paying reparations for emancipation for nearly two centuries while the world calls its poverty a product of its own failures. The Haitian Declaration is also the only founding document in history written explicitly to justify revolution against slavery.

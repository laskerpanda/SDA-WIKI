# "One Child, One Teacher, One Book, One Pen" — UN Speech (2013)

**Author:** Malala Yousafzai
**Type:** Primary Source · Speech
**Source:** [United Nations — full transcript and video](https://www.un.org/press/en/2013/sgsm15109.doc.htm) — public domain
**Era/Period:** Era 4 — Age of Global War and Globalization
**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.2 · WH.6_12.6
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.9 · 9-10.W.4 · 9-10.C.1

---

## What This Text Is About

On July 12, 2013 — her 16th birthday — Malala Yousafzai addressed the United Nations Youth Assembly in New York. Nine months earlier, on October 9, 2012, a Taliban gunman had boarded her school bus in Pakistan's Swat Valley and shot her in the head at point-blank range. She survived. The Taliban had targeted her because she had been speaking publicly about girls' right to education since age 11, writing an anonymous diary for the BBC, and speaking to journalists despite Taliban threats.

The speech is short (about 1,200 words), formally structured, and extraordinarily controlled for a 16-year-old who had been nearly killed ten months before. She does not ask for sympathy. She argues that education is the tool that defeats extremism, that girls' education specifically is the most threatened and most powerful form of education, and that it is not the bullets of terrorists but books and pens that will change the world. She has since been awarded the Nobel Peace Prize (2014), the youngest recipient in history.

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.9](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r9) — The speech is an argument structured for a specific audience (the UN General Assembly and world media) and a specific purpose (mobilize international support for girls' education and pressure on the Pakistani government). Students analyze: What is her central claim? How does she use ethos (her own story as evidence), logos (the specific arguments for education), and pathos (the specific restraint of her emotional appeal — she refuses to be defined by victimhood)? What makes her rhetorical choices powerful?
- [C.1](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10c1) — The speech is one of the best contemporary models of formal presentation available. Malala uses: a clear claim stated in the opening, organized development with specific evidence, deliberate pacing, and a conclusion that returns to and deepens the opening image. She does not read from a full script. The video is essential here — the delivery is as important as the text.
- [W.4](../../standards/ela/writing-standards.md#9-10w4) — The speech models qualified argument: Malala explicitly addresses counterarguments ("I'm not against anyone. I'm not against the Taliban...") and defines her position in relation to them without weakening it. Use as a W.4 model for how to acknowledge opposing views without conceding ground.
- [R.7](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r7) — The speech's register shifts deliberately: she uses formal UN language ("I speak not for myself but for those without voice...") and then suddenly becomes very direct and specific ("They thought that the bullets would silence us. But they failed."). Students analyze how these shifts create rhetorical effect.

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.2](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md) — The Taliban's attack on Malala was a deliberate act of ideological enforcement: the Taliban's interpretation of Islamic law prohibited girls' education, and her advocacy was a direct threat to that ideology's hold on the Swat Valley. The speech addresses how religious extremist ideology functions as political control — and specifically how education threatens it. Connect to WH.6_12.2 skinnies on ideology.
- [WH.6_12.6](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md) — The speech is the most contemporary primary source in the library. Students connect it backward: to the UN UDHR's Article 26 (right to education), to the Cold War competition over "development" that shaped Pakistan's political landscape, to post-9/11 U.S. involvement in Afghanistan and Pakistan, to the broader question of how colonial and Cold War legacies shape present-day conflicts. The speech is a present-day document that requires historical context to fully understand.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Taliban | An Islamic fundamentalist political movement that ruled Afghanistan 1996–2001 and has operated as an insurgency in both Afghanistan and Pakistan's tribal regions since — their interpretation of Islamic law prohibits girls' education |
| Swat Valley | A region of northwestern Pakistan that the Pakistani Taliban controlled from 2007 to 2009 and targeted with violence through the early 2010s |
| Malala Day | July 12 — Malala's birthday, designated by the UN Youth Assembly as "Malala Day" after her 2013 speech |
| IDPs | Internally Displaced Persons — the 1.8 million people the Pakistani Army's military operation to retake Swat displaced; a context Malala references |
| Nobel Peace Prize | Awarded to Malala in 2014, shared with Indian children's rights activist Kailash Satyarthi — she was 17, the youngest Nobel laureate in history |

## Key Passages

**Opening — reframing her story:**
> I don't know why I am here. Why am I here today? Why am I speaking? I am here because I believe in the power of education. I am speaking today because I want to speak up for the right of education for every child.

**On the power of books:**
> The Taliban thought that the bullets would silence us. But they failed. And then, out of that silence came thousands of voices. The terrorists thought that they would change our aims and stop our ambitions but nothing changed in my life except this: weakness, fear and hopelessness died. Strength, power and courage was born.

**On her argument's core claim:**
> One child, one teacher, one book, one pen can change the world. Education is the only solution. Education first.

**On addressing her opponents:**
> I'm not against anyone. I'm not against the Taliban and any other terrorist group. I'm here to speak up for the right of education for every child. I want education for the sons and the daughters of all the extremists especially the Taliban.

**Why these passages matter:** The opening refuses to center her own survival — she immediately pivots to the argument. The second passage uses paradox (bullets created voices). The third is the thesis, compressed to a slogan. The fourth is the most rhetorically surprising move: she refuses to define herself by opposition, which removes the easy counter that she's just attacking the Taliban. Each choice is deliberate and analyzable.

## Studio Ideas

1. **"Why Education Threatens Power"** — EQ: What is it about education that makes authoritarian systems fear it? · Standard: WH.6_12.2 + R.9 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (using Malala's speech, Douglass, and Fahrenheit 451, construct an argument: What specifically is it about literacy and education that poses a threat to authoritarian control? Why does suppressing it come first?)

2. **"The Rhetoric of Refusal"** — EQ: What is more powerful — demanding rights or refusing to be defined by their denial? · Standard: R.9 + C.1 · Demo Mode: Portfolio Annotation (annotate the speech for every moment Malala refuses to be defined by victimhood or to define herself by opposition. What rhetorical effect does this create? Compare to MLK's Letter — does he do the same thing?)

3. **"Present Tense"** — EQ: Does knowing the history behind a current event change how you understand it? · Standard: WH.6_12.6 + IR.1–5 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (research the historical context of the Taliban, Pakistan, and the Swat Valley — including U.S. involvement in the region after 9/11. Write an argument: Does that historical context change how you understand the attack on Malala? Does it change what you think should be done?)

4. **"Deliver This"** — EQ: What does formal presentation under real stakes look like? · Standard: C.1 · Demo Mode: Multimedia/Performance (choose one paragraph from Malala's speech. Prepare and deliver it to an audience — matching her formal register, no script-reading, with a clear sense of what you want the audience to feel and do. Then reflect: What was hard? What did you notice about her delivery that you had to figure out how to replicate?)

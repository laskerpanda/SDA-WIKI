**Author:** Confucius (Kong Qiu); compiled by his students
**Type:** Primary Source · Philosophy / Governance Text
**Source:** Public domain — [Chinese Text Project](https://ctext.org/analects) and [Legge translation via Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/4094)
**Era/Period:** Era 1 — Ancient Civilizations
**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.2 · WH.6_12.3
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.4 · 9-10.R.9

## What This Text Is About

Confucius (551–479 BCE) was a Chinese philosopher, teacher, and political advisor who lived during the Spring and Autumn period — an era of fragmented Chinese states, constant warfare, and political instability. His teachings, compiled after his death by his students into the *Analects* (literally "edited conversations"), argue that good government rests not on law and punishment but on virtue, ritual, and the cultivation of character. If rulers and ministers embody virtue, subjects will follow. If they don't, no amount of law can create a stable society.

**Why this text belongs in the reading library:** The *Analects* represent one of the most influential political philosophies in human history — shaping Chinese governance for over 2,000 years, influencing Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese political culture, and producing a conception of the relationship between individual character and political order that is fundamentally different from the Greek, Roman, and Abrahamic traditions that dominate Western curricula. Reading it alongside Hammurabi's Code and Pericles' Funeral Oration — all responding to the same basic question (how do you make a society work?) — reveals how differently the ancient world answered that question.

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.4](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r4) — The *Analects* is an aphoristic text — its "main ideas" are not argued linearly but stated as wisdom observations. Students practice identifying theme from aphorism: What is Confucius' central claim about governance? About human nature? About learning? Identifying themes from scattered observations is a different R.4 skill than tracing a developed argument.
- [R.9](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r9) — The *Analects* implicitly argue against a competing theory of governance (legalism — the idea that strict law and punishment, not virtue, are what make states stable). Students identify this implicit argument and compare it to arguments made by other governance texts in the library.
- [R.2](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r2) — Many passages are compressed to the point of requiring inference: "He who learns but does not think is lost. He who thinks but does not learn is in great danger." Students practice unpacking aphoristic language — what exactly does this mean? What examples would support it?

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.2](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-1-civilizations.md) — Confucianism functions as both a philosophical system and a governing ideology — analogous to how Christianity shaped European medieval governance, or how Islam shaped the political order of the *dar al-Islam*. Students understand that the major civilizations of the ancient world each developed their own framework for how moral order and political order relate.
- [WH.6_12.3](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-1-civilizations.md) — Confucius argues for a meritocratic political order (the *junzi*, or "gentleman scholar," should govern by virtue, not birth) at the same historical moment Pericles argues for democratic participation in Athens. Comparing the two reveals two fundamentally different answers to the question "who should govern and why?"

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| *Ren* | Benevolence, humaneness, goodness — the central virtue in Confucian ethics; the quality a person must cultivate to govern well |
| *Li* | Ritual propriety — the correct performance of social roles and relationships; Confucius argues this is the foundation of social order |
| *Junzi* | The "gentleman" or "exemplary person" — not nobility by birth but a person of cultivated virtue; Confucius' model citizen and ruler |
| *Zhengming* | "Rectification of names" — Confucius' argument that social order requires that words correspond to realities: a ruler who doesn't rule justly shouldn't be called a ruler |
| Five Relationships | The five key social relationships in Confucian thought: ruler-subject, parent-child, husband-wife, elder-younger sibling, friend-friend. Each has defined duties flowing both ways. |

## Selected Passages

**On governance by virtue (Book 2):**
> He who exercises government by means of his virtue may be compared to the north polar star, which keeps its place and all the stars turn towards it.

**On self-cultivation (Book 1):**
> Is he not a man of complete virtue, who feels no discomposure though men may take no note of him?

**On learning (Book 2):**
> Learning without thought is labor lost; thought without learning is perilous.

**On the rectification of names (Book 13):**
> If names be not correct, language is not in accordance with the truth of things. If language be not in accordance with the truth of things, affairs cannot be accomplished... Therefore a superior man considers it necessary that the names he uses may be spoken appropriately, and also that what he speaks may be carried out appropriately.

**On the ruler's responsibility (Book 12):**
> The superior man is satisfied and composed; the mean man is always full of distress.
>
> If the people have no faith in their rulers, there is no standing for the state.

**On benevolence (Book 12):**
> Fan Ch'ih asked about benevolence. The Master said, "It is to love all men." He asked about knowledge. The Master said, "It is to know all men."

## Comparing Governance Philosophies

| Question | Hammurabi | Pericles | Confucius |
|---|---|---|---|
| **What makes a state stable?** | Clear laws and proportional punishment | Civic participation and democratic equality | Virtuous rulers and proper social relationships |
| **Who should govern?** | The king (by divine appointment) | Citizens (by democratic participation) | The most virtuous (by self-cultivation) |
| **What is the citizen's role?** | Obey and appeal to law | Participate in self-governance | Fulfill one's social role virtuously |
| **What corrects bad governance?** | The law applies to the king too | Democratic accountability | The ruler's shame and loss of legitimacy |
| **What it leaves out** | Democratic voice | Women, non-citizens, enslaved | Independent political rights |

## Studio Ideas

1. **"Three Answers to One Question"** — EQ: What makes a government legitimate? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + R.9 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (compare Confucius' theory of virtuous governance to Pericles' theory of democratic governance — both are answering the same question. Which is more convincing? Which is more practical? Which is more just? Argue a position with evidence from both texts)

2. **"Virtue vs. Law"** — EQ: Is a good society built by good rules or good people? · Standard: WH.6_12.2 + W.4 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (Confucius argues virtue; Hammurabi argues law. Write an argument for which theory better explains how societies actually work — use at least two historical examples)

3. **"The Rectification of Names"** — EQ: Does language create reality, or describe it? · Standard: R.4 + C.6 · Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation (Confucius argues that if a ruler doesn't rule justly, they shouldn't be called a ruler — "names" should correspond to reality. Apply this principle to a contemporary political example: Is there a case where what we call something doesn't match what it actually is? Does it matter?)

4. **"Confucianism 2,500 Years Later"** — EQ: Can a philosophy survive 2,500 years of history and still be relevant? · Standard: WH.6_12.6 + IR.1–5 · Demo Mode: Portfolio Annotation (research how Confucianism has been used — and misused — to justify governance in China from the Han dynasty to Xi Jinping. Annotate three uses: what part of Confucius is being invoked? Is it an accurate reading? What does it reveal about how powerful ideas get appropriated?)

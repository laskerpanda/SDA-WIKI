# Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)

**Author:** United Nations General Assembly (drafted by a committee chaired by Eleanor Roosevelt)
**Type:** Primary Source · Founding International Document
**Source:** [United Nations](https://www.un.org/en/about-us/universal-declaration-of-human-rights) — public domain

**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.3 · WH.6_12.6
**USH Standards:** US.6_12.1 · US.6_12.4
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.9 · 9-10.IR.1–5

## What This Text Is About

On December 10, 1948 — three years after the liberation of the Nazi concentration camps and the Nuremberg trials — the United Nations General Assembly adopted the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR). It was the first international document to assert that all human beings possess inherent rights regardless of nationality, ethnicity, religion, or sex — and that these rights are not granted by governments but are universal and inalienable. The UDHR contains 30 articles covering civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights. It was not a treaty (legally binding) but a declaration (statement of shared principle) — a distinction that has defined both its influence and its limits ever since.

The UDHR was drafted by representatives from countries across the ideological spectrum of the early Cold War, including the United States, the Soviet Union, France, China, and Lebanon. It passed 48–0, with 8 abstentions (including the Soviet bloc and Saudi Arabia). It has since become the foundation for more than 70 international human rights treaties and is the most translated document in human history.

## Read It
*Public domain — UN document. [Full text at UN.org](https://www.un.org/en/about-us/universal-declaration-of-human-rights).*

### Preamble
> The General Assembly,

> Proclaims this Universal Declaration of Human Rights as a common standard of achievement for all peoples and all nations, to the end that every individual and every organ of society, keeping this Declaration constantly in mind, shall strive by teaching and education to promote respect for these rights and freedoms and by progressive measures, national and international, to secure their universal and effective recognition and observance, both among the peoples of Member States themselves and among the peoples of territories under their jurisdiction.

>

### Articles 1–10 — Core Rights
> **Article 1.** All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights. They are endowed with reason and conscience and should act towards one another in a spirit of brotherhood.
>
> **Article 2.** Everyone is entitled to all the rights and freedoms set forth in this Declaration, without distinction of any kind, such as race, colour, sex, language, religion, political or other opinion, national or social origin, property, birth or other status.
>
> **Article 3.** Everyone has the right to life, liberty and security of person.
>
> **Article 4.** No one shall be held in slavery or servitude; slavery and the slave trade shall be prohibited in all their forms.
>
> **Article 5.** No one shall be subjected to torture or to cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment.
>
> **Article 6.** Everyone has the right to recognition everywhere as a person before the law.
>
> **Article 7.** All are equal before the law and are entitled without any discrimination to equal protection of the law. All are entitled to equal protection against any discrimination in violation of this Declaration and against any incitement to such discrimination.
>
> **Article 8.** Everyone has the right to an effective remedy by the competent national tribunals for acts violating the fundamental rights granted him by the constitution or by law.
>
> **Article 9.** No one shall be subjected to arbitrary arrest, detention or exile.
>

### Articles 18–21 — Rights of Conscience and Political Life
> **Article 18.** Everyone has the right to freedom of thought, conscience and religion; this right includes freedom to change his religion or belief, and freedom, either alone or in community with others and in public or private, to manifest his religion or belief in teaching, practice, worship and observance.
>
> **Article 19.** Everyone has the right to freedom of opinion and expression; this right includes freedom to hold opinions without interference and to seek, receive and impart information and ideas through any media and regardless of frontiers.
>
> **Article 20.** (1) Everyone has the right to peaceful assembly and association.  
> (2) No one may be compelled to belong to an association.
>
> **Article 21.** (1) Everyone has the right to take part in the government of his country, directly or through freely chosen representatives.  
> (2) Everyone has the right of equal access to public service in his country.  
> (3) The will of the people shall be the basis of the authority of government; this will shall be expressed in periodic and genuine elections which shall be by universal and equal suffrage and shall be held by secret vote or by equivalent free voting procedures.

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.9](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r9) — The UDHR is an argumentative document structured as a declaration: its Preamble makes the case for why universal rights are necessary; its articles specify what those rights are. Students analyze the argument structure of the Preamble — what assumptions does it make? What historical events is it responding to? Compare the rhetorical approach to the French Declaration of the Rights of Man (1789) and the Haitian Declaration (1804).
- [IR.1–5](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-priority.md) — The UDHR is the natural anchor document for any research project on human rights, international law, decolonization, or Cold War politics. Every article is a potential research question: How well has Article X been protected? Which countries have violated it? What enforcement mechanisms exist?
- [W.4](../../standards/ela/writing-standards.md#9-10w4) — The Preamble is a model of argument-by-historical-premise: each "Whereas" clause establishes a premise; the conclusion follows from the accumulated premises. Students use this structure as a W.4 model.

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.3](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md) — The UDHR represents the post-WWII attempt to build an international order based on rights rather than power alone — the direct institutional response to the Melian logic that enabled fascism, imperialism, and genocide. Students trace the lineage: Four Freedoms Speech → Atlantic Charter → UN Charter → UDHR.
- [WH.6_12.6](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md) — The UDHR is the living document most directly relevant to the present. Every contemporary human rights debate — refugee rights, LGBTQ+ rights, economic rights, climate displacement — involves specific UDHR articles. Students research which articles are honored, which are violated, and what enforcement looks like (or doesn't).
- [US.6_12.1](../../standards/social-studies/us-history/unit-5-cold-war.md) — The UDHR was partly a Cold War document: the U.S. pushed civil and political rights; the Soviet Union pushed economic and social rights. This debate shaped the Cold War ideological competition and explains why the UDHR covers both.
- [US.6_12.4](../../standards/social-studies/us-history/unit-6-civil-rights.md) — Civil rights activists, including MLK, explicitly invoked the UDHR in arguing that the U.S. was violating its stated international commitments. The UDHR was a weapon in the domestic civil rights argument.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Inalienable rights | Rights that cannot be taken away or transferred — not granted by governments but inherent in human personhood |
| Declaration vs. treaty | A declaration is a statement of principle; a treaty is legally binding. The UDHR is a declaration — it carries moral authority but no enforcement mechanism |
| Eleanor Roosevelt | Chair of the UN Human Rights Commission that drafted the UDHR; instrumental in its scope and passage |
| Abstention | Choosing not to vote — the 8 nations that abstained (Soviet bloc, Saudi Arabia, South Africa) did not oppose but refused to endorse |
| Ratification | The formal adoption of an international treaty by a national government — subsequent human rights treaties (ICCPR, ICESCR) were treaties requiring ratification |

## Selected Articles

**Preamble:**
> Whereas recognition of the inherent dignity and of the equal and inalienable rights of all members of the human family is the foundation of freedom, justice and peace in the world...
>
> Whereas disregard and contempt for human rights have resulted in barbarous acts which have outraged the conscience of mankind, and the advent of a world in which human beings shall enjoy freedom of speech and belief and freedom from fear and want has been proclaimed as the highest aspiration of the common people...

**Article 1:**
> All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights. They are endowed with reason and conscience and should act towards one another in a spirit of brotherhood.

**Article 3:**
> Everyone has the right to life, liberty and security of person.

**Article 25:**
> Everyone has the right to a standard of living adequate for the health and well-being of himself and of his family, including food, clothing, housing and medical care and necessary social services, and the right to security in the event of unemployment, sickness, disability, widowhood, old age or other lack of livelihood in circumstances beyond his control.

**Article 26:**
> Everyone has the right to education. Education shall be free, at least in the elementary and fundamental stages...

**Why these articles matter:** Article 1 is the philosophical foundation. Article 3 is the minimum protection that every other article builds on. Articles 25 and 26 are the most contested: they claim economic and social rights — which the U.S. has historically resisted as properly governmental obligations. These are still live arguments.

## The Rights Chain in the Reading Library

The UDHR is the culmination of a chain of rights documents traceable through the library:

| Document | Year | What it contributed |
|---|---|---|
| [Hammurabi's Code](./hammurabi-code.md) | c. 1754 BCE | Law as protection against arbitrary power |
| [Pericles' Funeral Oration](./pericles-funeral-oration.md) | 431 BCE | Civic rights within democracy |
| [Magna Carta](./magna-carta.md) | 1215 | Limits on sovereign power; due process |
| [Common Sense](./common-sense-paine.md) | 1776 | Self-governance as a right |
| [Declaration of Rights of Man](./declaration-rights-man.md) | 1789 | Universal rights as political foundation |
| [Haitian Declaration](./haitian-declaration-independence.md) | 1804 | Rights extended to formerly enslaved people |
| [FDR Four Freedoms](./fdr-four-freedoms.md) | 1941 | Freedom framework for international order |
| **UDHR** | **1948** | **Universal codification of all prior frameworks** |

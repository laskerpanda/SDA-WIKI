# Pericles' Funeral Oration (431 BCE)

**Author:** Pericles (as recorded by Thucydides, *History of the Peloponnesian War*, Book II)
**Type:** Primary Source · Political Speech
**Source:** Public domain — multiple translations available; [MIT Classics Archive](http://classics.mit.edu/Thucydides/pelopwar.2.second.html) recommended
**Era/Period:** Era 1 — Ancient Civilizations
**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.3 · WH.6_12.4 · WH.6_12.6
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.9 · 9-10.W.4 · 9-10.C.1

---

## What This Text Is About

In the winter of 431 BCE, during the first year of the Peloponnesian War between Athens and Sparta, the Athenian general and statesman Pericles delivered a funeral oration honoring the first soldiers killed in the war. The speech — recorded (and likely shaped) by the historian Thucydides — is not primarily about the dead soldiers. It is a defense of Athenian democracy itself: what it is, why it is worth dying for, and what makes Athens different from Sparta and every other city-state. It is the earliest and arguably greatest argument for democracy as a form of government in Western history. Abraham Lincoln knew it well when he wrote the Gettysburg Address. It is also, read alongside the Melian Dialogue, part of a devastating two-text portrait of what Athenian democracy looked like from the inside versus what it did to others.

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.9](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r9) — The oration is a formal public argument: its claim is that Athens is worth defending because of what it represents, not just what it is. Students map the argumentative structure: What is Pericles' thesis? What evidence does he offer? Who is his audience? What does he want them to believe and do?
- [W.4](../../standards/ela/writing-standards.md#9-10w4) — The speech is a model for argument from values: Pericles doesn't argue that Athens is winning or that the war is going well — he argues that the values Athens represents are worth the cost. Use as a W.4 model for argument grounded in principle rather than outcome.
- [C.1](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10c1) — A foundational model of formal public oratory: clear audience, clear purpose, deliberate structure, and a conclusion that calls the audience to action. One of the most analyzed speeches in the history of rhetoric.
- [R.7](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r7) — Pericles' style choices: the move from praise of ancestors → praise of Athens → specific democratic values → praise of the soldiers → call to action. The deliberate structure builds toward the emotional conclusion.

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.3](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-1-civilizations.md) — The oration is the defining primary source document for understanding Athenian democracy: what Athenians believed democracy meant, how they distinguished it from oligarchy and tyranny, and why they considered it the superior form of government. Every subsequent argument for democracy — including the U.S. Declaration and Common Sense — draws on this tradition.
- [WH.6_12.4](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-1-civilizations.md) — The speech reveals Athenian values: openness, meritocracy, civic participation, the relationship between private life and public duty. It also reveals what Athenians did NOT include in their democracy (women, enslaved people, non-citizens) — a gap the oration carefully avoids mentioning.
- [WH.6_12.6](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-1-civilizations.md) — The Funeral Oration is the beginning of the democratic tradition that runs through Magna Carta, Common Sense, the French Declaration, and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. Students trace this lineage through the reading library.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Polis | The Greek city-state — the political and civic community; the unit of political life in ancient Greece |
| Democracy | From Greek *demos* (people) + *kratos* (rule) — government by the people; in Athens, direct participation of (male, citizen) residents in political decisions |
| Oligarchy | Rule by a small group — the Spartan model Pericles implicitly contrasts with Athenian democracy |
| Meritocracy | The idea that civic standing should be based on ability and contribution, not birth or wealth — Pericles argues this is an Athenian distinction |
| Thucydides | The Athenian historian who recorded the speech — note that he likely reconstructed it from memory and his own understanding of what Pericles would have said; the speech is Pericles *through* Thucydides |

## Key Passages

> Our constitution does not copy the laws of neighbouring states; we are rather a pattern to others than imitators ourselves. Its administration favours the many instead of the few; this is why it is called a democracy. If we look to the laws, they afford equal justice to all in their private differences; if no social standing, advancement in public life falls to reputation for capacity, class considerations not being allowed to interfere with merit...
> — *History of the Peloponnesian War*, Book II, trans. Crawley

> We are lovers of the beautiful, yet with economy, and we cultivate the mind without loss of manliness. Wealth we employ, not for talk and ostentation, but when there is a real use for it. To avow poverty with us is no disgrace; the true disgrace is in doing nothing to avoid it...
> — *History of the Peloponnesian War*, Book II, trans. Crawley

> Fix your eyes on the greatness of Athens as you have it before you day by day, fall in love with her, and when you feel her great, remember that this greatness was won by men with courage, with knowledge of their duty, and with a sense of honour in action...
> — *History of the Peloponnesian War*, Book II, trans. Crawley

**Why these passages matter:** The first passage articulates the democratic principle that still drives political argument today — merit over class. The second complicates it: Pericles praises Athens for combining beauty and economy, culture and toughness — a civic ideal that is both inspiring and exclusive. The third is the call to action, and it is the passage Lincoln echoed at Gettysburg.

## Read This Alongside: The Melian Dialogue

**This text should never be read alone.** It pairs with the [Melian Dialogue](./melian-dialogue.md) — also from Thucydides, fifteen years later.

In the Funeral Oration, Pericles argues that Athens is great because it is just and open, a model for others. In the Melian Dialogue, Athenian envoys tell the people of the small island of Melos: "The strong do what they can; the weak suffer what they must." The Athens that destroyed Melos was the same democracy Pericles praised.

The question this pair forces: **Is the Funeral Oration a description of what Athens was, an aspiration for what it should be, or a justification for what it actually did?**

## Studio Ideas

1. **"What Is Democracy For?"** — EQ: Is democracy the best system of government, and who gets to define 'best'? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + R.9 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (analyze Pericles' argument for democracy — what is his evidence? What does he leave out? Would his argument convince someone who didn't already live in Athens?)

2. **"The Gettysburg Connection"** — EQ: Can the same argument be used by a democracy that excludes people? · Standard: WH.6_12.6 + W.4 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (compare Pericles' Funeral Oration to Lincoln's Gettysburg Address — same argument, 2,000 years apart. What stays the same? What changes? Both speeches exclude someone: who, and does it matter?)

3. **"Two Faces of Athens"** — EQ: Is a democracy that acts like a tyrant still a democracy? · Standard: WH.6_12.3 + WH.6_12.5 + R.9 · Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation (read both the Funeral Oration and the Melian Dialogue. Is the Athens that praises openness and justice the same Athens that tells Melos "the strong do what they can"? How do you hold both?)

4. **"Values We Claim vs. Values We Practice"** — EQ: Does a nation's stated values matter if its actions contradict them? · Standard: WH.6_12.6 + C.1 · Demo Mode: Multimedia/Performance (create a 3-minute presentation that uses both the Funeral Oration and the Melian Dialogue as evidence for a claim about the gap between democratic ideals and imperial behavior — in ancient Athens or today)

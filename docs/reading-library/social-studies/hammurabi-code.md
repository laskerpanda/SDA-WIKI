# The Code of Hammurabi (c. 1754 BCE)

**Author:** Hammurabi, King of Babylon (written by royal scribes)
**Type:** Primary Source
**Source:** [Yale Avalon Project](https://avalon.law.yale.edu/ancient/hamframe.asp) — public domain
**Era/Period:** Era 1 — Civilizations and Religions
**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.2 · WH.6_12.3

---

## What This Text Is About

The Code of Hammurabi is one of the oldest written legal codes in recorded history, carved into an 8-foot stone stele around 1754 BCE in ancient Babylon. It contains 282 laws governing everything from trade and wages to family life, slavery, and punishment — organized around the principle that the strong shall not oppress the weak.

## Key Passage

> If a man puts out the eye of another man, his eye shall be put out.
> If he breaks another man's bone, his bone shall be broken.
> But if he puts out the eye of a freed man or breaks the bone of a freed man, he shall pay one gold mina.
> If he puts out the eye of a man's slave or breaks a bone of a man's slave, he shall pay one-half of its value.
> — Laws 196–199

This passage directly shows the tiered justice system of Babylonian society: the punishment for harming someone depends entirely on their social class. A nobleman's eye equals an eye; a slave's eye equals money.

## What This Text Is About

*Common Sense* is a 47-page pamphlet published on January 10, 1776, that made the case for American independence from Britain in plain, forceful language. Thomas Paine wrote it for ordinary people — not lawyers or politicians — and it became the best-selling printed work per capita in American history. Paine argued that monarchy is an irrational and corrupt system, that hereditary rule has no legitimate basis, and that establishing an independent republic was both possible and necessary.

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.9](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r9) — *Common Sense* is one of the most carefully constructed argumentative texts in the American canon. Students can map Paine's claims, identify his evidence (historical examples, biblical critique, economic argument), and evaluate his reasoning. Is his argument logical? What emotional appeals does he use?
- [R.7](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r7) — Paine's word choices are deliberate and inflammatory. "A long habit of not thinking a thing wrong gives it a superficial appearance of being right." Close reading of his rhetorical strategies (repetition, sarcasm, plain speech as persuasion technique).
- [W.4](../../standards/ela/writing-standards.md#9-10w4) — The gold standard for argumentative writing. Students can use Paine as a mentor text: How does he set up his claim? What's his structure? How does he handle counterarguments?
- [W.3](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10w3) — Extended informational writing: Explain Paine's argument and analyze why it was effective for its audience.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Monarchy | A government ruled by a single hereditary ruler (king or queen) |
| Hereditary | Passed down through bloodlines — the basis of monarchy Paine attacks |
| Republic | A government in which power rests with elected representatives and citizens |
| Pamphlet | A short printed booklet — the social media of the 18th century, cheap to produce and easy to distribute |
| Natural rights | Rights that belong to all people by nature, not granted by government (life, liberty, property) |

## Key Passage

> Society in every state is a blessing, but government even in its best state is but a necessary evil; in its worst state an intolerable one... Government, like dress, is the badge of lost innocence; the palaces of kings are built on the ruins of the bowers of paradise.
> — *Common Sense*, Introduction

And on monarchy specifically:

> Of more worth is one honest man to society and in the sight of God than all the crowned ruffians that ever lived.
> — *Common Sense*, Part II

The first passage shows Paine's Enlightenment framework: government is a concession to human weakness, not a natural good. The second shows his willingness to attack monarchy in directly contemptuous language — unusual and inflammatory for 1776.

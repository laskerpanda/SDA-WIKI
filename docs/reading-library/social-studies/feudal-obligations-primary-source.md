## What This Text Is About

The feudal system organized medieval European society around a hierarchy of obligations: kings granted land to lords, lords granted land to knights, and at the bottom of the chain, serfs worked the land and owed labor, goods, and loyalty to their lord. This entry collects three short primary sources that show feudalism from multiple angles — an oath ceremony, a manor survey, and a list of serf obligations — so students can see how the system actually worked in practice, not just in theory.

### Document 1 — Oath of Fealty and Homage (c. 1127)
#### Recorded by Galbert of Bruges, *De Multro* (On the Murder of Charles the Good)

*Galbert of Bruges was a notary who witnessed and recorded the ceremonies of homage at the court of William of Clito, Count of Flanders, in 1127. This is one of the most detailed surviving accounts of how the feudal ceremony actually worked.*

> First they did their homage thus. The count asked if he was willing to become completely his man, and the other replied, "I am willing"; and with clasped hands, surrounded by the hands of the count, they were bound together by a kiss.
>
> Second, he who had done homage gave his fealty to the representative of the count in these words: "I promise on my faith that I will in future be faithful to count William, and will loyally observe my homage to him completely against all persons in good faith and without deceit."
>
> Third, he took this oath upon the relics of the saints. Afterward, with a little rod which the count held in his hand, he gave investiture to all those who by this agreement had given their security and homage and accompanying oath.

### Document 2 — The Extent of Bernehorne (1307)
#### A Manor Survey from Sussex, England

*The "Extent" was an official survey of what a manor owed and what its people were required to do. This excerpt records the specific obligations of a villein (serf) named John of Cayworth at Bernehorne manor. Note how precisely each obligation is recorded — this was a legal document, not a description.*

> John of Cayworth holds one house and thirty acres of land, and owes yearly 2 shillings at Easter and Michaelmas.
>
> And he ought to harrow for two days at the Lenten sowing with one man and his own horse and his own harrow, the value of the work being 4d; and he is to receive from the lord on each day 3 meals, of the value of 5d, and then the lord will be at a loss of 1d. Thus his harrowing is of no value to the service of the lord.
>
> And he ought to carry the manure of the lord for two days with one cart, with his own two oxen, the value of the work being 8d; and he is to receive from the lord each day 3 meals of the price as above; and thus the service is worth 3d clear.
>
> And he ought to carry wood from the woods of the lord to the manor house for two days in summer, with a cart and three animals of his own, the value of the work being 9d. And he receives from the lord each day 3 meals of the above price; and thus the work is worth 4d clear.
>
> And he ought to find one man for two days to mow the meadow of the lord, who can mow, by estimation, one acre and a half; the value of mowing an acre being 6d; and thus the total value of this mowing is 9d. And he is to receive each day 3 meals of the above price; and thus the mowing is worth 4d clear.
>
> And he ought to gather and carry that same hay for one day, the value of the work being 3d. And he has 3 meals of the above price.
>
> And he ought to carry the hay of the lord for one day with a cart and three animals of his own, the price of the work being 6d. And he receives from the lord 3 meals of the price of 5d. And thus the work is worth 1d clear.
>
> And he ought to help to thresh in the autumn for two days, with two men, the value of the work being 10d. And he is to receive from the lord 3 meals of the above price for each man. And thus the threshing is worth no more than 2d clear.
>
> The total of his works is... 2 shillings, 11 pence.

### Document 3 — Obligations of a Serf (c. 1280)
#### From an English Manor Survey

*This document lists what a serf named Richard, son of Anketill, owed his lord at a typical English manor. Unlike the precise accounting of Bernehorne above, this reads more as a general description of yearly obligations.*

> Richard, son of Anketill, holds a half-virgate of land and owes for it yearly:
>
> He shall plow one acre in winter, and one in Lent, and one in summer.
>
> He shall reap for the lord in August for three days with his entire family, except his wife; and each day he shall receive a loaf of bread from the lord.
>
> He shall carry the lord's grain to market on his cart twice a year.
>
> He shall pay at Christmas 2 hens. At Easter, one egg for every hen he keeps, or a halfpenny.
>
> When he marries off a daughter, he shall pay the lord merchet — a fee for permission.
>
> When he dies, the lord shall have his best beast as heriot.
>
> He may not leave the land without the lord's permission. He may not sell his ox, his horse, or his cow without permission. He may not send his son to school or to be ordained as a priest without the lord's license.
>
> All these things he owes, and he holds his land at the will of the lord.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Fealty | The loyalty sworn by a vassal to his lord; the promise to be faithful and not to betray |
| Homage | The formal ceremony in which a vassal kneels before his lord, places his hands in the lord's hands, and swears allegiance |
| Investiture | The formal granting of a fief (land or office) by a lord to a vassal, symbolized by a rod or staff |
| Villein (serf) | A peasant legally bound to the land; not a slave (they could not be sold), but not free — they could not leave the land, marry, or educate their children without the lord's permission |
| Virgate | A unit of land roughly equal to 30 acres — the standard holding for a serf family |
| Heriot | The "death tax" — when a serf died, the lord took his best animal as payment |
| Merchet | A fee paid to the lord when a serf's daughter married; the lord's acknowledgment that even marriage required his permission |
| Manor | The lord's estate — the basic economic and social unit of medieval Europe, including the lord's house, the village, the fields, and the church |

## Key Passage

> He may not leave the land without the lord's permission. He may not sell his ox, his horse, or his cow without permission. He may not send his son to school or to be ordained as a priest without the lord's license.
> He holds his land at the will of the lord.
> — Document 3

This is the most important line in the collection: "he holds his land at the will of the lord." The serf does not own the land. He is not a slave — but his freedom is entirely conditional on a lord who could, in theory, remove him at any time. The phrase captures the fundamental insecurity of medieval peasant life.

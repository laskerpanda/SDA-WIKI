# "Dulce et Decorum Est" (1920)

**Author:** Wilfred Owen
**Type:** Primary Source · War Poetry
**Source:** Public domain — first published posthumously, 1920
**Full Text:** [Poetry Foundation](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poems/46560/dulce-et-decorum-est)
**Era/Period:** Era 4 — Age of Global War and Globalization
**WH Standards:** WH.6_12.2 · WH.6_12.5
**ELA Standards:** 9-10.R.4 · 9-10.R.7 · 9-10.R.8

## What This Text Is About

Wilfred Owen was a British Army officer who served in the trenches of World War I and was killed in action on November 4, 1918 — one week before the Armistice. He was 25. "Dulce et Decorum Est" was drafted in 1917 and published posthumously in 1920. It is widely considered the greatest anti-war poem in the English language.

The poem describes a gas attack on Owen's trench unit. In vivid, deliberately ugly language, Owen shows a soldier who fails to get his gas mask on in time — dying slowly while Owen can do nothing but watch. The title and final lines are a direct attack on the Latin phrase *Dulce et decorum est pro patria mori* — "It is sweet and fitting to die for one's country" — a quote from Horace used extensively in WWI-era propaganda and recruitment materials. Owen calls it "the old Lie."

This poem is a primary source for understanding how WWI was experienced by combatants, how WWI propaganda worked, and why the disillusionment of the 1920s ran so deep. It is also one of the best short texts in the library for R.7 (style and word choice) and R.8 (literary elements).

## Read It

*Full text — public domain. First published posthumously 1920.*

> Bent double, like old beggars under sacks,
> Knock-kneed, coughing like hags, we cursed through sludge,
> Till on the haunting flares we turned our backs,
> And towards our distant rest began to trudge.
> Men marched asleep. Many had lost their boots,
> But limped on, blood-shod. All went lame; all blind;
> Drunk with fatigue; deaf even to the hoots
> Of gas-shells dropping softly behind.
>
> Gas! GAS! Quick, boys!—An ecstasy of fumbling
> Fitting the clumsy helmets just in time,
> But someone still was yelling out and stumbling
> And flound'ring like a man in fire or lime.—
> Dim through the misty panes and thick green light,
> As under a green sea, I saw him drowning.
>
> In all my dreams before my helpless sight,
> He plunges at me, guttering, choking, drowning.
>
> If in some smothering dreams, you too could pace
> Behind the wagon that we flung him in,
> And watch the white eyes writhing in his face,
> His hanging face, like a devil's face of sin;
> If you could hear, at every jolt, the blood
> Come gargling from the froth-corrupted lungs,
> Obscene as cancer, bitter as the cud
> Of vile, incurable sores on innocent tongues,—
> My friend, you would not tell with such high zest
> To children ardent for some desperate glory,
> The old Lie: *Dulce et decorum est*
> *Pro patria mori.*

## ELA Standards This Connects To

- [R.7](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r7) — Owen's word choices are deliberate and precise. He uses clinical medical vocabulary ("guttering, choking, drowning"), archaic words that evoke heroic poetry ("guttering" echoes the sound of dying candles in old literature), and visceral physical language side by side — to force readers to experience the physical reality of gas warfare while aware they're in a poem. Students trace specific word choices and explain why Owen chose them.
- [R.8](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r8) — The poem's extended simile ("as under a green sea") transforms the gas cloud into something vast and drowning — the soldier is not just dying from poison but being swallowed by an unnatural ocean. The nightmare vision in lines 17–24 introduces the speaker as the poem's most complex character: a witness who can neither help nor stop watching. Students analyze the interaction of imagery, speaker's POV, and tone.
- [R.4](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r4) — The poem's claim (stated explicitly in the final lines) is that "the old Lie" of glorious death for one's country is a lie — and that anyone who knew what combat actually looked like would not tell it to children. Students identify the claim and trace how every earlier image in the poem builds toward it.
- [R.9](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md#9-10r9) — Owen is arguing against a specific text: the Horace quote used in propaganda. The poem is a direct rhetorical rebuttal — matching the propaganda's register (Latin, classical allusion) and then destroying it with what the speaker has actually seen. Use alongside the Kipling/Johnson pair for a sequence on propaganda vs. counter-testimony.

## Social Studies Standards This Connects To

- [WH.6_12.2](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md) — WWI was accompanied by the most extensive state propaganda campaign in history to that point. Governments used classical education — Horace, patriotic verse, heroic imagery — to construct the ideology of glorious sacrifice. Owen's poem directly attacks this ideology using the same classical tools. Students understand how ideology is constructed and how it can be dismantled.
- [WH.6_12.5](../../standards/social-studies/world-history/era-4-global-war.md) — The poem documents a specific military technology (mustard gas) and its effects — one of the causes of WWI's unprecedented casualty rates and one of the reasons combatants' experience was so different from civilian understanding of the war. The gap between the home front's understanding and the trenches' reality is a cause of the post-war disillusionment that shaped the 1920s and contributed to WWII's ideological context.

## Analysis Notes

**Structure:** The poem is in two parts — the march (exhaustion, dehumanization), then the gas attack (panic, death, aftermath) — followed by a two-line bridge and a final stanza that turns outward to address the reader directly.

**The most important move in the poem:** Lines 17–18. "If in some smothering dreams, you too could pace / Behind the wagon that we flung him in..." — Owen breaks the fourth wall. The poem stops being a personal memory and becomes a direct challenge: *if you could see what I saw, you would not repeat the lie*. The "you" addressed in the final stanza is the reader. This is how the poem transforms a witness account into a political argument.

**"The old Lie":** Horace's phrase was quoted in British classrooms, recruitment posters, newspaper editorials, and letters home from officers. It was the specific ideological claim Owen was writing against. By naming it at the end — in Latin, italicized — he exposes it as a lie in the same register it was told as truth.

## Studio Ideas

1. **"Counter the Lie"** — EQ: Is it ever right to tell young people that dying for their country is glorious? · Standard: WH.6_12.2 + R.9 · Demo Mode: Written Assessment (analyze Owen's argument against "the old Lie" — is it convincing? What would a recruiter in 1917 say in response? Write Owen's argument and the strongest counterargument, then evaluate which is more honest)

2. **"What War Actually Looks Like"** — EQ: Why does the reality of war keep being hidden from people who haven't experienced it? · Standard: WH.6_12.5 + R.7 · Demo Mode: Portfolio Annotation (annotate the poem line by line for word choice — for each image, explain: what does Owen choose to show? What does he choose to say explicitly vs. leave as image? Why?)

3. **"Propaganda vs. Testimony"** — EQ: Whose version of an event should we trust — the institution that organized it or the person who survived it? · Standard: WH.6_12.2 + IR.4 · Demo Mode: Verbal Conversation (find a WWI-era British recruitment poster that uses the Horace quote or similar language. Compare the poster's claim to Owen's poem — same event, completely different account. Which is a more reliable source? For what question?)

4. **"The Gap Between Home and Front"** — EQ: How did WWI create a generation that couldn't talk to civilians? · Standard: WH.6_12.5 + R.8 · Demo Mode: Extended Writing (research the concept of "shell shock" and the post-war silence about combat experience. Using Owen's poem as evidence, write an argument: What does the gap between combatant experience and civilian understanding cost a society?)

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Owen wrote "Dulce et Decorum Est" to counter the propaganda being used to recruit young men into a war that was killing them in industrial quantities — the Latin phrase "it is sweet and fitting to die for one's country" is the lie he is calling out. The poem matters today because the gap between how war is depicted in political speech, recruitment materials, and patriotic narrative versus what soldiers actually experience is still the same gap. Veterans' suicide rates, PTSD rates, and the long-term physical and psychological costs of service are poorly represented in the rhetoric used to justify sending people to war. Owen is not anti-soldier — he is pro-honesty about what you are asking soldiers to do and what it costs them. Every debate about military intervention, veteran care, the use of war as a foreign policy tool, and the moral obligations of a society to the people it sends into combat runs through the question Owen raised: are we being told the truth about what this costs?

**Grade 9 · World History Era 4 + ELA 9-10**
**Span:** 1900–present · WWI, WWII, Cold War, Decolonization, Globalization

## Project Ideas — Era 4

---

### 1. "What does a society's fiction reveal about its fears?"
**Essential question:** When a society produces a specific kind of story over and over, what does that tell us about what it's afraid of?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.2 + Era 4
**ELA Standard:** R.8 + W.4
**Source anchors:** *1984* + *Fahrenheit 451* + CrashCourse ep-42 (Cold War / McCarthyism)
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Multimedia
**Possible deliverables:** Analytical essay · Podcast episode · Video essay comparing both novels
**The argument:** Orwell and Bradbury were both writing in the early Cold War, both afraid of the same thing (totalitarian conformism). Students use R.8 to analyze the literary elements each author uses to embody that fear, and W.4 to argue which fear was more prescient — or to argue something about fear and fiction itself. WH.6_12.2 content throughout.
*See studio packet: [USH-02 Literature of Fear](../../units/studio-archive/ush-02-literature-of-fear.md)*

---

### 2. "What did 'never again' actually require?"
**Essential question:** After the Holocaust, the world said "never again" — what would that actually have required, and did anyone do it?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.5–6 + Era 4
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchors:** *Night* (Wiesel) + UN UDHR + Malala UN Speech
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Argument essay · Debate · Documentary
**The argument:** Wiesel's memoir shows what the Holocaust cost one person. The UDHR shows what the world promised in response. Malala shows what "never again" required 65 years later. Students read all three (R.9 + R.8) and write an argument about whether "never again" has worked — and what it actually demands.

---

### 3. "Whose stories get told after a war?"
**Essential question:** History is written by those who survive — but who among the survivors gets to write it?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.5 + Era 4
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4 or W.3
**Source anchors:** *Night* (Wiesel) + CrashCourse ep-41 (WWII Part 2) + Dulce et Decorum Est
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Multimedia
**Possible deliverables:** Essay · Documentary · Podcast
**The argument:** Green's WWII episode tells the structural story. Owen's poem tells it from the trench. Wiesel's memoir tells it from a camp. Students compare all three narrative positions (R.9 for the argumentative texts, R.8 for the literary ones) and write an argument about what gets lost when we rely on a single kind of narrator for history.

---

### 4. "Is it possible to resist a totalitarian state?"
**Essential question:** When a government controls everything — information, movement, who is human — what can an ordinary person actually do?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.2–3 + Era 4
**ELA Standard:** R.8 + W.4
**Source anchors:** *1984* or *Persepolis* + Mandela Rivonia Speech
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Essay · Podcast · Research presentation
**The argument:** Winston Smith (fictional) and Mandela (historical) both resist totalitarian states. Students compare their strategies and contexts using R.8 (for *1984* or *Persepolis*) and R.9 (for Mandela's speech), then write an argument about what conditions make resistance possible. WH.6_12.2–3 throughout.

---

### 5. "What did WWI destroy that never came back?"
**Essential question:** Some losses from a war are physical. What about the losses that can't be rebuilt?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.5 + Era 4
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.3 or W.4
**Source anchors:** Dulce et Decorum Est + CrashCourse ep-38 (WWI)
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Multimedia
**Possible deliverables:** Essay · Annotated poem analysis + historical context piece · Podcast
**The argument:** Green argues WWI destroyed the Enlightenment faith in progress. Owen's poem documents what the killing looked like from inside. Students read both (R.9 for the argumentative essay framework, R.8 elements for the poem) and write either an explanatory piece (W.3) on what the war destroyed culturally, or an argument (W.4) on whether that loss was necessary.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Era 4 is the era we're still living in. The global order built after World War II — the United Nations, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the International Criminal Court, NATO, the nuclear deterrence framework — is the world students are inheriting. And it is under more stress right now than at any point since it was built. The questions this era raises aren't history: What do you do when a nuclear-armed country invades a neighbor? What happens when democratic governments start using authoritarian tactics? Who enforces human rights when the countries that wrote the rules stop following them? Elie Wiesel wrote *Night* because he believed that if enough people understood what happened, it could never happen again. The ongoing debates about genocide prevention, refugee rights, and international accountability are the test of whether he was right. See [WH-05: The Cost of War](../../units/studio-archive/wh-05-the-cost-of-war.md) and the [ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk](../../reading-library/crosswalk/crosswalk-ela-ss.md) for the deepest connections between this era's texts and today.

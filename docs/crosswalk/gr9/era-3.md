# Era 3 Crosswalk — Age of Revolutions

**Grade 9 · World History Era 3 + ELA 9-10**
**Span:** 1400–1900 · Exploration, Enlightenment, Atlantic Revolutions, Industrialization, Imperialism

!!! tip "Start here — one Demo, two standards"
    **WH.6_12.3 + R.9 + W.4** — Era 3 is the richest crosswalk era. Pick *any* text from the Reading Library table below — [Common Sense](../../reading-library/social-studies/common-sense-paine.md), [Wollstonecraft](../../reading-library/social-studies/wollstonecraft-vindication.md), [Douglass](../../reading-library/social-studies/narrative-frederick-douglass.md), or [Kipling+Johnson](../../reading-library/social-studies/kipling-white-mans-burden.md). Analyze the argument (R.9). Write your own position (W.4). Each one already is WH.6_12.3 primary source evidence. Use the [WH.6_12.3 Skinny](../../skinnies/world-history/era-3/wh-6-12-3-political-systems-era3-skinny.md) or the [Common Sense + W.4 Crosswalk](../../reading-library/crosswalk/common-sense-w4-crosswalk.md) to scaffold.

## Project Ideas — Era 3

---

### 1. "When is rebellion justified?"
**Essential question:** What conditions have to exist before revolution is a legitimate response to injustice?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.3 + Era 3
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchors:** Common Sense + Declaration of Rights of Man + Haitian Declaration
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Argument essay · Structured debate · Comparative analysis
**The argument:** Students compare what Common Sense, the DROM, and the Haitian Declaration each argue justifies revolution. Using R.9, they identify the argumentative structure of at least two. Using W.4, they write their own position on when rebellion is justified. Three revolutions, one essay.

---

### 2. "Who gets left out of 'liberty and equality'?"
**Essential question:** Every revolution claims to fight for freedom — so why do some people always end up excluded from it?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.3–4 + Era 3
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchors:** Wollstonecraft + Haitian Declaration + CrashCourse ep-31
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Multimedia
**Possible deliverables:** Essay · Podcast · Documentary
**The argument:** Wollstonecraft asks why the French Revolution's "rights of man" excluded women. The Haitian Revolution asks why the same revolution's rhetoric didn't end slavery. Students read both arguments (R.9) and write their own (W.4): what does the pattern reveal about how political systems treat those they were not built for?

---

### 3. "How do you write a nation into existence?"
**Essential question:** Can a document actually create a country — and what makes some documents more powerful than others?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.5 + Era 3
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.3
**Source anchors:** Common Sense + Declaration of Rights of Man
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing
**Possible deliverables:** Explanatory essay · Comparative analysis · Documentary
**The argument:** Students analyze Common Sense and the DROM as rhetorical objects — what makes them persuasive? Using R.9 to analyze argument structure and W.3 to explain, they produce a piece on how language creates political legitimacy. See: [Common Sense + W.4 Crosswalk](../../reading-library/crosswalk/common-sense-w4-crosswalk.md).

---

### 4. "Who pays the cost of empire?"
**Essential question:** When one group benefits from a global system, who bears the cost — and does the beneficiary know?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.4–5 + Era 3
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchors:** Kipling + H.T. Johnson + CrashCourse ep-37 (Imperialism)
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Multimedia
**Possible deliverables:** Comparative essay · Podcast · Photo essay with argument
**The argument:** Students read Kipling's "White Man's Burden" alongside H.T. Johnson's direct rebuttal. They analyze both as arguments (R.9) — what is each writer claiming? who is their audience? what evidence do they use? — then write their own position (W.4) on who is right. WH.6_12.4 content + strongest W.4 model this era.

---

### 5. "Why did some people know slavery was wrong and do nothing?"
**Essential question:** What does it take for moral knowledge to become moral action — and what gets in the way?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.4–5 + Era 3
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchor:** Narrative of Frederick Douglass + CrashCourse ep-23 (Slave Trade)
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Verbal Conversation or Extended Writing
**Possible deliverables:** Essay · Podcast · Debate
**The argument:** Douglass writes explicitly about this — he knows his enslavers know it's wrong. Students use his argument (R.9) alongside Green's structural analysis of the slave trade (ep-23) to write an argument about the gap between moral understanding and political action. Rich W.4 entry point.

## Project Ideas — Era 1

These are proposed Studio inquiry angles. Any can be adapted for your own inquiry question.

---

### 1. "Who gets to make the rules?"
**Essential question:** When someone writes down a law, who benefits — and who is left out?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.3 + Era 1
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchor:** Hammurabi's Code + Pericles' Funeral Oration
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing (argument essay) or Written Assessment
**Possible deliverables:** Comparative essay · Annotated primary source analysis · Podcast episode
**The argument:** Students take a position on what Hammurabi's Code reveals about who the law was designed to protect — using R.9 to analyze the text's claims and W.4 to write their own argument.

---

### 2. "Is the powerful person always right?"
**Essential question:** When a stronger power tells a weaker one that might makes right, what can the weaker power do?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.3 + Era 1
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchor:** The Melian Dialogue
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Verbal Conversation or Extended Writing
**Possible deliverables:** Essay · Staged debate using Melian Dialogue format · Visual essay mapping the power argument
**The argument:** Thucydides' Melian Dialogue is one of the oldest recorded arguments about power. Students analyze the Athenian argument structure (R.9) and write a response from the Melian position (W.4), connecting to WH.6_12.3.

---

### 3. "How did belief become government?"
**Essential question:** How did early societies use religion to justify political authority — and did it work?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.2 + Era 1
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.3
**Source anchors:** Analects of Confucius + Pericles' Funeral Oration
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Explanatory essay · Documentary script · Annotated comparison chart
**The argument:** Students compare how Confucius and Pericles each used their ideology to justify political organization — using W.3 to explain the mechanism, and WH.6_12.2 as content evidence.

---

### 4. "What did ancient people actually know?"
**Essential question:** How advanced were ancient civilizations — and what does the answer reveal about how we define 'advanced'?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.1 + Era 1
**ELA Standard:** W.3 + R.4
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Multimedia
**Possible deliverables:** Documentary · Museum-style exhibit · Research presentation
**The argument:** Students examine a specific ancient technology or scientific achievement (irrigation, medicine, the arch, astronomical calendars) and write an explanatory piece arguing for its significance. Challenges presentism.

---

### 5. "What do laws reveal about values?"
**Essential question:** Can you tell what a society cared about just by reading its laws?
**WH Standard:** WH.6_12.3 + WH.6_12.6 + Era 1
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchor:** Hammurabi's Code
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Written Assessment or Portfolio Annotation
**Possible deliverables:** Analysis essay · Comparative chart + written reflection · Podcast
**The argument:** Students read selected laws from Hammurabi's Code and identify the values embedded in them (property, family, class), then connect to a modern law that reveals contemporary values. Direct WH.6_12.6 (past to present) entry point.

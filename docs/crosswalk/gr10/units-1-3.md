## Project Ideas — Units 1–3

---

### 1. "When a system produces suffering at scale, who's responsible?"
**Essential question:** When industrialization creates widespread poverty and dangerous conditions, is anyone to blame — or is it just the system?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.4–5 + Unit 1
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchor:** *The Jungle* (Sinclair)
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing
**Possible deliverables:** Argument essay · Investigative journalism piece (written in Sinclair's tradition) · Podcast
**The argument:** Sinclair wrote *The Jungle* as an argument for reform. Students analyze his argumentative strategy (R.9 — who is his audience? what does he want them to do? what evidence does he use?) and write their own argument about whether systemic harm requires systemic solutions or individual accountability. W.4 + US.6_12.4.

---

### 2. "What justified American imperialism — and does that justification hold up?"
**Essential question:** When a powerful country takes control of a weaker one "for its own good," how do we evaluate that claim?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.4 + Unit 2
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchors:** Kipling — "White Man's Burden" + H.T. Johnson — "Black Man's Burden"
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Comparative essay · Debate · Podcast
**The argument:** Students read both poems as arguments (R.9), identify the claims, evidence, and intended audiences of each, then write their own argument (W.4) evaluating the "civilizing mission" claim. See also [CrashCourse Ep 37 — Imperialism](../../crashcourse/world-history/ep-37-imperialism.md) for historical context.

---

### 3. "Who entered WWI knowing what they were getting into?"
**Essential question:** If every country entered WWI expecting a short, winnable war, what does that tell us about how leaders make decisions?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.5 + Unit 2
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4 or W.3
**Source anchor:** Dulce et Decorum Est + CrashCourse ep-38 (WWI)
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Multimedia
**Possible deliverables:** Essay · Documentary · Annotated comparison
**The argument:** Green's episode shows the strategic miscalculations; Owen's poem shows what the reality looked like. Students read both (R.9 for Owen's poetic argument, the episode for historical argument structure) and write either an explanatory piece (W.3) on what the leaders got wrong, or an argument (W.4) about who bears responsibility.

---

### 4. "Can a story change a law?"
**Essential question:** *The Jungle* led to the Pure Food and Drug Act. What does it take for a piece of writing to produce real policy change?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.4–5 + Unit 1
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.3
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Multimedia
**Possible deliverables:** Explanatory essay · Documentary · Research presentation
**The argument:** Students research the connection between *The Jungle* and the 1906 Pure Food and Drug Act, analyzing Sinclair's argument strategy (R.9) and then explaining (W.3) what made his writing capable of producing legislative action. Bonus: they find a contemporary example of journalism or creative work that influenced policy — connecting to US.6_12.6.

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Units 1–3 cover the decades when the United States industrialized, built a global empire, fought its first world war, and then watched the economy collapse — and every pattern from that era is recognizable today. The debate between Booker T. Washington and W.E.B. Du Bois about how to achieve civil rights never ended; it's still happening. The question of whether the United States should use military force to spread its values abroad — debated during the Spanish-American War and WWI — is asked about every conflict since. The Great Depression revealed that an unregulated economy can fail everyone at once, and the argument about what the government owes people when it does is the argument behind every debate about the social safety net. The muckrakers — Sinclair, Tarbell, Riis — invented the model of investigative journalism that is still the main check on corporate and government power. If you care about labor rights, press freedom, economic inequality, or when the U.S. should go to war, this is the era where those arguments started. See [USH-01: When the System Fails](../../units/studio-archive/ush-01-when-the-system-fails.md) and [USH-03: When the Floor Falls Out](../../units/studio-archive/ush-03-when-the-floor-falls-out.md).

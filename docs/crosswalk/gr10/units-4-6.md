## Project Ideas — Units 4–6

---

### 1. "What did 'the good war' cost — and who paid?"
**Essential question:** WWII is called America's "good war." Was it — and for whom?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.5 + Unit 4
**ELA Standard:** R.8 + W.4
**Source anchors:** *Night* (Wiesel) + CrashCourse ep-41 + FDR Four Freedoms
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Argument essay · Podcast · Documentary
**The argument:** FDR says the war is for "four freedoms." Green shows the WWII's moral complexity (Holocaust response, atomic bomb). Wiesel shows what one person's experience cost. Students read across all three (R.9 for FDR, R.8 for Wiesel) and write an argument about whether calling WWII "good" is accurate, for whom, and why it matters what we call it. Strong W.4 + US.6_12.5 crosswalk.

---

### 2. "Does propaganda work — and how would you know if it was working on you?"
**Essential question:** How does a government shape what its citizens believe — and what's the difference between education and propaganda?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.2–3 + Unit 5
**ELA Standard:** R.8 + W.4
**Source anchors:** *Fahrenheit 451* or *1984* + CrashCourse ep-42 (McCarthyism section)
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Multimedia
**Possible deliverables:** Essay · Video essay · Annotated text analysis + argument
**The argument:** Both Bradbury and Orwell wrote about the Cold War fear of conformism. Students use R.8 to analyze how each author literarily embodies that fear (character, setting, symbolism) and W.4 to write an argument about whether the fear was justified and whether it's still relevant. WH.6_12.2 and US.6_12.2 content throughout.
*See studio packet: [USH-02 Literature of Fear](../../units/studio-archive/ush-02-literature-of-fear.md)*

---

### 3. "What makes a protest strategy work?"
**Essential question:** The Civil Rights Movement used nonviolent resistance — was that a moral choice, a strategic one, or both?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.3–4 + Unit 6
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchor:** MLK — Letter from Birmingham Jail
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Argument essay · Debate · Podcast
**The argument:** MLK writes the "Letter from Birmingham Jail" while in jail, defending the strategy of nonviolent civil disobedience to skeptics. Students analyze his argument (R.9 — who is his audience? what is his claim? what evidence does he use?) and write their own argument about whether nonviolent protest is a more effective strategy than other forms of resistance. See: [Letter from Birmingham + W.4 Crosswalk](../../reading-library/crosswalk/letter-birmingham-w4-crosswalk.md).

---

### 4. "What does integration cost the person being integrated?"
**Essential question:** When Melba Pattillo Beals walked into Central High School, she was making history — but what was she giving up?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.3 + Unit 6
**ELA Standard:** R.8 + W.3 or W.4
**Source anchor:** *Warriors Don't Cry* (Beals)
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Essay · Podcast · Interview-style documentary
**The argument:** Students read Beals's memoir (R.8 — perspective, voice, memoir conventions) and write either an explanatory piece (W.3) on what integration demanded of individual Black students, or an argument (W.4) on whether the burden of desegregation was fairly distributed. US.6_12.3 content throughout.

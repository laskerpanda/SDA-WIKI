## Project Ideas — Units 7–9

---

### 1. "When does protest become history?"
**Essential question:** How does a protest movement decide it's winning — and how does it decide to keep going when it isn't?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.3–4 + Unit 7
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchors:** Mandela Rivonia Speech + Vietnam-era protest context
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Argument essay · Podcast · Research presentation
**The argument:** Mandela argued for justice from a defendant's chair, knowing he would likely be imprisoned for life. Students analyze his argumentative strategy (R.9 — what is his claim? who is his real audience?) and write their own argument (W.4) about what makes a protest movement capable of achieving its goals across time. Connects US.6_12.3 with WH.6_12.6.

---

### 2. "Who defines what counts as progress?"
**Essential question:** The US has changed dramatically over the 20th century — but changed toward what, and for whom?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.6 + Units 7–9
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchor:** UN UDHR + Malala UN Speech + student-selected current events source
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Argument essay · Research presentation · Podcast
**The argument:** The UN UDHR (1948) describes what the world committed to after WWII. Malala (2013) shows what that commitment still requires 65 years later. Students use both to argue: by the standards the world set for itself, how much progress has actually been made — and who gets to define the answer? Strong US.6_12.6 + W.4 crosswalk.

---

### 3. "What does it feel like to live inside a political revolution?"
**Essential question:** When a political system changes rapidly around you, how do ordinary people experience it — and what do they lose and gain?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.3 + Units 7–8 context
**ELA Standard:** R.8 + W.3
**Source anchor:** *Persepolis* (Satrapi)
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Essay · Visual essay (inspired by graphic memoir format) · Podcast
**The argument:** Satrapi tells the story of Iran's 1979 revolution through her own childhood. Students use R.8 to analyze how she uses the graphic memoir form (image, caption, perspective, irony) to show political change, then write a W.3 explanatory piece on what *Persepolis* reveals about how political change is experienced differently by different people. US.6_12.3 + comparative framework with any US political shift studied in Units 7–8.

---

### 4. "How do we know what we know about recent history?"
**Essential question:** The further in the past an event, the more we rely on historians. For recent events, we have everything — does that make it easier or harder to know the truth?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.5–6 + Unit 9
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + IR.4 (source credibility)
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Portfolio Annotation or Written Assessment
**Possible deliverables:** Annotated source log · Research presentation · Podcast on media literacy
**The argument:** Unit 9 covers events students have some memory of (9/11, social media, 2008 financial crisis). This Studio uses that proximity to interrogate how we know what we know — practicing IR.4 (source credibility) at the highest level, while using R.9 to analyze how contemporary journalism and government documents argue. Strong capstone Studio for the full-year US.6_12.5 research skill strand.

---

### 5. "What does the past owe the present?"
**Essential question:** When historical injustice produced ongoing inequality, what — if anything — do later generations owe?
**USH Standard:** US.6_12.6 + Unit 9
**ELA Standard:** R.9 + W.4
**Source anchors:** Malala UN Speech + UN UDHR + student-selected contemporary source
**Suggested Demo Mode:** Extended Writing or Verbal Conversation
**Possible deliverables:** Argument essay · Structured debate · Podcast
**The argument:** This is the capstone question of the full two-year SDA arc. Students use the whole year's reading library — from Hammurabi to Malala — to write the most ambitious argument they've attempted: what does history owe the present? What obligations does understanding injustice create? This Studio rewards Phoenix-track students who can propose their own angle. US.6_12.6 + W.4 at full extension.

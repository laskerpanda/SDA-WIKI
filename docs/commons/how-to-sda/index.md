# How to SDA — Complete Student Guide

**South High School · Self-Directed Academy · Grades 9–10**

This guide explains everything about how SDA works. Read it at the beginning of the year. Come back to it when you're confused.

---

## The Short Version

SDA is a mastery-based program. You don't earn credit by sitting in class — you earn it by showing that you know something and can make something with what you know. That happens through six-week learning cycles called **Studios**.

You will always be working in at least two Studios at once: one for English (ELA) and one for Social Studies (World History or US History). In SDA, they're not separate courses — they're one integrated program. Your English writing skills are how you demonstrate what you learned in history.

**The core equation:** Standard = PBC = Task → Elevate

A **standard** is what you need to know or be able to do (it's written by the state of North Dakota). A **PBC** (Project-Based Competency) is the specific thing you produce to prove you know it. The **task** is the concrete work you do to get there. And **elevate** is the goal — not checking boxes, but actually getting better.

---

## What Is a Studio?

A Studio is a six-week learning cycle. Each Studio has:

**1. A Shared Essential Question**
The whole class starts with the same question. Example: *"How did early religions justify political authority?"* Everyone investigates the same question — but from different angles.

**2. Your Individual Inquiry Angle**
You choose your specific focus within the essential question. Three students might all be in the same Studio but studying completely different civilizations, time periods, or examples. Your inquiry angle is the specific question *you* will investigate.

**3. Active Standards**
Each Studio has 4–5 standards you're working toward. These are a mix of English and Social Studies standards. They're not surprise — they're posted at the start and your Studio Proposal is built around them.

**4. A Public Exhibition**
At Week 6, your work goes in front of a real audience. Not just your teacher. This is the Publish phase. The audience might be other students, parents, community members, or experts. Your advisor tells you the format for each exhibition.

## The Six-Week Studio Lifecycle

Every Studio follows the same arc. Here's what each week is for:

### Week 1 — Launch
The essential question drops. You brainstorm, research broadly, and pick your inquiry angle. By the end of Week 1, you submit a **Studio Proposal** that includes:
- Your inquiry angle (the specific question you're investigating)
- Your **demonstration of learning mode** (how you'll prove you know the standard — you pick from 6 options)
- Your **deliverable concept** (what product you'll build and exhibit)
- Your guiding research question (the specific question driving your research)
- Your target proficiency score (2.0, 3.0, or 4.0 — you self-set this)

The proposal is a contract. It commits you to a plan. You can adjust it in conversation with your advisor, but you can't abandon it without a reason.

### Week 2 — Dig
Research. You're in primary and secondary sources, building your source log, annotating, and pulling skinnies when you hit knowledge gaps. This is the foundation phase — the quality of your Dig determines the quality of everything that comes after.

**What to do this week:**
- Start your source log (you need at least one primary and one secondary source)
- Annotate each source for key information and your research question
- Pull any skinnies you need for skills you haven't mastered yet
- Narrow or adjust your research question based on what you find

### Week 3 — Build
Start making the thing. First draft of your essay, podcast outline, video script, website framework, or whatever your deliverable is. You're not polishing — you're generating. Get it down.

**First draft is due at the end of Week 3.** It doesn't have to be good. It has to exist.

### Week 4 — Shape
Feedback and revision. You'll have a peer review session and an advisor conference this week. The goal is to understand what's working and what isn't — and to make concrete decisions about what to change.

**This is the most important week.** Students who skip the Shape phase rarely reach 3.0. Feedback is information. Ignoring it is leaving your grade on the table.

**What to do this week:**
- Come to your advisor conference with a specific list of things you're unsure about
- Document the feedback you receive
- Make a revision plan: at least three substantive changes you intend to make, and why

### Week 5 — Finish
Complete the demonstration of learning. Polish the deliverable. Prepare for exhibition. This week is about finishing, not generating.

**Your demonstration of learning evidence must be complete by the end of Week 5.** Your advisor will review it and give you a preliminary proficiency score before the exhibition.

### Week 6 — Publish
The exhibition. Your deliverable is presented to an audience. Your demonstration of learning evidence is used to score your standard on the 1.0–4.0 scale. You write a reflection.

After the exhibition, your studio is archived. It becomes part of the Studio Archive — future SDA students will be able to see what you made.

## Two Things Every Studio Requires

These are completely different. Both are required. Neither replaces the other.

### 1. Demonstration of Learning

This is proof that you know the standard. It must be completed by Week 5. Your proficiency score (1.0–4.0) is based on this.

You choose one of six modes at the start of the Studio:

| Mode | What It Is |
|------|-----------|
| **1 — Written Assessment** | A focused written response to a specific prompt targeting the standard |
| **2 — Extended Writing** | A standalone essay, analysis, or argument piece as direct evidence |
| **3 — Verbal Conversation** | An advisor conference where you talk through your understanding; advisor documents the evidence |
| **4 — Visual/Creative** | A sketchnote, annotated diagram, or infographic showing conceptual understanding |
| **5 — Multimedia/Performance** | A podcast, video, debate, or presentation demonstrating the standard |
| **6 — Portfolio Annotation** | You curate existing work and write metacognitive commentary connecting it to the standard using 2.0/3.0/4.0 language |

**Choose your mode in Week 1.** It's in your proposal. You can request to change it, but you'll need to explain why.

### 2. The Deliverable (Your Studio Product)

This is the PBL artifact. It's what gets exhibited at Week 6 and goes into the Studio Archive. It shows that you took what you know and built something with it.

**The relationship:** Demonstration of learning = "I know this." Deliverable = "I made something with what I know."

**Deliverable types you can propose:**
- Essay (explanatory, argumentative, or analytical)
- Podcast episode or series
- Documentary or video essay
- Website or digital exhibit
- Visual essay or photo essay
- Museum-style exhibit with artifacts and captions
- Debate (structured, with evidence)
- Research presentation with slides or visuals
- Original artwork with an artist statement
- Something else — if you have an idea, propose it in your Studio Proposal

## What Is a Skinny?

A **skinny** is a mini-lesson. It's a short, focused resource that teaches one specific skill or concept.

Skinnies are not required for every studio. They're tools you pull when you hit a gap — when there's something you don't know how to do yet that's blocking your progress.

**How to use a skinny:**
1. You realize you're stuck on something specific: "I don't know how to write a counterclaim paragraph" or "I don't understand what a primary source is"
2. You go to the Skinny Library and find the skinny for that skill
3. You work through it: read the 2.0 foundation, understand the 3.0 target, do the scale tasks
4. You pull what you need from it and apply it to your studio work

**Skinnies are in the wiki:** [Skinny Template](../../skinnies/templates/skinny-template.md)

Your advisor may also recommend specific skinnies based on your work. If your advisor says "pull the R.9 skinny before your next draft," pull it.

## What Is a Standard?

A standard is an official skill or knowledge statement from the state of North Dakota. It describes what you should be able to know or do.

In SDA, every studio is anchored by 4–5 standards. They tell you exactly what you're being assessed on.

**Standard codes you'll see:**
- `9-10.R.9` = Reading standard 9 for grades 9–10
- `9-10.W.4` = Writing standard 4 for grades 9–10
- `WH.6_12.2 + Era 1` = World History standard 2, applied to Era 1 content
- `US.6_12.5 + Unit 3` = US History standard 5, applied to Unit 3 content

For the full explanation of every standard, see [ELA 9-10 Scales](../../standards/ela/gr9-10-scales.md).

## The Proficiency Scale

Every standard is scored on a 1.0–4.0 scale. Here's what each score means:

| Score | What It Means | Grade |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| **4.0** | You go beyond the standard — in-depth inferences and applications that weren't directly taught | A (95%) |
| **3.5** | Beyond the standard with partial success | A- (91%) |
| **3.0** | You meet the standard — no major errors or omissions | B (83%) |
| **2.5** | Solid 2.0 knowledge + partial 3.0 evidence | B- (78%) |
| **2.0** | Foundation knowledge — vocabulary and basic processes, but missing the complex ideas | C (73%) |
| **1.5** | Partial 2.0 knowledge | D (63%) |
| **1.0** | With help, partial 2.0 understanding | F (50%) |

**Your goal every studio: 3.0.** That means no major errors or omissions on the standard as written. 4.0 is available to you if you go further — if you make connections and applications that weren't directly taught.

Setting your own target proficiency at the beginning of each studio is part of your proposal. This isn't just a formality — the target shapes how you plan your work.

## What Are Ownership Levels?

You're always at one of three ownership levels. Your level determines how much structure you get and how much independence you have.

### Trailblazer (Level 1)
You're new to SDA or are working toward the skills needed for more independence. You get:
- More structured support from your advisor
- Pre-assigned or limited inquiry angle options
- Required advisor approval before you begin a new studio
- More check-ins throughout the cycle

**What to do to move up:** Show consistent evidence of independent research, timely completion of studio phases, and quality work at 2.5 or above.

### Maverick (Level 2)
You're growing. You can:
- Propose your own inquiry angles within the essential question
- Collaborate freely with other students
- Work with less check-in frequency
- Experiment with more ambitious deliverable types

**What to do to move up:** Consistently demonstrate 3.0 or above across multiple studios. Show that you can self-manage the full six-week cycle without prompting.

### Phoenix (Level 3)
Full agency. You can:
- Propose entirely self-designed studios (your own essential question, not just an angle)
- Mentor Trailblazer students
- Access flex scheduling
- Submit more adventurous deliverables (long-form projects, community-facing exhibitions)

**Phoenix track requires:** A written proposal, advisor approval, and a track record of 3.0+ across at least three studios.

**Note:** Levels are reviewed every two weeks. You can apply to move up. You can also move down if the work isn't there.

## How the Research Standards Work (IR Bundle)

The five Inquiry and Research standards (IR.1–IR.5) are always active, every studio, every cycle. They're assessed together as a bundle.

Here's what each one is asking you to do:

| Standard | Plain Language | When It Shows Up |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **IR.1** | Write a good research question — specific enough to investigate | Week 1: your proposal's guiding question |
| **IR.2** | Find and interpret primary and secondary sources | Week 2: your source log and annotations |
| **IR.3** | Organize your research so you can use it | Weeks 2–4: your notes and source structure |
| **IR.4** | Evaluate whether a source is credible | Week 2: source evaluation in your log |
| **IR.5** | Cite your sources correctly | Weeks 3–5: in-text citations and bibliography |

**To earn a 3.0 on the IR bundle**, you need to show all five:
- A research question that evolved through the Dig
- At least one primary and one secondary source, interpreted (not just collected)
- An organized source log
- Written source evaluations noting bias, perspective, and purpose
- Correct in-text citations and a complete bibliography

If you need help with any of these, there are skinnies for all five: [Research Questions Ir1](/skinnies/ela9/research-questions-ir1.md) and related pages.

## How Exhibition Works

Every Studio ends with a public exhibition. Here's what to expect:

**The audience:** Varies by studio. Sometimes it's other SDA students. Sometimes it's parents or community members. Sometimes it's an expert or a panel. Your advisor will tell you who's coming.

**What you're presenting:** Your deliverable. You're not just "presenting your paper" — you're showing the artifact you built and explaining what you learned.

**What you're being scored on:** Not the deliverable itself. The proficiency score is based on your demonstration of learning evidence (which was completed in Week 5). The exhibition is about making your work visible — not about earning a higher grade on the spot.

**Communication standards (C.1 and C.6)** are assessed at the exhibition:
- C.1 — Your presentation: format, multimedia choices, delivery
- C.6 — Your discussion: how you listen, respond, and engage with questions

**After the exhibition:** You write a reflection. It goes in your portfolio. The studio is archived.

## Portfolio Defense

Once a semester, you defend a portfolio to a panel. The panel includes your advisor and at least one other person (could be an administrator, a community member, or another teacher).

**The five required artifacts:**
1. A historical analysis (primary source annotation or bias analysis)
2. An explanatory essay
3. An argument essay
4. A communication artifact (seminar prep, debate outline, presentation recording)
5. A reflection (growth analysis)

**Sample defense questions:**
- How did your argument writing improve this semester?
- How does a historical system you studied still shape today's world?
- What would you do differently in your studio process?

The portfolio defense is about your growth over the semester — not any single studio. It's where you make the case for your development.

## How to Get Help

**Your advisor (Reese):** This is your first resource. If you're stuck, say so clearly: "I'm stuck on [specific thing]" is more useful than "I need help."

**Skinnies:** The Skinny Library in this wiki exists exactly for knowledge gaps. Pull a skinny before asking your advisor — often you'll find what you need.

**The Reading Library:** [Reading Library](../../reading-library/index.md) — if your research isn't finding good sources, check here first.

**Other students:** Maverick and Phoenix students can collaborate freely. Trailblazers should confirm with their advisor before deep collaboration.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**"Can I change my deliverable type after submitting my proposal?"**
Yes, with advisor approval and a written reason. Don't change it just because the original idea got hard — that's usually a sign you need to push through, not pivot.

**"What if I don't finish by Week 6?"**
You have until the exhibition to complete your demonstration of learning. Extensions are available with advisor approval, but they must be requested before the exhibition — not after.

**"What if I got a 2.0 and want a higher score?"**
You can resubmit evidence for a standard within the same semester. You need to show substantially new or improved work, not just the same thing cleaned up. Talk to your advisor.

**"Can I do my deliverable in a group?"**
Yes, for Maverick and Phoenix students. Group deliverables require each person's individual contributions to be clearly documented. The demonstration of learning is always individual.

**"What is the Studio Archive?"**
Every completed studio is logged permanently. Future SDA students can browse it before proposing. Your work becomes part of the institutional memory of the program.

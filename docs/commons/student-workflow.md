# Student Workflow — How to Run a Studio

**Self-Directed Academy · South High School**

This page tells you what to actually do, week by week. Every studio follows the same six-phase arc. Use this as your map.

## Start Here — The Essential Question

Every studio starts with an **Essential Question (EQ)**. The EQ is the shared question the whole class anchors on. Your job is to find your specific angle inside it.

**Where to find your EQ:**

Go to your studio packet. The EQ is at the top.

| Studio | Essential Question |
|--------|--------------------|
| [WH-01 — Who Should Govern?](../units/studio-archive/wh-01-who-should-govern.md) | Who should govern, and what gives them the right? |
| [WH-02 — Rights Chain](../units/studio-archive/wh-02-rights-chain.md) | When people write down their rights, what changes — and what doesn't? |
| [WH-03 — Writing Against Empire](../units/studio-archive/wh-03-writing-against-empire.md) | When a powerful system is doing harm, what does it take to challenge it — and what does it cost? |
| [WH-04 — Reformation and Revolution](../units/studio-archive/wh-04-reformation-and-revolution.md) | Can one document change the world — and if so, how? |
| [WH-05 — The Cost of War](../units/studio-archive/wh-05-the-cost-of-war.md) | Who pays the cost of war — and who gets to tell the story? |
| [WH-06 — The Connected World](../units/studio-archive/wh-06-connected-world.md) | What connects the world — and who decides who counts as part of it? |
| [USH-01 — When the System Fails](../units/studio-archive/ush-01-when-the-system-fails.md) | When an institution fails the people it's supposed to serve, what does it take to fix it — and who has to do the work? |
| [USH-02 — Literature of Fear](../units/studio-archive/ush-02-literature-of-fear.md) | What does a society's fiction reveal about its fears — and whose fears count? |

**What is an inquiry angle?**
The EQ is shared. Your angle is yours. Three students can all be in WH-01 and investigate three completely different civilizations. Your angle is the specific question *you* will investigate within the EQ.

A good angle:
- Connects to the EQ
- Is specific enough to research in 6 weeks
- Is something you're actually curious about
- Can be argued — reasonable people could disagree

## Week 1 — Launch

**Goal: Understand the question. Choose your angle. Commit to a plan.**

**Step 1 — Read your studio packet**
Open the studio packet for your current studio. Read:

- The Essential Question
- The Inquiry Angle Menu (suggested angles — you can use one or propose your own)
- The reading list (skim the titles — which ones pull you in?)
- The active standards (these are what you'll be scored on)

**Step 2 — Do a first-pass skim of 2–3 sources**
Before you commit to an angle, read one passage from two or three texts in the reading library. You're not researching yet — you're figuring out what you're interested in.

**Step 3 — Choose your inquiry angle**
Write your angle as a question. Test it:

- Can I argue a position on this? → Good
- Can I Google a single answer in 30 seconds? → Too narrow or too factual — revise it
- Does it connect to the active standards? → Required

**Step 4 — Fill out your Studio Contract**
Open the Studio Contract (Google Doc). Make a copy — rename it: *[Your Name] — [Studio Name] Contract*. Fill out all 8 parts. This is your plan for the whole studio.

!!! tip "Studio Contract"
    **[Open Studio Contract (Google Doc)](https://docs.google.com/){target="_blank"}** · [Download PDF](../assets/downloads/sda-studio-contract.pdf)

    Parts 1–3 are the most important to get right in Week 1. Part 4 (proficiency targets) requires you to look at the relevant skinny.

**Step 5 — Get Reese approval**
Share your completed contract with Reese before you start Week 2. He'll either approve it or send notes. You don't start digging until it's approved.

**What you submit at the end of Week 1:**
- Completed Studio Contract (shared with Reese via Google Drive)

---

## Week 2 — Dig

**Goal: Build your evidence base. Find your argument.**

**Step 1 — Set up your Source Log**
Open the Source Log (Google Doc or PDF). Make a copy — rename it: *[Your Name] — [Studio Name] Source Log*.

!!! tip "Source Log"
    **[Open Source Log (Google Doc)](https://docs.google.com/){target="_blank"}** · [Download PDF](../assets/downloads/sda-source-log.pdf)

**Step 2 — Find your sources**
You need at minimum:

- **1 primary source** — a document, speech, artifact, or firsthand account from the time period
- **1 secondary source** — a historian, journalist, or expert analyzing the primary source or event

The [Reading Library](../reading-library/index.md) is your first stop. It has primary sources organized by era and unit, with standard connections already mapped.

**Step 3 — Annotate each source**
For each source, record in your Source Log:

- What it says (summary in your own words)
- What it means for your research question
- The author's perspective and purpose
- Whether it's reliable — and why

**Step 4 — Pull any skinnies you need**
If you hit a knowledge gap, pull a skinny before asking Reese. Common ones for this phase:

- [IR.1–5 Research Bundle](../skinnies/ela9/ir1-5-research-bundle-skinny.md) — how to write research questions, find and evaluate sources, cite correctly
- [R.9 Informational Text](../skinnies/ela9/r9-informational-argumentative-skinny.md) — how to analyze an argument in a primary source

**Step 5 — Narrow your research question**
Based on what you found, sharpen your guiding question. Most good research questions evolve in Week 2. Update your Studio Contract if your angle shifted.

**What you submit at the end of Week 2:**
- Source Log (at least 2 sources annotated)
- Updated Studio Contract if your angle changed

---

## Week 3 — Build

**Goal: Get a first draft down. Done, not perfect.**

**Step 1 — Choose your demo format and start**
Look at Part 3 of your Studio Contract — your Demo of Learning mode. That determines what you're making this week:

| Demo Mode | What You're Building in Week 3 |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Written Assessment | Draft response to your prompt |
| Extended Writing | Draft essay — intro, body paragraphs, conclusion |
| Verbal Conversation | Outline of your argument + talking points |
| Visual / Creative | Rough layout or sketch with annotations |
| Multimedia / Performance | Script, outline, or storyboard |
| Portfolio Annotation | Curated evidence + draft commentary |

**Step 2 — Pull writing skinnies if needed**
- [W.4 Argument Writing](../skinnies/ela9/w4-argument-writing-skinny.md) — claim, evidence, counterclaim
- [W.3 Explanatory Writing](../skinnies/ela9/w3-explanatory-writing-skinny.md) — organizing explanatory evidence
- [R.4 Main Idea and Theme](../skinnies/ela9/r4-main-idea-theme-skinny.md) — if you're analyzing a text's argument

**Step 3 — First draft due end of Week 3**
It doesn't have to be good. It has to exist. A rough draft you can revise is worth more than a perfect outline you never wrote.

**What you submit at the end of Week 3:**
- First draft of your Demo of Learning (or your deliverable draft if they're the same)

---

## Week 4 — Shape

**Goal: Get feedback. Make a revision plan. Improve.**

This is the most important week. Students who skip the Shape phase rarely reach 3.0.

**Step 1 — Peer review**
Exchange your draft with another student. Use the proficiency scale from the relevant skinny to give specific feedback:
- What's at 3.0 already?
- What's still at 2.0?
- What's missing entirely?

**Step 2 — Advisor conference**
Come to your conference with:
- Your draft
- A list of 2–3 specific things you're unsure about
- Your proficiency targets from Part 4 of your Studio Contract

**Step 3 — Write a revision plan**
After feedback, document:
- At least 3 substantive changes you will make
- Why each change moves your work closer to 3.0 (or 4.0)

This isn't optional. The revision plan is part of your IR evidence.

**What you submit at the end of Week 4:**
- Peer review notes (yours + theirs)
- Written revision plan

---

## Week 5 — Finish

**Goal: Complete your Demo of Learning. Polish everything.**

**Step 1 — Complete your Demo of Learning**
Your Demo evidence must be finalized by the end of Week 5. Reese will give you a preliminary proficiency score before the exhibition.

Review Part 4 of your Studio Contract — do your revisions show 3.0 work on the standard?

**Step 2 — Polish your deliverable**
If your studio has a Week 6 deliverable, finish it. Practice your presentation if applicable.

**Step 3 — Fill out your Reflection (first pass)**
Start the Reflection template now. You'll finalize it after the exhibition, but getting your thoughts down before is easier than starting cold on Week 6.

!!! tip "Reflection Template"
    **[Open Reflection (Google Doc)](https://docs.google.com/){target="_blank"}** · [Download PDF](../assets/downloads/sda-reflection.pdf)

**What you submit at the end of Week 5:**
- Completed Demo of Learning evidence
- Deliverable (final or near-final)

---

## Week 6 — Publish

**Goal: Show your work. Reflect. Archive.**

**Step 1 — Exhibition**
Present your deliverable to the audience. Your advisor will tell you the format (in-class, parents, community, panel).

Bring:
- Your deliverable (printed, displayed, or loaded and ready)
- Your Studio Contract (shows your original plan)
- Your confidence — you've done the work

Communication standards C.1 and C.6 are assessed here:
- **C.1** — How you present: format, delivery, preparation
- **C.6** — How you respond to questions: listening, engaging, defending your ideas

**Step 2 — Finalize your Reflection**
Complete the Reflection template after the exhibition. Answer honestly:
- What did you learn about the standard?
- What would you do differently?
- What are you most proud of?

**Step 3 — Submit everything**
Final checklist:

- [ ] Demo of Learning evidence (submitted to Reese)
- [ ] Deliverable (exhibited and/or published)
- [ ] Completed Reflection
- [ ] Source Log (final)
- [ ] Studio Contract (final — filed in your Google Drive folder)

Your studio is now archived. Future SDA students can see what you made.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**"Can I change my inquiry angle after Week 1?"**
Yes, with Reese approval and an updated Studio Contract. Don't change it just because it got hard — that's usually a sign you need to push through.

**"What if I can't find good sources?"**
Check the [Reading Library](../reading-library/index.md) first — it's organized by era and standard. If you still can't find what you need, ask Reese before Week 2 ends.

**"What if I don't finish by Week 6?"**
Extensions are available with advisor approval — but you must request one *before* the exhibition, not after. Plan backwards from Week 6.

**"What's the difference between the Demo and the Deliverable?"**
Demo = proof you know the standard (scored 1.0–4.0). Deliverable = something you share with an audience (exhibited at Week 6). They can overlap, but they're not the same thing.

**"What if I got a 2.0 and want a higher score?"**
You can resubmit evidence within the same semester. You need to show substantially improved work — not the same thing cleaned up. Talk to Reese.

**"Can I work with other students?"**
Maverick and Phoenix students can collaborate freely. Trailblazers confirm with Reese first. Group deliverables always require individual Demo of Learning evidence.

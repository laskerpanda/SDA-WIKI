# How to Use the Reading Library

**South High School · Self-Directed Academy**

The Reading Library is a collection of primary sources and literary texts that appear across SDA Studios. This guide explains what's in it, how it's organized, and how to actually use it during a Studio.

---

## What the Reading Library Is

The Reading Library is not a list of books you're assigned to read cover-to-cover. It's a collection of **specific texts** — speeches, manifestos, letters, memoir excerpts, poems, and chapters — that connect to the history and standards in your Studios.

Each entry in the library has:
- The full text (or a significant excerpt)
- Context: who wrote it, when, why, and what was happening in the world
- Standards connections: which WH standards and ELA standards it addresses
- Annotation prompts: questions to guide your reading
- Crosswalk links: how this text connects to other texts and Studios

## When to Use It

**During the Dig (Week 2):** The reading library is your first stop for primary sources. Before you go searching the internet for historical documents, check here — most of what you need for Era 1–4 Studios is already in the library with context and annotation guidance already written.

**When you hit a knowledge gap:** If you're writing about the French Revolution and realize you've never actually read any document *from* the French Revolution, pull the Declaration of the Rights of Man from the library. Ten minutes in the primary source is better than an hour paraphrasing a summary.

**When you need a second source type:** If your Studio is primarily research-based but your writing feels thin, a primary source from the reading library can give you direct evidence — a specific quote, a specific claim — that strengthens your argument.

**For ELA demonstrations of learning:** Many of the ELA reading standards (especially R.9) specifically require you to analyze argumentation in a text. The Social Studies texts in this library are argumentative texts — Paine was arguing for independence, Wollstonecraft was arguing for women's rights, Douglass was arguing against slavery. They're ideal for R.9 work.

## How to Read a Library Entry

Every entry follows the same structure. Here's what each section does:

**The text itself (or excerpt):** Read it first. Don't read about it — read it.

**Historical context:** Read this after your first read-through, when you have questions. The context section answers: who wrote this, to whom, under what circumstances, and what were they trying to accomplish?

**Standards alignment table:** Shows you exactly which standards this text addresses. Use this during your Studio proposal to decide whether a text is a good fit for your inquiry angle.

**Annotation prompts:** These are questions to guide a second, slower read. You're not required to answer all of them — choose the ones that connect to your inquiry question.

**Crosswalk links:** These connect this text to other texts in the library. If you're studying imperialism and you read the Kipling poem, the crosswalk will point you to H.T. Johnson's response — they need to be read together.

**Pairs with CrashCourse:** Many entries link to a CrashCourse episode that gives you the historical context in video form first. Watch the episode, then read the primary source.

## Using the Library for Annotation

Annotation is reading with a purpose. When you annotate a library text, you're not just underlining — you're having a conversation with it.

**Three basic moves:**

1. **Identify the claim.** What is this writer arguing? Underline or note the sentence where they state their main position.

2. **Identify the evidence.** What reasons or examples do they use to support the claim? Put a bracket around each piece of evidence.

3. **Note your response.** Where do you agree, disagree, or have a question? Write a short note in the margin (or in your source log): "This is convincing because..." or "I'm not sure this is true because..." or "This connects to [something else I read]."

**The standard this builds:** R.9 — analyzing argumentative text. Annotating a primary source from the Reading Library directly builds your R.9 evidence.

## Citing Library Texts

When you quote or paraphrase a reading library text in your Studio work, cite it like any other source. Use the author, title, and year from the library entry header.

Example MLA format:
> Paine, Thomas. *Common Sense*. 1776.

If you're quoting from the library entry itself (using our context or annotation prompts rather than the original text), note that: "As quoted in SDA Commons Wiki Reading Library."

For full citation guidance: [IR.1–5 Research Bundle Skinny](../skinnies/ela9/ir1-5-research-bundle-skinny.md)

# The Cost of Victory — Studio Packet

**Studio Code:** USH-04
**Subject Area:** US History — Unit 4 (World War II)
**Suggested Cycle:** Year 2, Cycle 3
**Duration:** 6 weeks

---

## The Essential Question

**When a nation believes it is fighting on the right side of history, how does it decide which of its own values it can sacrifice — and who pays the price of that decision?**

---

## Why This Studio Matters

World War II is often called the "Good War" — and for good reason. The Allies were fighting fascism, genocide, and imperial conquest. The United States entered after being attacked. The stakes were genuine, the enemy was real, and the outcome mattered enormously. It's one of the clearer cases in history of fighting for something worth fighting for.

And yet. The United States — the nation fighting for the Four Freedoms, for democracy, for human dignity — imprisoned 120,000 Japanese Americans without charge, without trial, and without evidence. The military that defeated the Nazis was segregated. The country that condemned the Holocaust turned away Jewish refugees before the war. And on August 6 and 9, 1945, the United States deliberately targeted civilian populations in Hiroshima and Nagasaki with the most destructive weapons ever built, killing an estimated 130,000 to 226,000 people, the vast majority of them civilians.

This studio takes the "Good War" seriously — and asks the hardest question you can ask about it: What did the United States justify in the name of victory? *Night* by Elie Wiesel puts the Holocaust at human scale — not statistics, but one person's experience of what happens when a society decides some lives don't count. FDR's Four Freedoms speech tells you what America said it was fighting for. The gap between those two documents is what this studio lives in.

## ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk

Several texts in this studio do double duty across ELA and Social Studies standards:

- ***Night* (Wiesel)** — When you analyze it for **R.8** (how Wiesel uses narrative structure and detail to function as testimony), you are simultaneously building evidence for **US.6_12.2 + Unit 4** (multiple perspectives on the Holocaust) and **WH.6_12.2-6 + Era 4** (if you're connecting to the World History thread). *Night* appears in the [ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk](../../reading-library/crosswalk/crosswalk-ela-ss.md) as one of the highest-leverage dual-use texts in the library.

- **FDR's Four Freedoms** — Analyzing it for **R.9** (argument structure, rhetorical choices) also satisfies **US.6_12.1 + Unit 4** (primary source analysis). One speech, two standards.

- ***Slaughterhouse-Five* (Vonnegut)** — Literary analysis for **R.8** (how Vonnegut uses satire and nonlinear narrative) connects directly to **US.6_12.2 + Unit 4** (multiple perspectives on the Allied bombing campaigns and civilian cost of WWII).

A single Demo of Learning can satisfy both an ELA and an SS standard at once if your Studio Contract names both and your analysis addresses both. Talk to Reese in Week 1 if you want to use a crosswalk approach.

## Inquiry Angle Menu

**Trailblazer angles:**
- FDR said America was fighting for "freedom from fear" for "everyone, everywhere." Then he signed Executive Order 9066, imprisoning 120,000 Japanese Americans — including American citizens — without trial. Using both documents, explain what this contradiction reveals about how democratic nations justify restricting freedom during wartime.
- Elie Wiesel dedicated *Night* to transmitting his experience: "Never shall I forget." What does Wiesel want his readers to do with this testimony? Analyze his narrative choices — what he describes in detail, what he leaves out, how he structures time — and explain what they do to the reader.

**Maverick angles:**
- Truman's stated reason for using the atomic bomb: it would end the war faster and save the lives of hundreds of thousands of American soldiers who would have died in a land invasion of Japan. Using evidence from both sides, evaluate this justification. What does the argument assume? What does it leave out?
- The Holocaust happened in modern, educated, bureaucratic Germany — not a medieval society but one of the most technologically and culturally advanced nations in Europe. What does *Night* reveal about how ordinary people participate in genocide? What does it suggest about the conditions that make it possible?
- Compare the treatment of Japanese Americans (Executive Order 9066) with the treatment of German Americans and Italian Americans during WWII. Why the difference? What does this reveal about the role race played in how the United States defined its enemies?

**Phoenix angles:**
- The Nuremberg Trials (1945–46) established that "following orders" is not a sufficient defense for war crimes. But American pilots who dropped atomic bombs were following orders. American soldiers who enforced Japanese internment were following orders. Where do you draw the line — and how do you draw it without applying a double standard? Construct an argument using specific evidence from the war.
- *Night* and *Slaughterhouse-Five* are both books written by survivors about mass civilian death during WWII — but they're completely different in tone, style, and purpose. Wiesel is a witness demanding moral reckoning. Vonnegut is sardonic and detached. Analyze why each writer made the choices he did — and what each approach can and can't do as a response to atrocity.

## Six-Week Arc

| Week | Phase | What You're Doing |
|------|-------|------------------|
| **1** | Launch | Read FDR's Four Freedoms (January 6, 1941). Then read the text of Executive Order 9066 (February 19, 1942) — 44 days after Pearl Harbor. What do these two documents, written by the same president 13 months apart, reveal about what America was actually willing to fight for? Submit your Studio Contract. |
| **2** | Dig | Deep read *Night* for R.8: How does Wiesel structure his narrative? What does he choose to describe in detail, and what does he pass over quickly? What does this tell you about what he wants you to understand? For the SS strand: research the atomic bomb decision from multiple perspectives — what did Truman know, what did he decide, and what were the alternatives? |
| **3** | Build | Draft your deliverable. Your central question: What did the United States justify in the name of winning the "Good War" — and was that justification enough? Use specific evidence. |
| **4** | Shape | Peer review and advisor conference. Identify where your argument is strongest and where it's relying on assumption rather than evidence. The atomic bomb question in particular: make sure you've engaged with the strongest version of the counterargument. |
| **5** | Finish | Complete demonstration of learning. Polish deliverable. Prepare for exhibition. |
| **6** | Publish | Exhibition. Reflection. Archive. |

## Deliverable Ideas

1. **"What FDR Promised"** — A primary source analysis essay: Compare FDR's Four Freedoms speech to Executive Order 9066. What did the president say America stood for, and what did he do 13 months later? What does the gap between these two documents reveal about how democratic governments justify restricting freedom during wartime?

2. **"The Decision"** — An argument essay: Evaluate Truman's decision to use the atomic bomb. Was it justified? Use evidence from multiple perspectives: the U.S. military's reasoning, Japan's situation in the summer of 1945, survivor testimony from Hiroshima, and the precedent the decision set.

3. **"Never Shall I Forget"** — A literary analysis of *Night*: Analyze Wiesel's memoir as a deliberate act of witness. What is he trying to make his readers understand? How do his narrative choices — his structure, his details, his tone — serve that purpose?

4. **"The Good War's Other Side"** — A research exhibit or documentary: Document one case where the United States' actions during WWII contradicted the values it said it was fighting for (internment, segregated military, turning away Jewish refugees, Allied firebombing campaigns). Analyze: What was the justification? Who paid? Does the justification hold up?

5. **"Hiroshima and After"** — A comparative analysis: The atomic bomb was used in 1945 and has never been used in war since. Analyze the precedent the Hiroshima decision set — using the historical record of U.S. nuclear policy, the Cold War arms race, and contemporary debates about nuclear weapons. What did that decision commit the world to?

## Scale Tasks

### 2.0 — Foundation
- Define: Holocaust, internment, atomic bomb, D-Day, Nuremberg Trials, Hiroshima, Four Freedoms, Executive Order 9066
- Correctly summarize the major events and timeline of WWII: causes, major turning points, homefront
- Identify and correctly describe Wiesel's experiences in *Night*
- Describe the main arguments for and against using the atomic bomb

### 3.0 — Target
- Analyze *Night* for literary elements: how does Wiesel use narrative structure, detail, and witness to function as testimony rather than historical account?
- Build a clear argument about the atomic bomb decision: take a position, present your strongest evidence, and engage honestly with the counterargument
- Connect the gap between FDR's stated values (Four Freedoms) and American actions during the war (internment, segregated military) to the studio's essential question about what nations justify in the name of victory
- Evaluate a specific WWII decision from multiple perspectives — naming who benefited and who paid

### 4.0 — Transfer
- OR: Argue whether the Nuremberg standard — "following orders is not a defense for war crimes" — can be applied consistently to American decisions during WWII. What does consistent application reveal?
- OR: Use the WWII pattern (good cause + morally compromised means) to analyze a more recent American military decision — arguing whether the pattern holds and what it reveals about how democracies make wartime choices
- OR: Analyze *Night* and *Slaughterhouse-Five* as two different literary responses to mass civilian death — argue which approach (witness vs. satire) is more effective, and for what purposes

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The question at the center of this studio — what does a country fighting for the right cause get to do in pursuit of victory? — is asked every time a democratic nation goes to war. The atomic bombs killed somewhere between 130,000 and 226,000 people, most of them civilians, and stopped a war. The internment of Japanese Americans was a direct violation of constitutional rights, carried out by the president who gave the Four Freedoms speech. These weren't anomalies — they were the pattern. And that pattern keeps repeating: enhanced interrogation (torture), drone strikes with civilian casualties, indefinite detention without trial — each justified as necessary to win a war that is, of course, on the right side of history. Elie Wiesel's *Night* is the most important counter to this logic: it records what happens when a nation decides that some lives are acceptable losses. The Holocaust was not an accident or an anomaly — it was the product of exactly the kind of reasoning this studio examines. If you want to follow the thread to Vietnam and beyond, [USH-05: What the Government Didn't Say](ush-05-what-the-government-didnt-say.md) asks what happened when Americans found out the government was applying the same logic to them.

## Exhibition Format

**Suggested format:** Panel discussion or Socratic seminar.

Students present their analysis of the studio's central moral question to a panel that includes advisor and at least one outside guest. The panel poses one challenge question to each student's argument. Students are expected to respond with evidence, not just restatement.

**What gets scored at exhibition:**
- **C.1** — Presenting a complex moral argument clearly; using your evidence to support your position in front of an audience
- **C.6** — Responding to challenge questions about your reasoning; listening to other students' arguments and engaging with differences

## Sensitive Content Note

*Night* contains detailed descriptions of Holocaust atrocities, including the death of family members, starvation, violence, and the systematic murder of prisoners. This content is essential to the text and treated with complete seriousness — it is not gratuitous, but it is unflinching. Students who need support should speak with their advisor before Week 2. The atomic bomb discussion involves photographs and survivor testimony from Hiroshima and Nagasaki, including civilian casualties.

# The Literature of Fear — Studio Packet

**Studio Code:** USH-02
**Subject Area:** US History — Unit 5 (Cold War) + World History Era 4
**Suggested Cycle:** Year 2, Cycle 2 (or Year 1, Cycle 6)
**Duration:** 6 weeks

---

## The Essential Question

**What does a society's fiction reveal about its fears — and whose fears count?**

---

## Why This Studio Matters

In 1953, Ray Bradbury published *Fahrenheit 451* — about book burning — while the US government was blacklisting artists and the House Un-American Activities Committee was interrogating citizens about their reading habits. In 1949, George Orwell published *1984* — about totalitarian surveillance — in the shadow of Stalin's USSR. In 2000, Marjane Satrapi published *Persepolis* — about a childhood under the Iranian Revolution — as a graphic memoir that made the abstract political concrete and personal.

These are not coincidences. Literature about fear is produced in eras of fear — and the fiction a society consumes tells you exactly what that society is afraid of, what it's willing to do about it, and who it decides the fear doesn't apply to.

This studio builds the WH.6_12.2+6 (ideology and legacy) and US.6_12.2 (rights and citizenship in the Cold War) standards while developing the full R.8 (literary analysis) and R.9 (cross-text argument) toolkit. It's the studio where students learn to read historical context in fictional form.

## ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk

USH-02 sits at the hinge point between two courses: it spans **World History Era 4** and **US History Unit 5** at the same time. That means every major text in this studio can do triple duty — ELA literary analysis, World History, and US History — often in a single Demo of Learning. Here's how:

- ***1984* (Orwell)** — R.8 literary analysis (dystopian world-building, surveillance, doublethink as literary theme) simultaneously satisfies **WH.6_12.2-3 + Era 4** (ideology and political systems — specifically how totalitarianism works) AND **US.6_12.2 + Unit 5** (multiple perspectives on the Cold War and communist/capitalist ideological conflict). *1984* bridges World History and US History — it's one of the few texts in the library that does triple duty. If you use it here, make that connection explicit in your Demo: name both courses and both standards in your Studio Contract.

- ***Fahrenheit 451* (Bradbury)** — R.8 analysis (how Bradbury constructs his dystopia, what the book burning represents as a literary symbol) connects directly to **US.6_12.3 + Unit 7** (cause and effect: Cold War fear culture, McCarthyism, and government information control — *Fahrenheit 451* is Bradbury's direct response to the Red Scare and book-banning). It also connects to **US.6_12.2 + Unit 5** (the cultural atmosphere of the Cold War). In other words, analyzing the literary symbol is also analyzing the historical cause. It appears in the [ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk](../../reading-library/crosswalk/crosswalk-ela-ss.md) table.

- ***Persepolis* (Satrapi)** — R.8 analysis (graphic memoir format; how Satrapi uses visual language and personal narrative as political argument) simultaneously satisfies **WH.6_12.2-3 + Era 4** (the Iranian Revolution as a case study in Cold War-era ideological conflict) AND **US.6_12.2 + Unit 9** (multiple perspectives — the Iranian perspective on the U.S.-Iran relationship). *Persepolis* is one of the few texts that bridges World History, US History Unit 5, AND US History Unit 9. Students who use it here have a head start for [USH-07: The Unipolar Moment](ush-07-unipolar-moment.md).

The bridge to notice: USH-02 is the studio most explicitly positioned at the hinge point between World History and US History. If you're enrolled in both courses, this is your highest-leverage studio for earning credit in both at once.

One Demo of Learning can satisfy both an ELA and an SS standard if your Studio Contract names both — you don't have to do two separate projects. Talk to Reese in Week 1 to set this up. See the [ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk](../../reading-library/crosswalk/crosswalk-ela-ss.md) for the full map of dual-use texts across all studios.

## Inquiry Angle Menu

**Trailblazer angles:**
- Choose one scene from *Fahrenheit 451* and explain: What specifically is Bradbury afraid of? What historical events or trends in 1953 America does this scene reflect?
- *Persepolis* is set in Iran during the Islamic Revolution — not in Cold War America. But it appears in a studio about Cold War cultural anxiety. What connects these two contexts?

**Maverick angles:**
- Compare *Fahrenheit 451* and *1984* as political arguments. Both are dystopian fiction. Both argue that something is wrong with the direction their society is heading. But what exactly are they afraid of — and are they afraid of the same thing?
- Bradbury said "I wasn't writing about censorship, I was writing about how TV was destroying the desire to read." Orwell said *1984* was "a warning, not a prophecy." Both authors tried to control how their books were interpreted after publication. Does an author's stated intent determine a text's meaning?
- *Persepolis* is a graphic memoir, not a novel. How does the graphic format change what Satrapi can show about the Iranian Revolution that a written memoir couldn't?

**Phoenix angles:**
- "The literature of fear tells you what a society believes it deserves to lose — not what it's actually losing." Using evidence from all three texts, evaluate this claim.
- These three texts were all written by people who were in or adjacent to the systems they were criticizing (Bradbury in Cold War America, Orwell post-Spain and post-WWII, Satrapi in revolutionary Iran). How does being inside the culture you're critiquing change what you can say about it?
- Connect the "literature of fear" framework to a contemporary text you identify yourself — a novel, film, or other cultural product that encodes contemporary cultural anxiety. What does it reveal about what your era fears?

## Six-Week Arc

| Week | Phase | What You're Doing |
|------|-------|------------------|
| **1** | Launch | Read Beatty's speech from *Fahrenheit 451* (Part I, "The Hearth and the Salamander"). Beatty is the villain arguing for book burning — and his argument is disturbingly coherent. What is his argument? Does any part of it make sense? Submit your Studio Proposal. |
| **2** | Dig | Deep read your selected texts. For R.8: Mark every image, metaphor, structural choice, or scene that encodes a historical anxiety. For context: read the "Historical Context" section of your library entry — what was happening when this was written that the author was responding to? |
| **3** | Build | Draft your deliverable. Your central challenge: How do you use a fictional text as historical evidence? What specifically in the text counts as evidence for your historical claim? |
| **4** | Shape | Peer review and advisor conference. Find the place where your argument relies on plot summary instead of analysis. Revise to move from "this is what happens" to "this is what it means and why the author constructed it this way." |
| **5** | Finish | Complete demonstration of learning. Polish deliverable. Prepare for exhibition. |
| **6** | Publish | Exhibition. Reflection. Archive. |

## Demonstration of Learning Options

| Mode | Prompt for This Studio |
|------|----------------------|
| **1 — Written Assessment** | In 400–600 words: Choose one passage from any text in this studio and analyze how specific literary choices (imagery, structure, word choice, point of view) encode a historical anxiety. Do not summarize — analyze. |
| **2 — Extended Writing** | Argument essay (700–1,000 words): Argue what *Fahrenheit 451*, *1984*, or *Persepolis* reveals about the fears of its historical moment that a history textbook couldn't. Use literary evidence as historical evidence. |
| **3 — Verbal Conversation** | Advisor conference: Walk through the historical context of your chosen text, then explain how at least three specific literary choices encode that context. Demonstrate that you're reading the text as both a work of fiction and a historical artifact. |
| **4 — Visual/Creative** | Annotated close reading: Select a chapter or scene and create a visual annotation that maps how the literary choices connect to the historical moment. Each annotation must make a historical claim, not just a literary observation. |
| **5 — Multimedia/Performance** | Documentary or video essay: "Reading the Cold War in Fiction" — a guide for general audiences on how to read dystopian/fear literature as historical evidence. Must demonstrate R.8+9 and WH.6_12.2 standard evidence. |
| **6 — Portfolio Annotation** | Curate prior work and annotate for WH.6_12.2+6, US.6_12.2, and R.8+9. Commentary must reference specific passages and demonstrate the skill of reading literary evidence historically. |

## Deliverable Ideas

1. **"Beatty's Argument"** — A rhetorical analysis of Captain Beatty's speech in *Fahrenheit 451*: What is Beatty arguing? Is he wrong? Bradbury gives his villain the most coherent argument in the book — why? What does that choice reveal about what Bradbury was afraid of?

2. **"Three Fears, Three Countries"** — A comparative analysis of what *Fahrenheit 451* (American), *1984* (British), and *Persepolis* (Iranian) each fear — and what the comparison reveals about how Cold War anxiety looked different in different national contexts.

3. **"When Bradbury Was Right"** — A research essay examining the historical parallels between *Fahrenheit 451*'s fictional society and 1953 American society (HUAC, McCarthyism, TV). Was Bradbury describing the present or predicting the future? Use historical evidence alongside the text.

4. **"The Child Who Saw Everything"** — A literary analysis of *Persepolis* as a graphic memoir: How does the child's perspective reveal things about the Iranian Revolution that an adult, political perspective couldn't? What does the graphic format add? Use specific panels as evidence.

5. **"2025 Edition"** — A contemporary connection exhibit: Choose one fear from this studio's texts (censorship, surveillance, ideological conformity, political violence) and research how it manifests today. Does the "literature of fear" framework from these texts help explain contemporary cultural products that encode the same fear?

## Scale Tasks

### 2.0 — Foundation
- Define: dystopia, McCarthyism, Cold War, totalitarianism, censorship, cultural anxiety
- Correctly describe the historical context in which each novel was written
- Identify at least two specific literary elements used in each text

### 3.0 — Target
- Analyze how at least three specific literary choices in a text encode the historical moment it was written in
- Compare two texts' implicit arguments: What does each fear? How does each construct that fear through literary choices?
- Demonstrate that you can use fictional evidence historically — that you understand the difference between "this is what happens in the book" and "this is what the book reveals about its moment"

### 4.0 — Transfer
- Construct an argument about what dystopian/fear literature reveals about historical periods that straightforward historical accounts can't — using specific evidence from multiple texts
- OR: Apply the "literature of fear" framework to a contemporary cultural product you identify yourself
- OR: Argue whether the fears in these texts have been realized, avoided, or transformed — with specific historical evidence

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

Orwell and Bradbury wrote their dystopias as warnings, not predictions — but the warning has never been more directly applicable. The surveillance infrastructure that Orwell imagined — a government that can watch everything, knows what you search, tracks where you go, reads your messages — exists and is operated by both governments and corporations. The algorithmic selection of what you see on social media is a form of what Bradbury described: not book-burning, but something more efficient — making sure you never encounter the book in the first place. And the Cold War fear this studio is about — what happens to a society that organizes itself around existential terror? — maps directly onto current debates about national security, surveillance, and the trade-off between freedom and safety. *1984* and *Fahrenheit 451* are not cautionary tales about the past. They're diagnostic tools for the present. If you want to connect the Cold War context to what came after, [USH-05: What the Government Didn't Say](ush-05-what-the-government-didnt-say.md) covers Vietnam and the Pentagon Papers — the moment when Cold War fear became Cold War deception.

## Exhibition Format

**Suggested format:** Symposium or documentary screening + discussion.

If students produced documentary or video deliverables, screening + Q&A. If written/analytical: symposium where each student presents their central argument (5 minutes) and takes challenge questions (3 minutes).

Final question for every presenter: "What contemporary cultural product encodes the same fear you identified in your text — and how do you know?"

**What gets scored at exhibition:**
- **C.1** — formal presentation: organized argument, handling of challenge
- **C.6** — discussion: engaging with classmates' analyses; adding evidence

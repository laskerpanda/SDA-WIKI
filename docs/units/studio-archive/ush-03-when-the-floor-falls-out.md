# When the Floor Falls Out — Studio Packet

**Studio Code:** USH-03
**Subject Area:** US History — Unit 3 (Roaring 20s and Great Depression)
**Suggested Cycle:** Year 2, Cycle 2
**Duration:** 6 weeks

---

## The Essential Question

**When the economy collapses and millions of people lose everything, what does the government owe them — and what does it cost to find out?**

---

## Why This Studio Matters

The 1920s looked, to a lot of Americans, like the country had finally arrived. The war was over, the economy was booming, consumer culture exploded, and jazz was changing music everywhere. F. Scott Fitzgerald published *The Great Gatsby* in 1925, a novel that captured the decade's glittering surface and the rot underneath. His characters live inside a dream — beautiful, expensive, and hollow — and they keep reaching for something that keeps moving away.

Then the crash came. In October 1929, the stock market collapsed, triggering bank failures, mass unemployment (25% at its peak), and the Dust Bowl. Twelve million families lost their savings. By 1932, Hoovervilles — shantytowns named mockingly after the president — had appeared in every major American city. Hoover believed the government should not intervene directly. FDR disagreed. He won the 1932 election on that disagreement, and the New Deal he built — Social Security, the FDIC, the SEC, federal work programs — still shapes American life today.

This studio puts two questions side by side: *What was the dream the 1920s were selling, and who couldn't buy in?* And: *When that dream collapsed, what did the people left holding nothing deserve from their government?* It builds US History standards on economic change and government's role while developing argument writing (W.4) and literary analysis of how Fitzgerald uses the novel to make an argument about American society (R.8).

## ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk

*The Great Gatsby* is a dual-use text for this studio. When you analyze it for **R.8** — how Fitzgerald uses symbol, character, and setting to make an argument about the American Dream — you are also building evidence for **US.6_12.4 + Unit 3**, because the novel is a direct window into how 1920s Americans understood prosperity and the gap between the dream and the reality. One Demo of Learning can satisfy both standards at once if your Studio Contract lists both and your analysis addresses both dimensions: the literary technique AND its historical meaning.

The same goes for the FDR Four Freedoms speech (extension): analyzing FDR's argument for **R.9** simultaneously builds **US.6_12.4 + Unit 3** evidence about how the New Deal philosophy extended into WWII mobilization.

See the [ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk](../../reading-library/crosswalk/crosswalk-ela-ss.md) for the full map of how texts serve both subject areas across all studios.

## Inquiry Angle Menu

**Trailblazer angles:**
- Gatsby throws lavish parties and invites everyone — but almost nobody actually knows him. Using the novel, explain what Fitzgerald is saying about the American Dream: what it promises, who it's actually for, and what it costs people who believe in it.
- Herbert Hoover believed Americans should pull themselves up by their own bootstraps — that government aid would make people dependent and weaken the national character. FDR disagreed. Using historical evidence, explain what the Depression revealed about the limits of "rugged individualism."

**Maverick angles:**
- Fitzgerald's *Gatsby* was published in 1925 — four years before the crash. But reading it now, you can see the crash coming. What does the novel reveal about the 1920s economy that people living through it weren't willing to see? What were the warning signs?
- The New Deal helped millions of Americans — but it explicitly excluded Black farmers, domestic workers, and many women. Using the historical record, evaluate the New Deal: was it a genuine transformation of American society, or a rescue program for white working-class men?
- Dorothea Lange's photograph "Migrant Mother" (1936) became the iconic image of the Great Depression. Analyze it as a primary source: What choices did Lange make? What does the photograph argue? What does it leave out? Why did it become *the* image of the Depression rather than thousands of other photographs from the same period?

**Phoenix angles:**
- Fitzgerald wrote that "the rich are different from you and me." Hemingway responded: "Yes, they have more money." Using *Gatsby* and the historical record of the 1920s and Depression, argue which one was right — and what the difference between their views reveals about how Americans understand wealth and failure.
- The United States has now had multiple major economic crises (1929, 2008, 2020). Each time, the government has had to decide what it owes its citizens. Using the Depression/New Deal as your primary case and at least one more modern example, argue whether the American government has learned anything about what it owes the people it serves in a crisis — and what the evidence says about whether those lessons stick.

## Six-Week Arc

| Week | Phase | What You're Doing |
|------|-------|------------------|
| **1** | Launch | Open with this line from *Gatsby*: *"So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past."* Read the first chapter. Then read FDR's First Inaugural: "The only thing we have to fear is fear itself." What do these two documents — written 8 years apart — say about what Americans were reaching for? Submit your Studio Contract. |
| **2** | Dig | Deep read your chosen texts. For R.8: How does Fitzgerald use Gatsby's parties, the green light, and the Valley of Ashes to make an argument about the American Dream? For the SS strand: research the specific policies Hoover chose and what FDR replaced them with. Who got New Deal help and who didn't? |
| **3** | Build | Draft your deliverable. Your central question: what did the 1920s dream cost, and what did the government owe when it collapsed? Use specific evidence from both the literary text and the historical record. |
| **4** | Shape | Peer review and advisor conference. Find the assumption in your argument you haven't examined. Is your claim about the government's obligation based on evidence — or on what you already believed before you started? Revise to strengthen the evidence or qualify the claim. |
| **5** | Finish | Complete demonstration of learning. Polish deliverable. Prepare for exhibition. |
| **6** | Publish | Exhibition. Reflection. Archive. |

## Deliverable Ideas

1. **"The Green Light"** — A literary analysis essay on *The Great Gatsby*: What does the American Dream look like in this novel? What does Fitzgerald say about who it's available to and what it costs to pursue it? Connect the novel to the actual 1920s economy.

2. **"Rugged Individualism vs. The New Deal"** — An argument essay: Was FDR's New Deal the right response to the Depression — or did it change the relationship between government and citizens in ways that created long-term problems? Take a clear position with historical evidence.

3. **"Who the New Deal Left Out"** — A research exhibit: The New Deal helped millions of Americans but deliberately excluded Black agricultural workers and domestic laborers due to political compromises with Southern Democrats. Document who was left out, why, and what this reveals about reform in America.

4. **"Migrant Mother"** — A primary source analysis and visual essay: Using Dorothea Lange's Depression photographs as your central source, analyze how photographers shaped how Americans understood the Depression. What did they choose to show — and what did they leave out?

5. **"Then and Now"** — A comparative analysis: Connect the 1929 crash and New Deal to a more recent economic crisis (2008 financial crisis, COVID economic collapse). What did the government do differently? What stayed the same? What does the comparison reveal about what Americans believe the government owes them in a crisis?

## Scale Tasks

### 2.0 — Foundation
- Define: Roaring 20s, Great Depression, New Deal, rugged individualism, Dust Bowl, Social Security, Harlem Renaissance
- Correctly summarize the plot and main characters of *The Great Gatsby*
- Identify the major New Deal programs and who they were designed to help
- Describe the basic cause-and-effect chain from the 1929 crash to the Depression

### 3.0 — Target
- Analyze Fitzgerald's use of at least two literary elements (symbol, character, setting, narrative structure) to make an argument about the American Dream
- Connect the novel's themes to the actual historical context of the 1920s: what did Fitzgerald see that his contemporaries were ignoring?
- Evaluate the New Deal: who did it help, who did it exclude, and what does that pattern reveal about how American reform works?
- Build an argument about what the government owes its citizens in a crisis — with a clear claim, specific historical evidence, and acknowledgment of the strongest counterargument

### 4.0 — Transfer
- OR: Argue that *The Great Gatsby* is (or isn't) still a relevant critique of American economic culture — using evidence from both the novel and contemporary America
- OR: Evaluate whether the pattern of New Deal exclusions (helping some people but not others for political reasons) reflects something structural about how American reform works — using at least one more example beyond the New Deal
- OR: Construct a framework for what a government owes its citizens in economic crisis — applying it to the Depression and at least one other economic crisis to test whether it holds

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The Great Depression is the last time the American economy failed so completely that it collapsed the basic assumptions most people had about how economic life worked. The debates it produced — What does the government owe people when the economy fails them? How much should the government regulate markets to prevent the next crash? What's the right balance between economic freedom and economic security? — have never been settled. They're the debates behind every argument about the minimum wage, unemployment insurance, Social Security, Medicare, and the Affordable Care Act. The 2008 financial crisis — banks failing, homeowners losing their houses, unemployment spiking — was the closest the U.S. has come to repeating 1929, and the response (bail out the banks, stimulus packages, expanded unemployment) was a direct application of the New Deal framework. The COVID-19 economic shock produced the same arguments again. If you want to understand why Americans argue so bitterly about the government's role in the economy, the Great Depression is where to look. See [USH-01: When the System Fails](ush-01-when-the-system-fails.md) for the earlier reform tradition this studio builds on.

## Exhibition Format

**Suggested format:** Gallery walk or formal presentation.

Students display or present their deliverable to classmates, advisor, and invited guests (parents, other teachers). Format for formal presentations: 5-minute presentation, then 2 questions. Gallery walk: students circulate, leave written feedback, then the class debrief focuses on comparing different angles on the same essential question.

**What gets scored at exhibition:**
- **C.1** — Presenting your argument or analysis clearly to an audience that hasn't read your sources; handling the format professionally
- **C.6** — Responding to questions about your evidence and reasoning; engaging with a challenge to your claim

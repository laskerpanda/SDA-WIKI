# One Idea, 130 Years of War — Studio Packet

**Studio Code:** WH-04
**Subject Area:** World History — Era 2 (Reformation) and Era 3 (Atlantic Revolutions)
**Suggested Cycle:** Year 1, Cycle 2
**Duration:** 6 weeks

---

## The Essential Question

**Can one document change the world — and if so, how?**

---

## Why This Studio Matters

In 1517, a German friar nailed 95 arguments to a church door. He wanted a debate. He got the Thirty Years War. In 1848, two philosophers published a pamphlet. They wanted to describe the future of capitalism. They got the Cold War. In 1776, a pamphlet called *Common Sense* sold 500,000 copies in a country of 2.5 million people.

This studio investigates the mechanics of how written documents trigger historical change — and why the change is almost never what the author intended. It builds a powerful WH.6_12.5 (causes and effects) skill set alongside the WH.6_12.2 (ideology and belief) framework, while developing W.4 argument writing and R.9 analytical reading.

## ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk

Every primary document in this studio does double duty across ELA and Social Studies:

- **Luther's 95 Theses** — When you analyze it for **R.9** (argumentative structure: what claims is Luther making, and how does he build his case?), you're simultaneously building evidence for **WH.6_12.2-5 + Era 2** (how ideological challenges triggered political and religious change). One document, one Demo, two standards. Luther is also a case study in how R.9 analysis reveals what a document actually says vs. what readers heard — and the gap between those two things is the entire SS argument.

- ***Common Sense* (Paine)** — This is the #2 highest-leverage crosswalk text in the reading library. Reading it for **R.9** (argument structure, rhetorical choices, how Paine constructs his case) simultaneously satisfies **WH.6_12.4-5 + Era 3** (causes and effects of the Atlantic Revolutions; why *Common Sense* didn't just describe revolution but helped create it). It has its own dedicated crosswalk entry because it's also the best **W.4 mentor text** in the library — the structure Paine uses is exactly what a strong argument essay looks like. See the [Common Sense + W.4 Crosswalk](../../reading-library/crosswalk/common-sense-w4-crosswalk.md) for the full breakdown.

- **The Communist Manifesto (selections)** — Literary analysis for **R.9** (how Marx and Engels build a causal argument — their evidence, their structure, what they assume vs. what they prove) connects directly to **WH.6_12.5 + Era 3** (causes and effects: what did the Manifesto predict, and what actually happened — and what does the gap between those two things reveal?). The *Manifesto* is the clearest case in this studio where close reading (ELA) *is* the historical analysis (SS).

A single Demo of Learning can satisfy both an ELA and an SS standard if your Studio Contract names both and your analysis addresses both dimensions. Talk to Reese in Week 1 if you want to use a crosswalk approach.

See the [ELA ↔ SS Crosswalk](../../reading-library/crosswalk/crosswalk-ela-ss.md) for the full map of how texts serve both subject areas across all studios.

## Inquiry Angle Menu

**Trailblazer angles:**
- Luther's 95 Theses led to the Thirty Years War. Walk the causal chain: 95 Theses (1517) → Reformation → German princes' political use of it → Peace of Augsburg (1555) → Thirty Years War (1618–48). At what point could this chain have been broken? What would have had to change?
- Paine's *Common Sense* argued that monarchy was an inherently bad system — not just British monarchy, but monarchy itself. Find three arguments he makes. Which is the most persuasive? Which is the weakest?

**Maverick angles:**
- Marx and Engels predicted an inevitable communist revolution in industrialized countries. The revolution came first in Russia (not industrialized), and the result was something Marx would have called a betrayal of communist principles. What went wrong? Did the Manifesto cause it?
- The printing press made the Reformation possible: Luther's theses traveled further in three months than any document had traveled in a century. Find a contemporary parallel: a document or idea that technology spread faster than its author intended — with consequences the author didn't anticipate.
- All three documents (95 Theses, Common Sense, Communist Manifesto) were written by people who thought they were describing reality. They turned out to be creating it. Is there a difference?

**Phoenix angles:**
- "Documents don't change history. The conditions that make a document explosive are already present — the document is just the match." Argue for or against this claim using evidence from at least two documents.
- Compare the reception of the 95 Theses in 1517 and the Communist Manifesto in 1848. Both were initially dismissed. Both eventually triggered massive historical change. What conditions were necessary for each to become explosive — and why did those conditions take the time they did?

## Six-Week Arc

| Week | Phase | What You're Doing |
|------|-------|------------------|
| **1** | Launch | Read this prompt: "In 1517, Martin Luther's academic debate memo about Church corruption accidentally launched 130 years of war, permanently fragmented Western Christianity, and created the conditions for the modern state system. He died before seeing most of it." Read the introduction to the 95 Theses. Does that claim surprise you? Submit your Studio Proposal. |
| **2** | Dig | Deep read your selected documents. For each: What did the author think they were doing? What actually happened? What was already happening in the world that made the document's reception explosive? Secondary source: find historical context for at least one document's reception. |
| **3** | Build | Draft your deliverable. You need a clear answer to: What makes a document historically explosive? Is it the document, the context, the technology, the opposition, or something else? |
| **4** | Shape | Peer review and advisor conference. Identify the weakest link in your causal chain — the place where someone could argue that the document didn't really cause what you say it caused. Revise with that addressed. |
| **5** | Finish | Complete demonstration of learning. Polish deliverable. Prepare for exhibition. |
| **6** | Publish | Exhibition. Reflection. Archive. |

## Demonstration of Learning Options

| Mode | Prompt for This Studio |
|------|----------------------|
| **1 — Written Assessment** | In 500–700 words: Trace the causal chain from one document to one specific historical consequence at least 50 years later. At each step in the chain, identify whether the connection is direct or requires additional context. |
| **2 — Extended Writing** | Argument essay (700–1,000 words): Argue whether documents drive historical change or whether historical conditions determine which documents become important. Use evidence from at least two documents in this studio. |
| **3 — Verbal Conversation** | Advisor conference: Walk through one document — explain what the author thought they were doing, what actually happened, what the causal chain looked like, and at what point the author lost control of their own idea. |
| **4 — Visual/Creative** | Causal chain diagram: Map the causal chain from one document to its long-term consequences, with annotations at each step explaining what connected them and what could have broken the chain. |

## Deliverable Ideas

1. **"The Chain Reaction"** — A visual + essay exhibit tracing the causal chain from one document to its long-term consequences. Designed for a general audience. Annotation at each link: What connected these events? What could have broken the chain?

2. **"The Map the Author Didn't Draw"** — An essay analyzing the gap between what an author intended and what their document caused. Central question: Who owns an idea once it's published? Focus on one document (95 Theses, Communist Manifesto, or Common Sense).

3. **"Technology and Ideas"** — A research presentation comparing the printing press's role in the Reformation to a technology that accelerated ideological spread in the 20th or 21st century. What makes a technology an "accelerant" for ideas?

4. **"The Unintended Consequences Exhibit"** — A museum-style multi-panel exhibit covering three documents and their unintended consequences. Each panel: the document, what the author intended, what actually happened, and what that gap reveals.

5. **"What Marx Got Wrong"** — A research essay or documentary: What did Marx predict? What actually happened? Why did communist revolutions happen in agrarian countries (Russia, China, Cuba) instead of industrial ones? What does this reveal about the relationship between ideas and material conditions?

## Scale Tasks

### 2.0 — Foundation
- Define: Protestant Reformation, pamphlet, causal chain, ideology, unintended consequences
- Correctly describe what each document argues and what happened as a result
- Identify the printing press's role in the Reformation

### 3.0 — Target
- Trace a complete causal chain from one document to a consequence at least two steps removed from the original
- Analyze the gap between the author's intent and the document's historical effect — what caused the gap?
- Compare two documents: What made each one explosive in its specific historical context? What conditions were necessary?

### 4.0 — Transfer
- Construct an argument about what makes documents historically consequential — using evidence from multiple documents and addressing the strongest counterargument
- OR: Apply the causal-chain framework to a contemporary document or idea you identify yourself — something that is generating consequences its author didn't intend
- OR: Argue whether the unintended consequences of these documents were the documents' "fault" — and what that question even means

## Why This Is Relevant Today?

The question at the center of this studio — can one document or one idea actually change the world? — is the question behind every social movement, every declaration, every manifesto written since Luther nailed his theses to a church door. The answer this era gives is: yes, but not in the way the author intended, and not without violence, and not without creating new problems alongside the ones it solved. The Protestant Reformation was supposed to reform the church; it created 130 years of religious war. The American Revolution was supposed to secure liberty; it preserved slavery for 80 more years. The Communist Manifesto was supposed to liberate the working class; it became the justification for regimes that killed millions. These aren't reasons to dismiss the ideas — they're reasons to take seriously the question of how ideas actually travel from a document to a changed world. That question is live right now in every debate about whether writing a law, signing a treaty, or passing a resolution actually does anything. If you're working in [WH-02](wh-02-rights-chain.md) alongside this studio, the two studios are in direct conversation.

## Exhibition Format

**Suggested format:** Symposium presentation + Q&A.

Each student presents their causal chain analysis (5–7 minutes), then takes questions specifically about the links in the chain — audience members try to find places where the chain is weak.

**What gets scored at exhibition:**
- **C.1** — formal presentation: organized argument, handling challenges to your causal chain
- **C.6** — discussion: responding to counterarguments with evidence

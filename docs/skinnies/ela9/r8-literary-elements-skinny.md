# Literary Elements and Author's Craft — Skinny

**Standard:** 9-10.R.8 — Analyze the development and interaction of literary elements and determine how they impact meaning, using strong textual evidence (includes: complex characters, symbolism, mood, setting; author's POV influenced by background/culture; multiple interpretations of the same scene)
**Applies across:** All studios — active with literary texts (*Night*, *1984*) and when analyzing author's perspective in primary sources
**Integrated with:** R.2 (inference), R.4 (theme), R.7 (style), W.3 (explanatory)
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 45–60 minutes
**Subject:** ELA — Literary Text Analysis

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Literary elements are the building blocks authors use to construct meaning in fiction and narrative nonfiction. At the 2.0 level, you need to identify these elements. At the 3.0 level, you need to analyze how they work together to create meaning.

- **Character:** A person (or being) in a literary text. **Complex characters** have contradictory qualities, change over time, and resist simple categorization as "good" or "bad." A **dynamic character** changes significantly during the story; a **static character** stays the same.
- **Characterization:** How an author reveals character — through direct description, dialogue, actions, other characters' reactions, or the character's own thoughts. *Showing* (letting actions reveal character) vs. *telling* (directly stating what a character is like).
- **Symbolism:** A concrete object, person, place, or event in the text that represents something beyond itself — an idea, quality, or concept. In *Night*, the flames of the crematoriums function symbolically as both literal destruction and the annihilation of Wiesel's faith.
- **Mood:** The emotional atmosphere the text creates for the reader. Created through setting, word choice, pacing, and imagery. Distinct from *tone* (the author's attitude).
- **Setting:** The time and place of a narrative — but at the literary analysis level, setting is also about how place and time shape character and theme. The physical environment of a narrative is never just background; it interacts with plot and character.
- **Author's Point of View (POV):** The perspective from which the narrative is told. Also: how the author's own background, culture, experiences, and historical moment shaped what they chose to write and how.
- **Multiple Interpretations:** Many literary scenes or passages can be read in more than one way — different readers, bringing different backgrounds and questions, will find different meaning in the same text. Strong literary analysis acknowledges this.
- **Irony:** A gap between what is stated and what is meant, or between expectation and reality. *Dramatic irony*: the reader knows something a character doesn't. *Situational irony*: what happens is the opposite of what was expected. *Verbal irony*: saying the opposite of what you mean.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Identify whether a character is complex or simple, dynamic or static — with textual evidence.
- [ ] Identify a symbol and explain what it represents, using evidence from the text.
- [ ] Describe the mood of a passage and identify which specific elements (setting, word choice, imagery) create it.
- [ ] Identify the narrative POV and explain one way it shapes what the reader knows and how.
- [ ] Find a moment in the text that supports more than one interpretation.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can identify that Eliezer in *Night* is the protagonist, that the flames at Birkenau are symbolic, and that the mood of Chapter 3 is "dark" or "sad" — without yet analyzing how the specific fire imagery interacts with Wiesel's characterization of his own faith, or how the author's survivor POV shapes what the memoir includes and omits.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**9-10.R.8 — Analyze the development and interaction of literary elements and determine how they impact meaning**

To meet this standard, you must show how literary elements work together — not analyzing character in isolation, then setting in isolation, but analyzing how character and setting interact to create meaning, or how symbolism reinforces theme.

**3.0 Example:**
Reading *Night*, a student achieves a 3.0 by analyzing how the **interaction of setting and characterization** in Chapter 3 (arrival at Birkenau) creates one of the memoir's central meanings. The physical setting — the flames, the selection, the smell — is described with spare, almost toneless language. Eliezer's emotional state is shown primarily through what he notices and doesn't notice: he describes the selection process in a matter-of-fact tone that, the student argues, is not emotional numbness but the beginning of the dissociation the Nazis deliberately engineered through the dehumanization of the arrival process. The student connects setting (the orchestrated visual horror of the flames) to characterization (Eliezer's response) to theme (the dehumanization process begins before the physical violence). The analysis shows how these elements interact to mean something the sum of their individual descriptions doesn't capture.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student identifies that the setting at Birkenau is horrifying and that Eliezer is scared.
A 3.0 student analyzes how the specific way Wiesel describes the setting interacts with his characterization of his own emotional state to create meaning about how dehumanization works.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate the author's choices — not just analyzing how elements create meaning, but assessing whether the choices are effective, and engaging seriously with alternative interpretations.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Author's POV and its limits:** Wiesel's memoir is narrated from the perspective of the surviving Eliezer. What does his survivor's POV allow him to know and tell? What can he not know — what perspectives are absent from his narrative? How does this shape the reader's understanding of the Holocaust?
2. **Multiple interpretations of one scene:** The final image of *Night* — Eliezer looking at himself in a mirror and seeing "a corpse gazing back at me" — can be interpreted multiple ways: literal emaciation, psychological death of the pre-war self, or warning to the reader. Analyze all three interpretations using textual evidence. Which is best supported? Can they all be simultaneously true?
3. **Orwell's background and 1984:** Orwell wrote *1984* in 1948, drawing on his experience in the Spanish Civil War (where he was betrayed by Stalinist communists) and his observations of Soviet propaganda. Analyze how his biographical background shaped specific choices in the novel — what he included, what he emphasized, what anxieties the novel expresses.

**4.0 Example:** A student analyzes how Orwell constructs **Winston Smith as a complex character** in *1984* by making him simultaneously the reader's identification figure and a deeply unreliable narrator. Winston believes he is a rational resister who sees through the Party's lies — but his initial attraction to Julia is possessive rather than loving, his "rebellion" is passive (writing in a diary, having an affair) rather than active, and he submits to O'Brien's authority almost immediately once the torture begins. The student argues that Orwell made Winston complex in this specific way — heroic aspiration but limited courage — because he wanted to write about how ordinary people are complicit in their own oppression, not just passive victims.

## Demonstration of Learning — Choose One

**How will you show you know this standard?** Pick one mode.

| # | Mode | What You Do for This Specific Standard |
|---|------|----------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Written Assessment** | Choose one literary text. Write an analysis showing how TWO literary elements interact to create meaning. (Don't analyze each element separately — show the interaction.) Use at least three specific passages as evidence. |
| 2 | **Extended Writing** | Write a 300–400 word literary analysis focused on one complex character. Analyze: (1) what makes them complex (contradictions, changes), (2) how the author uses characterization techniques to reveal the complexity, (3) what the character's complexity means thematically. |
| 3 | **Verbal Conversation** | Record a 2-minute explanation: (1) Identify one scene from a literary text that supports at least two different interpretations. (2) Explain each interpretation with evidence. (3) State which you find more convincing and why — or argue that both are simultaneously valid. |
| 4 | **Visual/Creative** | Choose a symbol from *Night* or *1984*. Create a visual analysis: (1) draw or describe the symbol's literal object, (2) map the moments in the text where it appears with brief descriptions, (3) analyze how its meaning develops or deepens across the text, (4) connect it to the theme it supports. |
| 5 | **Multimedia/Performance** | Create a 2-minute "Literary Commentary" video on one scene from *Night* or *1984*. Read a passage aloud, then analyze: what literary elements are at work, how do they interact, what meaning does this create, and why did the author make these choices? |
| 6 | **Portfolio Annotation** | Select a previous project where you wrote about a literary text. Write a 150-word reflection: Did you identify literary elements in isolation or analyze their interaction? Did you consider the author's POV and background? What would your analysis look like if you addressed the interaction of elements? |

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Identify one example each of: a complex character, a symbol, a mood-creating passage, and a moment where the author's POV is visible. For each, provide a specific textual reference.
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Write a paragraph analyzing how two literary elements interact in one scene — showing how they work together to create a meaning neither would create alone.
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Write 150 words engaging with a competing interpretation of one scene. Present both interpretations with evidence, then evaluate which is better supported — or argue they are equally valid and explain why.

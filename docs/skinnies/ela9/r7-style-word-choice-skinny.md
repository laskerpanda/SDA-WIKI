# Style, Word Choice, and Author's Craft — Skinny

**Standard:** 9-10.R.7 — Analyze the impact of specific style, syntax, and word choices on meaning, mood, and tone, including figurative and ambiguous language
**Applies across:** All studios — especially active with literary texts and political speeches/primary sources
**Integrated with:** R.2 (comprehension), R.4 (theme/claim), R.8 (literary elements), W.1 (style in your own writing)
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 45–60 minutes
**Subject:** ELA — Text Analysis

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Authors don't choose words randomly. Every word choice, sentence length, and structural decision shapes what the reader feels, what they understand, and what they believe. Analyzing style means asking: *Why did the author choose these words, and what do they do to me as a reader?*

- **Style:** The distinctive way a writer uses language — sentence length, vocabulary level, tone, figurative language, rhythm. Style is how something is said, as distinct from what is said.
- **Syntax:** The structure and arrangement of words in a sentence. Long, complex sentences vs. short, simple ones create different effects. Fragments can create urgency or emphasis. Parallel structure (repeating the same grammatical pattern) creates rhythm and emphasis.
- **Word Choice (Diction):** The specific words an author selects. Every word has a **denotation** (dictionary meaning) and **connotation** (emotional associations). "Thin" and "gaunt" have similar denotations but different connotations — "gaunt" implies illness, deprivation, or suffering.
- **Tone:** The author's attitude toward the subject or audience, communicated through word choice, syntax, and what they choose to include or omit. Example: MLK's tone in "Letter from Birmingham Jail" is controlled, formal, and respectful — despite writing from jail, he never sounds defeated or bitter.
- **Mood:** The emotional atmosphere a text creates in the reader. Mood is what you *feel* as you read; tone is what the author *feels* toward the subject.
- **Figurative Language:** Language that means something beyond its literal meaning.
  - *Metaphor:* A direct comparison without "like" or "as" ("Life is a journey")
  - *Simile:* A comparison using "like" or "as" ("Life is like a journey")
  - *Allusion:* A reference to another text, person, or event ("I am not the first man to be jailed for justice" — allusion to Paul, Socrates, etc.)
  - *Personification:* Giving human qualities to non-human things
  - *Anaphora:* Repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses ("I have a dream...I have a dream...")
- **Ambiguous Language:** Language that can be interpreted in more than one way — sometimes intentionally, to allow readers to bring their own meaning; sometimes accidentally.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Identify a specific word or phrase and explain its connotation (not just definition).
- [ ] Identify a figurative language device (metaphor, simile, allusion, anaphora) and name what is being compared or referenced.
- [ ] Describe the tone of a passage in one precise adjective + explanation.
- [ ] Identify how sentence length or structure affects the pace and feeling of a passage.
- [ ] Distinguish between the literal meaning of a phrase and its figurative meaning.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can identify that MLK uses a simile in "Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere" — actually, that's not a simile, which reveals the 2.0 problem: ability to spot a device but not to accurately identify it or explain what it *does* to meaning.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**9-10.R.7 — Analyze the impact of specific style, syntax, and word choices on meaning, mood, and tone**

To meet this standard, you must explain not just WHAT a stylistic device is, but WHAT EFFECT it has — how it shapes meaning, creates mood, or establishes tone. The word is not "identify" — it's "analyze the impact."

**3.0 Example:**
Reading MLK's "Letter from Birmingham Jail," a student achieves a 3.0 by analyzing his use of **allusion** in the passage: "Just as the Apostle Paul left his village of Tarsus and carried the gospel of freedom beyond his particular hometown, and just as the Apostle Paul answered the Macedonian call for aid, so am I compelled to carry the gospel of freedom beyond my own home town." The student explains the impact: By alluding to Paul — a figure the white clergy who are criticizing him deeply revere — MLK reframes the question. Instead of being asked "why are you in Birmingham causing trouble," he positions himself as following in the footsteps of a revered Christian missionary. The allusion doesn't just make the letter sound more educated; it changes the moral terms of the entire argument. It makes dismissing King as an outside agitator equivalent to dismissing Paul as an outside agitator.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student identifies that MLK uses allusion to Paul in the letter.
A 3.0 student analyzes what that allusion *does* — how it reshapes the moral argument and makes a specific rhetorical move.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate patterns of style across a text — not analyzing single devices in isolation but showing how an author's stylistic choices work together to create a sustained effect.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Pattern analysis:** In *Night*, Wiesel uses very short, declarative sentences throughout — especially when describing the most traumatic moments. Analyze this pattern: What is the effect of the short, simple syntax at moments of extreme horror? How does it differ from what a long, complex sentence at the same moment would have done?
2. **Tone shift:** Find a moment in any reading library text where the tone shifts — where the author's attitude toward the subject or audience changes. Analyze what creates the shift (word choice, syntax, a specific device) and what it reveals about the author's purpose.
3. **Ambiguity as choice:** The title *1984* is somewhat arbitrary (Orwell originally considered other years). But the ending of *1984* is genuinely ambiguous — is Winston's final "He loved Big Brother" defeat, survival strategy, or something else? Analyze the ambiguity using the text's final passages.

**4.0 Example:** A student analyzes Paine's style in *Common Sense* as a sustained pattern of *democratization* — deliberately replacing elevated, abstract language with concrete, accessible language to reach an audience of ordinary colonists rather than educated elites. The student identifies three specific stylistic patterns: (1) Paine rarely uses Latin-derived vocabulary when Anglo-Saxon words are available ("kingly" not "monarchical"; "common sense" not "rational apprehension"); (2) his metaphors are domestic (kings compared to robbers; government compared to clothing, worn when needed); (3) his sentences, while not short, use mainly coordinating conjunctions ("and," "but") rather than subordinating ones ("although," "notwithstanding"), making his logic feel like common sense rather than formal argument. The student argues that Paine's style is as revolutionary as his content.

## Reading Library Texts for Practice

- [MLK — Letter from Birmingham Jail](../../reading-library/social-studies/mlk-letter-birmingham.md) — Rich allusion, anaphora, parallel structure, controlled formal tone despite extreme circumstances
- [Common Sense — Thomas Paine](../../reading-library/social-studies/common-sense-paine.md) — Deliberate democratization of political writing; metaphor, accessible diction
- [Night — Elie Wiesel](../../reading-library/ela/night-wiesel.md) — Spare, declarative syntax as expression of trauma; the effect of what is NOT said
- [Declaration of the Rights of Man](../../reading-library/social-studies/declaration-rights-man.md) — Formal declarative style; effect of the enumerated list structure; "universal" language that implies specific audience
- [Wollstonecraft — Vindication](../../reading-library/social-studies/wollstonecraft-vindication.md) — Argumentative tone blending formal analysis and passionate indignation; effect of direct address to readers

## Demonstration of Learning — Choose One

**How will you show you know this standard?** Pick one mode.

| # | Mode | What You Do for This Specific Standard |
|---|------|----------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Written Assessment** | Choose one 1–2 paragraph passage from any reading library text. Write a close analysis: (1) identify 2–3 specific word choices, syntax decisions, or figurative language devices, (2) explain the impact of each on meaning, mood, or tone, (3) analyze how they work together — not in isolation. |
| 2 | **Extended Writing** | Write a 300–400 word style analysis of one author from the reading library. Identify a pattern in their style (not just one device), explain what effect the pattern creates, and evaluate whether it is effective for the author's purpose. |
| 3 | **Verbal Conversation** | Record a 2-minute close reading. Open to a specific passage and read it aloud. Then explain: What specific words or structures are doing work here? What is their effect on tone or meaning? What would be different if the author had made a different word choice? |
| 4 | **Visual/Creative** | Choose one passage (8–10 lines) and annotate it completely: mark every figurative device, every significant word choice, every structural decision. For each annotation, write one sentence explaining the impact. Then write a 2–3 sentence synthesis: how do all these choices work together? |
| 5 | **Multimedia/Performance** | Create a 2-minute "Rewrite" video. Take a passage from one reading library text and rewrite it in a completely different style (e.g., rewrite a passage from *Night* in Paine's argumentative style, or rewrite MLK in plain bureaucratic language). Then explain: What is lost? What does the difference reveal about the original author's choices? |
| 6 | **Portfolio Annotation** | Select a previous project where you quoted from a text. Write a 150-word reflection: Did you analyze the author's word choices, or just use the quotes as evidence for your argument? What would a genuine style analysis of those passages have added? |

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Find three examples of figurative language in any reading library text. For each, identify the device (metaphor, allusion, simile, anaphora, etc.) and explain the literal meaning vs. the figurative meaning.
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Write a paragraph analyzing ONE word choice or stylistic device in a reading library text. Identify what it is, explain its connotation or effect, and argue why the author chose it rather than a simpler alternative.
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Write 150 words analyzing a pattern of stylistic choices in one author's work — not a single device, but a recurring approach. Evaluate: what effect does this pattern create, and is it effective?

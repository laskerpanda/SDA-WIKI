# Discussion and Debate — Skinny

**Standard:** 9-10.C.6 — Participate in collaborative discussions and formal debates to explore ideas, analyze evidence, and reach conclusions, demonstrating active listening, idea building, and civil discourse
**Applies across:** All studios — Socratic seminars, project critiques, panel discussions, debate formats
**Integrated with:** C.1 (formal presentation), W.4 (argument), R.9 (argument analysis), W.6 (revision through peer feedback)
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** Ongoing — applies to all collaborative discussion formats
**Subject:** ELA — Communication

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Collaborative discussion and debate are not the same as arguing or talking. They are structured academic activities with specific purposes: to explore ideas, analyze evidence together, and reach more nuanced conclusions than anyone could reach alone. At the 2.0 level, you can participate — contribute ideas, respond to others. At the 3.0 level, you can use discussion to actually build understanding collaboratively.

- **Collaborative Discussion:** A structured conversation in which participants work toward shared understanding of a complex question, not just trading opinions. Everyone's goal is to advance the group's thinking, not just express their own.
- **Socratic Seminar:** A specific discussion format: participants sit in a circle, the teacher facilitates minimally, and the group interrogates a text or question together. The standard: contribute by either asking a genuine question, building on someone's idea, or offering evidence-based pushback.
- **Formal Debate:** A structured format in which two positions are argued. Each side presents a claim, evidence, and rebuttal. Unlike Socratic seminar, a debate has winners and losers (by the quality of argument, not by who is "right").
- **Active Listening:** Genuinely attending to what someone else is saying — not just waiting to talk. Evidenced by: building on specific points they made ("You said X, and I think that connects to..."), asking follow-up questions about their specific claim, or acknowledging where their argument was stronger than expected.
- **Idea Building:** The practice of extending, refining, or connecting someone else's contribution before adding your own: "Building on what [X] said..." or "I think that's right, and also..." or "I'd push back on that a little — if that's true, then doesn't that mean..."
- **Civil Discourse:** Engaging with opposing views respectfully and substantively — critiquing the argument, not the person. Civil discourse is not the same as agreement or niceness: you can strongly disagree and still be civil. Incivility is attacking the person, dismissing without engagement, or talking over others.
- **Evidence-Based Contribution:** Making a point that is supported by a specific text, source, or piece of reasoning — not just assertion. "I think the French Revolution was more about economics than ideology" is an assertion; "The 1789 cahiers show that two-thirds of peasant grievances were about taxes and debt rather than political rights" is an evidence-based contribution.
- **Rebuttal:** A direct response to someone else's claim — explaining specifically why you disagree or what they've missed.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Prepare at least 2 evidence-based points before entering any Socratic seminar.
- [ ] Listen for specific claims (not just general positions) so you can build on or rebut them specifically.
- [ ] Distinguish between building (extending an idea) and pivoting (changing the subject to what you wanted to say).
- [ ] Attribute ideas: "Building on what [X] said..." or "I'd push back on [X's] point about..."
- [ ] Identify when you've changed your mind during discussion — that's the goal.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who participates in seminar by contributing ideas and responding generally to others — but whose contributions don't specifically reference what others have said, and whose positions don't change over the course of the discussion regardless of what evidence emerges.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**9-10.C.6 — Participate in collaborative discussions and formal debates demonstrating active listening, idea building, and civil discourse**

To meet this standard, your participation must show that the discussion is actually changing your thinking — or that you are changing others' thinking by building on their specific contributions rather than just rotating through prepared points.

**3.0 Example:**
In a Socratic seminar on whether the Haitian Revolution was a success or failure, a student enters with the prepared position that it was a success. Midway through, another student argues: "The revolution ended the slave trade but left Haiti economically isolated — France imposed crippling reparations that the Haitian government was paying until 1947. So the political liberation was real but it was immediately weaponized economically." The 3.0 student: (1) Actively listens — doesn't respond until the full argument is made; (2) Builds on it specifically: "That's a point I hadn't considered — but I'd push back on the framing. If success requires economic freedom, then does any post-colonial state qualify as a 'success'? Because France extracted wealth from its colonies for decades; the Haitian reparations are more brutal but not categorically different. The question is whether our standard of 'success' is fair or whether we're holding Haiti to a standard we'd never apply to the U.S." (3) The student's final position is more qualified than their starting position — not because they caved to social pressure, but because the argument improved their thinking.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student contributes 3 prepared points and responds to others generally ("I agree with what they said").
A 3.0 student builds on specific claims, traces their own thinking aloud as it develops, and acknowledges when an opposing argument has genuinely changed or complicated their position.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must advance the seminar itself — asking the question that opens a new dimension, identifying where the group is talking past each other, or synthesizing multiple contributions into a clearer framing of the problem.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Metacommentary:** During or after a discussion, comment on the discussion itself: "I notice we've been debating whether the revolution was a success without agreeing on what 'success' means. Can we define terms first?" This kind of move — identifying a shared confusion and naming it — is a 4.0 contribution.
2. **Steel-manning the opposition in debate:** In a formal debate, present the strongest possible version of the opposing argument before presenting your own case. "The strongest argument against my position is X. Here's why I still disagree." This is both civil discourse and high-level argument strategy.
3. **Synthesis:** Toward the end of a seminar, offer a synthesis of the most important competing claims: "I think we've established X, and most of us agree on Y, but the real disagreement is between [student A's position] and [student B's position] about Z. Can we focus there?" This moves the group from scattered discussion to targeted argument.

**4.0 Example:** In a seminar on Wollstonecraft, a student notices that two classmates are arguing past each other: one says Wollstonecraft was a feminist ahead of her time; the other says she reinforced class biases. The 4.0 student offers: "I think you're both right and both missing something. Wollstonecraft was ahead of her time on gender within her class — she was extending Enlightenment principles to women. But she never applied the same principles to class or race. So the question isn't whether she was a feminist; it's whether we can call a feminism that only includes educated middle-class white women 'ahead of its time.' That's the actual disagreement here." This contribution doesn't just build on the existing discussion — it restructures the question so the group can actually address the underlying disagreement.

## Formats You'll Encounter in SDA

**Socratic Seminar:** Open discussion; all students responsible for advancing the conversation; use evidence from texts; teacher doesn't evaluate you for having the right answer — only for the quality of your contribution.

**Panel Discussion:** Small group presents multiple perspectives on a contested question; rest of the class can ask questions; each panelist represents a defensible position (not necessarily their own).

**Structured Academic Controversy (SAC):** In pairs, each person argues a position they were assigned — not necessarily their own view. Then sides switch. Then the pair works together to find a nuanced position. Forces genuine engagement with the opposing argument.

**Formal Debate:** Two sides; each side has an opening statement, cross-examination, and closing. Judged on the quality of argument, not who is right.

## Demonstration of Learning — Choose One

**How will you show you know this standard?** Pick one mode.

| # | Mode | What You Do for This Specific Standard |
|---|------|----------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Written Assessment** | Write a 200-word "Discussion Reflection" after any Socratic seminar: (1) What was the most compelling contribution someone else made? (2) How did it change or complicate your thinking? (3) What was the most important thing you contributed? (4) Where did the group get stuck, and why? |
| 2 | **Extended Writing** | Write a "Structured Academic Controversy" document: present the strongest case for one position on a contested historical question, then the strongest case for the opposing position, then your own nuanced view after considering both. Show that you can steelman both sides. |
| 3 | **Verbal Conversation** | Participate in a 15-minute Socratic seminar on a contested question from your current studio. Demonstrate: at least 3 contributions that build specifically on other students' ideas; at least one rebuttal with evidence; at least one moment where you acknowledge the opposing argument has strengthened or complicated your position. |
| 4 | **Visual/Creative** | Create a "Discussion Map" for a seminar: draw the flow of ideas (who said what, how it connected to what came before, where the key turning points were). Annotate the moments where the group's understanding deepened or where someone's position changed. |
| 5 | **Multimedia/Performance** | Record a 3–4 minute "Structured Debate" with a partner on a contested historical question: each side gives an opening statement (1 min), rebuttal (30 sec), and closing (30 sec). Evaluate: Was the rebuttal responsive to the specific argument made, or to a straw man? |
| 6 | **Portfolio Annotation** | Select a project that involved group discussion. Write a 200-word reflection: Did your contributions build on specific ideas from others, or were they independent of what others said? Did your position change during the discussion? If not — why not? What does that tell you? |

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Before a seminar, prepare three evidence-based contributions — specific claims with specific sources. Write them out: "I'll argue [X] based on [specific evidence from Y source]."
- [ ] **3.0 task:** After a seminar, write three "building" statements you made or could have made: "Building on what [X] said about [specific claim], I'd add/push back by..." Focus on specific claims, not general positions.
- [ ] **4.0 task:** After a seminar, identify one place where the group was talking past each other. Write the metacommentary you could have offered: "I think the actual disagreement here is between [position A] and [position B] about [specific question]. Let me try to name it..."

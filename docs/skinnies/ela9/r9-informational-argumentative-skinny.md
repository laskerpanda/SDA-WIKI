# Informational and Argumentative Text Analysis — Skinny

**Standard:** 9-10.R.9 — Analyze the development and interaction of informational and argumentative elements over the course of a nonfiction text and how they impact purpose, using textual evidence (includes: author's POV and bias; multiple accounts in different media; argumentative reasoning and rhetorical techniques)
**Applies across:** All studios — most heavily used; nearly every primary source involves R.9
**Integrated with:** W.4 (argument writing), W.3 (explanatory), R.2 (inference), WH/USH standards (all primary source work)
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 45–60 minutes
**Subject:** ELA — Informational/Argumentative Text

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

When you read a nonfiction text — a political speech, a historical document, a news article, an essay — it is either providing information, making an argument, or both. To read these texts at the 9-10 level, you need to identify how the argument is built, what techniques the author uses, and what the author's perspective and biases might be.

- **Informational Text:** A nonfiction text whose primary purpose is to convey facts, explain concepts, or describe events without arguing for a particular position.
- **Argumentative Text:** A nonfiction text whose primary purpose is to persuade the reader to accept a claim or take an action. Argumentative texts have: a **claim** (position), **evidence** (support for the claim), and **reasoning** (the logic connecting evidence to claim).
- **Rhetorical Techniques:** Strategies an author uses to persuade an audience.
  - *Logos:* Appeal to logic and reason (statistics, facts, logical arguments)
  - *Ethos:* Appeal to credibility and authority (establishing why the speaker should be trusted)
  - *Pathos:* Appeal to emotion (stories, vivid language, appeals to shared values)
  - *Kairos:* Appeal to timeliness — "this is the right moment to act"
- **Author's Point of View (POV):** The perspective from which the author writes — shaped by their background, experiences, social position, and historical moment.
- **Bias:** A systematic tendency to favor certain perspectives or conclusions over others, often unconsciously. Bias does not automatically make a source invalid — it means you must read it knowing where the author is coming from.
- **Counterclaim:** An argument that opposes the author's claim. Strong argumentative texts address counterclaims and explain why they are wrong or insufficient.
- **Fallacy:** A flawed argument — reasoning that seems logical but has a flaw. Common fallacies: *ad hominem* (attacking the person instead of the argument), *straw man* (misrepresenting the opponent's position), *false dilemma* (presenting only two options when more exist), *slippery slope* (claiming one thing will automatically lead to an extreme outcome).

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Identify whether a text is primarily informational or argumentative — or both.
- [ ] State the central claim of an argumentative text in one precise sentence.
- [ ] Identify the three rhetorical appeals (logos, ethos, pathos) used in a text, with specific examples.
- [ ] Explain the author's point of view and identify at least one way it shapes the text.
- [ ] Identify one potential bias in a text and explain how it might affect the text's reliability.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can identify that MLK uses emotional language (pathos) and logical arguments (logos) in "Letter from Birmingham Jail" — without yet analyzing how his specific rhetorical choices work together to address the specific audience (white clergy) and counter the specific objection (timing), or evaluating the strength of his reasoning.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**9-10.R.9 — Analyze the development and interaction of informational and argumentative elements and how they impact purpose**

To meet this standard, you must analyze how argumentative elements (claim, evidence, reasoning, rhetorical techniques, POV) work together in a text — and connect that analysis to the author's purpose. You're not just identifying the parts; you're showing how they build toward the author's goal.

**3.0 Example:**
Reading MLK's "Letter from Birmingham Jail," a student achieves a 3.0 by analyzing how the letter's argumentative structure directly serves its purpose. The letter is addressed to white moderate clergy who argued that the civil rights movement should "wait" and use the courts rather than direct action. MLK's argumentative strategy is calibrated precisely for this audience: (1) He establishes *ethos* through extensive biblical and historical allusion — showing he is not a radical outsider but part of a revered tradition the clergy respect. (2) He dismantles the "wait" argument not with anger but with *logos* — defining "just" vs. "unjust" laws and showing that waiting has never produced justice. (3) He uses *pathos* strategically — the example of explaining to a young daughter why she cannot go to the public amusement park — to make the injustice humanly concrete without alienating his audience. The student shows how these elements work together to serve the specific purpose: not to inspire civil rights supporters, but to persuade skeptical moderates.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student identifies the three rhetorical appeals in the letter.
A 3.0 student shows how the specific deployment of those appeals serves the specific purpose with the specific audience — how the argument is designed for these readers.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate the quality of the argument — not just analyze how it works, but assess whether it works, whether the reasoning is sound, and whether the rhetorical techniques are honest or manipulative.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Cross-text comparison:** Both Thomas Paine (*Common Sense*) and MLK ("Letter from Birmingham Jail") use ethos to establish their authority. Analyze: What is each author's ethos claim? What is its basis? Is it honest? Is it effective with the intended audience? How do their approaches to ethos differ?
2. **Fallacy evaluation:** Find one potentially flawed argument in any reading library text — not a factual error, but a reasoning error (slippery slope, false dilemma, ad hominem, straw man). Analyze: Is it actually a fallacy, or a legitimate simplification for a broad audience? Does the presence of this weakness undermine the overall argument?
3. **Multiple accounts:** The Magna Carta and MLK's Letter were both produced by people challenging authority, using similar arguments (unjust power must be limited, rights cannot be arbitrarily violated). Compare how they develop the same type of argument differently for their different audiences and historical moments. What does comparing them reveal about how the same underlying argument works in different contexts?

**4.0 Example:** A student evaluates Wollstonecraft's argument in *Vindication* for its logical strength and its strategic limits. Logically, the argument is strong: if Enlightenment principles (reason is the basis of rights, rational beings deserve freedom) are true, then women — who are rational — deserve freedom and education. The reasoning follows from premises that her opponents already accept. BUT the student identifies a strategic limit: Wollstonecraft's argument is premised on reason being the basis of all rights — which implicitly excludes people deemed "irrational" (enslaved people, colonized peoples, people with cognitive disabilities). Wollstonecraft never addresses this, which reveals how her own class position (educated, white, European) shaped a blind spot in her "universal" argument. The student evaluates: This doesn't disprove her argument about women, but it reveals the argument's dependence on premises she hasn't examined.

## Demonstration of Learning — Choose One

**How will you show you know this standard?** Pick one mode.

| # | Mode | What You Do for This Specific Standard |
|---|------|----------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Written Assessment** | Choose one argumentative text from the reading library. Write a 3-paragraph analysis: (1) the central claim and audience, (2) how two specific rhetorical techniques serve the author's purpose with that specific audience, (3) the author's POV and how it shapes the argument. |
| 2 | **Extended Writing** | Write a 300–400 word rhetorical analysis of one reading library text. Show how the text's argumentative elements (claim, evidence, reasoning, appeals, POV) work together to serve the author's purpose. Do not summarize — analyze how the argument is constructed. |
| 3 | **Verbal Conversation** | Record a 2-minute analysis: (1) State the text's central claim precisely, (2) explain who the audience is and how you know, (3) analyze one specific rhetorical technique and explain why it was chosen for this specific audience and purpose. |
| 4 | **Visual/Creative** | Create a "Rhetorical Map" of one argumentative text. Show: the claim at the center; branches for logos, ethos, and pathos with specific textual examples; the author's POV and how it shapes the argument; any counterclaims addressed; and your evaluation of which appeal is strongest. |
| 5 | **Multimedia/Performance** | Create a 2-minute "Argument Breakdown" video. Take one argument from a reading library text and break it down on camera: (1) State the claim, (2) identify the evidence and reasoning, (3) explain one rhetorical technique and why it was used, (4) evaluate one strength and one weakness of the argument. |
| 6 | **Portfolio Annotation** | Select a previous project where you analyzed a primary source. Write a 150-word reflection: Did you analyze the argumentative structure, or just use the source as evidence? Did you consider the author's POV and bias? What would a genuine R.9 analysis have added? |

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Choose an argumentative text. Identify: (1) the central claim in one sentence, (2) one example each of logos, ethos, and pathos with a specific quote, (3) one aspect of the author's POV.
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Write a paragraph analyzing how two argumentative elements work together to serve the author's purpose with the specific audience. Show the interaction, not just the identification.
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Write 150 words evaluating the quality of one argument: Does the reasoning hold up? Is there a logical fallacy or unstated assumption? Does the author's POV create a blind spot? Use specific evidence.

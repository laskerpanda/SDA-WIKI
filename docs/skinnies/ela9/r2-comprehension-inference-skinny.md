# Reading Complex Texts — Comprehension and Inference — Skinny

**Standard:** 9-10.R.2 — Comprehend a variety of texts with multiple levels of complexity while developing inferences and providing relevant textual evidence and reasoning
**Applies across:** All studios — active whenever reading a primary source or literary text
**Integrated with:** WH.6_12.1–6 (primary/secondary source analysis), W.3 (explanatory), W.4 (argument)
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 45–60 minutes
**Subject:** ELA — Reading Comprehension

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Reading at the 9-10 level means more than understanding what a text says on the surface. You must understand what the text *implies* — the meaning that's present but not stated directly.

- **Comprehension:** The ability to understand what a text says — the literal content, the events, the facts.
- **Inference:** A conclusion you draw from evidence in the text, not stated directly. Example: If a character in a story never speaks up in conversations, you might *infer* they are anxious or afraid — the text doesn't say so, but the evidence supports the conclusion.
- **Textual Evidence:** A direct quote or specific paraphrase from the text that supports your inference. Without textual evidence, an inference is just a guess.
- **Relevant Evidence:** Evidence that actually connects to the inference you're making — not just any quote from the text.
- **Levels of Complexity:** Texts can be complex in different ways: vocabulary complexity (unusual or technical words), syntactic complexity (long, difficult sentences), conceptual complexity (ideas that require background knowledge to fully understand), and ambiguity (the text can mean more than one thing).
- **Context Clues:** Information in the surrounding text that helps you figure out the meaning of an unfamiliar word or phrase without looking it up.
- **Text Structure:** How a text is organized — chronological, cause-and-effect, compare-contrast, problem-solution, argument. Identifying structure helps comprehension.
- **Paraphrase:** Restating a text's meaning in your own words — not quoting, not summarizing, but capturing the meaning in new language. A strong paraphrase shows you understood.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Identify what a difficult passage says literally before drawing any inferences.
- [ ] Distinguish between what the text states directly and what you are inferring.
- [ ] Find and quote the specific textual evidence that supports your inference.
- [ ] Explain the reasoning connecting your evidence to your inference.
- [ ] Identify the type of complexity in a difficult passage (vocabulary, syntax, concept, ambiguity).

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can correctly summarize what Elie Wiesel describes in Chapter 3 of *Night* — the arrival at Birkenau — without yet drawing an inference about what the scene reveals about how the Nazi system used dehumanization as a deliberate method, supported by specific textual evidence.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**9-10.R.2 — Comprehend a variety of texts with multiple levels of complexity while developing inferences and providing relevant textual evidence and reasoning**

To meet this standard, you must demonstrate that you understood the text AND drew a valid inference from it, backed by relevant evidence and explained reasoning. The three-part structure — **inference + evidence + reasoning** — is the core.

**3.0 Example:**
Reading MLK's "Letter from Birmingham Jail," a student achieves a 3.0 by going beyond summary (King wrote a letter from jail defending civil disobedience) to make an inference: **King's use of biblical and historical allusions strategically repositions him from "troublemaker" to "tradition-bearer."** Evidence: "Just as the Apostle Paul left his village of Tarsus...so am I compelled to carry the gospel of freedom beyond my own home town." Reasoning: By comparing himself to Paul — a figure white clergy revere — King makes it harder for them to dismiss him as an outsider agitator. He's not breaking with tradition; he's fulfilling it. This is an inference because the text never explicitly states King's rhetorical strategy — but the evidence and reasoning make it the most logical conclusion.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student summarizes what King said in the letter.
A 3.0 student infers what King was *doing* rhetorically — and backs it with specific evidence and explained reasoning.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate competing inferences — explaining why yours is better supported than an alternative, or drawing a synthesis inference that connects evidence across a whole text rather than a single passage.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Competing inferences:** Someone reading *1984* might infer that Orwell's message is "totalitarianism is inevitable" — while another reader might infer "totalitarianism can be resisted but wasn't." Find evidence that could support each inference and evaluate which is better supported by the full text.
2. **Cross-text inference:** Both *Night* and the "Letter from Birmingham Jail" involve people facing unjust systems. What does comparing them allow you to infer about what individuals can do against systemic evil — and what they cannot? Support this synthesis inference with evidence from both texts.
3. **Ambiguity:** Some texts are deliberately ambiguous. Identify a passage in one of the reading library texts where two different inferences are equally well-supported by the evidence. Explain what this ambiguity tells you about the author's purpose.

**4.0 Example:** A student reading Wollstonecraft's *Vindication* argues that the deepest inference from the text is that Wollstonecraft does not argue for equality with men — she argues that women who act the way men expect them to act (vain, emotional, dependent) have been *made that way* by their education and circumstances. This is a different argument than "women are equal" — it's "women are the products of an unjust system, and the system must change." The student supports this with multiple passages across the text, shows why the simpler "she argued for equality" inference is incomplete, and explains why the fuller inference changes how you read the whole text.

## Demonstration of Learning — Choose One

**How will you show you know this standard?** Pick one mode.

| # | Mode | What You Do for This Specific Standard |
|---|------|----------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Written Assessment** | Choose a 2–3 paragraph passage from any reading library text. Write: (1) a summary of what it literally says, (2) an inference about what it implies, (3) at least two pieces of textual evidence with quotation, (4) your reasoning connecting evidence to inference. |
| 2 | **Extended Writing** | Write a 300–400 word inference-based analysis of one text from the reading library. Identify the inference you're making (NOT just summary), provide multiple pieces of evidence, and explain your reasoning. Address the most obvious alternative inference and explain why yours is better supported. |
| 3 | **Verbal Conversation** | Record yourself doing a close read of a passage. Say aloud: what you notice, what inference you're drawing, what evidence supports it, and what your reasoning is. Show the difference between summary and inference. |
| 4 | **Visual/Creative** | Create an "Inference Map" for one text. In the center: your inference about the text's deeper meaning. Branching out: the evidence (specific quotes). Connecting arrows annotated with: the reasoning. On the side: the alternative inference you considered and why you rejected it. |
| 5 | **Multimedia/Performance** | Record a 2-minute "Close Read" video. Open to a specific passage, read it aloud, then explain: (1) what it says literally, (2) your inference, (3) specific evidence, (4) your reasoning. Show the thinking process, not just the conclusion. |
| 6 | **Portfolio Annotation** | Select a previous project where you read a primary source or literary text. Write a 150-word reflection: What inferences did you draw? What evidence did you use? If you were to revise, what inference would you make now that you didn't make then? |

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Choose a passage from any reading library text. Write a 2–3 sentence summary of what it says literally. Identify one word or phrase you needed to use context clues to understand.
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Write an inference-evidence-reasoning response: "I infer [X] because [evidence] and the reasoning connecting them is [Y]." Make sure the inference is not stated directly in the text.
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Write 150 words identifying a competing inference to the one you made, explaining what evidence could support it, and defending why your inference is better supported.

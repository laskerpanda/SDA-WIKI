# Argument Writing — Skinny

**Standard:** 9-10.W.4 — Write to persuade an audience by establishing relevant context, stating a clear position/thesis, incorporating valid and reliable evidence from a variety of sources to support specific claims and to refute counterclaims, and using logical reasoning to avoid fallacies
**Applies across:** All studios — the most frequently used writing standard
**Integrated with:** W.2 (organization), W.6 (process), R.9 (argument analysis), IR.1–5 (research)
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes (includes drafting)
**Subject:** ELA — Writing

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Argument writing is not just "writing about something you believe." It is a structured attempt to change what a specific reader thinks or does, using evidence and logic — not emotion, assertion, or insult. At the 2.0 level, you can write a basic argument with a position and some evidence. At the 3.0 level, you build a full argument that can hold up against opposing views.

- **Argument (Academic):** A claim supported by evidence and reasoning, designed to persuade a specific audience. Academic argument is NOT a fight — it is a structured case.
- **Claim / Thesis / Position:** The central statement you are arguing — what you want the reader to believe or accept. A good thesis is: (1) specific (not vague), (2) arguable (reasonable people could disagree), (3) significant (worth arguing about), and (4) stated in a way that implies or previews your reasoning.
- **Evidence:** Facts, data, expert opinion, examples, or textual quotations that support your claim. Evidence must be valid (from reliable sources) and relevant (actually connects to your claim).
- **Reasoning:** The explanation of HOW the evidence supports the claim — the logical link. Evidence without reasoning is just a list of facts; reasoning without evidence is just assertion. The combination is argument.
- **Counterclaim:** The strongest opposing position — what someone who disagrees with you would say. Addressing the counterclaim shows you've considered the other side and strengthens your own argument.
- **Refutation:** Your response to the counterclaim — explaining why the opposing position is wrong, incomplete, or less well-supported than your own.
- **Logical Fallacy:** A flaw in reasoning that makes an argument invalid, even if the conclusion might be correct.
  - *Ad hominem:* Attacking the person instead of the argument
  - *Straw man:* Misrepresenting the opposing argument to make it easier to attack
  - *False dilemma:* Presenting only two options when more exist
  - *Slippery slope:* Claiming X will inevitably lead to extreme Y without showing why
  - *Appeal to authority (inappropriate):* Citing an expert outside their area of expertise
- **Relevant Context:** Background information that helps the reader understand why the question matters and what's at stake.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Write a thesis that is arguable (someone could reasonably disagree), specific (not vague), and significant.
- [ ] Provide at least two pieces of evidence from different sources, each properly introduced and explained.
- [ ] Write at least one complete counterclaim (what the other side would say) and refute it.
- [ ] Explain the reasoning connecting each piece of evidence to your claim.
- [ ] Identify and avoid at least one logical fallacy.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who writes: "I think the atomic bomb was justified because it ended the war faster and saved lives" — without yet providing evidence beyond assertion, acknowledging the strongest counterargument (the bomb was not the only way to end the war and targeted civilians), or addressing whether "saving lives" is an adequate justification for deliberately killing 200,000 civilians.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**9-10.W.4 — Write to persuade with a clear position, valid evidence, counterclaim refutation, and logical reasoning**

To meet this standard, your argument must include all the components and use them correctly: a specific arguable thesis, evidence from multiple reliable sources, explicit reasoning connecting evidence to claim, a genuine counterclaim (not a straw man), and a refutation that actually addresses the opposing argument.

**3.0 Example:**
A student arguing that the **Versailles Treaty's punitive terms** were more responsible for WWII than Hitler's personal ideology writes: **Thesis:** "While Hitler's ideology was necessary for WWII, it was not sufficient — without the specific economic and political destabilization created by the Versailles Treaty, Hitler would have remained a fringe figure rather than a national leader." **Supporting claim 1:** German hyperinflation and unemployment created by war reparations gave extremist movements an audience (evidence: German unemployment statistics 1930–1933). **Supporting claim 2:** The "war guilt clause" provided a nationalist grievance that Hitler exploited (evidence: Hitler's Mein Kampf citations of Versailles). **Counterclaim:** Some historians argue that Versailles was actually lenient by historical standards, and that the real cause was Hitler's specific ideology and organizational genius. **Refutation:** Even if Versailles was lenient in absolute terms, it was perceived as unjust by a majority of Germans — and political grievance is about perception, not objective comparison. The student's reasoning is explicit throughout: connecting each piece of evidence to the thesis and explaining why the counterclaim doesn't disprove the argument.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student writes: "Versailles caused WWII because it made Germany angry." (Assertion, no evidence, no counterclaim.)
A 3.0 student constructs the full argument with thesis, evidence + reasoning, genuine counterclaim, and refutation.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, your argument must address the strongest version of the opposing position — not a straw man — and acknowledge the genuine complexity of the question while still defending a clear position.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Steel-manning the opposition:** Before writing your argument, write the strongest possible case for the opposing position — the best version, not the weakest. Then write your argument so it genuinely addresses this strong opposing case.
2. **Qualified claim:** Some questions don't have a clean yes/no answer — the honest argument requires a qualified claim. ("Was the Haitian Revolution successful? Yes — in achieving independence. No — in achieving the economic freedom that true liberation would require.") Write an argument with a qualified, nuanced thesis that does justice to the complexity.
3. **Argument from analogy:** One type of reasoning uses historical parallels: "Appeasement failed in 1938; therefore, appeasement will fail today." Analyze the limits of this reasoning. When are historical analogies valid evidence? When are they misleading?

**4.0 Example:** A student argues that MLK was *wrong* to be optimistic about white moderate support in the Letter from Birmingham Jail. The student steel-mans King's position: King was writing to persuade, and optimism about his audience's potential is a valid rhetorical choice. But the student then argues: King's critique of the white moderate as "more devoted to order than justice" is ultimately more accurate than his hope for their transformation — and the subsequent history of civil rights backlash (white flight, anti-busing movements, Southern political realignment) supports this darker reading. The student acknowledges this is a complex historical judgment, provides specific evidence, and defends the claim while acknowledging what MLK got right.

## Reading Library Texts as W.4 Models

These texts are exemplary argument models — read them as *how to argue*, not just *what they argue*:
- [MLK — Letter from Birmingham Jail](../../reading-library/social-studies/mlk-letter-birmingham.md) — Counterclaim and refutation throughout; ethos, logos, pathos working together; qualified claims ("I did not engage in direct action without first seeking to negotiate")
- [Common Sense — Thomas Paine](../../reading-library/social-studies/common-sense-paine.md) — Progressive escalation of claim; demolishing the "stability" argument for monarchy; appeals to colonial self-interest
- [Wollstonecraft — Vindication](../../reading-library/social-studies/wollstonecraft-vindication.md) — Logic-first argument; acknowledging what opponents have gotten right before dismantling their conclusions

## Demonstration of Learning — Choose One

**How will you show you know this standard?** Pick one mode.

| # | Mode | What You Do for This Specific Standard |
|---|------|----------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Written Assessment** | Write a 4–5 paragraph argument on a question connected to your current studio. Must include: (1) thesis with context, (2) at least two body paragraphs with evidence + reasoning, (3) a genuine counterclaim paragraph with refutation, (4) a conclusion. |
| 2 | **Extended Writing** | Write a 400–600 word argument essay on a historically or currently contested question. Steel-man the opposition, then construct your argument so it genuinely addresses the strongest version of the opposing case. |
| 3 | **Verbal Conversation** | Record a 3-minute spoken argument: (1) state your thesis, (2) give your strongest piece of evidence with reasoning, (3) state the best opposing argument, (4) refute it, (5) conclude. No reading from a script — arguing, not reciting. |
| 4 | **Visual/Creative** | Create an "Argument Blueprint": a visual outline of your argument showing: thesis box, evidence + reasoning boxes (color-coded by source), counterclaim box, refutation box, and conclusion. Annotate each box with your actual content. Use the blueprint to self-assess: Is each connection between boxes explained? |
| 5 | **Multimedia/Performance** | Create a 2–3 minute "Debate Position" video. Present your argument on one side of a contested historical question. Show: your thesis, your evidence, your response to the strongest counterargument. Assume your audience disagrees with you and that you need to change their mind. |
| 6 | **Portfolio Annotation** | Select a previous argument piece. Write a 200-word reflection using this checklist: Does the thesis make an arguable claim? Is there evidence from at least two sources? Is the reasoning explicit? Is there a genuine counterclaim (not a straw man)? Is there a refutation? What would you revise? |

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Write a thesis for an argument. Then test it: Is it arguable? (Can someone reasonably disagree?) Is it specific? (Could you tell me which evidence you'd need?) Is it significant? (Does it matter?) Revise if it fails any test.
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Write a counterclaim + refutation paragraph. First, write the strongest version of the opposing argument (not a straw man). Then write your refutation — explaining specifically why this argument is wrong, incomplete, or less well-supported than your own.
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Write 200 words acknowledging what is genuinely uncertain or complicated about your argument. What evidence would change your mind? What is the strongest piece of evidence your opponent has? How do you deal with it honestly?

# Language Usage — Skinny

**Standard:** 9-10.L.1 — Demonstrate command of the conventions of standard English grammar and usage when writing or speaking; use language choices appropriate to task, purpose, and audience
**Applies across:** All studios — the baseline standard active in every piece of writing and every presentation
**Integrated with:** W.1 (clarity), W.3 (explanatory), W.4 (argument), C.1 (formal presentation)
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** Applied throughout all writing — not a standalone task
**Subject:** ELA — Language

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Language usage is not just about following grammar rules — it is about controlling the impression your writing makes. Errors signal carelessness; appropriate register signals that you know your audience and purpose. At the 2.0 level, you can apply standard conventions correctly. At the 3.0 level, you use language actively as a tool — choosing constructions, register, and phrasing for their effect, not just their correctness.

### Grammar and Usage Conventions

- **Subject-Verb Agreement:** The subject and verb must agree in number. *The evidence [suggests], not suggest.* *The causes [are], not is.*
- **Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement:** A pronoun must agree with the noun it replaces. *Each student must complete [their] work* (singular "their" is acceptable in standard usage for gender-neutral reference). Watch for: "The government changed [its] policy" (not "their" — government is singular).
- **Verb Tense Consistency:** Don't shift tenses without a reason. Historical present is acceptable for literary analysis ("MLK [argues] in paragraph 3...") but must be used consistently throughout a piece.
- **Comma Splice:** Joining two independent clauses with only a comma is an error. Fix with: a period, a semicolon, or a coordinating conjunction (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so).
  - *Splice:* "The treaty failed, no one enforced it."
  - *Fixed:* "The treaty failed; no one enforced it." OR "The treaty failed because no one enforced it."
- **Fragment:** An incomplete sentence — missing a subject, a verb, or both. Sometimes used intentionally for effect; usually an error when unintentional.
  - *Fragment:* "A policy that many argued was too harsh and that historians now consider a turning point."
  - *Fixed:* "Many argued the policy was too harsh — a turning point historians still debate."
- **Modifier Placement:** Modifying phrases should be placed near what they modify. *Dangling modifier:* "Having read the treaty, its flaws were obvious." (Who read it? The flaws?) Fixed: "Having read the treaty, I found its flaws obvious."
- **Parallel Structure:** Items in a list or comparison should use the same grammatical form. *Broken:* "The causes were economic instability, rising nationalism, and governments had miscalculated." *Fixed:* "The causes were economic instability, rising nationalism, and government miscalculation."

### Register and Audience

- **Register:** The level of formality in your language. Academic writing uses formal register; casual conversation uses informal. The wrong register for the context is not just stylistic — it undermines credibility.
  - *Informal:* "Basically, the Weimar Republic was a mess and it basically fell apart pretty fast."
  - *Formal:* "The Weimar Republic's structural weaknesses — proportional representation, Article 48's emergency powers, and the crushing economic impact of reparations — made it vulnerable to collapse under sustained pressure."
- **Appropriate Vocabulary:** Using words precisely and appropriately for your audience. In academic writing: define technical terms the first time you use them; use field-specific vocabulary when it's more precise, not just to sound sophisticated.
- **Sentence Variety:** Varying sentence length and structure — long complex sentences for developed ideas, short sentences for emphasis. Monotonous sentence structure (all sentences the same length and structure) is harder to read.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Read your final draft aloud — you will hear errors you miss reading silently.
- [ ] Check every sentence for subject-verb agreement, especially in long sentences where the subject and verb are far apart.
- [ ] Check for comma splices wherever you have two independent clauses.
- [ ] Before editing, identify your audience and register — then check whether your language is appropriate.
- [ ] Use the grammar/spell-checker as a first pass, not a final pass — it misses many errors and flags correct constructions.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student whose writing demonstrates control of basic grammar conventions in short passages, but whose longer pieces show inconsistent tense, comma splices, and occasional register shifts — and who hasn't yet learned to read their own writing with the reader's eye rather than the writer's.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**9-10.L.1 — Command of standard English grammar and usage; language choices appropriate to task, purpose, and audience**

To meet this standard, your writing must demonstrate not just rule-following but intentional language choice — using grammar as a tool, varying register deliberately, and showing that you know when a rule applies and when a stylistic choice is appropriate.

**3.0 Example:**
A student writing a formal argument essay about the Versailles Treaty uses:
- Consistent historical present for analysis ("the treaty [imposes] reparations that Germany [is] structurally unable to pay") and simple past for historical narrative ("Clemenceau [insisted] on punitive terms");
- Formal register throughout, with vocabulary precision: "punitive" rather than "mean," "reparations" rather than "payment," "structural instability" rather than "problems";
- Sentence variety: "The Weimar Republic was structurally vulnerable. Its proportional representation system fragmented power among competing parties, none of which had the majority needed to govern effectively. This fragmentation, combined with Article 48's emergency powers provision, made authoritarian takeover not just possible but likely." (Short declarative → long complex explanation → synthesis sentence)
- When the student's argument shifts from explanation to evaluation, the register adjusts slightly — less neutral, more direct — to signal the shift in purpose.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student produces grammatically correct writing with consistent register.
A 3.0 student uses grammar and register as tools — varying sentence structure for effect, choosing vocabulary precisely, and matching language to the specific purpose and audience.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must demonstrate conscious stylistic choice — not just using grammar correctly, but using language to create specific effects, and being able to explain why specific choices serve your purpose.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Style analysis → style application:** Pick a paragraph from any reading library text and analyze the grammar and style choices: What kinds of sentences does the author use? What is the register? What is the effect of specific word choices? Then write a paragraph of your own in a similar style on a different topic — demonstrating that you can adopt and adapt another writer's techniques.
2. **Register-switching exercise:** Take a paragraph from your most recent essay and rewrite it for a completely different audience (a younger student, a policymaker, a skeptic who disagrees with your argument). What changes? What stays the same? This reveals which choices are about register and which are about content.
3. **Deliberate fragment or short sentence:** Find a place in your writing where a grammatically unconventional choice (a fragment, a one-word sentence, a very long complex sentence) would serve the rhetorical purpose better than the conventional choice. Use it deliberately and explain why.

**4.0 Example:** A student revising an essay on the Holocaust notices that their writing becomes passive and distanced when describing the bureaucratic processes: "The victims were transported to the camps. Records were maintained. Selections were performed." The student recognizes that passive voice here is actually a problem — it mirrors the language of the perpetrators who used passive constructions to depersonalize their actions. The student revises to active voice with named subjects: "SS guards separated arriving prisoners into two groups. Administrators recorded names and numbers. Officers decided who would work and who would die immediately." The revision is not just grammatically more active — it is ethically more honest. The student can explain the choice.

## Language Patterns to Know

**Passive vs. Active Voice:**
- *Passive:* "The war was started by the Austro-Hungarian government's ultimatum." (what happened to whom)
- *Active:* "The Austro-Hungarian government started the war with an ultimatum designed to be rejected." (who did what)
Active voice is usually clearer and more direct. Passive voice is appropriate when the actor is unknown, unimportant, or when the thing acted upon is the important element ("Millions were killed by the Nazi regime" — the victims are the emphasis, not the perpetrators).

**Subordination and Coordination:**
- *Coordination* (equal ideas): "The revolution succeeded, and its legacy shaped future liberation movements."
- *Subordination* (one idea depends on another): "Although the revolution succeeded politically, its economic legacy was shaped by the reparations France imposed."
Subordination shows relationships between ideas; overusing coordination flattens everything to equal importance.

**Word Precision:**
- Affect (verb: to influence) vs. Effect (noun: a result): "The famine [affected] migration patterns"; "The [effect] of the famine was mass emigration."
- Then (sequence) vs. Than (comparison): "First the siege, [then] the surrender." "The French army was larger [than] expected."
- Which (non-restrictive, adds information) vs. That (restrictive, identifies): "The treaty[, which] was signed in 1919[,] contained 440 articles." (removable) vs. "The treaty [that] ended WWI contained 440 articles." (identifies which treaty)

**How will you show you know this standard?** Pick one mode.

| # | Mode | What You Do for This Specific Standard |
|---|------|----------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Written Assessment** | Submit a final draft of any piece of writing alongside a "Grammar Annotation": mark every sentence where you made a deliberate grammatical or stylistic choice (not just following default rules), and write a brief note explaining the choice and its intended effect. |
| 2 | **Extended Writing** | Write a 300-word analysis of the language choices in any reading library text. Focus on: register, sentence variety, specific word choices and their connotations, one grammatical construction used for effect. Then write a paragraph of your own in a similar style on a different topic. |
| 3 | **Verbal Conversation** | Read a 2-minute passage from any reading library text aloud, then analyze: What is the register? What kind of sentence structures does the author use? What specific word choices create the strongest effect? How do these choices serve the purpose? |

Where did you make deliberate sentence structure choices? What word choices would you revise, and what would you replace them with? Why? |

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Take any paragraph from your writing and do a grammar check: mark every verb and check tense consistency; mark every place you join two independent clauses and check for comma splices; identify the register (formal or informal).
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Rewrite one paragraph from your writing with deliberate sentence variety: (1) a short sentence for emphasis, (2) a long complex sentence with subordination, (3) a sentence that begins with a subordinating clause. Compare your revised version to the original — what effect does the variety create?
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Choose one word in your writing that you used for precision (a technical term or a word with specific connotations). Write 50 words explaining: What alternatives did you consider? Why did you choose this word? What would be different if you had used a more common synonym?

# Main Idea, Claim, and Theme — Skinny

**Standard:** 9-10.R.4 — Determine main idea(s), claim(s), or theme(s) as they develop over the course of the text and support with textual evidence
**Applies across:** All studios — active whenever reading a full text or extended primary source
**Integrated with:** W.3 (explanatory), W.4 (argument), R.2 (comprehension/inference)
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 45–60 minutes
**Subject:** ELA — Reading

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Main idea, claim, and theme are three related but different concepts. Getting the distinction right is essential for reading accurately — the type of text determines which term applies.

- **Main Idea:** The central point of a nonfiction informational text — what the author most wants you to understand. Example: The main idea of Hammurabi's Code is that a just ruler establishes consistent, written laws that apply to everyone.
- **Claim:** The central argument being made in a persuasive or argumentative text — the position the author wants you to accept. Example: MLK's central claim in "Letter from Birmingham Jail" is that nonviolent direct action is the morally correct and strategically necessary response to injustice.
- **Theme:** The central insight or truth about human experience explored in a literary text — not the plot, but the underlying idea. Example: A theme of *Night* is that systematic dehumanization can erode an individual's sense of self even while they resist it.
- **Supporting Evidence:** The specific passages, examples, or details the author uses to develop the main idea, claim, or theme over the course of the text.
- **Development:** How the main idea, claim, or theme changes, deepens, complicates, or resolves over the full arc of the text. Development means the central idea isn't stated once and repeated — it grows.
- **Central vs. Supporting:** Not every idea in a text is the main one. Distinguishing between the central idea and supporting ideas is a core reading skill.
- **Explicit vs. Implicit:** Some texts state their main idea/claim directly ("I am writing to argue that..."). Others (especially literary texts) present a theme through what happens to characters — you must infer it.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Identify whether a text is informational (→ main idea), argumentative (→ claim), or literary (→ theme).
- [ ] State the main idea/claim/theme in one precise sentence — not just a topic ("justice") but a complete idea ("MLK argues that justice delayed is justice denied").
- [ ] Identify at least three specific passages where the author develops the central idea.
- [ ] Explain how the central idea has deepened or changed by the end of the text compared to its beginning.
- [ ] Distinguish the central idea from the supporting details.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can state that the theme of *Night* is "the Holocaust was terrible" — instead of identifying a specific theme about human experience ("the systematic destruction of identity precedes the destruction of the body") and tracing how that theme develops across the text with specific evidence.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**9-10.R.4 — Determine main idea(s)/claim(s)/theme(s) as they develop over the course of the text and support with textual evidence**

To meet this standard, you must identify the central idea AND show how it develops — tracing it through specific passages across the full text, from beginning to end.

**3.0 Example:**
Reading Wollstonecraft's *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*, a student achieves a 3.0 by identifying the central claim: **Women's apparent inferiority to men is not natural — it is produced by an education system that deliberately keeps women ignorant, vain, and dependent.** The student then traces how this claim develops: early in the text, Wollstonecraft establishes that "femininity" as currently defined is a set of cultivated weaknesses, not natural traits. In the middle sections, she analyzes how education specifically suppresses women's rational development. In the conclusion, she argues for a complete restructuring of education to allow women to develop reason and virtue. The student shows how the claim deepens — from "women are treated unjustly" to "the educational system is the mechanism of that injustice" to "the solution requires structural change."

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student states: "Wollstonecraft argues that women deserve equal rights."
A 3.0 student identifies the specific mechanism in the claim (education produces inferiority) and traces how that argument develops across the full text.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate how the author develops the central idea — not just identify and trace it, but assess the choices the author made and what effect those choices have on meaning.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Multiple themes in one text:** *Night* has more than one theme — identify at least two and show how they are both developed across the text. Are they in tension with each other, or do they reinforce each other?
2. **Claim that complicates itself:** MLK's "Letter from Birmingham Jail" makes a central claim about nonviolent direct action — but the letter also anticipates and addresses counterarguments, which complicates the simple version of the claim. Trace the full argument: What is the central claim, how does it develop, and how do MLK's responses to objections deepen or complicate it?
3. **Theme vs. message:** There's a difference between a literary theme ("identity can survive extreme dehumanization") and a moral message ("resist oppression"). Analyze a text to distinguish the two: What is Orwell *showing* (theme), and what is Orwell *saying we should do* (message)?

**4.0 Example:** A student analyzes how Paine's central claim in *Common Sense* evolves across the pamphlet. The early sections make a relatively modest claim: monarchy is an irrational form of government. The middle section deepens it: the British constitutional monarchy in particular is illegitimate because it violates natural rights. The final sections radicalize it: independence is not just justified but morally obligatory — delay is betrayal. The student argues that this *progressive intensification* of the claim is Paine's deliberate rhetorical strategy, designed to bring a reader from "well, monarchy is flawed..." to "we must declare independence immediately" by escalating logical and emotional pressure. The student evaluates whether this strategy is intellectually honest or manipulative.

## Demonstration of Learning — Choose One

**How will you show you know this standard?** Pick one mode.

| # | Mode | What You Do for This Specific Standard |
|---|------|----------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Written Assessment** | Choose one text from the reading library. Write: (1) one sentence stating the central main idea, claim, or theme precisely, (2) a brief description of how it appears at the beginning, (3) how it develops or deepens in the middle, (4) where it arrives by the end. Use at least three specific passages as evidence. |
| 2 | **Extended Writing** | Write a 300–400 word analysis of how a central theme or claim develops across one full text. Show how the idea changes, deepens, or complicates from beginning to end. Avoid summary — focus only on the development of the central idea. |
| 3 | **Verbal Conversation** | Record yourself explaining: (1) What type of text it is and what central concept applies (main idea, claim, or theme), (2) the central idea stated precisely, (3) three moments in the text where it develops, (4) how the idea has changed from beginning to end. |
| 4 | **Visual/Creative** | Create a "Development Arc" visual for one text. Show the central idea at the beginning, key development moments, and where it arrives at the end. Annotate each moment with a specific passage from the text. |
| 5 | **Multimedia/Performance** | Record a 2-minute "Literary Commentary" video. Open the text, read a passage from the beginning, middle, and end, and explain how the central idea you've identified is different (deeper, more complex, more urgent) at each point. |
| 6 | **Portfolio Annotation** | Select a previous project where you wrote about a text's main argument or theme. Write a 150-word reflection: Did you identify the central idea precisely (one sentence, complete thought) or vaguely (one topic word)? Did you trace its development, or just state it and move on? What would you add? |

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Choose a text. Identify whether its central concept is a main idea, claim, or theme — and explain why. State it in one complete sentence (not a topic word).
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Write a paragraph tracing how the central idea develops from beginning to middle to end in one text. Use at least three specific passages as evidence.
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Write 150 words evaluating the author's choices in developing the central idea. What strategy did they use? Was it effective? Was it intellectually honest?

# Age of Revolutions: Legacies to the Present — Skinny

**Standard:** WH.6_12.6 — Explain how past events connect to the present
**Era/Unit (if SS):** Era 3 — Age of Revolutions (~1400–1900 CE)
**ELA Crosswalk:** 9-10.W.4 — Argument writing; 9-10.IR.1–5 — Research and source evaluation
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes
**Subject:** World History + ELA crosswalk

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Era 3 produced the ideological, political, and economic frameworks that still govern the modern world. Democratic constitutions, capitalism, industrialism, modern racism, global inequality, and anti-colonial movements — all have direct roots in this era. Understanding these legacies is essential for understanding why the world looks the way it does today.

- **Democratic Tradition:** The American and French Revolutions produced the template for modern democratic constitutions. The U.S. Constitution (1787) and French Declaration of Rights of Man (1789) are ancestors of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) and virtually every modern democratic constitution.
- **Capitalism:** The economic system in which private individuals own the means of production and compete in markets. The Industrial Revolution produced modern capitalism; the ideological justification came from Adam Smith's *Wealth of Nations* (1776). Modern globalization, multinational corporations, and wealth inequality are all descendants of Industrial Revolution capitalism.
- **Modern Racism:** The ideology that divides humans into ranked "races" based on physical characteristics and assigns different rights and worth based on race. Modern racial ideology was systematically developed in the 17th–19th centuries primarily to justify the Atlantic Slave Trade and later colonialism.
- **Anti-Colonial Movements:** Resistance to European imperialism, rooted in Era 3. The Haitian Revolution (1804) was the first successful anti-colonial independence movement; 20th-century decolonization (India, Algeria, Congo) used arguments and strategies with roots in Era 3 revolutionary ideology.
- **Women's Rights Movement:** Mary Wollstonecraft's *Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792) is the founding document of modern feminism. The 19th–20th century suffrage movement, 1960s feminist movement, and contemporary gender equality debates all draw on arguments Wollstonecraft articulated in response to the French Revolution.
- **Global Inequality:** The economic gap between the industrialized Global North and the Global South (former colonial territories) was created during Era 3. The wealth extracted through colonial trade, the Atlantic Slave Trade, and industrial production for colonial markets built the economic advantage that still separates wealthy from poor nations.
- **Environmental Impact:** The Industrial Revolution introduced fossil fuel burning at scale. The CO₂ currently trapping heat in the atmosphere includes molecules emitted by 19th-century British coal burning. Climate change is an Era 3 legacy.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Identify at least three modern realities with direct roots in Era 3.
- [ ] Explain the specific path from the Atlantic Slave Trade to modern racial ideology.
- [ ] Connect the Industrial Revolution to climate change as a causal chain.
- [ ] Identify the founding document of modern feminism and explain its argument.
- [ ] Explain how the Haitian Revolution's legacy connects to contemporary debates about reparations.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can state that the American Revolution produced democracy and the Industrial Revolution produced capitalism — without yet tracing the specific paths by which these systems created modern global inequality, or analyzing how Era 3 legacies shape specific contemporary debates.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.6 — Explain how past events connect to the present (Era 3)**

To meet this standard, you must trace the specific transmission path from an Era 3 event to a modern reality — not just assert that they are connected. You need to explain the mechanism: how did this thing survive, transform, and arrive in its current form?

**3.0 Example:**
A student achieves a 3.0 by tracing the legacy of the **Atlantic Slave Trade** to the modern racial wealth gap in the United States. The path: enslaved Africans produced the agricultural wealth that capitalized early American industrialization → Emancipation (1865) freed enslaved people without land, capital, or legal protection → Reconstruction promises were broken → Jim Crow system kept Black Americans economically subordinate for a century → discriminatory policies (redlining, exclusion from GI Bill benefits) prevented wealth accumulation in the 20th century → the current racial wealth gap in America (the median white family has ~8× the wealth of the median Black family) is the direct result of this centuries-long system. The student traces the specific mechanism from Era 3's slave trade to the 2026 racial wealth gap.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student knows slavery was wrong and that racism exists today.
A 3.0 student traces the specific economic mechanism linking the Atlantic Slave Trade to contemporary wealth inequality.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must critically evaluate how Era 3 legacies are interpreted and contested today — who invokes them, how they are used, and whether the modern invocation accurately represents the historical reality.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Founding documents today:** The U.S. Constitution and Declaration of Independence are regularly cited in contemporary political debates. Evaluate: Do the modern interpretations of these documents accurately reflect their historical context? Who was included vs. excluded from their original scope, and how has that scope changed over time?
2. **Climate legacy:** The atmospheric CO₂ causing climate change was primarily produced by the wealthy industrialized nations during Era 3 and Era 4. The countries most affected by climate change are primarily the former colonial territories of the Global South. Analyze: Is this a coincidence, or is it a direct consequence of the same colonial system that produced Era 3 industrialization?
3. **Reparations debate:** France required Haiti to pay 150 million gold francs in reparations for the property (including enslaved people) that Haitian revolutionaries "destroyed" when they freed themselves. Haiti finished paying this debt in 1947. Some argue France (and other nations) should pay reparations to Haiti and to descendants of enslaved Africans in the Americas. Analyze this debate using Era 3 history: What was Haiti's debt? Who benefited from slavery? Who bears continuing consequences?

**4.0 Example:** A student argues that the most important Era 3 legacy is one rarely named as such: the **fossil fuel economy**. The Industrial Revolution's adoption of coal burning as the primary energy source created an economic infrastructure — coal companies, railroad companies, industrial cities, global trade in manufactured goods — that became so embedded in the global economy that transitioning away from it has proven politically impossible for 150 years. The student then evaluates whether the current climate crisis is primarily a technological problem (we need better energy technology) or a political problem (we have the technology but lack the political will to disrupt Era 3's fossil fuel interests).

## Reading Library Connection

- [Common Sense — Thomas Paine (1776)](../../../reading-library/social-studies/common-sense-paine.md) — Paine's arguments about democracy and natural rights are still invoked in contemporary political debates. ## Deliverable Ideas (Your Studio Product)

**These are NOT the demonstration of learning. These are PBL artifact ideas.**

1. **Haiti's Debt: A Historical Justice Analysis** — Research France's demand that Haiti pay reparations for lost "property" (including enslaved people), Haiti's 150-year debt, and the contemporary reparations debate. Write a 1-page argument: What does Haiti's history tell us about the relationship between Era 3 colonialism and contemporary global inequality?
2. **"The Rights of Woman" Then and Now** — Create a comparative analysis of Wollstonecraft's 1792 arguments and a contemporary feminist text or movement. Identify what has been achieved, what remains contested, and what new arguments have been added. Include a 1-page written analysis.
3. **Industrial Revolution → Climate Crisis: The Causal Chain** — Build a documented causal chain from the first English coal-burning factories (~1760) to the current atmospheric CO₂ level (~420 ppm). For each major step, identify: who made the decision, what economic incentives were involved, and whether the decision-makers knew the risks. Conclude with a 1-paragraph argument about moral responsibility.

**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes
**Subject:** World History + ELA crosswalk

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Era 3 is when religious and ideological monopolies broke apart. The Protestant Reformation shattered Western Christian unity; the Enlightenment challenged the idea that religion should govern political life; the Age of Revolution produced new ideologies (liberalism, nationalism, later socialism) that competed directly with religious authority for people's ultimate loyalty.

- **Protestant Reformation (1517 CE):** Martin Luther's publication of his 95 Theses challenged the Catholic Church's authority — specifically the sale of indulgences. The Reformation split Western Christianity permanently into Catholic and Protestant branches.
- **Martin Luther:** German monk who argued (1) that salvation comes through faith alone, not through Church rituals or payments; (2) that the Bible, not the Pope, was the ultimate religious authority; (3) that all believers were equal before God — the "priesthood of all believers." The printing press made his ideas impossible to suppress.
- **Calvinism / Predestination:** John Calvin's doctrine that God had predestined (pre-selected) who would be saved and who would be damned — removing the Church's power to grant or withhold salvation. Calvinism spread to France (Huguenots), Scotland (Presbyterians), and England (Puritans, later Pilgrims).
- **Enlightenment (~1685–1815 CE):** A philosophical movement arguing that reason, not religious tradition, should guide human affairs. Key Enlightenment thinkers: Locke (natural rights), Rousseau (social contract), Voltaire (religious tolerance), Montesquieu (separation of powers). The Enlightenment provided the ideological foundation for the American and French Revolutions.
- **Natural Rights:** The Enlightenment concept (associated with John Locke) that all humans are born with inherent rights — life, liberty, property — that no government can legitimately take away. This idea directly inspired the American Declaration of Independence and French Declaration of the Rights of Man.
- **Secularism:** The principle that government and civic institutions should be separate from religion. The French Revolution's radical secularism (closing churches, executing priests, renaming months) was a product of Enlightenment ideology applied violently to political life.
- **Nationalism:** The ideology that a people sharing language, culture, and history should form their own sovereign nation-state. Nationalism emerged in the late 18th and 19th centuries as a rival to both religious identity and dynastic loyalty. It drove the unification of Italy and Germany and the breakup of the Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian empires.
- **Abolition:** The ideology and movement to end slavery. Abolitionists used both religious arguments (slavery violates the God-given dignity of humans) and Enlightenment arguments (slavery violates natural rights). The tension between these frameworks was central to abolitionist debates.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Explain what Luther's "95 Theses" specifically challenged about the Catholic Church.
- [ ] Distinguish between the religious arguments and the Enlightenment arguments for natural rights.
- [ ] Explain what "secularism" means and how it differs from atheism.
- [ ] Identify at least two modern political ideas that have roots in Enlightenment ideology.
- [ ] Explain what "nationalism" is and why it was a new ideology in the 18th–19th centuries.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can state that Martin Luther challenged the Catholic Church in 1517 and that the Enlightenment produced ideas about natural rights — without yet explaining how the Reformation's "priesthood of all believers" logic connects to democratic ideas, or how Enlightenment rationalism led directly to the French Revolution's violence.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.2 — Explain historical changes related to religions and ideologies (Era 3)**

To meet this standard, you must explain the causal connections between Era 3's ideological changes and their political consequences. How did a religious challenge (the Reformation) lead to political changes? How did a philosophical movement (Enlightenment) become a revolutionary force? How did new secular ideologies (nationalism, liberalism) compete with and transform religious ones?

**3.0 Example:**
A student achieves a 3.0 by explaining how the Protestant Reformation's core logic — that individuals could read and interpret the Bible themselves, without Church mediation — had political consequences that Luther himself did not intend. If ordinary people could interpret Scripture for themselves, why couldn't they also interpret law, or evaluate their rulers' legitimacy? The student traces the path from Luther's "priesthood of all believers" → Calvinist resistance theory (Protestants resisting Catholic monarchs) → English Civil War Puritans arguing for Parliament's authority over the king → Locke's natural rights theory (influenced by Puritan political thought) → American and French Revolutions. The student shows how a religious idea about Scripture interpretation became a political idea about popular sovereignty.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student knows the Reformation challenged the Church.
A 3.0 student traces how the Reformation's logic led to democratic political ideas — a consequence Luther feared and opposed.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate competing ideologies of Era 3 and assess what they got right and wrong — and how their competition shaped the modern world.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Ideology and violence:** Both the Protestant Reformation and the French Revolution were ideological movements that produced enormous violence (Thirty Years' War, Terror). Is violence an inherent product of ideological revolution, or is it a product of specific circumstances? Use both cases as evidence.
2. **Religion vs. Enlightenment:** The Enlightenment was critical of religion, and many Enlightenment thinkers were deists or atheists. But many abolitionists used religious arguments while Enlightenment ideas were used to *justify* slavery (e.g., arguments about "natural" racial hierarchy). Analyze: was the Enlightenment a progressive force? Were religious arguments more effective against slavery than secular ones?
3. **Nationalism's double edge:** Nationalism in Era 3 was often a force for liberation (independence movements, unification of fragmented peoples). By Era 4, it had become the force that produced World War I and the Holocaust. Explain how the same ideology could produce liberation in one century and catastrophe in the next.

**4.0 Example:** A student argues that the Enlightenment's "reason" was not the neutral, universal force its proponents claimed — it was a tool deployed by specific groups (white, male, European property owners) to argue for *their* freedom while simultaneously rationalizing the enslavement of Africans, colonization of indigenous peoples, and exclusion of women. The student cites Locke's investments in slave-trading companies and Jefferson's slave-owning as evidence that Enlightenment "natural rights" were systematically not applied universally, and argues that this selective application was not a contradiction of Enlightenment ideology but a feature of how it was practiced.

Analyze her argument as an example of using the dominant ideology to challenge the dominant ideology's own exclusions.

## Deliverable Ideas (Your Studio Product)

**These are NOT the demonstration of learning. These are PBL artifact ideas.**

1. **"The Logic of Rebellion" — Reformation to Revolution** — Trace the ideological pathway from Luther's "priesthood of all believers" (1517) to the American Declaration of Independence (1776) to the Haitian Revolution (1791). Show how the same core idea (individuals can interpret truth/authority for themselves) produced very different political revolutions depending on who was invoking it.
2. **Wollstonecraft's Challenge: Then and Now** — Analyze Wollstonecraft's argument in *Vindication* (1792). Write a 1-page analysis: what was her argument, why was it radical for its time, and where do the same arguments appear in contemporary feminist discourse?
3. **The Terror: When Ideology Goes Wrong** — Research the Reign of Terror (1793–1794). Write a 1-page argument: Was the Terror a betrayal of Enlightenment ideals, or was it a logical consequence of applying Enlightenment rationalism to political life without limits?

# The Age of Revolutions: Social & Economic Shifts

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Before we dive into the "why" of these revolutions, we need to define the "what." Use these terms as your foundation:

*   **Revolution:** A fundamental and sudden change in political power or social structure.
    *   *Political:* Changing who is in charge (e.g., overthrowing a King).
    *   *Social:* Changing how people live and their status in society (e.g., ending slavery).
    *   *Economic:* Changing how wealth is distributed or how goods are traded.
*   **The Estates System:** The social hierarchy in pre-revolutionary France. 
    *   *First Estate:* Clergy (church officials).
    *   *Second Estate:* Nobility (landowners/aristocrats).
    *   *Third Estate:* Everyone else (peasants, city workers, and the middle class).
*   **Bourgeoisie:** The "middle class" of the 18th century—merchants, lawyers, and manufacturers who had money but no political power.
*   **Sans-culottes:** Literally "those without breeches." These were the urban laborers and shopkeepers of the French Revolution who pushed for more radical changes than the wealthy bourgeoisie.
*   **Haitian Revolution:** The only successful slave revolt in history that resulted in the creation of an independent nation (Haiti).
*   **Toussaint Louverture:** A former slave and military leader who became the primary leader of the Haitian Revolution.
*   **Mercantilism:** An economic system where a mother country (like France) controlled trade and resources from colonies to build wealth. This created tension between the "mother" country and the "colonies."
*   **Social Mobility:** The ability for an individual to move up in social status or wealth. Revolutions often happen when social mobility is blocked by the ruling class.
*   **Primary Source Bias:** The idea that every historical document is filtered through the author's perspective. A wealthy merchant will describe a revolution differently than a starving peasant.
*   **Propaganda:** Information, especially of a biased or misleading nature, used to promote a particular political cause or point of view.

**2.0 Example:** *A student can identify that the Third Estate in France was unhappy because they paid all the taxes while the First and Second Estates held all the power.*

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

To reach a 3.0, you must move beyond just naming facts to **analyzing impact.** You need to explain how economic struggles (like high bread prices or unfair taxes) and social inequalities (like the lack of rights for commoners or enslaved people) actually drove people to take action.

**The Standard (WH.6_12.4):** Analyze how the economic causes of the French Revolution and the social causes of the Haitian Revolution differently affected individuals based on their status in society.

**3.0 Example:** *A student analyzes a primary source from 1791, such as a merchant's diary during the French Revolution, and explains how rising bread prices led to urban unrest among the sans-culottes, while noting that the author’s wealthy background makes them less likely to mention the specific hardships of the peasantry.*

**The Difference:**
*   **2.0:** "The French people were hungry because of bad harvests." (Fact)
*   **3.0:** "Because the Third Estate bore the entire tax burden while bread prices skyrocketed, members of the sans-culottes organized protests to demand economic relief from the monarchy." (Analysis of cause and specific group impact).

---

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must **synthesize** and **evaluate**. This means comparing two different revolutions and making a judgment about which one achieved more lasting change for the "common person."

**4.0 Example:** *A student compares the French Revolution's goal of "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity" with the Haitian Revolution’s goal of ending chattel slavery. They argue that while the French Revolution provided legal rights to the bourgeoisie, the Haitian Revolution achieved a more radical and permanent social shift by dismantling the entire economic system of slavery.*

## Reading Library Connection

*   [Declaration Rights Man 1789](/reading-library/social-studies/declaration-rights-man.md) — This document shows how the *bourgeoisie* wanted to move away from monarchy toward a system where wealth and talent (not just birth) determined status.
*   [Haitian Declaration Independence 1804](/reading-library/social-studies/haitian-declaration-independence.md) — This highlights the unique social urgency of the Haitian Revolution: the immediate need for freedom from slavery.
*   [Declaration Woman](/reading-library/social-studies/olympe-de-gouges/declaration-woman.md) — Use this to identify **bias**; it shows that even "revolutionary" documents often excluded women, revealing the limits of the revolution's social reach.

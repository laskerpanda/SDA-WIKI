## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Era 3 is the era of political revolution. Absolute monarchies were challenged and overthrown; republics were established; constitutions were written; empires were built and challenged. The central question of Era 3 is: what gives a government its legitimacy — divine right, military power, popular consent, or something else?

- **Absolute Monarchy:** A political system in which the monarch holds supreme, unchecked authority. The theoretical justification: kings rule by divine right — God ordained their authority, and challenging the king was challenging God. Louis XIV of France ("L'état, c'est moi" — "I am the state") was the model absolute monarch.
- **Divine Right of Kings:** The doctrine that monarchs derive their authority from God and are accountable to God alone — not to parliaments, courts, or subjects. Challenged by the Reformation (why should the king interpret God's will?) and the Enlightenment (why should God, not reason, determine political authority?).
- **Constitutional Monarchy:** A political system in which the monarch's power is limited by a constitution or parliament. England's Glorious Revolution (1688) produced a constitutional monarchy with the English Bill of Rights.
- **Republic:** A political system in which sovereignty rests with the people (or their elected representatives), not with a hereditary monarch. The United States (1776) and France (1792) both became republics after revolution — with very different results.
- **Social Contract:** The Enlightenment idea (Locke, Rousseau) that government derives its legitimacy from the consent of the governed. Locke argued that if a government violates natural rights, the people have the right to revolt. This idea directly justified the American and French Revolutions.
- **Colonialism:** The political and economic system by which European powers controlled territories and peoples in Africa, Asia, and the Americas. Colonialism was a political system from the colonized people's perspective — one imposed by conquest, maintained by force, and justified by racial ideology.
- **Revolution:** A rapid, fundamental change in political power and organization. Era 3 revolutions: the Glorious Revolution (England, 1688), the American Revolution (1776), the French Revolution (1789), the Haitian Revolution (1791–1804), and multiple Latin American independence movements.
- **Haitian Revolution (1791–1804):** The only successful slave revolution in history. Enslaved Africans in Saint-Domingue (Haiti) used French Revolutionary ideology (liberté, égalité, fraternité) against France itself to win independence. Haiti became the world's first Black republic — and was immediately punished economically by France and the United States.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Explain the difference between absolute monarchy and constitutional monarchy.
- [ ] Define the "social contract" and identify which thinker developed it.
- [ ] Explain why the Haitian Revolution was more radical than either the American or French Revolutions.
- [ ] Identify at least two effects of colonialism on colonized peoples.
- [ ] Distinguish between a republic and a democracy (all democracies are republics, not all republics are democracies).

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can describe the structure of absolute monarchy and state that the French Revolution overthrew the king — without yet analyzing how different political systems affected ordinary people's lives, or why the Haitian Revolution was a more radical challenge to the political order than the American or French Revolutions.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.3 — Analyze the effects of different political systems on people (Era 3)**

To meet this standard, you must analyze how specific political systems shaped individuals' lives — not just describe the systems. In Era 3, the key analytical question is: what did political revolution actually change for ordinary people, and for whom did it change nothing (or make things worse)?

**3.0 Example:**
A student achieves a 3.0 by analyzing how the American Revolution's political outcomes differed based on who you were. For wealthy white male property owners: the revolution produced a republic with legal rights, voting, and representation — a genuine political transformation. For enslaved Black Americans: the revolution changed nothing — the Constitution explicitly protected slavery, and many founders increased their enslaved workforces after independence. For Native Americans: the revolution was a catastrophe — the new republic was more aggressive in territorial expansion than the British crown had been. For women: they gained no political rights. The student analyzes the same political event — the creation of a republic — and shows its radically different effects based on social position.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student knows the American Revolution created a republic with democratic principles.
A 3.0 student analyzes who actually benefited from those principles — and who didn't.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate competing political systems and their outcomes across multiple cases — comparing revolutions, analyzing what determines whether political change is genuine or cosmetic, and connecting Era 3 political transformations to today.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Comparing revolutions:** The American and Haitian Revolutions both used "liberty" and "natural rights" as justifications. Analyze how the same ideology produced different outcomes — and why the Haitian Revolution was more threatening to the existing political order.
2. **Colonialism as a political system:** Analyze colonialism as a political system from the colonized people's perspective. What were its effects on individuals? How did colonized people resist, and what political ideologies did they use? (Hint: many anti-colonial movements used Enlightenment ideology against colonial powers — the same move Wollstonecraft made with gender.)
3. **Revolution and stability:** The French Revolution went from a constitutional monarchy to a republic to the Terror to Napoleon's empire in 10 years. The American Revolution produced a relatively stable republic. Analyze: what made the difference? Was it ideology, social conditions, external threats, or something else?

**4.0 Example:** A student argues that the Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) was the true test of Enlightenment political ideology — because it demanded that "all men are created equal" actually mean *all men*, including enslaved Africans. The student analyzes how the international response (France, Britain, and the United States all worked to isolate and economically punish Haiti after independence) reveals what the American and French Revolutions' political principles actually meant in practice: liberty for white men, hostility to liberty for everyone else. The student then connects Haiti's economic isolation to the country's ongoing poverty and argues that the political choices made in 1804–1825 have never been fully reversed.

**Key Timestamps (French Revolution ep):**
- **[05:00]:** The estates system — who had political power before the Revolution. *Why it matters:* The Revolution's starting point was a profoundly unequal political system; understanding what it overthrew helps you analyze what it actually changed.
- **[10:00]:** The Declaration of Rights of Man and Citizen — who was included. *Why it matters:* The Declaration's language was universal, but its application was not. This gap is the central political problem of Era 3.
- **[13:00]:** The Terror. *Why it matters:* Revolutionary political systems can become more oppressive than what they replaced. This is a pattern to analyze across revolutions.

**Watch Prompt:** For each political system change described, ask: (1) Who gained power? (2) Who lost power? (3) Who was not included in the new system at all?

## Reading Library Connection

- [Common Sense — Thomas Paine (1776)](../../../reading-library/social-studies/common-sense-paine.md) — Read as an argument *against* monarchy and *for* republican government. Identify Paine's specific arguments: What is wrong with monarchy? What makes a republic better? How does he respond to the "we've always had a king" argument?
- [Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen (1789)](../../../reading-library/social-studies/declaration-rights-man.md) — Read as a founding document of a new political system. What rights does it establish? What obligations does it impose on the government? Who does it exclude? Compare to the American Declaration of Independence.
- [Mary Wollstonecraft — Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792)](../../../reading-library/social-studies/wollstonecraft-vindication.md) — Wollstonecraft wrote this in direct response to the Declaration of Rights of Man. She asked: if these rights are natural and universal, why are women excluded? This is the most direct contemporary challenge to the new political systems' exclusions.

## Deliverable Ideas (Your Studio Product)

**These are NOT the demonstration of learning. These are PBL artifact ideas.**

Research what it promised vs. what it delivered for three specific groups: women, enslaved people, and poor workers. 2. **The Haitian Revolution: Why It Mattered** — Research the Haitian Revolution in depth. Create a visual presentation arguing why the Haitian Revolution was the most radical political challenge to the existing world order in this era — and why it was punished so severely by France and the United States.

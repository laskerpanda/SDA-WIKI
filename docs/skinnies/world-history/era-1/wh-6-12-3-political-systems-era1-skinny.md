# How Greek and Roman Politics Shape Our World — Skinny

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Before we can analyze how these systems changed history, you must understand the "building blocks" of political science. These are the terms used to describe how people organize power and make decisions.

**Key Terms:**
*   **Democracy:** A system of government where power is held by the people. 
    *   *Direct Democracy (Athens):* Citizens vote directly on laws and policies (e.g., "Everyone in the room votes today").
    *   *Representative Democracy (Modern):* Citizens elect officials to make decisions on their behalf.
*   **Republic:** A form of government where citizens elect representatives to rule, but it is not a "pure" democracy because not every citizen has an equal say in every decision.
*   **Senate:** A deliberative body or council of people who represent a region or group and make laws. (The Roman Senate was the primary governing body).
*   **Assembly (Ekklesia):** In ancient Athens, this was the gathering of all citizens to make decisions about the city-state.
*   **Citizenship:** The status of being a legal member of a community with specific rights (like voting) and responsibilities (like paying taxes or serving in the military). 
    *   *Note:* In both Athens and Rome, "citizenship" was exclusive—it did not include women, enslaved people, or most non-citizens.
*   **Oligarchy:** A government ruled by a small group of people (usually based on wealth or family status).
*   **Tyranny:** Rule by one person who takes power by force and uses it unfairly.
*   **Veto:** From the Latin *veto*, meaning "I forbid." It is the power to stop a law or action from happening.
*   **Checks and Balances:** A system that ensures no single branch of government becomes too powerful by giving other branches the ability to "check" (limit) its power.
*   **Primary Source:** An immediate, first-hand account of an event or time period (e.g., a speech given in 400 BCE, not a textbook written in 2024).

**Core Skills Checklist:**
- [ ] Define the difference between a Direct Democracy and a Republic.
- [ ] Identify who was included—and excluded—from political life in Athens.
- [ ] Explain how the Roman Senate functioned as a governing body.
- [ ] Identify the "Veto" as a tool used to balance power.
- [ ] Distinguish between an Oligarchy and a Democracy.

**2.0 Proficiency Example:** 
*A student at this level can identify that while both Athens and Rome influenced modern government, they were different: Athens practiced a direct democracy for its citizens, while Rome established a republic with a senate.*

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**Standard WH.6_12.3:** Analyze the effects of different political systems on people.

To meet this standard, you must move beyond just defining these terms; you must explain **how they affected real people.** You need to look at how a system like the Roman Republic or Athenian Democracy created winners and losers—specifically looking at how power was distributed among men, women, enslaved people, and non-citizens.

**3.0 Proficiency Example:** 
*A student at this level can analyze how the Roman Republic’s structure affected different groups differently. For example: "While the Roman Republic gave male citizens a voice through the Senate and Assemblies, it systematically excluded women and enslaved people from any political power, creating a hierarchy where only wealthy, land-owning men held the most influence."*

**The Difference:** 
A **2.0 student** can tell you what a "Senate" is; a **3.0 student** can explain how the existence of a Senate limited or expanded the power of certain groups of people in ancient times.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach the 4.0 level, you must perform **synthesis**. This means taking two different ideas and combining them to create a new insight about our world today.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1.  **Comparative Analysis:** Compare the Athenian "Assembly" with modern-day "Town Hall" meetings or local government sessions. How do both systems attempt to give people a voice?
2.  **Evolutionary Tracking:** Trace the concept of the "Veto" from its origins in the Roman Republic to its current use in the United States government (e.g., the Presidential Veto). 
3.  **Systemic Critique:** Argue which system—the Athenian Direct Democracy or the Roman Republic—provided a more stable foundation for a large, diverse population, and explain why.

**4.0 Proficiency Example:**
*A student at this level can synthesize these ideas: "While Athens' direct democracy allowed for immediate citizen participation, it was only sustainable for a small city-state. In contrast, the Roman Republic’s use of representative bodies like the Senate created a scalable system that influenced modern republics by balancing the need for representation with the practicalities of governing a large territory."*

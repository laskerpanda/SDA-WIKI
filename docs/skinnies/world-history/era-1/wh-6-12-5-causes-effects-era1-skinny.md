# Causes and Effects of Global Events in the Ancient World — Skinny

**Standard:** WH.6_12.5 — Analyze causes and effects of global events using primary and/or secondary sources
**Era/Unit (if SS):** Era 1 — Emergence of Civilizations and Religions Around the Globe
**ELA Crosswalk:** 9-10.W.4 — Argument writing; 9-10.IR.1–5 — Research and source evaluation
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes
**Subject:** World History + ELA crosswalk

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

History doesn't just "happen" — events have causes that explain why they occurred, and effects that show what changed as a result. In the ancient world, events that seemed local (a drought, a military defeat, a king's death) often had consequences that reached across entire regions.

- **Cause:** The reason(s) an event happened. Causes can be *immediate* (the direct trigger) or *underlying* (the deeper conditions that made the event possible).
- **Effect:** The result or consequence of an event. Effects can be *short-term* (immediate, visible changes) or *long-term* (changes that unfold over decades or centuries).
- **Primary Source:** A document, artifact, or record created by someone who directly witnessed or participated in an event. Examples: Hammurabi's Code, Egyptian papyrus records, Greek military accounts.
- **Secondary Source:** An interpretation or analysis of events created after the fact by someone who wasn't there. Examples: a textbook chapter on the fall of Rome, a documentary about ancient Greece.
- **Source Reliability:** How trustworthy a source is, based on factors like author's purpose, proximity to events, potential bias, and corroboration by other sources.
- **Empire:** A large political unit that controls many peoples, lands, and cultures under a single authority, usually through military conquest and political domination.
- **Plague/Epidemic:** A widespread disease outbreak. The **Antonine Plague** (~165–180 CE) killed 5–10 million people across the Roman Empire, destabilizing agriculture, the military, and trade.
- **Migration:** Large-scale movement of people from one region to another — often caused by drought, military defeat, famine, or political instability; often causes cascading effects in receiving regions.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Distinguish between an immediate cause and an underlying cause for a specific ancient event.
- [ ] Identify whether a source about an ancient event is primary or secondary, and explain what that distinction means for reliability.
- [ ] List at least two effects of a major ancient event (short-term and long-term).
- [ ] Explain what "multiple causation" means — that most historical events have more than one cause.
- [ ] Identify who wrote a primary source, who they wrote it for, and what that tells you about potential bias.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can state that Rome fell because of military invasions from Germanic tribes — without analyzing the underlying causes (economic instability, overextension, internal political conflict, plague) that made Rome vulnerable to those invasions.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.5 — Analyze causes and effects of global events using primary and/or secondary sources**

To meet this standard, you must use primary and/or secondary sources to support a cause-and-effect analysis. You're not just listing causes and effects — you're *arguing* for the most significant causes and *using sources as evidence* to support your analysis.

**3.0 Example:**
A student achieves a 3.0 by analyzing the **fall of the Western Roman Empire** with multiple causes: not just the Germanic invasions (immediate cause) but the **Antonine Plague** (which killed an estimated 10% of the population and depleted the military), the **economic strain of maintaining an overstretched empire** (taxation rose as the empire expanded, which drove small farmers off the land), and **internal political instability** (the "Crisis of the Third Century" saw 26 different emperors in 50 years). The student uses at least one primary or secondary source as evidence — for example, citing Tacitus's accounts of Germanic military pressure or citing a secondary historian's analysis of plague mortality data.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student lists Germanic invasions as the cause of Rome's fall.
A 3.0 student identifies multiple causes, ranks them by importance, and uses at least one source to support the analysis.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate the *quality* of your sources (not just cite them), synthesize multiple sources with conflicting interpretations, or draw connections between ancient cause-and-effect chains and contemporary events.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Source evaluation:** Find two different secondary sources with different interpretations of why Rome fell (e.g., economic decline vs. military weakness vs. cultural change). Evaluate which interpretation is better supported by primary source evidence. Which do you find more convincing, and why?
2. **Cross-civilization causation:** The Persian Wars (~490–479 BCE) had far-reaching effects on Greek culture — the victory produced the cultural confidence that fueled the Classical Age of Athens. Trace the chain from military event to cultural consequence, using primary sources (Herodotus, Thucydides) to support your analysis.
3. **Ancient to modern:** Identify an ancient cause-and-effect chain that is still visible in today's world. Example: Alexander the Great's conquests spread Greek language and culture across three continents — which is why the New Testament was written in Greek. What does this tell us about how long historical effects can last?

**4.0 Example:** A student argues that the **Antonine Plague** was the most under-recognized cause of Rome's decline by analyzing its multiple effects: military manpower shortage that required the empire to hire Germanic mercenaries (who later became the invaders), economic contraction from loss of agricultural labor, and destabilization of the succession system (Marcus Aurelius broke the "five good emperors" tradition by appointing his own son Commodus, partly because he feared the plague had shortened his own life). The student uses Galen's medical writings (primary source) and modern demographic estimates (secondary) to build the argument.

## Reading Library Connection

- [Hammurabi's Code](../../../reading-library/social-studies/hammurabi-code.md) — Use Hammurabi's Code as a primary source for cause-and-effect analysis. The laws themselves are evidence of problems the society faced: laws against debt slavery tell you debt slavery was common; laws about irrigation disputes tell you water rights were a source of conflict. What causes can you infer from the effects Hammurabi was trying to prevent?
- **Secondary source practice:** When you read any textbook or summary about an ancient event, ask: Is the author claiming one primary cause, or multiple? Is that claim supported by specific evidence, or just asserted?

## Deliverable Ideas (Your Studio Product)

**These are NOT the demonstration of learning. These are PBL artifact ideas.**

1. **The "Hidden Causes" Research Brief** — Choose one ancient event whose commonly-taught cause is an oversimplification (e.g., "Rome fell because of Germanic invasions"). Research three additional causes and write a 1-page brief arguing that the conventional explanation is incomplete. Cite at least three sources (at least one primary).
2. **Cause-and-Effect Timeline with Sources** — Build an annotated timeline of one ancient empire's rise and fall. For each turning point, note the causes, the effects, and cite the source where you found the evidence. Distinguish primary from secondary sources throughout.
3. **The "Ripple Effects" Documentary Script** — Write a 3-minute documentary script tracing one ancient event's effects across at least two civilizations and two centuries. Show how the ripples kept moving long after the original event.

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Choose one ancient event. List: one immediate cause, one underlying cause, one short-term effect, one long-term effect. Identify whether each source you used was primary or secondary.
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Write a paragraph analyzing multiple causes for one ancient event. Rank the causes by importance and defend your ranking with at least one specific piece of evidence from a primary or secondary source.
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Write a 200-word evaluation of two sources on the same ancient event. Which is more reliable and why? What does each source reveal that the other doesn't?

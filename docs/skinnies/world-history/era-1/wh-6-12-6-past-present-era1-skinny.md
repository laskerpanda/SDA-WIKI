# Ancient Foundations to the Present — Skinny

**Standard:** WH.6_12.6 — Explain how past events connect to the present
**Era/Unit (if SS):** Era 1 — Emergence of Civilizations and Religions Around the Globe
**ELA Crosswalk:** 9-10.W.4 — Argument writing; 9-10.IR.1–5 — Research and source evaluation
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes
**Subject:** World History + ELA crosswalk

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

The ancient world did not disappear when ancient civilizations fell. The decisions, systems, and ideas created thousands of years ago still shape the laws, governments, religions, and languages of today. To score at the 2.0 level, you need to identify specific ancient foundations that are still present in the modern world.

- **Legacy:** What a civilization left behind — the lasting contributions to law, culture, language, religion, or political systems that continue to influence later societies.
- **Democracy:** A political system in which citizens participate in governance. The Athenian version (5th century BCE) was limited to adult male citizens — but its principles of popular assembly, debate, and accountability influenced every democratic system that followed.
- **Republic:** A government in which elected representatives make decisions on behalf of citizens. The Roman Republic (509–27 BCE) developed the Senate, separation of powers, and the concept of civic virtue — all directly borrowed by the framers of the U.S. Constitution.
- **Common Law / Legal Tradition:** The principle that laws should be written down, publicly known, and consistently applied. Hammurabi's Code (~1754 BCE) and the Twelve Tables of Rome (~450 BCE) are ancestors of this tradition.
- **Monotheistic Religions:** Judaism, Christianity, and Islam — all rooted in the ancient Middle East, specifically in the Abrahamic tradition that emerged in the ancient Near East. ~4 billion people today practice a religion with ancient origins in this region.
- **Alphabet:** Most Western alphabets (Latin, Greek, Cyrillic) trace back to the **Phoenician alphabet** (~1050 BCE), which in turn influenced Greek, which influenced Roman/Latin, which became the basis for modern Western script.
- **Olympic Games:** Originally a Greek religious festival at Olympia (~776 BCE). The modern Olympic movement (1896–present) consciously borrowed the name, the multi-sport format, and the ideal of international peaceful competition.
- **Philosophical Tradition:** Greek philosophy — Socrates, Plato, Aristotle — created the frameworks for logic, ethics, political theory, and scientific method that still underlie Western intellectual traditions.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Identify at least three modern institutions, practices, or ideas with direct ancient roots.
- [ ] Explain what "legacy" means and why identifying it matters for historical understanding.
- [ ] Distinguish between a *direct* inheritance (e.g., the U.S. Senate was consciously modeled on Rome's) and an *indirect* influence (e.g., ancient Greek philosophy generally shaped Enlightenment thinking).
- [ ] Identify a modern religion that has ancient roots and name the specific ancient source.
- [ ] Read a modern law or government document and identify language or concepts with ancient precedents.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can state that the United States has a Senate because of ancient Rome — without explaining what specific features of the Roman Senate the founders consciously replicated, or why they chose Rome as a model rather than ancient Athens.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.6 — Explain how past events connect to the present**

To meet this standard, you must explain the *mechanism* of the connection — not just assert that the ancient world influenced the modern one. You need to trace the specific path by which an ancient idea, institution, or practice survived, was transmitted, was transformed, and arrived in today's world.

**3.0 Example:**
A student achieves a 3.0 by explaining how the **Roman concept of a republic** influenced the U.S. Constitution — tracing the specific path: Roman Republic (509–27 BCE) → preserved in Latin texts through the medieval period → rediscovered during the Renaissance → incorporated into Enlightenment political theory (Montesquieu, Locke) → James Madison and the Constitutional Convention explicitly cited Roman precedents in *The Federalist Papers* → the U.S. Senate, the concept of a republic vs. a direct democracy, and the separation of legislative/executive/judicial power all echo Roman design choices. The student doesn't just say "Rome influenced America" — they trace the transmission chain.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student knows the U.S. has a Senate like Rome.
A 3.0 student explains the specific path by which the Roman model survived, was transmitted, and was deliberately borrowed — and what was changed vs. kept.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must critically evaluate the connection: not just trace it, but assess whether the modern version is a genuine continuation, a selective borrowing, or a distortion of the ancient original. You might also identify ancient ideas that should inform modern debates but are being ignored.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Critical evaluation:** The founders cited ancient Rome as a model for the republic. But Rome was also a slave society whose republic lasted less than 500 years before becoming an empire. What did the founders borrow, what did they ignore, and what does that selective borrowing reveal about their own political goals?
2. **Religious legacy and conflict:** Three of the world's major religions (Judaism, Christianity, Islam) all have ancient roots in the same geographic region (the Levant). Trace how the shared origins of these faiths contributed to both cooperation and conflict across history — and explain how understanding those origins illuminates modern religious conflicts.
3. **What we forgot:** Identify one ancient idea or practice that has been lost or forgotten but could be valuable today. Argue why it matters. Examples: the Greek concept of *isonomia* (equality under the law for all citizens), the Iroquois Confederacy's democratic model, or ancient water management systems.

**4.0 Example:** A student argues that the Western legal tradition's concept of "innocent until proven guilty" is usually attributed to English common law but actually has roots in Roman legal procedure — specifically in the *Digest of Justinian* (534 CE), which codified the principle that "proof lies upon the one who affirms, not the one who denies." The student then evaluates what happened to that principle in the U.S. criminal justice system today (mass incarceration, plea bargain pressure), arguing that the nominal inheritance of the principle has been undermined in practice.

## Deliverable Ideas (Your Studio Product)

**These are NOT the demonstration of learning. These are PBL artifact ideas.**

1. **"Ancient DNA" of Modern Democracy** — Create an annotated political family tree tracing the concept of democratic governance from Athens → Rome → Magna Carta → Enlightenment → U.S. Constitution. For each node, include: what was inherited, what was modified, and what was lost.
2. **Living Religions Research Brief** — Research one of the three Abrahamic religions (Judaism, Christianity, or Islam) and trace its ancient origins. Create a 1-page brief: ancient roots, key texts that survived, major moments of transformation, and current global reach. Evaluate: what from the ancient version is still central today?
3. **The "What We Owe Ancient Rome" Argument** — Identify five specific features of modern American civic life that are directly inherited from ancient Rome (legal terminology, architectural style, Senate structure, concept of "republic," civic virtue). Write a 1-page argument about which inheritance has been most valuable and which has been most problematic.

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** List three modern institutions, practices, or concepts with direct ancient roots. For each, name the ancient civilization, the specific idea or institution, and its modern form.

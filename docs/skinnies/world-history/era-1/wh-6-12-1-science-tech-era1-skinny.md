# Ancient Science and Technology — Skinny

**Standard:** WH.6_12.1 — Analyze historical achievements related to science and technology
**Era/Unit (if SS):** Era 1 — Emergence of Civilizations and Religions Around the Globe
**ELA Crosswalk:** 9-10.W.4 — Produce clear, coherent argument writing; 9-10.R.9 — Analyze informational texts
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes
**Subject:** World History + ELA crosswalk

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Early civilizations didn't just survive — they invented. The technologies they created solved specific problems (feeding more people, tracking time, moving water) and ended up reshaping human society entirely.

- **Technology:** A tool, method, or system that humans develop to solve a problem or improve their ability to do work.
- **Agriculture:** The deliberate cultivation of crops and domestication of animals. The shift from foraging to farming (~10,000 BCE) is called the **Neolithic Revolution** — it made permanent settlements possible.
- **Irrigation:** A system of channels, canals, or dikes that moves water from a source to crops. Mesopotamian farmers used irrigation to farm land far from the Tigris and Euphrates rivers.
- **Cuneiform:** The world's earliest writing system, developed in Mesopotamia around 3200 BCE. Created by pressing a wedge-shaped reed into clay tablets; used for record-keeping.
- **Hieroglyphics:** Egyptian writing system using pictorial symbols; used on monuments, papyrus, and tomb walls.
- **Metallurgy:** The science of working with metals — smelting, alloying, shaping. The **Bronze Age** (~3300–1200 BCE) and **Iron Age** (~1200–550 BCE) are named for the dominant metals of each era.
- **Astronomy:** The study of stars, planets, and celestial cycles. Early astronomers in Mesopotamia and Egypt used the sky to create calendars and predict seasonal changes for planting.
- **Pythagorean Theorem:** The mathematical relationship a² + b² = c² in right triangles, formalized by Greek mathematician Pythagoras (~570–495 BCE); used in construction and navigation.
- **Aqueduct:** A Roman engineering structure that transported water from distant sources into cities. Roman aqueducts moved millions of gallons of water daily without pumps — purely by gravity.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Identify whether a technology is a *tool* (physical object) or a *system* (organized method or process).
- [ ] Explain what problem a specific ancient technology solved.
- [ ] Distinguish between technologies that spread within one civilization vs. those that spread across civilizations through trade or conquest.
- [ ] Identify the era in which a technology emerged (Bronze Age vs. Iron Age, etc.).
- [ ] Connect a technology to a primary source that demonstrates its use.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can explain that the wheel was invented in Mesopotamia around 3500 BCE and that it made transporting heavy goods faster — without yet explaining how that changed trade networks or social stratification.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.1 — Analyze historical achievements related to science and technology**

To meet this standard, you must move beyond naming inventions — you must analyze what they *achieved*. That means explaining the **problem the technology solved**, the **social or political changes it caused**, and **what that tells us about the civilization that created it**.

**3.0 Example:**
A student achieves a 3.0 by analyzing how **writing** (cuneiform) transformed Mesopotamian society beyond communication. Because scribes could now record laws, debts, and trade agreements, political authority could extend beyond what a ruler could personally oversee — written records made large-scale empire possible. The student explains both what writing did and what it *enabled*: bureaucracy, law codes (Hammurabi's Code), and long-distance trade with standardized contracts.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student identifies that Mesopotamians invented cuneiform and used it to keep records.
A 3.0 student explains why that invention made political and economic systems fundamentally different — that once you can write down laws and debts, you no longer need the king in the room.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must transfer your analysis across eras, civilizations, or time periods — connecting ancient technological achievement to consequences that echo today.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Cross-civilization comparison:** Compare how Egyptian and Roman engineering (pyramids vs. aqueducts) reflect each civilization's different priorities and political systems. What does each choice of technology reveal about what each society valued?
2. **Technology and inequality:** Analyze how the development of metallurgy (bronze, then iron weapons) changed military power — and how military advantage translated into political domination and social hierarchy.
3. **Long-term consequence:** The Neolithic Revolution (the shift to agriculture) produced surpluses, which enabled specialization of labor, which led to social stratification. Trace this chain from technology to social structure. Does the same pattern apply to a modern technology (e.g., the printing press or the internet)?

**4.0 Example:** A student produces an argument that the development of the **iron plow** was a more significant achievement than the wheel because it enabled agriculture to expand into previously unworkable land — which directly caused population growth, territorial expansion, and the intensification of slavery as a labor system to work new farmland.

## Deliverable Ideas (Your Studio Product)

**These are NOT the demonstration of learning. These are PBL artifact ideas.**

1. **The "Ancient Patent Office"** — Design patent applications for three ancient inventions (in the style of a real patent: problem statement, solution description, diagram, novelty claim). Include a 1-paragraph analysis of what each patent reveals about the civilization's priorities.
2. **Technology Timeline with Consequences** — Build an annotated timeline of 8–10 ancient technologies from 10,000 BCE to 500 CE. For each entry: name the technology, the civilization, the date, and one sentence about its most significant historical consequence.
3. **Comparative Engineering Report** — Write a formal 2-page "engineering analysis" comparing Egyptian pyramid construction to Roman aqueduct construction. Focus on: the problem each solved, the scale of organization required, what it reveals about each civilization's values and political structure.

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Identify and describe three ancient technologies (one from each of: agriculture, writing/record-keeping, and construction/engineering). For each, name the civilization and approximate date.
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Choose one technology and write a paragraph explaining both what it achieved AND one major social or political change it caused — beyond just "it made things easier."
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Write a 200-word argument connecting an ancient technology's development to a consequence still visible in today's world. Use at least one specific piece of evidence.

**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes
**Subject:** World History + ELA crosswalk

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

The medieval world built much of the institutional and cultural infrastructure that modern Western societies still use. From constitutional law to university systems to the architecture of European cities, the Middle Ages left a deeper mark on today than most people realize.

- **Constitutional Tradition:** The principle that political authority is limited by law. The **Magna Carta (1215)** is the first major document in the Western constitutional tradition establishing that the king was not above the law — a principle that runs through the English Bill of Rights, the U.S. Constitution, and every modern democratic constitution.
- **University System:** The medieval Catholic Church created the first European universities (Bologna, Oxford, Cambridge, Paris — all founded before 1215). The university model — faculty, degrees, disciplines, academic freedom — still shapes higher education today.
- **Common Law:** The system of law based on precedent (past court decisions), developed in medieval England. The U.S. legal system is based on English common law — which is based on medieval legal development.
- **Nation-State:** The concept that a specific people with shared language, culture, and history should have their own sovereign government. Nation-states didn't fully exist in the medieval period, but the seeds were planted as feudal kingdoms consolidated into proto-national identities (France, England, Spain).
- **Cathedral / Gothic Architecture:** Medieval Gothic cathedrals (Notre Dame de Paris, Cologne Cathedral, Westminster Abbey) are still standing, still functioning, and still visited by millions annually. The architectural techniques developed for cathedrals (pointed arches, ribbed vaults, flying buttresses) influenced later European architecture.
- **The Protestant Reformation (1517):** Began by Martin Luther at the very end of Era 2, the Reformation split Western Christianity into Catholic and Protestant branches. The religious conflicts it spawned (Thirty Years' War, English Civil War) shaped the political map of Europe that still exists. Most Protestant denominations in existence today trace back to the Reformation.
- **Printing Press and Mass Literacy:** Gutenberg's printing press (~1440) made mass literacy possible; the Bible was the first major book printed; the Reformation was driven by the ability to spread ideas cheaply. Mass literacy and mass media are still with us in transformed form.
- **The Black Death's Demographic Legacy:** The Black Death killed 30–50% of Europe's population — and shaped everything that came after it: labor markets, wages, social mobility, the conditions for the Renaissance. Modern understanding of how pandemics spread draws directly on Black Death research.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Identify at least three modern institutions with direct medieval roots.
- [ ] Explain what the Magna Carta established and why it matters for modern constitutional law.
- [ ] Connect a specific medieval event (Black Death, Fall of Constantinople, printing press) to a specific modern reality.
- [ ] Identify a modern religion that traces its specific form to the Protestant Reformation.
- [ ] Explain why the university system in its current form is a medieval invention.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can state that the Magna Carta was an important document about limiting royal power — without yet explaining the specific path from Magna Carta (1215) → English common law → English Bill of Rights (1689) → U.S. Constitution (1787) → modern American civil liberties.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.6 — Explain how past events connect to the present (Era 2)**

To meet this standard, you must trace the specific transmission path from a medieval event or institution to its modern form. "Things changed" is not enough — you need to explain *how* they changed, *who* transmitted them, and *what* was modified along the way.

**3.0 Example:**
A student achieves a 3.0 by tracing the constitutional tradition from the Magna Carta (1215) to the present. The student explains: the Magna Carta established that the king could not imprison free men without legal judgment and that the king could not impose taxes without baronial consent. These principles were largely ignored for centuries but were revived by English Parliamentarians in the 1600s as justification for limiting Charles I's power. The English Civil War and Bill of Rights (1689) codified these limits. The American colonists, educated in English common law and Parliament's arguments, cited Magna Carta in their arguments against British taxation. The U.S. Constitution's Fifth Amendment ("No person shall be...deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law") is direct descendant language. The student traces the specific path — 1215 → 1689 → 1787 — and identifies what was preserved, modified, and why.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student knows the Magna Carta was important.
A 3.0 student traces the specific transmission path and identifies what survived and why.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate the medieval legacies critically — not just identify that they exist, but assess whether they are being honored, distorted, or selectively invoked in modern contexts.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **The Magna Carta today:** The Magna Carta is cited as a foundation of civil liberties — but it was originally a document protecting the rights of wealthy barons, not ordinary people. Evaluate: Is citing Magna Carta as a source of democratic rights historically accurate? Who has benefited from this legacy, and who has been excluded?
2. **Religious legacy and conflict:** The Protestant Reformation split Western Christianity into factions that have been fighting — sometimes literally — ever since. The modern conflict between Catholics and Protestants in Northern Ireland (the "Troubles," 1968–1998) is a direct descendant of Reformation-era divisions. Trace this connection.
3. **Pandemic response:** Modern epidemiology (the science of how diseases spread) drew heavily on analysis of the Black Death. Evaluate: What lessons from the Black Death were applied to COVID-19 pandemic response? What lessons weren't applied, and with what consequences?

**4.0 Example:** A student argues that the medieval concept of academic freedom — the idea that universities are protected spaces for intellectual inquiry separate from direct Church or state control — is under pressure in modern contexts (government attempts to defund or politically influence universities, cancel culture debates, academic freedom cases). The student traces this principle from its medieval origins (the University of Bologna's student-controlled model, Oxford's faculty autonomy fights with the Church) to its current contested status, and argues that understanding the medieval origins clarifies what is and isn't being protected in contemporary debates.

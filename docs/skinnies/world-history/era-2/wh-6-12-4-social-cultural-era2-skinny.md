# Medieval Social, Cultural, and Economic Life — Skinny

**Standard:** WH.6_12.4 — Analyze the influence of social, cultural, and economic development on individuals
**Era/Unit (if SS):** Era 2 — Middle Ages and the Renaissance (~500–1600 CE)
**ELA Crosswalk:** 9-10.R.9 — Analyze informational texts; 9-10.W.3 — Explanatory/narrative writing
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes
**Subject:** World History + ELA crosswalk

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Medieval society was organized around obligations — lords owed protection, serfs owed labor, the Church owed salvation. Understanding these systems means understanding why individuals in medieval Europe had very different lives depending on where they were born in the hierarchy — and how the Black Death changed everything.

- **Feudalism:** The hierarchical system of land ownership and political allegiance: king → lords → knights → serfs. Land was the primary form of wealth; control over land determined almost everything else about a person's life.
- **Serf:** A peasant bound to the land in a feudal system. Unlike enslaved people, serfs were legally tied to a specific lord's land, not to the lord personally — but their freedom of movement, marriage, and occupation was severely restricted. Serfs made up the majority of the medieval European population.
- **Guild:** An organization of craftspeople or merchants who set standards, trained apprentices, regulated prices, and protected members' interests. Guilds were the medieval version of both a union and a professional licensing board.
- **The Black Death (~1347–1353 CE):** A bubonic plague pandemic that killed an estimated 30–50% of Europe's population in six years. The Black Death traveled along the Silk Road from Central Asia to Europe and devastated every social class — but its effects were socially transformative in ways that undermined feudalism.
- **Social Mobility:** Movement up or down the social hierarchy. Medieval society had very limited social mobility — a serf was expected to remain a serf. But the Black Death, paradoxically, created upward mobility by creating a severe labor shortage: surviving serfs could demand better wages and terms.
- **Renaissance:** The "rebirth" of classical learning and art, centered in Italy from approximately 1300–1600 CE. The Renaissance represented a cultural shift toward humanism, individual achievement, and secular values — a reaction against medieval religious hierarchy.
- **Patron:** A wealthy individual or institution that funded artists, writers, and scholars. The Medici family of Florence were the most famous Renaissance patrons; patronage systems shaped which art got made and whose ideas got spread.
- **Merchant Class:** The wealthy traders and bankers who accumulated wealth through commerce rather than land ownership. In the medieval and Renaissance periods, the merchant class gradually accumulated political power that rivaled the nobility — changing the social structure from below.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Describe the daily life of a serf vs. a lord vs. a guild member in medieval Europe.
- [ ] Explain how the feudal hierarchy determined what was possible for an individual's life.
- [ ] Identify the Black Death's most significant social effect (labor shortage → bargaining power for survivors).
- [ ] Explain what "patronage" meant for Renaissance art and how it shaped which ideas were expressed.
- [ ] Distinguish between the medieval concept of individual identity (defined by role/duty) and the Renaissance humanist concept (individual potential and achievement).

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can describe the feudal hierarchy and state that the Black Death killed a large percentage of Europe's population — without yet analyzing how the labor shortage created by the Black Death altered the bargaining power of serfs and contributed to the end of feudalism.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.4 — Analyze the influence of social, cultural, and economic development on individuals (Era 2)**

To meet this standard, you must connect large-scale social and economic systems to how they shaped specific individuals' lived experience. In Era 2, the central tension is between the rigidity of the feudal hierarchy and the forces that disrupted it: the Black Death, the growth of trade and the merchant class, and the Renaissance's new ideas about individual worth.

**3.0 Example:**
A student achieves a 3.0 by analyzing how the Black Death affected a specific category of individual: the surviving serf. Before 1347, serfs were legally bound to the land with little bargaining power — lords could set terms, and serfs had to accept them. After 1347–1353, with 30–50% of the population dead, lords faced an acute labor shortage: crops needed to be harvested, farms needed to be worked, and there were fewer people to do it. Surviving serfs discovered they could leave unfavorable lords and find better terms elsewhere — or simply refuse to work under the old conditions. The student explains how an *economic development* (catastrophic population loss creating labor scarcity) directly reshaped the social and legal condition of individuals who had previously been at the bottom of the hierarchy.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student knows the Black Death killed millions.
A 3.0 student analyzes how it changed the bargaining position of those who survived — and why that led to the eventual decline of serfdom.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate how multiple forces (plague, trade, religious change, Renaissance ideas) interacted to transform medieval society — and connect this transformation to the modern world.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Economic disruption and social change:** The Black Death is one of history's clearest cases of an economic shock (labor shortage) producing rapid social change (end of serfdom, rise of wages). Analyze how it parallels or differs from a modern economic disruption that changed social hierarchies.
2. **The Renaissance and individual identity:** Medieval people were primarily defined by their social role and religious duty; Renaissance humanism introduced the idea that individuals could be judged by their achievements. Analyze how this ideological shift changed what was possible for different people — especially women and the merchant class.
3. **Who the Renaissance excluded:** The Renaissance celebrated individual achievement, classical learning, and humanist values — but primarily for wealthy men in Italian city-states. Who was excluded from the Renaissance? What was happening to serfs, women, Jews, and enslaved Africans (the Atlantic Slave Trade began during this era) at the same time?

**4.0 Example:** A student argues that the Black Death was more socially transformative than the Renaissance because it materially changed the conditions of ordinary people's lives (labor rights, wages, mobility) — whereas the Renaissance primarily changed the cultural production of elites. The student then argues that this hierarchy of "material conditions vs. cultural ideas" is a framework that can be applied to other historical transformations.

## Deliverable Ideas (Your Studio Product)

**These are NOT the demonstration of learning. These are PBL artifact ideas.**

1. **"The World Turned Upside Down" — Black Death Social History** — Research and present a case study of how the Black Death changed ONE specific social relationship in medieval Europe (e.g., serf-lord labor relations in England, the decline of a specific guild, the Church's loss of credibility). Use at least two sources. Present as a 2-page research brief with an argument about the most significant change.
2. **Renaissance Exclusions — Who Was Left Out?** — Research the experiences of one group excluded from Renaissance opportunity: women, Jews, enslaved Africans, or rural peasants. Create a 6-panel visual story documenting their experience during this "rebirth" era.
3. **Medieval to Modern Social Mobility Study** — Compare social mobility in medieval Europe (how difficult was it to change your social position?) to social mobility in the United States today (using current research data). Write a 1-page argument: which society has/had greater barriers to social mobility, and why?

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Describe the daily life of three people at different levels of the medieval hierarchy (a serf, a guild craftsperson, and a noble). For each, identify: their obligations, their freedoms, and what determined their position.
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Write a paragraph analyzing how one social or economic development (Black Death labor shortage, rise of the merchant class, or the Renaissance's new ideas about individual worth) changed what was possible for a specific category of person.
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Write a 200-word argument evaluating: did the Renaissance make life better for ordinary people, or was its celebration of individual potential primarily for the wealthy elite? Use specific evidence.

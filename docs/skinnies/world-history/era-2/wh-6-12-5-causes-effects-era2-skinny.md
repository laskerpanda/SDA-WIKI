# Causes and Effects of Medieval Global Events — Skinny

**Standard:** WH.6_12.5 — Analyze causes and effects of global events using primary and/or secondary sources
**Era/Unit (if SS):** Era 2 — Middle Ages and the Renaissance (~500–1600 CE)
**ELA Crosswalk:** 9-10.W.4 — Argument writing; 9-10.IR.1–5 — Research and source evaluation
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes
**Subject:** World History + ELA crosswalk

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

The medieval period is full of events that look like they happened locally — a plague in Central Asia, a Mongol conquest in China — but whose effects rippled across civilizations. Understanding Era 2 means understanding how connected the medieval world already was, centuries before European colonialism made that connection explicit.

- **Immediate Cause:** The direct trigger for a historical event — what happened right before that set the event in motion.
- **Underlying Cause:** The deeper conditions that made an event possible — what had been building for years or decades before the immediate trigger.
- **Mongol Conquests (1206–1368 CE):** Genghis Khan and his successors built the largest land empire in history, from China to Eastern Europe. Effects: destruction of the Abbasid Caliphate and the House of Wisdom (1258), facilitation of the Silk Road's expansion under the *Pax Mongolica*, and the creation of conditions for the Black Death's westward spread.
- **Pax Mongolica ("Mongol Peace"):** The period of relative stability across the Mongol Empire (~1270–1350 CE) that allowed unprecedented trade and travel across Eurasia. Marco Polo's journey was made possible by the Pax Mongolica.
- **The Black Death (~1347–1353 CE):** Caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis*, spread by fleas on rats. Traveled from Central Asia along trade routes to the Middle East and then to Europe. Killed 30–50% of Europe's population; also devastated the Middle East and Central Asia.
- **The Fall of Constantinople (1453 CE):** The Ottoman Turks conquered Constantinople, ending the Byzantine Empire and blocking the overland Silk Road to Europe. This event accelerated European interest in finding sea routes to Asia — directly motivating the Age of Exploration.
- **Primary Source Evaluation:** In Era 2, primary sources include chronicles written by medieval monks, court records, personal letters (like those of medieval merchants), and diplomatic correspondence. Key evaluation questions: Who wrote it? Who was the intended audience? What did the writer have to gain?
- **Corroboration:** Checking one source's claims against other sources. A medieval chronicle written by a monk may exaggerate Church heroism; comparing it to a secular court record or a Muslim account of the same events helps identify bias.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Identify immediate vs. underlying causes for at least two major medieval events.
- [ ] Explain how the Black Death's spread was a consequence of trade routes (the Silk Road) — connecting economic development to disease transmission.
- [ ] Evaluate a medieval primary source by identifying its author, purpose, and potential bias.
- [ ] Explain how the Fall of Constantinople caused effects far beyond the Mediterranean (motivating European sea exploration).
- [ ] Distinguish between short-term and long-term effects of a medieval event.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can state that the Fall of Constantinople in 1453 ended the Byzantine Empire — without yet analyzing how this single event set off a chain of consequences that led to Columbus's voyage and the European colonization of the Americas.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.5 — Analyze causes and effects of global events using primary and/or secondary sources (Era 2)**

To meet this standard, you must trace cause-and-effect chains that cross civilizations and use primary or secondary sources as evidence. In Era 2, the key is that medieval events were more globally connected than most people assume — and understanding those connections requires source analysis.

**3.0 Example:**
A student achieves a 3.0 by tracing the causal chain from the **Mongol Conquests → Pax Mongolica → Silk Road expansion → Black Death spread → European population collapse → labor shortage → end of serfdom → economic transformation → Renaissance conditions**. Each step in the chain is a connection that needs to be explained, not just listed. The student uses at least one primary source as evidence — for instance, a medieval chronicle describing the plague's arrival in a specific city, and analyzes what that source reveals about how contemporaries understood the event vs. what historians now know about its actual causes.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student can list causes and effects for the Black Death.
A 3.0 student traces a multi-step causal chain that crosses civilizations and uses a primary source to support the analysis.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate competing interpretations of causation — historians disagree about what caused major medieval events, and you need to assess the evidence for different interpretations.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Competing interpretations:** Some historians argue the Crusades were primarily caused by religious fervor; others argue they were caused by political and economic pressure. Research both arguments and evaluate which is better supported by primary source evidence.
2. **Hidden connections:** The Fall of Constantinople (1453) → Age of Exploration → Columbus (1492) → Columbian Exchange → European wealth → Reformation (1517) is a causal chain that spans 64 years and three continents. Analyze this chain and identify the link you think was most contingent — the one that could most plausibly have NOT happened if circumstances were slightly different.
3. **Whose sources?** The Black Death's primary sources are mostly written by European monks and merchants. We have very few first-person accounts from the Middle East or Central Asia, where the plague also devastated populations. How does the skewed survival of sources affect what historians can claim about the Black Death's causes and effects? What are we missing?

**4.0 Example:** A student argues that the Fall of Constantinople was the most consequential event of the 15th century — not because it ended the Byzantine Empire (which had been weakening for a century), but because it catalyzed European sea exploration by blocking the overland Silk Road. The student then evaluates this claim: Was the Silk Road really blocked? (Partly yes, partly no — trade continued via Ottoman middlemen, but at higher cost and risk.) The student concludes that the "blocked Silk Road" explanation is somewhat overstated by European historians, who had an interest in justifying exploration as a necessity rather than as expansionist ambition.

## Deliverable Ideas (Your Studio Product)

**These are NOT the demonstration of learning. These are PBL artifact ideas.**

1. **"The Most Consequential Medieval Event" Argument** — Research three candidate events (Mongol Conquests, Black Death, Fall of Constantinople). Write a 1-page argument defending your choice for which had the most far-reaching and long-lasting effects. Support with at least three sources.
2. **Source Reliability Audit** — Select three primary sources from the medieval period (a Church chronicle, a merchant's letter, and a Muslim account of the Crusades). For each, analyze: author's purpose, potential bias, what it corroborates, and what it omits. Write a 1-page conclusion about which is most reliable for understanding the event.
3. **The Silk Road Cause-and-Effect Map** — Build a comprehensive annotated map of the Silk Road. For each major event connected to the Silk Road (spread of Buddhism, Mongol rise, Black Death spread, knowledge transmission, Fall of Constantinople and its effects), annotate the specific causal connection.

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Choose one medieval event. List: one immediate cause, one underlying cause, one short-term effect, one long-term effect. Identify whether each source you used was primary or secondary.

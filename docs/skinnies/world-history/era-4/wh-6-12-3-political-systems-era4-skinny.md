# Age of Global War: Political Systems, War, and Decolonization — Skinny

**Standard:** WH.6_12.3 — Analyze the effects of different political systems on people
**Era/Unit (if SS):** Era 4 — Age of Global War and Globalization (~1900–present)
**ELA Crosswalk:** 9-10.W.4 — Argument writing; 9-10.R.9 — Analyzing informational/argumentative texts
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes
**Subject:** World History + ELA crosswalk

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Era 4 saw more political systems compete for dominance than any previous era — and more people die as a result of those competitions. Fascism, communism, liberal democracy, colonial empires, and newly independent nations all shaped the lives of people caught inside them. Understanding this era means analyzing how different political systems affected the people who lived under them.

- **Weimar Republic:** Germany's democratic government (1919–1933), created after WWI. The Weimar Republic was destroyed by the Nazi Party through legal means: Hitler was elected, then used emergency powers to dismantle democracy. Lesson: democracies can legally vote themselves out of existence.
- **Totalitarian State:** A government that exercises total control over all aspects of life — politics, culture, religion, family, and thought. Nazi Germany, Soviet Russia, and Maoist China were totalitarian states. Effects on individuals: loss of privacy, constant surveillance, forced conformity, and violent punishment for dissent.
- **Colonial Rule:** The political system imposed by European powers on colonized peoples. Colonial governments were explicitly designed to extract resources and maintain control — not to serve the colonized population. Effects: economic exploitation, suppression of local governance, cultural destruction, and racial hierarchy enforced by law.
- **Decolonization (~1940s–1970s):** The process by which European colonies gained independence. Most of Africa and Asia achieved independence in this period, often through a combination of armed struggle, civil disobedience, and international pressure. Key examples: India (1947), Ghana (1957), Algeria (1962), Zimbabwe (1980).
- **Civil Rights and Apartheid:** Racial political systems — Jim Crow in the U.S. and apartheid in South Africa — used law to enforce racial hierarchy after the colonial era. These systems affected every aspect of individuals' lives: where they lived, worked, could vote, and whom they could marry.
- **The United Nations (1945):** The international political body created after WWII to prevent future wars through collective security, diplomacy, and human rights standards. The UN's Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) represented a new attempt to define the political rights owed to all individuals regardless of their nation's political system.
- **Cold War Political Competition:** The U.S. and Soviet Union backed opposing political systems in proxy conflicts around the world. Often, U.S. "support for democracy" meant supporting authoritarian governments that opposed communism; Soviet "support for liberation" meant supporting authoritarian governments that opposed capitalism. Both superpowers' actual commitments were to strategic interest, not political principle.

**Basic Processes:**
- [ ] Explain how the Nazi Party legally dismantled the Weimar Republic's democratic system.
- [ ] Distinguish between colonial rule and decolonization — what changed politically, what remained economically?
- [ ] Explain what "decolonization" achieved and what its limits were.
- [ ] Identify at least two specific effects of totalitarian political systems on ordinary people's daily lives.
- [ ] Explain what the UN and Universal Declaration of Human Rights were created in response to.

**A student who can score a 2.0:** A student who can describe totalitarian political systems as controlling and identify WWI and WWII as major wars — without yet analyzing how specific political systems affected different categories of people differently, or why decolonization produced formal independence without economic independence.

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.3 — Analyze the effects of different political systems on people (Era 4)**

To meet this standard, you must analyze how a specific political system affected specific individuals or groups — showing that the same political system had very different effects depending on who you were within it.

**3.0 Example:**
A student achieves a 3.0 by analyzing how **apartheid South Africa** (1948–1994) functioned as a political system and what it meant for people at different positions within it. For white South Africans: legally protected economic and political privilege — first-class schools, housing, employment, and political rights. For Black South Africans: the Pass Laws (required to carry identification at all times), the Bantustans (forced relocation to "homelands"), prohibition from most skilled employment, separate and inferior schools, and no political representation. The student analyzes what the system was designed to achieve (maintaining white economic and political control), how it was maintained (law enforcement, imprisonment of ANC leaders, international silence), and what its effects were on individual daily life — not just its historical summary.

**The Difference:**
A 2.0 student knows apartheid was a racist political system in South Africa.
A 3.0 student analyzes how apartheid specifically shaped the daily experience of people at different positions within it.

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a 4.0, you must evaluate competing political systems and their promises vs. their realities — and connect Era 4 political developments to contemporary debates about political systems.

**Transfer Tasks:**
1. **Decolonization's unfulfilled promises:** Decolonization gave African and Asian nations formal political independence, but economic structures (debt, commodity dependence, neocolonialism) kept many nations economically subordinate to former colonial powers. Evaluate: was decolonization complete? What would *genuine* decolonization have required?
2. **Democracy under pressure:** The Weimar Republic fell legally — not through a coup but through elections and emergency powers. Analyze how democracies can be dismantled through legal means. What safeguards are necessary? Where do you see contemporary parallels?
3. **Cold War "democracy":** The United States consistently supported anti-communist dictatorships during the Cold War (e.g., Pinochet in Chile, the Shah in Iran, Marcos in the Philippines). Evaluate: what does this tell us about the actual meaning of the U.S. commitment to "democracy" in this era? How does this affect how we should evaluate Cold War history?

**4.0 Example:** A student argues that the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) was a genuine ideological achievement — but its enforcement mechanisms were too weak to prevent its signatories from violating it. The student analyzes the gap between the UDHR's stated principles and the actual behavior of its signatories (the U.S. enforcing Jim Crow while signing; the Soviet Union's Gulag; France fighting a colonial war in Algeria the same year it signed). The student concludes that the UDHR represents the articulation of a standard, not its achievement — and that the gap between the articulated standard and actual practice is itself a historical and political problem that continues today.

## Deliverable Ideas (Your Studio Product)

**These are NOT the demonstration of learning. These are PBL artifact ideas.**

1. **Decolonization: Independence Without Economic Freedom?** — Research one post-colonial nation (Kenya, Algeria, or Democratic Republic of Congo). Analyze: what did independence in the 1950s–1960s actually change for ordinary people? What economic structures remained from the colonial era? Write a 1-page brief arguing whether political independence was sufficient.
2. **The UDHR: Ideal vs. Reality** — Research the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948). Identify three specific articles and evaluate: how well were these rights protected by UN member nations in the decade after the UDHR was signed? Use specific examples. Write a 1-page argument: was the UDHR a genuine achievement or a hollow promise?
3. **How Democracies Die: Weimar Then and Now** — Research the fall of the Weimar Republic (1930–1933). Identify the specific legal and political mechanisms Hitler used to dismantle democracy. Evaluate: Are any of these mechanisms present in contemporary democratic systems? What safeguards exist or don't exist?

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Describe three Era 4 political systems (totalitarian, colonial, or post-colonial democracy). For each, state: who holds power, how they maintain it, and what the effects are on ordinary people.

# Skinny — US.6_12.1 Science & Technology · Unit 5 Cold War

**Standard:** US.6_12.1 — Science, Technology, and Society
**Unit:** 5 — Cold War (1945–1991)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did technology shape the Cold War? What did the nuclear arms race, the space race, and new surveillance technologies mean for American society?

---

## The Big Idea

The Cold War was fundamentally a technological competition. Both superpowers raced to build bigger bombs, launch satellites, develop missiles, and intercept each other's communications. This competition produced enormous technological innovation — and an existential threat. The Cold War shows technology as both achievement and danger simultaneously.

---

## Key Technologies — Unit 5

| Technology | Cold War Role | Social Effect |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Nuclear weapons / H-bomb | Mutual Assured Destruction (MAD); existential deterrence | Permanent anxiety; civil defense culture; "duck and cover" |
| ICBMs (Intercontinental Ballistic Missiles) | Delivery system for nuclear weapons; makes oceans irrelevant | Arms race; arms control treaties |
| Sputnik (1957) | USSR launches first satellite; US shocked | Space race; NASA founded; science education funded |
| U-2 spy planes | High-altitude reconnaissance; surveillance technology | Powers incident (1960); escalates Cold War |
| Television | Mass communication; Cold War propaganda; Vietnam War footage | Living-room war; shapes public opinion |
| Computer / ARPANET | Military communication network; foundation of internet | Cold War produces the internet |
| Nuclear submarines | Second-strike capability; undetectable; stabilizes MAD | Permanent military presence underwater |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.1 in Unit 5 is about the relationship between technology and fear.

**Example argument move:**
> "The internet was built by ARPA (Advanced Research Projects Agency) to maintain military communication after a nuclear attack. The fear of nuclear war produced one of the most transformative technologies in human history. The Cold War didn't just threaten civilization — it also accidentally created the infrastructure civilization now runs on."

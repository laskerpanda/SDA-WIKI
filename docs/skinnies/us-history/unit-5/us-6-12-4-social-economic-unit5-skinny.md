# Skinny — US.6_12.4 Social & Economic Change · Unit 5 Cold War

**Standard:** US.6_12.4 — Social and Economic Change
**Unit:** 5 — Cold War (1945–1991)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did the Cold War reshape American society and economy? What did the postwar boom look like — and who was included?

---

## The Big Idea

The postwar era (1945–1973) was the longest sustained period of economic growth in American history — but it was built on specific foundations: the GI Bill, cheap suburban housing, a booming manufacturing economy, and the exclusion of Black Americans from most of these benefits. The consumer culture of the 1950s was real, but it was not universal.

---

## Key Social Changes — Unit 5

| Change | What Happened | Who Benefited |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Suburbanization | Levittown and suburbs built for returning veterans | White families; FHA mortgages explicitly excluded Black homebuyers |
| Baby boom (1946–64) | 76 million births; youngest generation ever | All groups; but unequal economic foundations |
| Consumer culture | TV, cars, appliances; credit cards (1950s) | Primarily white middle class |
| Cold War conformism | Social pressure to be "normal"; fear of being labeled subversive | LGBTQ+ Americans; political dissidents; artists |
| Women's roles | "Feminine mystique" — pressure to be housewives; Betty Friedan responds | Women pushed back to domestic sphere |
| Black Americans | Excluded from suburbs, GI Bill; civil rights movement builds in response | — |
| Korean War / Veterans | 36,000 Americans die; "forgotten war" | Veterans underserved compared to WWII vets |

**Economic context:**
- US produces 50% of world GDP in 1945; 25% by 1970 (as Europe/Japan recover)
- Union membership peaks at 35% in 1954; provides middle-class wages
- Military spending (4–10% of GDP) drives economic growth

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.4 in Unit 5 is about the gap between the 1950s myth and the 1950s reality.

**Example argument move:**
> "The 1950s are remembered as an era of prosperity and normalcy. But that prosperity was built on deliberate exclusion: FHA mortgage policies explicitly denied loans in 'redlined' neighborhoods (majority Black), meaning the suburb boom was for white families only. The prosperity was real; it just wasn't shared."

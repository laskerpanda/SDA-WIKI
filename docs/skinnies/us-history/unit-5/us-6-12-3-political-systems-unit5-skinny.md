# Skinny — US.6_12.3 Political Systems · Unit 5 Cold War

**Standard:** US.6_12.3 — Political Systems and Democratic Institutions
**Unit:** 5 — Cold War (1945–1991)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did the Cold War reshape American political institutions? What new powers did the executive branch claim — and what happened to checks and balances?

---

## The Big Idea

The Cold War produced a permanent national security state. The CIA, NSA, Pentagon, and the military-industrial complex all grew from Cold War imperatives. The executive branch accumulated enormous power — to wage secret wars, conduct surveillance, and override Congress — justified by the permanent emergency of superpower competition. This concentration of power is still with us.

---

## Key Institutional Changes — Unit 5

| Institution / Law | What It Created | Significance |
|------------------|----------------|--------------|
| National Security Act (1947) | Created CIA, NSC, Joint Chiefs; unified military | Permanent intelligence/security apparatus |
| Truman Doctrine (1947) | US will support any government threatened by communism | Open-ended commitment; proxy wars worldwide |
| Marshall Plan (1948) | US rebuilds Western Europe economically | Soft power; economic containment of communism |
| McCarthyism (1950–54) | Senate hearings; accusations of communist sympathy | Civil liberties crisis; "Are you now or have you ever been..." |
| HUAC / Hollywood blacklist | House investigation of communist influence in culture | Artists, writers, actors blacklisted; free speech curtailed |
| Military-industrial complex | Eisenhower's warning (1961) — defense contractors + military + Congress | Permanent lobbying for military spending |
| War Powers Resolution (1973) | Attempted to limit presidential war-making | Executive largely ignored it |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.3 in Unit 5 is about institutional drift — powers created for emergencies that never go away.

**Example argument move:**
> "Eisenhower, who built the national security state, warned in his farewell address about the 'military-industrial complex' — the alliance between defense contractors, the military, and Congress that would perpetually justify military spending. He was right. The US now spends more on defense than the next 10 countries combined. Understanding the Cold War is understanding how an emergency security apparatus becomes permanent."

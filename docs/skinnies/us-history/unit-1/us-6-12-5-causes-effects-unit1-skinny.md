# Skinny — US.6_12.5 Causes & Effects · Unit 1 Industrial Revolution

**Standard:** US.6_12.5 — Causes and Effects of Historical Events
**Unit:** 1 — Industrial Revolution & Progressive Era (1870s–1920)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

What caused the Industrial Revolution in America? What did it produce — intended and unintended consequences?

---

## The Big Idea

Causal chains in history are rarely simple. The Industrial Revolution had multiple causes (technology, capital, natural resources, immigration, policy) and produced effects that rippled across economics, politics, society, and culture. US.6_12.5 asks you to trace those chains — and to think about which causes were most important and which effects were most consequential.

---

## Causes of American Industrialization

| Cause | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| Natural resources | Coal, iron ore, timber — abundant and accessible |
| Capital accumulation | Post-Civil War wealth; investment in production |
| Immigration | Millions of workers willing to take low-wage industrial jobs |
| Railroads | Created national market; enabled mass distribution |
| Patent system | Protected invention; incentivized innovation |
| Laissez-faire policy | Government largely stayed out; no regulations to slow growth |
| Post-Civil War contracts | Federal demand for goods during war jump-started production |

## Effects of Industrialization

| Effect | Type | Time Frame |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Massive wealth creation | Economic | Immediate |
| Labor exploitation / unsafe conditions | Social | Immediate |
| Urban growth | Social/Demographic | 1870–1920 |
| Immigration surge | Demographic | 1880–1920 |
| Monopolies and trusts | Economic/Political | 1880–1910 |
| Progressive movement / labor reform | Political | 1890–1920 |
| Environmental degradation | Environmental | Ongoing |
| American economic dominance by 1900 | Economic/Global | By 1900 |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.5 is where you show historical thinking, not just knowledge. The most powerful move: *argue which cause was most important or which effect was most consequential — and defend that with evidence.*

**Example argument move:**
> "Immigration was the most indispensable cause of industrialization — not because workers were needed (any population would have provided workers), but because the specific conditions of immigration created a labor force with few alternatives and limited political power, which allowed the exploitation that made industrial profits possible."

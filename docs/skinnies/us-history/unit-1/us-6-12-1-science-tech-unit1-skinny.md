# Skinny — US.6_12.1 Science & Technology · Unit 1 Industrial Revolution

**Standard:** US.6_12.1 — Science, Technology, and Society
**Unit:** 1 — Industrial Revolution & Progressive Era (1870s–1920)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did new technologies reshape American society during industrialization? What were the consequences — intended and unintended — of those changes?

---

## The Big Idea

The Industrial Revolution was driven by technology: steam power, railroads, the telegraph, the assembly line, electricity. Each new technology changed not just what people could produce but how they lived, worked, and organized society. Technology created wealth — and created new forms of inequality.

---

## Key Content — Unit 1

| Technology | What It Did | Consequence |
|------------|------------|-------------|
| Railroads | Connected national market; enabled mass production | Created monopolies; displaced Native communities |
| Telegraph / telephone | Instant long-distance communication | Accelerated business; shrank perceived geography |
| Assembly line / interchangeable parts | Faster, cheaper manufacturing | Deskilled labor; created repetitive factory work |
| Electricity (Edison) | Powered factories; lit cities | Extended workday; accelerated urbanization |
| Steel (Bessemer process) | Cheaper, stronger building material | Enabled skyscrapers, bridges, railroads |

---

## The Argument Move

When you write about US.6_12.1, you're not just describing technology — you're analyzing **cause and effect** and **tradeoffs**. The question is always: *who benefited from this technology, who paid the cost, and did anyone choose that distribution?*

**Example argument move:**
> "The railroad connected the continent, but it also enabled the displacement of Plains Indians and the consolidation of corporate power. Technology rarely has neutral effects — it tends to amplify the power of whoever controls it."

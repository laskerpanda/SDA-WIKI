# Skinny — US.6_12.4 Social & Economic Change · Unit 1 Industrial Revolution

**Standard:** US.6_12.4 — Social and Economic Change
**Unit:** 1 — Industrial Revolution & Progressive Era (1870s–1920)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did industrialization change American society? Who benefited, who was harmed, and how did different groups respond to the new economic order?

---

## The Big Idea

Industrialization didn't just change how goods were made — it changed how people lived. The shift from farm to factory created a new working class, drew millions of immigrants to American cities, produced new forms of poverty alongside new wealth, and created the conditions for labor organizing, muckraker journalism, and the Progressive movement. This is the standard where social history lives.

---

## Key Content — Unit 1

| Group | Experience of Industrialization |
|-------|-------------------------------|
| Industrial workers | 12-hour days, dangerous conditions, no safety net, low wages, child labor |
| Immigrants | Often took hardest, lowest-paid jobs; faced discrimination; formed ethnic communities |
| Women | Limited to domestic work or low-wage factory jobs; suffrage movement builds |
| Farmers | Squeezed by railroad monopolies and falling crop prices; Populist movement |
| Black Americans | Jim Crow in South; limited factory access in North; Great Migration begins later |
| Wealthy industrialists | Enormous wealth accumulation; social status; political influence |

**Key social patterns:**
- Urbanization — cities grow from immigration + rural migration
- Rise of the middle class — new professional and managerial jobs
- Child labor — 2 million children working in mines, factories, fields in 1900
- Settlement houses — Jane Addams' Hull House; social reform through community organizing

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.4 is about who bears the costs of economic change. The richest argument move is comparing groups: *who benefited from the same system that harmed someone else, and what does that reveal about how power works?*

**Example argument move:**
> "Carnegie made $480 million in his lifetime. The steelworkers who produced his wealth averaged $10 a week for 12-hour days. This wasn't a coincidence — it was a business model. Understanding industrialization means understanding whose sacrifice produced whose wealth."

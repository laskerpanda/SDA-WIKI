# Skinny — US.6_12.3 Political Systems · Unit 1 Industrial Revolution

**Standard:** US.6_12.3 — Political Systems and Democratic Institutions
**Unit:** 1 — Industrial Revolution & Progressive Era (1870s–1920)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did political institutions respond to industrialization? What reforms changed American democracy during the Progressive Era — and what did they fail to change?

---

## The Big Idea

The Progressive Era produced the most significant expansion of American democracy since Reconstruction — and most of it happened through constitutional amendments and new federal agencies. The era shows both the capacity of democratic systems to reform themselves and the limits of those reforms (especially for Black Americans and agricultural workers).

---

## Key Content — Unit 1

| Reform | What It Did | When |
|--------|------------|------|
| Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) | Gave federal government power to break up monopolies | 1890 (weakly enforced until TR) |
| 16th Amendment | Authorized federal income tax | 1913 |
| 17th Amendment | Direct election of US Senators (was state legislature appointment) | 1913 |
| Federal Reserve Act | Created central banking system | 1913 |
| Clayton Antitrust Act | Strengthened antitrust; exempted unions | 1914 |
| Pure Food and Drug Act | Required labeling; banned adulterated food | 1906 |
| 18th Amendment (Prohibition) | Banned alcohol | 1919 |
| 19th Amendment | Women's suffrage | 1920 |

**What did NOT change:** Black voting rights (suppressed by Jim Crow); labor rights for agricultural/domestic workers; immigration restriction (Chinese Exclusion Act already in place)

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.3 is about evaluating democratic institutions — not just listing reforms. The key question: *did the political system actually change the distribution of power, or did it manage the symptoms?*

**Example argument move:**
> "The Progressive Era produced real democratic reforms — direct election of senators, women's suffrage, antitrust enforcement. But it systematically excluded Black Americans from its benefits, which reveals whose voices were considered part of 'the people' the reforms were for."

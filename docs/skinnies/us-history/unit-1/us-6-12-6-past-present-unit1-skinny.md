# Skinny — US.6_12.6 Past to Present · Unit 1 Industrial Revolution

**Standard:** US.6_12.6 — Connecting History to the Present
**Unit:** 1 — Industrial Revolution & Progressive Era (1870s–1920)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

What from the Industrial Revolution is still present today? How does understanding this period help explain the world students live in?

---

## The Big Idea

The Industrial Revolution didn't end — it continued and accelerated. The companies, regulations, economic patterns, and political debates it created are still active. The debates of the Progressive Era (corporate power vs. democracy; worker protections vs. economic freedom; immigration and national identity) are live debates right now.

---

## Then → Now Connections

| Then (1870–1920) | Now |
|-----------------|-----|
| Standard Oil monopoly → Sherman Antitrust Act | FTC antitrust cases against Google, Amazon, Meta |
| Meatpacking conditions → Pure Food and Drug Act | FDA regulates food and drugs today |
| Child labor in factories | Child labor still exists globally; US debates minimum wage ages |
| 12-hour workdays, no weekends | 40-hour work week, OSHA, minimum wage — all result of labor movement |
| Immigration surge from Southern/Eastern Europe | Immigration debates about Southern/Western hemisphere |
| "Robber barons" vs. "Captains of Industry" debate | Billionaire class today — are Elon Musk/Jeff Bezos Carnegies? |
| Muckraker journalism | Investigative journalism — ProPublica, NYT, WaPo |
| Social Darwinism ("the rich deserve it") | Meritocracy discourse today |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.6 is your chance to make history feel relevant. The strongest move: *find a specific present-day situation that directly connects to a Unit 1 dynamic, and argue that understanding the history illuminates the present.*

**Example argument move:**
> "Amazon's warehouse workers today face conditions that muckrakers like Upton Sinclair would recognize — long hours, surveillance, injury rates that exceed industry averages, and aggressive resistance to unionization. Understanding the Industrial Revolution means understanding that this pattern isn't new, and that the Progressive Era shows both how workers can change it and why change is so slow."

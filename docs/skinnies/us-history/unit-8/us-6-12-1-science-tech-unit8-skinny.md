# Skinny — US.6_12.1 Science & Technology · Unit 8 1970s–80s

**Standard:** US.6_12.1 — Science, Technology, and Society
**Unit:** 8 — 1970s–1980s (Reagan Era, Cold War End)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did technology change American life in the 1970s and 1980s? What new technologies emerged — and what did they displace?

---

## The Big Idea

The 1970s and 80s were the transition decade: from industrial to post-industrial economy, from analog to digital technology. Personal computers, VCRs, cable television, the Walkman, video games — the consumer technology of the 80s set the stage for the internet age. Meanwhile, deindustrialization removed the manufacturing jobs that had sustained the working class since WWII.

---

## Key Technologies — Unit 8

| Technology | 1970s–80s Role | Social Effect |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| Personal computer (Apple II, 1977; IBM PC, 1981) | Computing moves from institutions to individuals | New economy; knowledge workers; deskilling others |
| Cable television (CNN, 1980; MTV, 1981) | 24-hour news; music videos; niche channels | Media fragmentation; 24-hour news cycle begins |
| VCR / home video | Movies at home; entertainment industry transformed | Blockbuster; time-shifting; piracy debates |
| Reagan's SDI ("Star Wars") | Space-based missile defense; ultimately doesn't work | Arms race escalation; $100B+ spent |
| Nuclear power / Three Mile Island (1979) | Near-meltdown; fear of nuclear power | Nuclear energy expansion halted in US |
| Deindustrialization | Manufacturing jobs move overseas as technology changes production | Rust Belt; working-class devastation |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.1 in Unit 8 is about technology and economic inequality.

**Example argument move:**
> "The personal computer revolution created enormous wealth in Silicon Valley and destroyed manufacturing jobs in Detroit. Technology didn't just change what people did — it changed who had economic power. The same decade that produced the Apple II produced the Rust Belt. Understanding the 1980s means understanding that technological progress and economic equality can move in opposite directions."

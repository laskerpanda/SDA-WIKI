# Skinny — US.6_12.2 Ideas, Beliefs & Ideology · Unit 8 1970s–80s

**Standard:** US.6_12.2 — Ideas, Beliefs, and Institutions
**Unit:** 8 — 1970s–1980s (Reagan Era, Cold War End)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

What ideologies defined the 1970s and 80s? How did Reagan conservatism change American politics — and what did it argue against?

---

## The Big Idea

Reagan's 1980 election marked the most significant ideological shift in American politics since the New Deal. Reagan argued that the government was the problem — that New Deal liberalism had created a dependency culture, overtaxed the productive, and weakened American strength internationally. This was a direct challenge to the Keynesian consensus of the previous 40 years. Some of Reagan's ideas worked; some didn't; all of them reshaped the debate.

---

## Key Ideologies — Unit 8

| Ideology | Core Claim | Significance |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Reaganism / supply-side economics | Cut taxes on wealthy; reduce regulation; wealth will "trickle down" | Taxes cut dramatically; deficits explode; debate continues |
| Anti-government conservatism | "Government is not the solution to our problem; government is the problem" | Permanent shift in how Americans talk about government |
| Religious right / Moral Majority | Christianity should shape public policy; social conservatism | New political constituency; abortion, school prayer, family values |
| Neoliberalism | Free markets, deregulation, privatization as global model | Spreads through IMF, World Bank; affects global South |
| Anti-communism (renewed) | Détente failed; Soviet Union is "evil empire"; must be opposed everywhere | Arms buildup; proxy wars (Nicaragua, Afghanistan) |
| Feminist counterculture | ERA; reproductive rights; women in workforce | ERA fails; *Roe* under attack; women enter workforce anyway |

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.2 in Unit 8 is about the ideological reversal — an argument that wins.

**Example argument move:**
> "Reagan's most lasting achievement wasn't his tax cuts or his arms buildup — it was redefining the ideological terms of debate. After Reagan, even Democrats argued about how to cut government, not whether to. The Overton window shifted: positions that were mainstream Democratic positions in 1970 (strong union support, robust social programs, high taxes on wealthy) became fringe positions by 1990. Ideology shapes politics long after the politicians who created it are gone."

# Skinny — US.6_12.6 Past to Present · Unit 8 1970s–80s

**Standard:** US.6_12.6 — Connecting History to the Present
**Unit:** 8 — 1970s–1980s (Reagan Era, Cold War End)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

What from the 1970s and 80s is still present today? How does this period explain current political and economic debates?

---

## Then → Now Connections

| Then (1970s–80s) | Now |
|-----------------|-----|
| Supply-side / trickle-down economics | Still GOP orthodoxy; 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act |
| Anti-government conservatism | Tea Party → MAGA; government shutdown politics |
| Religious right / Moral Majority | Evangelical political influence; *Dobbs v. Jackson* (2022) |
| Mass incarceration ("War on Drugs") | 2.3 million incarcerated; bipartisan criminal justice reform debate |
| Deindustrialization / Rust Belt | Persistent; 2016 election driven partly by deindustrialized communities |
| Union decline | Ongoing; UAW strikes 2023; Amazon/Starbucks organizing |
| AIDS crisis (Reagan's silence) | Model for pandemic response debates; LGBTQ+ health equity |
| Income inequality acceleration | Highest since 1920s; central political issue |
| Cold War end / unipolar moment | US military interventions of 1990s–2000s follow from "sole superpower" |
| Financial deregulation | 2008 crisis; Dodd-Frank reform; debates about re-regulation |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.6 in Unit 8 is about ideological persistence.

**Example argument move:**
> "Reagan's argument — 'government is not the solution to our problem; government is the problem' — is still the operating premise of one major American political party 45 years later. It has shaped every tax debate, every healthcare debate, every infrastructure debate since 1981. Understanding why that argument was persuasive in 1980 (stagflation, Vietnam, Watergate) helps you understand why it persists — and why its critics argue it's become a reflex rather than a response to current conditions."

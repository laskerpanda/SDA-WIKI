# Skinny — US.6_12.3 Political Systems · Unit 2 Imperialism & WWI

**Standard:** US.6_12.3 — Political Systems and Democratic Institutions
**Unit:** 2 — Imperialism & World War I (1890s–1919)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did imperialism and WWI change American political institutions? What happened to civil liberties during wartime — and what does that reveal about how democracy works under pressure?

---

## The Big Idea

Wartime tests democratic institutions. During WWI, the US government suppressed dissent through the Espionage and Sedition Acts, used propaganda to manufacture consent, and prosecuted anti-war activists. The tension between national security and civil liberties — which recurs in American history (WWII internment, McCarthy era, post-9/11 Patriot Act) — is first clearly visible here.

---

## Key Content — Unit 2

| Event / Law | What It Did | Democratic Implications |
|------------|------------|------------------------|
| Platt Amendment (1901) | Made Cuba a US protectorate after Spanish-American War | Sovereignty of "liberated" nations limited by US |
| Roosevelt Corollary (1904) | US claims right to intervene in Latin America | Monroe Doctrine as imperial claim, not just defensive |
| Espionage Act (1917) | Made it illegal to interfere with military recruitment | Anti-war speech criminalized |
| Sedition Act (1918) | Made it illegal to criticize the government, war, or flag | 1,500+ prosecuted; free speech curtailed |
| Committee on Public Information | Government propaganda bureau | State-managed public opinion |
| *Schenck v. US* (1919) | Supreme Court upholds Espionage Act; "clear and present danger" | First First Amendment ruling; sets 20th century precedent |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.3 in Unit 2 asks: *what do democratic governments do when they believe the stakes are high enough — and is that justified?* The argument is about the limits of democracy, not just its institutions.

**Example argument move:**
> "The Espionage and Sedition Acts reveal something important about democracy under pressure: when governments believe national survival is at stake, they often treat dissent as the enemy. The question isn't whether this happened — it clearly did — but whether it was justified. Justice Holmes said 'clear and present danger.' Eugene Debs, who was sentenced to 10 years for an anti-war speech, thought otherwise."

# Skinny — US.6_12.6 Past to Present · Unit 2 Imperialism & WWI

**Standard:** US.6_12.6 — Connecting History to the Present
**Unit:** 2 — Imperialism & World War I (1890s–1919)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

What from the era of American imperialism and WWI is still present today? How does understanding this period explain current events?

---

## The Big Idea

The debates of Unit 2 — when should America use military force? what are the limits of free speech in wartime? what does the US owe the countries it has dominated? — are live debates. American foreign policy, civil liberties law, and immigration policy all trace directly back to this era.

---

## Then → Now Connections

| Then (1890s–1919) | Now |
|------------------|-----|
| Monroe Doctrine / Roosevelt Corollary | US policy in Latin America still shaped by these frameworks |
| Philippine-American War; annexation debate | Puerto Rico's status as unincorporated territory — still unresolved |
| Espionage Act (1917) | Still on the books; used to prosecute Edward Snowden |
| "Clear and present danger" (*Schenck v. US*) | First Amendment law today descends from this case |
| War propaganda / Committee on Public Information | Government media strategy; embedded journalism in Iraq/Afghanistan |
| Zimmermann Telegram (intelligence intelligence shaping policy) | Intelligence community role in foreign policy decisions |
| Immigration restriction (1921, 1924 quotas) | Immigration debates; quota system echoes today |
| "Make the world safe for democracy" | American foreign policy rhetoric from Wilson to Bush to Obama |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.6 in Unit 2 is about foreign policy continuity. The argument: *American imperialism wasn't a phase that ended — it transformed.*

**Example argument move:**
> "The Espionage Act of 1917 was passed to stop anti-war speech during WWI. Over 100 years later, it was used to prosecute Edward Snowden for leaking classified information about NSA surveillance. The law's authors couldn't have imagined the internet — but the legal tool they created to suppress dissent is still being used for the same basic purpose."

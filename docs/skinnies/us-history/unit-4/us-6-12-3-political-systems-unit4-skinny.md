# Skinny — US.6_12.3 Political Systems · Unit 4 WWII

**Standard:** US.6_12.3 — Political Systems and Democratic Institutions
**Unit:** 4 — World War II (1939–1945)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did WWII test American democratic institutions? What decisions were made that expanded or contracted civil liberties — and what were the consequences?

---

## The Big Idea

Wartime always tests democracy. WWII produced the most dramatic civil liberties violation of the 20th century (Japanese American internment) while simultaneously mobilizing democratic institutions for the most successful military effort in American history. The war also set up the postwar international order — the UN, the Nuremberg trials, the UDHR — which reflected democratic values at a global scale.

---

## Key Political Events — Unit 4

| Event | Democratic Implications |
|-------|------------------------|
| Japanese American Internment (EO 9066, 1942) | 120,000 citizens imprisoned without charges; *Korematsu v. US* (1944) upholds it |
| Office of War Information / propaganda | Government manages public information; limits on press freedom |
| War Powers Act | Congress delegates enormous authority to executive during wartime |
| Lend-Lease Act | FDR aids allies before US entry; executive initiative |
| Nuremberg Trials (1945–46) | International law applied to war crimes; "following orders" rejected as defense |
| United Nations (1945) | International democratic/security framework; US as founding member |
| Bretton Woods / IMF / World Bank | US designs postwar international economic order |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.3 in Unit 4 is about the paradox of protecting democracy by limiting it.

**Example argument move:**
> "Japanese American internment is the clearest example of democratic institutions failing under pressure. The Supreme Court — in *Korematsu* — upheld imprisonment of citizens based solely on their ancestry. The government's argument was military necessity. But 40 years later, the government admitted there was no military justification. The lesson: democratic institutions can be used to violate democratic principles, and the Supreme Court is not immune."

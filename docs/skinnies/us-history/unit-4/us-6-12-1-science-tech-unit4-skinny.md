# Skinny — US.6_12.1 Science & Technology · Unit 4 WWII

**Standard:** US.6_12.1 — Science, Technology, and Society
**Unit:** 4 — World War II (1939–1945)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did technology shape WWII — and what moral questions did new weapons create?

---

## The Big Idea

WWII was the most technologically intensive war in history to that point. Industrial capacity determined the outcome: the US could outproduce Germany and Japan combined. But technology also produced the atomic bomb — a weapon so destructive it raised questions about whether there were limits on what humanity should build.

---

## Key Technologies — Unit 4

| Technology | Role | Moral Dimension |
|-----------|------|----------------|
| Mass production / war mobilization | US factories outproduce Axis; GDP doubles | Civilian economy converted to war machine |
| Radar | Early warning; air defense; naval advantage | — |
| Codebreaking (Enigma / Magic) | Allies read Axis communications | Intelligence as decisive weapon |
| Aircraft (bombers, fighters) | Strategic bombing; air superiority | Firebombing of Dresden, Tokyo; civilian casualties |
| Atomic bomb (Manhattan Project) | Destroys Hiroshima and Nagasaki; Japan surrenders | 200,000+ dead; raises question of limits |
| V-2 rockets (German) | First ballistic missiles | Precursor to nuclear delivery; Cold War arms race |

**Manhattan Project:** US spent $2 billion (1940s dollars); 130,000 people worked on it; only a handful knew what it was building. First test July 16, 1945. Hiroshima August 6; Nagasaki August 9; Japan surrenders August 15.

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.1 in Unit 4 is about the moral responsibility that comes with technological capability.

**Example argument move:**
> "The atomic bomb worked exactly as designed — which is precisely the problem. A weapon that kills 200,000 people in two days raises a question technology cannot answer: just because we can build something, does that mean we should use it? Truman said yes. Many of the scientists who built it said no. The debate has never ended."

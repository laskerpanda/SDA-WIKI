# Skinny — US.6_12.3 Political Systems · Unit 3 Roaring 20s & Depression

**Standard:** US.6_12.3 — Political Systems and Democratic Institutions
**Unit:** 3 — Roaring 20s & Great Depression (1919–1939)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did political institutions respond to the prosperity of the 1920s and the crisis of the Depression? What did the New Deal do to the relationship between citizens and government?

---

## The Big Idea

The New Deal permanently expanded the role of the federal government in American life. Before 1933, the federal government did not provide unemployment insurance, old-age pensions, or direct economic regulation at scale. After the New Deal, it did. This was a structural change to American democracy — and it was contested then and is contested now.

---

## Key Content — Unit 3

| Program / Reform | What It Did | Significance |
|-----------------|------------|--------------|
| Emergency Banking Act (1933) | Closed failed banks; FDR's first act | Restored confidence in banking system |
| FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance) | Insured bank deposits up to $5,000 | Bank runs become rare; still exists today |
| Social Security Act (1935) | Old-age pensions, unemployment insurance | First US social safety net; still active |
| Wagner Act (1935) | Protected workers' right to organize unions | Enabled labor movement growth |
| Securities and Exchange Commission | Regulated stock market | Prevented some speculation abuses |
| Supreme Court battle (1937) | FDR tries to "pack" Court with pro-New Deal justices | Court backs down; FDR wins; separation of powers tested |
| 20th Amendment (1933) | Shortened lame-duck period | Response to Hoover's paralysis |

**What the New Deal did NOT do:** End the Depression (WWII did); include Black workers in most programs (agricultural/domestic workers excluded); challenge racial segregation

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.3 in Unit 3 is about a permanent shift in what government does.

**Example argument move:**
> "Social Security was the most consequential political reform of the 20th century — not because it solved the Depression, but because it permanently changed what Americans expected from government. Before 1935, old age meant depending on family or charity. After 1935, it meant a government check. That expectation has never been reversed."

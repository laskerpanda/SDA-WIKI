# Skinny — US.6_12.6 Past to Present · Unit 3 Roaring 20s & Depression

**Standard:** US.6_12.6 — Connecting History to the Present
**Unit:** 3 — Roaring 20s & Great Depression (1919–1939)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

What from the 1920s and Depression is still present today? How does understanding this period explain current economic and political debates?

---

## The Big Idea

The New Deal's legacy is everywhere: Social Security, FDIC, SEC, labor law, unemployment insurance. Every political debate about the size of government, the role of regulation, and the social safety net is in some ways a debate about the New Deal. The 1929 crash's lessons shaped the 2008 response.

---

## Then → Now Connections

| Then (1919–1939) | Now |
|-----------------|-----|
| Social Security (1935) | 70 million Americans receive Social Security today; central political issue |
| FDIC bank insurance | Every US bank account insured up to $250,000 |
| SEC stock market regulation | Stock market regulation; insider trading law |
| Wagner Act / labor unions | Union rights still governed by NLRA; union membership debates |
| Dust Bowl / environmental disaster | Climate-driven agricultural disruption; drought cycles |
| 1929 crash → 1930s Depression | 2008 financial crisis; Fed learning from 1930s mistakes |
| Hoovervilles / visible poverty | Homelessness crisis in US cities |
| Immigration restriction (1924 quotas) | Immigration policy debates; quota system influence |
| Keynesian economics (deficit spending) | COVID relief spending, infrastructure bills — all Keynesian |
| Harlem Renaissance | Ongoing centrality of Black American art and culture |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.6 in Unit 3 is about New Deal permanence and economic cycle repetition.

**Example argument move:**
> "The 2008 financial crisis was the first major test of whether the US had learned from 1929. The Fed did the opposite of what it did in the 1930s — it flooded the economy with money instead of contracting the supply. The bank bailouts were politically unpopular but economically sound for the same reason the New Deal was: the alternative was worse. Understanding 1929 is how policymakers avoided repeating it in 2008."

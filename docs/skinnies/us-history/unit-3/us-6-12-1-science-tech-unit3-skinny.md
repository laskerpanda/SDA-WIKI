# Skinny — US.6_12.1 Science & Technology · Unit 3 Roaring 20s & Depression

**Standard:** US.6_12.1 — Science, Technology, and Society
**Unit:** 3 — Roaring 20s & Great Depression (1919–1939)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did new technologies define the 1920s boom — and what role did technology play in the crash and the Depression that followed?

---

## The Big Idea

The 1920s were the first modern consumer technology decade: radio, automobiles, film, refrigerators, washing machines. Mass production made these goods affordable; consumer credit made them purchasable. The same economic logic that created the boom — buy now, pay later — applied to the stock market and contributed to the crash. The Depression showed that technology creates wealth but not stability.

## Key Content — Unit 3

| Technology | 1920s Role | Darker Side |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Automobile (Ford Model T) | Mass consumer product; suburbs; road culture | Displaced horses; changed city design; oil dependency |
| Radio | Mass media; news, entertainment, advertising | Propaganda tool; homogenizes culture |
| Film (Hollywood) | Mass entertainment; celebrity culture | Racial segregation in film; propaganda potential |
| Consumer credit | Made expensive goods affordable; drove demand | When credit collapsed, demand collapsed with it |
| Stock market speculation | Technology of leveraged investment (buying on margin) | Margin calls amplified the crash |
| Assembly line (Fordism) | Made mass production possible | Workers become interchangeable; deskilling |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.1 in Unit 3 connects technology to economic cycles. The core argument: *the same technology that created the boom contained the seeds of the crash.*

**Example argument move:**
> "Consumer credit was the technology that made the 1920s boom possible — and it was the same technology that made the crash catastrophic. When the economy turned, millions of Americans owed money on cars, radios, and appliances they suddenly couldn't afford to keep. The Depression wasn't just a stock market crash; it was a consumer credit crisis."

# Skinny — US.6_12.4 Social & Economic Change · Unit 3 Roaring 20s & Depression

**Standard:** US.6_12.4 — Social and Economic Change
**Unit:** 3 — Roaring 20s & Great Depression (1919–1939)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did the prosperity of the 1920s and the collapse of the Depression change American society? Who rose, who fell, and who was left behind?

---

## The Big Idea

The 1920s produced enormous wealth and a new consumer culture — but not for everyone equally. Then the Depression erased much of that wealth for most Americans while the wealthy lost proportionally less. The Dust Bowl added an environmental disaster on top of an economic one. Social safety nets didn't exist, which meant the poor had nowhere to fall to.

---

## Key Social Changes — Unit 3

| Group | 1920s | Depression |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| Middle class | New consumer goods; rising incomes; suburb growth | Lost savings in bank failures; unemployment |
| Working class | Wages rose slightly; limited union power | 25% unemployment; breadlines; Hoovervilles |
| Black Americans | Harlem Renaissance; Great Migration continues; still face intense racism | Last hired, first fired; excluded from New Deal programs |
| Women | New freedoms (flapper culture, more workforce participation) | Told to give jobs to men; domestic roles reasserted |
| Farmers | Struggled even in 1920s (crop prices fall post-WWI) | Dust Bowl adds catastrophe; 500,000 displaced |
| Immigrants | Restricted by 1921/1924 acts; ethnic communities under pressure | Further marginalized; some deported |

**The Harlem Renaissance (1920s):**
- Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Countee Cullen — literary movement
- Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington — jazz as Black American art form
- Political dimension: Harlem Renaissance as assertion of dignity and cultural power

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.4 in Unit 3 is about how crises reveal existing inequality.

**Example argument move:**
> "The Depression didn't create inequality — it exposed it. Black Americans were the first fired because they were already in the most precarious positions. Women were pushed back to domestic roles because their workforce participation had always been conditional on male approval. Crises don't produce inequality from nowhere; they accelerate patterns that were already there."

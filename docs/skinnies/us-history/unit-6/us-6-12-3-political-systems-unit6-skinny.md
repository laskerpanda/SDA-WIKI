# Skinny — US.6_12.3 Political Systems · Unit 6 Civil Rights

**Standard:** US.6_12.3 — Political Systems and Democratic Institutions
**Unit:** 6 — Civil Rights Movement (1954–1968)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did the Civil Rights Movement change American political institutions — and what resistance did those changes face?

---

## The Big Idea

The Civil Rights Movement produced the most significant legislative expansion of civil rights since Reconstruction — but only after years of activism forced the political system to act. The movement shows how democratic institutions respond to pressure from below — and also how slowly and incompletely they respond.

---

## Key Political Moments — Unit 6

| Event | What Happened | Significance |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) | Supreme Court unanimously overturns *Plessy v. Ferguson* | "Separate but equal" unconstitutional |
| Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56) | 381-day boycott; Supreme Court rules bus segregation unconstitutional | Proves economic pressure works; MLK emerges |
| Little Rock Crisis (1957) | Eisenhower sends 101st Airborne to enforce school integration | Federal vs. state authority |
| Civil Rights Act (1964) | Prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin | Most comprehensive civil rights law since Reconstruction |
| 24th Amendment (1964) | Abolished poll tax in federal elections | One barrier to Black voting removed |
| Voting Rights Act (1965) | Eliminated literacy tests; federal oversight of voting | Most powerful voting rights law ever; gutted by *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) |
| Fair Housing Act (1968) | Prohibited housing discrimination | Least enforced; housing segregation persists |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.3 in Unit 6 is about the relationship between protest and policy.

**Example argument move:**
> "The Civil Rights Act passed in 1964 after a decade of organized protest — sit-ins, Freedom Rides, the Birmingham campaign, the March on Washington. Before those events, Congress had blocked civil rights legislation for decades. The movement didn't work through the political system; it created the conditions that forced the political system to act. That's the lesson: legal change follows political pressure, not the other way around."

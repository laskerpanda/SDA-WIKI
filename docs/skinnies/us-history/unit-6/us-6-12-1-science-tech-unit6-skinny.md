# Skinny — US.6_12.1 Science & Technology · Unit 6 Civil Rights

**Standard:** US.6_12.1 — Science, Technology, and Society
**Unit:** 6 — Civil Rights Movement (1954–1968)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did technology shape the Civil Rights Movement? What role did media, communication, and documentation play in the movement's strategy?

---

## The Big Idea

Television was the Civil Rights Movement's most powerful ally — not because activists planned it that way, but because Bull Connor's fire hoses and police dogs in Birmingham, broadcast into living rooms across America, turned public opinion. The movement understood this and used it strategically. Technology didn't create the movement, but it amplified its message to an audience that would otherwise never have seen what segregation looked like.

---

## Key Technologies — Unit 6

| Technology | Role in Civil Rights |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Television | Broadcast Birmingham, Selma violence into white homes; turned public opinion |
| Radio | Movement communication; spread speeches; freedom songs |
| Phone / telegraph networks | NAACP legal coordination; SNCC organizing |
| Print journalism / photography | Emmett Till's open casket photograph — Jet magazine; galvanized movement |
| Recording technology | MLK speeches preserved and distributed; oral history |
| Long-distance bus system | Freedom Rides used existing transportation infrastructure as protest target |

**Emmett Till photograph (1955):** Mamie Till insisted on an open casket for her son, who was murdered in Mississippi. *Jet* magazine published the photographs. The visual evidence of what racism did galvanized support for the movement in ways words alone hadn't.

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.1 in Unit 6 is about technology as a tool of social change.

**Example argument move:**
> "MLK said he was grateful to Bull Connor — because Connor gave the movement television. The strategy of nonviolent direct action only works if the violence of the opposition becomes visible. Without television, the Birmingham campaign is a local news story. With television, it becomes the moment public opinion shifts. The Civil Rights Movement didn't just use technology — it understood how to make technology work for justice."

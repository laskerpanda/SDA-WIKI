# Skinny — US.6_12.2 Ideas, Beliefs & Ideology · Unit 6 Civil Rights

**Standard:** US.6_12.2 — Ideas, Beliefs, and Institutions
**Unit:** 6 — Civil Rights Movement (1954–1968)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

What ideologies shaped the Civil Rights Movement — and what ideologies opposed it? What was the movement arguing for, and how did it argue?

---

## The Big Idea

The Civil Rights Movement was a battle of ideas as much as a political campaign. MLK's philosophy of nonviolent direct action was a deliberate ideological choice — not just a moral position but a strategic one. The white moderate's objection (you're moving too fast, use legal channels) was itself an ideological position. Malcolm X's challenge to integration and nonviolence was a competing ideology. Understanding the movement means understanding the debate within it.

---

## Key Ideologies — Unit 6

| Ideology | Core Claim | Who Held It |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Nonviolent direct action (MLK / Gandhi) | Strategic exposure of opposition's violence; moral high ground; builds coalition | SCLC; MLK; many SNCC members early |
| Legal incrementalism (NAACP) | Use courts; change law one case at a time; *Brown v. Board* strategy | Thurgood Marshall; NAACP |
| Black nationalism / self-determination (Malcolm X) | Integration is not the goal; Black dignity requires Black self-determination | Nation of Islam; Malcolm X |
| Black Power (Stokely Carmichael, 1966) | Pride, self-reliance, political power — not just legal equality | SNCC later; Black Panther Party |
| White moderate objection | Agree with goals; oppose timing/methods; "law and order" | White clergy (MLK's "Letter" audience); most white Americans |
| Massive resistance (white South) | Segregation is right; federal government has no authority here | Southern states; Citizens Councils; KKK |

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.2 in Unit 6 is about a debate within a movement — and an argument about strategy.

**Example argument move:**
> "MLK's 'Letter from Birmingham Jail' is addressed to white clergymen who said they supported civil rights but opposed the demonstrations. MLK's argument is that the 'white moderate who prefers a negative peace which is the absence of tension to a positive peace which is the presence of justice' is a greater obstacle than the outright racist. He's identifying ideology as the problem — the belief that injustice can be corrected slowly, on the oppressor's timeline."

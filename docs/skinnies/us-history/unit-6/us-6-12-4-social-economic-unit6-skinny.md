# Skinny — US.6_12.4 Social & Economic Change · Unit 6 Civil Rights

**Standard:** US.6_12.4 — Social and Economic Change
**Unit:** 6 — Civil Rights Movement (1954–1968)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did the Civil Rights Movement change American society? What did integration cost — and who bore that cost?

---

## The Big Idea

The Civil Rights Movement achieved legal equality but not economic equality. School integration was real but produced white flight and resegregation. Voting rights were secured but economic gaps persisted. The movement revealed that formal equality and actual equality are different things — and that the burden of producing integration often fell on Black Americans, not on those who had built segregation.

---

## Key Social Changes — Unit 6

| Change | What Happened | What Remained |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| School integration | *Brown* (1954); Little Rock (1957); gradual, contested desegregation | White flight; private schools; resegregation by 1980s |
| Voting rights | Voting Rights Act (1965) dramatically increases Black voter registration | Voter suppression tactics evolve; ID laws, polling place closures |
| Employment | Civil Rights Act (1964) prohibits workplace discrimination | Wage gap persists; employment discrimination difficult to prove |
| Housing | Fair Housing Act (1968) | Redlining effects persist; wealth gap in homeownership |
| Cultural recognition | Harlem Renaissance → broader American culture | Ongoing debates about representation |
| Personal cost of integration | Melba Beals at Little Rock; daily harassment; physical danger | *Warriors Don't Cry* documents this cost |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.4 in Unit 6 is about the gap between legal change and lived experience.

**Example argument move:**
> "Melba Pattillo Beals integrated Central High School in 1957 and was spat on, threatened, and assaulted every day for a year. The law said she had the right to be there. The law did not make anyone want her there. *Warriors Don't Cry* documents what it cost one person to exercise a right that the white students around her took for granted. Understanding the Civil Rights Movement means understanding that legal change and social change are not the same thing."

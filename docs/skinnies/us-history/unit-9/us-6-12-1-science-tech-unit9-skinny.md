# Skinny — US.6_12.1 Science & Technology · Unit 9 1990s–2000s

**Standard:** US.6_12.1 — Science, Technology, and Society
**Unit:** 9 — 1990s–2000s (Globalization, 9/11, Contemporary)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did the internet, globalization, and post-9/11 surveillance technology reshape American life? What does the most recent technology revolution mean for democracy, privacy, and society?

---

## The Big Idea

The internet changed everything — and students live on the other side of that change. Understanding the 1990s means understanding how the technology students use daily was built, how it changed the economy and culture, and what tradeoffs it created. Post-9/11, surveillance technology raised questions about privacy and security that remain unresolved.

---

## Key Technologies — Unit 9

| Technology | 1990s–2000s Role | Social Effect |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| World Wide Web (1991) | Public internet; changes commerce, communication, information | Everything — eventually |
| Cell phones → smartphones | Communication anywhere; iPhone (2007) changes everything | Constant connectivity; attention economy |
| Social media (2004–) | Facebook, YouTube, Twitter; user-generated content | Information fragmentation; polarization; new organizing tools |
| GPS | Navigation; location tracking | Convenience; surveillance potential |
| Post-9/11 surveillance (NSA, PATRIOT Act) | Mass data collection; phone metadata; PRISM | Privacy vs. security; Snowden reveals scope (2013) |
| Amazon / e-commerce | Retail transformation; warehouse jobs replace retail | Consumer convenience; labor conditions; local retail destroyed |
| Google | Information access democratized | Also: algorithmic control of what information reaches you |

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.1 in Unit 9 is the most personally relevant — students are living inside this technology shift.

**Example argument move:**
> "The PATRIOT Act (2001) was passed six weeks after 9/11 with almost no debate. It authorized surveillance the NSA used to collect phone data on virtually every American. When Edward Snowden revealed the scope of this in 2013, the debate that should have happened in 2001 finally happened. The technology existed before the legal framework to govern it did — and still does. Understanding this pattern (technology outpacing governance) is one of the most important things you can take from this unit."

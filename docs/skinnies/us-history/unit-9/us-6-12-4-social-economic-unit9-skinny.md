# Skinny — US.6_12.4 Social & Economic Change · Unit 9 1990s–2000s

**Standard:** US.6_12.4 — Social and Economic Change
**Unit:** 9 — 1990s–2000s (Globalization, 9/11, Contemporary)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did globalization, the tech boom, 9/11, and the 2008 financial crisis change American society and economy? Who benefited, who was harmed?

---

## The Big Idea

The 1990s–2000s produced the most rapid economic and cultural change since industrialization. Globalization redistributed manufacturing jobs globally. The dot-com boom created enormous wealth in tech — then crashed. 9/11 changed how Americans thought about security. The 2008 financial crisis wiped out middle-class wealth accumulated over decades. These changes happened fast and were experienced very differently depending on class, race, and location.

---

## Key Social and Economic Changes — Unit 9

| Change | What Happened | Who Was Affected |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Tech boom / dot-com (1990s) | Internet economy; tech stock explosion; NASDAQ crashes 2000 | Tech workers boom; traditional retail decline |
| NAFTA (1994) | Free trade agreement US-Canada-Mexico | Manufacturing jobs move to Mexico; consumer prices lower |
| Globalization acceleration | WTO; China enters global trade; US manufacturing hollows out | Working class devastated; consumer prices lower |
| 9/11 and aftermath | 3,000 killed; security state expands; two wars | Muslim Americans face discrimination; veterans' lives changed |
| 2008 financial crisis | Housing bubble collapses; banks fail; recession | Worst since Depression; 8 million jobs lost; Black/Latino wealth wiped out disproportionately |
| Opioid epidemic (begins 1990s) | Pharmaceutical companies oversell opioids; addiction crisis | Rural white communities particularly affected |
| Income inequality | Top 1% share grows dramatically; middle class stagnates | Everyone below top; especially working class |

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.4 in Unit 9 is about proximity — these events are recent or ongoing.

**Example argument move:**
> "The 2008 financial crisis is often described as affecting 'everyone.' It didn't affect everyone equally. Black and Latino families had a higher percentage of their wealth in home equity (because they were excluded from stock market wealth-building). When housing prices collapsed, they lost a disproportionate share of their net worth. The racial wealth gap — already large from decades of exclusion — widened dramatically. Understanding the 2008 crisis means understanding who bore the cost of decisions they didn't make."

# Skinny — US.6_12.3 Political Systems · Unit 9 1990s–2000s

**Standard:** US.6_12.3 — Political Systems and Democratic Institutions
**Unit:** 9 — 1990s–2000s (Globalization, 9/11, Contemporary)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did American political institutions respond to the post-Cold War era and 9/11? What constitutional questions did the War on Terror raise?

---

## Key Political Moments — Unit 9

| Event | Constitutional Issue | Outcome |
|-------|---------------------|---------|
| Clinton impeachment (1998) | Lying under oath about affair; partisan impeachment | Acquitted by Senate; precedent: partisan impeachment possible |
| Bush v. Gore (2000) | Supreme Court stops Florida recount; Bush wins | 5-4 partisan ruling; Court's legitimacy questioned |
| PATRIOT Act (2001) | Broad surveillance authority; limited judicial oversight | Passed 98-1 in Senate six weeks after 9/11 |
| Department of Homeland Security | New cabinet department; largest reorganization since 1947 | New bureaucracy; intelligence coordination |
| Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF, 2001) | Gave president authority to use force against those responsible for 9/11 | Used to justify everything from Afghanistan to drone strikes |
| Abu Ghraib (2004) | US military tortures Iraqi prisoners; photographs surface | International credibility; Geneva Conventions debate |
| *Hamdi v. Rumsfeld* (2004) | Can US citizen be held as enemy combatant? | Supreme Court says no; due process required |
| Citizens United (2010) | Corporations can spend unlimited money in elections | Campaign finance transformed; dark money |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.3 in Unit 9 is about democratic backsliding — or the fear of it.

**Example argument move:**
> "*Bush v. Gore* (2000) is the most controversial Supreme Court decision since *Dred Scott*. Five justices stopped a recount that might have changed the presidential election, then explicitly said the ruling applied only to this case and couldn't be used as precedent. A court that decides a presidential election on partisan lines and refuses to set precedent raises serious questions about institutional legitimacy — questions that have only intensified since."

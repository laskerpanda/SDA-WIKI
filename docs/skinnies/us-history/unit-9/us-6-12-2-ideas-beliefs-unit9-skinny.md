---

## What This Standard Asks

What ideologies shaped the post-Cold War era, 9/11 response, and contemporary America? What arguments justified the War on Terror — and what arguments challenged it?

---

## The Big Idea

After the Cold War, American foreign policy needed a new ideological framework. Two emerged: liberal internationalism (spread democracy through international institutions) and neoconservatism (spread democracy through force). 9/11 gave neoconservatism its moment. The War on Terror was justified through a specific ideological argument — you are either with us or against us; preemptive war is legitimate; democracy can be imposed. The failures of Iraq and Afghanistan tested those arguments.

---

## Key Ideologies — Unit 9

| Ideology | Core Claim | Significance |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| "End of history" (Fukuyama, 1992) | Liberal democracy has won; it will spread everywhere | Hubris before 9/11; challenged by events |
| Neoconservatism | US should use military force to spread democracy; no need for international consensus | Bush Doctrine; Iraq War |
| Bush Doctrine | Preemptive war is legitimate; US will act unilaterally if necessary | Break from post-WWII international law framework |
| Islamist terrorism (Al-Qaeda's ideology) | Western influence corrupts Muslim societies; violence is legitimate response | 9/11; ongoing |
| "War on Terror" framework | Terrorism is an existential threat requiring wartime response | PATRIOT Act; surveillance state; Abu Ghraib |
| Polarization / culture war | American political identity increasingly tribal; facts contested | Accelerates from 1990s to present |

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.2 in Unit 9 is about how crises produce ideological commitments that outlast their context.

**Example argument move:**
> "The Bush Doctrine — preemptive war — was a major break from the post-WWII international legal framework the US had helped build. Nuremburg established that aggressive war is a crime. The UN Charter prohibits it. The Bush administration argued that 9/11 created an exception. The Iraq War was the result. Understanding the ideology is understanding how governments justify breaking the rules they themselves created."

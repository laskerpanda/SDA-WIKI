# Skinny — US.6_12.4 Social & Economic Change · Unit 7 Vietnam Era

**Standard:** US.6_12.4 — Social and Economic Change
**Unit:** 7 — Vietnam Era & Watergate (1964–1974)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

How did Vietnam and the social upheavals of the 1960s change American society? What movements emerged — and what did they achieve?

---

## The Big Idea

The 1960s and early 70s produced simultaneous social movements: Civil Rights, anti-war, women's liberation, American Indian Movement, Chicano movement, LGBTQ+ rights (Stonewall, 1969). These movements were interconnected — they shared language, tactics, and sometimes leadership. The era shows what happens when multiple marginalized groups demand change at the same time.

---

## Key Social Changes — Unit 7

| Movement / Event | What Happened | Legacy |
|-----------------|-------------|--------|
| Anti-war protest | Teach-ins, marches, draft card burning, Kent State | Changed public opinion; built coalition politics |
| Women's liberation | NOW (1966); Title IX (1972); *Roe v. Wade* (1973) | Expanded women's rights in education, workplace, reproduction |
| American Indian Movement (AIM) | Wounded Knee standoff (1973); Indian education rights | Limited legislative gains; heightened awareness |
| United Farm Workers (Chavez, Huerta) | Grape boycott; labor rights for agricultural workers | California labor gains; model for boycott tactics |
| Stonewall (1969) | LGBTQ+ resistance to police raid; beginning of gay rights movement | Annual Pride commemorations; path to legal equality |
| Great Society / War on Poverty | LBJ's domestic programs; Medicare, Medicaid, Head Start | Permanent social programs; debated effectiveness |
| Vietnam veteran experience | 58,000 dead; 304,000 wounded; PTSD not recognized | Agent Orange lawsuits; VA inadequacy; belated recognition |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.4 in Unit 7 is about movement interconnection.

**Example argument move:**
> "The 1960s social movements were interconnected in ways that went beyond their specific demands. The Civil Rights Movement taught the anti-war movement how to organize. The anti-war movement's tactics were borrowed by the women's liberation movement. The United Farm Workers borrowed from the Montgomery Bus Boycott. Each movement learned from the others — and the era's social transformation was larger than any single movement could have produced alone."

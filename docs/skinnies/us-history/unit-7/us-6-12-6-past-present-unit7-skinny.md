# Skinny — US.6_12.6 Past to Present · Unit 7 Vietnam Era

**Standard:** US.6_12.6 — Connecting History to the Present
**Unit:** 7 — Vietnam Era & Watergate (1964–1974)
**Grade:** 10 · US History

---

## What This Standard Asks

What from the Vietnam era and Watergate is still present today? How does this period explain current debates about presidential power, the press, and public trust?

---

## Then → Now Connections

| Then (1964–1974) | Now |
|-----------------|-----|
| Pentagon Papers → press freedom | WikiLeaks; Snowden; classified information debates |
| Watergate tapes → executive privilege | Every presidency since has claimed executive privilege |
| War Powers Resolution (1973) | Presidents have largely ignored it; still debated |
| Vietnam syndrome | Iraq and Afghanistan brought it back; "endless wars" debate |
| Draft resistance | All-volunteer military; debates about who serves |
| Credibility gap | "Fake news"; public distrust of institutions at record high |
| Watergate reform (campaign finance) | *Citizens United* (2010) reversed much of it |
| PTSD recognition (delayed, Vietnam era) | Still inadequate VA care; veteran suicide rates |
| Stonewall / LGBTQ+ movement | Marriage equality (2015); ongoing rights debates |
| Nixon's "silent majority" framing | Still used to claim mainstream support against protesters |

---

## The Argument Move

US.6_12.6 in Unit 7 is about institutional memory — we keep facing the same questions.

**Example argument move:**
> "The Espionage Act (1917) was used to prosecute Edward Snowden (2013) for leaking NSA surveillance documents. The Pentagon Papers precedent (*NY Times v. US*, 1971) was cited in Snowden's defense. The War Powers Resolution (1973) was invoked when Obama conducted operations in Libya (2011). The legal and political frameworks of the Vietnam era are still the tools we use to argue about government power and accountability — because we haven't replaced them."

# SDA Commons — Self-Directed Academy

**South High School · Fargo Public Schools · Grades 9–10**

This wiki is the knowledge infrastructure for the Self-Directed Academy. Students use it to understand how the program works, find skinnies, navigate standards, and access the reading library. It grows every studio cycle.

---

## Start here: What is SDA?

SDA is a mastery-based pathway inside South High. Instead of earning credit by sitting in class for a semester, you earn it by **demonstrating that you actually know something**. That demonstration happens through six-week learning cycles called **Studios**.

You are enrolled in two courses simultaneously: English I or II, and either World History or US History. In SDA, these aren't separate — they're one integrated program. A single studio produces evidence for both your English standard and your Social Studies standard.

**The core equation:**

> Standard = PBC = Task → Elevate

A **standard** is the official description of a skill or concept. Every standard in SDA maps to a **Project-Based Competency (PBC)** — a concrete thing you produce to show you know it. That PBC is a **task** you can actually do. And the goal is never just to complete the task — it is to **elevate**: to get better, to go deeper, to do something worth sharing.

## The Three Spaces

SDA runs through three interconnected spaces. Know where you are in each one.

### Space 1 — The Studio (where learning happens)

A Studio is a six-week learning cycle. Each one has:

- A **shared essential question** that the whole class anchors on — for example: *"How did early religions justify political authority?"*
- **Individual inquiry angles** — you pick your own angle inside that question. Three students might all be in the same Studio but investigating completely different civilizations.
- A **Phoenix track** — if you're ready, you can propose your own Studio entirely. That requires advisor approval and a written proposal.
- **Active standards** — 4–5 standards total (English + Social Studies) that you're building toward this cycle
- A **public exhibition** in Week 6 — your work goes in front of a real audience

### Space 2 — The Commons (shared resources)

The Commons is this wiki. It contains:

- **[The Skinny Library](/skinnies/templates/skinny-template.md)** — mini-lessons you pull when you hit a gap in a specific skill
- **The Studio Catalog** — pre-packaged studios you can choose from (see the catalog section in [How to SDA](/commons/how-to-sda/index.md))
- **[The Reading Library](/reading-library/index.md)** — sources, texts, and documents organized by standard and era

### Space 3 — The Command Center (teacher tracking)

The Command Center is the teacher-facing dashboard. It tracks your ownership level, your standard scores, and your studio progress. You don't live here — Reese does. But your scores flow there from Headrush.

## The Six-Week Studio Lifecycle

Every studio follows the same arc. If you understand this, you understand SDA.

| Week | Phase | What You're Doing |
|------|-------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Launch** | The essential question drops. You brainstorm, pick your inquiry angle, and submit your Studio Proposal. You declare: (a) what demo mode you'll use and (b) what your deliverable will be. |
| 2 | **Dig** | Research. Primary sources. Source log. Annotation. Pull skinnies when you hit a gap. |
| 3 | **Build** | Start making the thing. First draft of your essay, podcast, video, website — whatever your deliverable is. |
| 4 | **Shape** | Feedback from peers and your advisor. Revise. Document what changed and why. |
| 5 | **Finish** | Complete the demonstration of learning. Polish the deliverable. Prepare for exhibition. |
| 6 | **Publish** | Public exhibition. Your advisor scores your standard on the 1.0–4.0 scale based on your demo evidence. Your deliverable goes in the Studio Archive. |

## Two Things Every Studio Requires

These are not the same thing. Both matter.

### Demonstration of Learning

Proof that you know the standard. This must happen **before or alongside** the deliverable. You pick one of six modes when you submit your proposal:

| Mode | What It Looks Like |
|------|--------------------|
| 1 — Written Assessment | A focused written response to a specific prompt targeting the standard |
| 2 — Extended Writing | A standalone essay or analytical piece as direct evidence |
| 3 — Verbal Conversation | An advisor conference where you talk through your understanding |
| 4 — Visual/Creative | A sketchnote, infographic, or annotated diagram showing conceptual understanding |
| 5 — Multimedia/Performance | A podcast, video, debate, or presentation demonstrating mastery |
| 6 — Portfolio Annotation | You select existing work and write metacognitive commentary connecting it to the standard using 2.0/3.0/4.0 language |

**The proficiency score you receive is based on this demonstration.**

### Deliverable (The Studio Product)

The PBL artifact. This is the thing you make with what you know. It's what gets exhibited at Week 6 and goes into the Studio Archive.

Examples: essay, podcast episode, documentary, website, visual essay, museum exhibit, debate, photo essay, podcast series, research presentation, original artwork with artist statement.

**The distinction:** Demonstration of learning = "I know this." Deliverable = "I made something with what I know."

## Standards in SDA: How They Work

Every studio has 4–5 active standards drawn from this mix:

| Slot | Standard | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Social Studies | 1 WH or USH standard (always paired with an era or unit) | Content anchor for the whole studio |
| Reading | One of: R.2, R.4, R.7, R.8, R.9 | Primary source + literary text analysis |
| Writing | W.3 (explanatory) OR W.4 (argument) | Alternates each studio |
| Research | IR.1 + IR.2 + IR.3 + IR.4 + IR.5 (bundled) | Active every single studio, every cycle |
| Communication | C.1 and/or C.6 | Assessed at the Week 6 exhibition |

### The Proficiency Scale

All standards are scored on the **NDSBL 1.0–4.0 scale**:

| Score | What It Means |
|-------|--------------|
| 4.0 | Beyond the standard — in-depth inferences and applications that go further than what was taught |
| 3.5 | Beyond the standard with partial success |
| 3.0 | **Meeting the standard** — this is the target. No major errors or omissions. |
| 2.5 | Solid 2.0 knowledge + partial 3.0 knowledge |
| 2.0 | Foundation knowledge — vocabulary and basic processes, but missing the complex ideas |
| 1.5 | Partial 2.0 knowledge |
| 1.0 | With help, partial understanding of the 2.0 content |

**Grade mapping:** 4.0 = A (95%) · 3.5 = A- (91%) · 3.0 = B (83%) · 2.5 = B- (78%) · 2.0 = C (73%) · 1.5 = D (63%) · 1.0 = F (50%)

## Student Ownership Levels

You are always at one of three levels. Your level determines how much structure you get and how much independence you have.

| Level | Name | What It Means |
|-------|------|---------------|
| 1 | **Trailblazer** | Structured support. Advisor helps you narrow your inquiry angle. Proposals require approval before you begin. |
| 2 | **Maverick** | Growing independence. You collaborate freely, propose your own angles, make more of your own calls. |
| 3 | **Phoenix** | Full agency. You can propose entirely self-designed studios. You can mentor peers. Flex scheduling is available. |

Levels are reviewed every two weeks. You apply to move up. You can move down if the work isn't there.

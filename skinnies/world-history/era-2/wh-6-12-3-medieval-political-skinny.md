# How Medieval Political Systems Controlled People — Skinny

**Standard:** WH.6_12.3 — Analyze the effects of different political systems on people
**Era/Unit (if SS):** Era 2 — Middle Ages and the Renaissance (~500–1600 CE)
**ELA Crosswalk:** 9-10.W.4 — Write to persuade; argument writing with evidence and counterclaims
**NDSBL Target:** 3.0 score
**Estimated Time:** 60–90 minutes
**Subject:** World History + ELA crosswalk

---

## What You Need to Know (Score 2.0)

Before we can analyze how these systems *controlled* people, you must understand the "mechanics" of the Middle Ages. Use these definitions as your foundation:

- **Feudalism:** A social and political system where land was exchanged for service or loyalty. It created a strict hierarchy: King $\rightarrow$ Lord $\rightarrow$ Knight $\rightarrow$ Serf.
- **Vassal:** A person (usually a noble or knight) who received land from a higher lord in exchange for military service and political loyalty.
- **Fief:** The actual piece of land granted to a vassal.
- **Serf vs. Peasant:** While both were farmers, a **peasant** was a tenant farmer who could technically move or own small plots; a **serf** was "bound" to the land—they were not allowed to leave the manor without permission and were essentially tied to the lord's estate.
- **Manorial System:** The economic side of feudalism. It was the system of running a large estate (a manor) where peasants lived and worked in exchange for protection.
- **Pope / Papacy:** The Pope is the head of the Catholic Church. In the Middle Ages, the "Papacy" held massive political power, often rivaling the power of kings.
- **Excommunication:** A "social death sentence." If a person was excommunicated by the Church, they were kicked out of the church and banned from all religious services—a devastating tool for controlling kings.
- **Investiture Controversy:** A major conflict between the Pope and the Holy Roman Emperor over who had the right to appoint church officials (basically: Who is the boss of the Church?).
- **Magna Carta (1215):** The "Great Charter." It was a document that forced King John of England to admit that even the King was not above the law.
- **Common Law:** A system of law based on established customs and previous court rulings rather than just the whim of a ruler.
- **Divine Right of Kings:** The belief that a king’s authority comes directly from God, meaning they only had to answer to God, not to their people.

**Checklist for 2.0 Mastery:**
- [ ] I can identify who was at the top and bottom of the feudal pyramid.
- [ ] I can explain the difference between a peasant and a serf.
- [ ] I can define "Excommunication" as a political weapon.
- [ ] I can state what the Magna Carta did for English law.
- [ ] I can identify who held power in the Manorial system.

**2.0 Example:** *A student looks at an illustration of a medieval manor and correctly identifies that the serfs are "bound" to the land while the knights provide protection, explaining how this trade-off kept both groups in their specific roles.*

## The Standard (Score 3.0)

**WH.6_12.3:** Analyze the effects of different political systems on people.

To hit a **3.0**, you must move beyond just defining these terms. You must explain *how* these systems actually changed lives. In the Middle Ages, power was split between two main "bosses": the **Feudal Lord** (who controlled your land and safety) and the **Church** (which controlled your soul and social standing).

**3.0 Analysis Example:**
Instead of just saying "The Church was powerful," a 3.0 student would say: *"Because of the threat of Excommunication, even powerful kings were forced to compromise with the Pope during the Investiture Controversy. This meant that for common people like peasants and merchants, their daily lives were governed by both local lords who controlled their physical safety and the Church, which dictated their social behavior."*

**Distinction:**
- **2.0:** "The Magna Carta limited the King's power."
- **3.0:** "The Magna Carta was a turning point because it established that even the King had to follow Common Law, beginning the shift from 'Divine Right' toward a system where laws protected certain rights of the people."

---

## Going Further (Score 4.0)

To reach a **4.0**, you must perform a "transfer" task. This means taking these medieval concepts and applying them to a different context or making a complex comparison.

**4.0 Example:**
*Compare the "Divine Right of Kings" with modern-day "Constitutional Republics." A student might write an analysis arguing that while the Magna Carta was originally only meant to protect the rights of wealthy nobles, it created the legal precedent for modern democracy by establishing that no leader is above the law.*

## Deliverable Ideas (Your Studio Product)

1. **The "Power & Policy" Brief:** A one-page briefing for a modern government official explaining how the Magna Carta's limitation of royal power influenced our current system of checks and balances.
2. **The "Life in the Manor" Narrative:** A creative non-fiction piece written from the perspective of a serf, describing their daily life under the Manorial System and the restrictions placed upon them by their lord.
3. **The Great Debate Script:** A script for a mock debate between a 13th-century Bishop and a 21st-century politician regarding the role of religion in government policy.

## Scale Tasks

- [ ] **2.0 task:** Identify the roles of the King, Lord, Knight, and Serf in a diagram and explain what each person "gave" to get their status.
- [ ] **3.0 task:** Analyze how the combination of Feudalism (land control) and Church Authority (spiritual control) created a system where common people had very little personal freedom. Use the Magna Carta as evidence of the beginning of change.
- [ ] **4.0 task:** Evaluate whether the Magna Carta was a "genuine" step toward democracy or simply a pragmatic deal between elites, and connect this to how modern constitutions still attempt to limit executive power today.
